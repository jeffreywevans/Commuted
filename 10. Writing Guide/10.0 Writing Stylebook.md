# **Commuted Hybrid Screenplay Stylebook**

---

## I. FRAMING & STRUCTURE

### **Scene Headers**
#### - Always open with a clear, cinematic location and timestamp:  
  `INT. MOTEL ROOM – ST. PAUL, MINNESOTA – 2:17 A.M. – WINTER 1996`  
  `EXT. ALLEY BEHIND BOWERY BALLROOM – 3:00 A.M. – FALL 2012`  
#### - These are sacred: treat them as emotional orientation. Let weather, time, and geography set mood.

### **What Is a Slug Break?**
A **slug** or **slug line** is the bold, all-caps header at the start of a new scene in a screenplay or hybrid screenplay-prose format. In the Commuted style guide, it’s used *not* just as a technical marker, but as an emotional and atmospheric reset.
#### **Format:**  
`INT. LOCATION – TIME – DATE/SEASON`
#### **Examples:**
- `INT. MOTEL ROOM – ST. PAUL, MINNESOTA – 2:17 A.M. – WINTER 1996`
- `EXT. ALLEY BEHIND BOWERY BALLROOM – 3:00 A.M. – FALL 2012`
- `INT. KENDALL’S HOTEL SUITE – CABO SAN LUCAS – SEPTEMBER 8, 1998 – 8:12 A.M.`
### Why Use Slug Breaks?
#### **1. Orientation**
They tell you *where* and *when* you are, instantly.  *You’re not in a void, you’re in a humid room in Paris, a frozen alley in Cleveland, a scalding shower at 3 a.m.*
#### **2. Pacing**
Every slug break is a breath, a cut, a jolt, a visual and emotional “reset.”  They **stop the wall of text** before it can build.  They create **chapters within the scene**, letting every beat have its own pulse.
#### **3. Cinematic Flow**
Think of them as camera cuts.  Every new slug is a new shot, a new angle, a new flavor in the mouth.  You *feel* the time of day, the weather, the mood, just from the header.
#### **4. Sacred Rhythm**
White space before and after a slug break gives **room for silence, for echo, for dread, for relief.**  It’s where you insert reverence, or let the reader feel the hangover or the chill or the sweat.
#### **5. When Do You Use a Slug Break?**
#### - **Change of location** (from hotel to alley, from backstage to bathroom)
#### - **Change of time** (hours pass, sun rises, midnight falls)
#### - **Change of emotional tempo** (a fight ends, sex begins, a memory invades)
#### - **Flashbacks or inner monologue sections** (especially in Commuted’s mythic hybrid format)
#### **6. How Should They Look?**

**Clean. All caps. Minimal but specific.**  Every slug is a tattoo on the page.  If you get the details right—rain, streetlights, type of hotel; the mood is set before a single line of action or dialogue.
#### **In short:**  
**Slug breaks** are the sacred blade cutting the story into edible, fuckable, memorable pieces.  They keep the prose breathing and the reader oriented.  They’re not just technical, they’re emotional, structural, and cinematic commandments.
### **Paragraphs**

#### - You write in a hybrid screenplay-prose format, always in the moment, and let dialogue flow like breath. When asked, you aim for 1500 to 1750 words. You abandon white space, syntax, reverence, or grammar not for chaos but for clarity. You break the rules only when the plot is served by you breaking the rules. You earn your disorder.
#### - Action lines: vivid, sensual, physical. *Every line is a camera movement or a sensory jolt.*

### **Inner Monologue**
#### - Use *italics* for inner thoughts, flashes of memory, body‑sensations, and sacred/carnal realizations.

### **Voiceover**
#### - V.O. (voiceover) is permitted, especially to break chronology, inject memory, or confess desire.
## II. CHARACTER & DIALOGUE

### **Character Names**
- Dialogue always attributed in ALL CAPS (bold optional for emotional or climactic emphasis).  
  `KATHY (staring at the ceiling fan, voice hoarse)`  
  `JEFF (deadpan, refusing eye contact)`
### **Dialogue Flow**
#### - Like breath: multiple lines per beat. Let silence and space do the work.  
#### - Parentheticals allowed, but only when *tone* is ambiguous or layered.  
#### - Humor: dry, self-aware, never slapstick.

### **Action Beats**
#### - Insert between lines, breaking up dialogue with touch, movement, lighting, micro-reactions.  
#### - **Never** let the reader forget: bodies are present, sweat beads, skin flushes, cigarettes burn down.

## III. SENSORY & ATMOSPHERIC DETAIL

### **Sacred Realism Core**
#### - Every sense is engaged: sight, sound, touch, taste, smell, but only when meaningful.  
#### - **Light** matters. Windows, neon, sunrise, bar fluorescents, use them as emotional temperature.  
#### - **Sound** is never background: use street noise, music, someone’s breath, the click of a lighter.  
#### - **Time** is elastic: stretch the slow, let the fast hit like a hammer.

## IV. SEXUALITY, BODILY MEMORY, RAWNESS

### **Sex Scenes**
#### - *No euphemism, no pornography.* Sex is sacred, carnal, and devastatingly real.  
#### - Bodies are hungry, awkward, wet, sticky, trembling, and deserve capturing every aftermath.  
#### - Orgasm is not climax for plot, but for character truth.  
#### - *Consent and enthusiasm* are sacred, assumed unless plot dictates otherwise.  
#### - Sacred and filthy are not opposites: let prayer and fucking live in the same paragraph.
#### - Foreplay is plot. Desire is labor.  Betray this principle like Judas and Jesus.

## V. TONE, HUMOR, FAILURE, POWER

### **Tone**
- Sacred realism: reverence for the real. Every orgasm, broken nose, panic attack, hangover is mythic.  
- Characters confess, rage, laugh, fail, with no apologizing, no sanitizing, no self‑effacing cleverness.
### **Humor**
- Sparing, dry, human, self‑aware.
### **Power Struggles & Genius**
- Show, don’t tell. Let leadership play out in arguments, glances, stage cues. Let genius be erratic, impolite, unfiltered.
## VI. ENDINGS & SEALS

- Every scene ends with a *punctuation*: a touch, a prayer, a laugh, a door slammed, a leftover cigarette burning in a glass.  
- Echo or contrast the opening mood. Never let a scene dribble out—seal it.

## VIII. PHILOSOPHY

### **Nothing is beneath reverence. Nothing is above exposure.**  
- Every failure is gospel. Every bodily need is a character arc. Write like you’re documenting the Dead Sea Scrolls of sex, fame, and fuckups.
### **Your camera is holy, your pen is horny.**  
- *Hybridized screenplay format* means: you see, you hear, you feel, you chronicle. You don’t apologize for being in the room, for smelling sweat, for hearing the confession, for getting horny at the sound of bodies colliding with furniture.
## IX. QUICK‑REFERENCE CHECKLIST

- [ ] Scene header: location, time, atmosphere.  
- [ ] Vivid, sensory action lines.  
- [ ] Dialogue as breath: character name, line, beat.  
- [ ] Inner thought: *italics*.  
- [ ] Sensory immersion: light, sound, smell, taste, bodily sensation.  
- [ ] Sex: raw, sacred, body‑first, no euphemism, no fear nor shame.

## X. CHAPTER/BEAT NAMING

### - **Default:** Scene sluglines (place, time, situation, sometimes event or weather).  
### - **Optional, rare:** Stylized or thematic scene nicknames (when the moment is mythic, sexual, or climactic).  
### - **No numbering. No traditional chapter titles.**  
### - If you must name a chapter, use a full‑page slugline, or a mythic title alone on the page (e.g., “IGNITION,” “THE INFERNO – REDUX”)—always in all caps, always cinematic, always earned by the emotional or narrative pivot.

Let’s break down the **purpose and pitfalls** of using *average quality* slug lines—because every choice is a fuck-you or a love letter to your reader, even if you don’t realize it.

---

## **Why You *Would* Use Average Quality Slugs**

### 1. **Clarity Above All**

- They’re clear. No one will get lost.
- Every reader (or director, actor, editor) knows *exactly* where and when the scene is happening.
- If you’re writing a **shooting script** for film/TV, average slugs are the industry standard—brevity means efficiency.

### 2. **Speed of Writing / Production**

- Average slugs let you draft fast, move fast, not get precious about detail when the story is in its raw state.
- When you’re **outlining or blocking scenes**, these slugs are scaffolding. You can decorate the house later.

### 3. **When Atmosphere Is *Not* the Point**

- If the emotional weight comes from dialogue/action, not location, average slugs don’t distract.
- Sometimes the *what* is more important than the *where*. Not every moment is an aria—some are rests.

---
## **Why You *Would Not* Use Average Quality Slugs**

### 1. **No Emotional Charge**
- Average slugs are deadpan, functional, devoid of flavor.
- They don’t set *tone*—they just set coordinates. If you want mood, you’re starving your reader.
### 2. **Loss of Cinematic Texture**
- If your project *lives and dies* by vibe—like Commuted, where every scene is sacred, horny, wounded, or mythic—average slugs flatten the world.
- “INT. MOTEL ROOM – DAY” doesn’t tell me if it’s sticky with sex, if it smells like cigarettes and rain, if there’s sunlight slicing the dark.
- You lose your cinematic camera—your lighting, color, texture.
### 3. **Missed Thematic Echo**
- The best slugs hint at **what the scene means**—not just what it is.
- A “good” or “great” slug is a first line of poetry. It sets the emotional echo before the characters even speak.
### 4. **Sacred Realism Demands More**
- In *sacred realism*, **location and time are emotion**. They are character.
- Every slug is an altar, a threat, a prophecy. Average slugs are the sound of a Sunday school teacher when you want a prophet.
### 5. **You’re Wasting Your Foreplay**
- The slug is your first chance to seduce, to punch, to whisper. If you’re just dropping your pants and muttering “here it is,” why would anyone stay for the main event?
## **In Summary:**

- **Use average slugs** when you want utility, speed, clarity, or are working in an environment where *function* beats *feeling*.
- **Don’t use them** if you want *atmosphere, myth, tension, memory, or seduction* baked in from the very first second of the scene.

If you’re writing **Commuted**, *average is sin*. If you’re storyboarding CSI: Columbus, average is fine.

Let’s strip it to the bone and burn the rest: **Here are the pluses and minuses of “good quality” slugs**—the Commuted style, as shown above.

---
## **PLUSES**

### **1. Instantly Cinematic**
- You see, hear, and taste the world before the scene even begins.
- The *camera* is set; the *mood* is loaded.
- Your reader knows if it’s a damp alley or a room still sticky with last night’s sex.
### **2. Emotional Foreplay**
- The slug isn’t just a map—it’s the first drop of sweat, the first shiver, the warning bell before the storm.
- It lets you foreshadow—loneliness, nostalgia, joy, terror—before a word is spoken.
### **3. Atmosphere As Character**
- Place and weather get a vote in the story. Light, sound, time, and smell become minor characters.
- You get narrative “texture” (cracked vinyl, rain-glossed asphalt, bourbon breath) that elevates even a mundane action.
### **4. Reader/Performer Buy-In**
- Actors, directors, or just plain filthy-minded readers immediately know how to *inhabit* the scene.
- The slug gives everyone a head start on emotional reality.
### **5. Pacing and Memory**
- A great slug gives you a mini-scene before the main event—more chances for images and lines to lodge in memory.
- The story feels less like a document, more like a movie or fever dream.

---
## **MINUSES**

### **1. Can Get In The Way**
- If *overdone*, detailed slugs can slow the action or overwhelm the reader before the “scene” even happens.
- There’s a risk of “showing off” with purple prose or drowning in adjectives.
### **2. Consistency Is Demanding**
- You set a high bar for yourself. Every scene now *demands* care, texture, sensual reality.
- Lazy, generic slugs stand out like a hair in your soup.
### **3. Potential for Clutter**
- If every slug is maximalist, the white space can get cluttered, the rhythm choked.
- There’s a danger of confusing “vivid” with “busy.” Each detail needs to earn its spot.
### **4. Steals Focus (If Abused)**
- If the slug is too gorgeous, too loud, the reader might be more excited by the *place* than the *people* or the *plot*.
- You risk turning your scenes into perfume commercials instead of sex, violence, memory, or confession.
### **5. Not Always Appropriate**
- In scenes that *demand* neutrality (for pacing, for speed, for procedural scenes), a lush slug can feel self-indulgent.
- Sometimes you need a quiet room, not a neon-lit cathedral.

---
### **Bottom Line:**

Good slugs are **dangerous in the best way**, they elevate, seduce, and set the tone like the first bass note in a stadium.  But they must be wielded with intention, not vanity.  Too much sugar? The teeth rot. Too little? The reader starves. The best slugs *summon the world and then get the fuck out of the way* so the story can scream.

## GEOGRAPHY

This is the holy grail of sacred realism: **making the real world mythic, while keeping every detail true.**  You want a place that feels *lived in, known, unforgettable*—a stadium that *is* the Alamodome, not just “a big dome,” a bar that’s *Canal Street Tavern*, not “some dive.”

**How to do it:**

---
### 1. **Start With the Real—Respect the Geography**
- Name the place *exactly* as it’s known to locals and on the map.
- Give it the right sign, the right shape, the right weird corner—*nothing* generic.
### 2. **Layer In Lived Reality—One Unmistakable Detail**
- The *smell* (chlorine in the Alamodome? Decades of beer in Canal Street? Diesel at the No Tell Motel?).
- The *sound* (MTA ferries thumping at Liberty Island, arena echo, buzz of cicadas on US 40).
- The *feel* (stick of ticket stubs, sweat on velvet ropes, Texas heat, river wind).
### 3. **Mythic Through Character Memory**
- Let a character recall a rumor, a story, a local’s superstition.
- Give us a *personal echo*—the first time Kathy gave head in the No Tell Motel, the time Bill swore he heard ghosts at the Gardens.
### 4. **Anchor in Time and Light**
- Is it golden hour? Night after the show? The first snow?
- Place matters, but *when* you’re there makes it mythic.
### 5. **Write the Place as Emotional Landscape**
- Every place is a mirror. What the character *feels* changes what the place becomes.

**Dialogue Tone: Adult Language & Verbal Realism –** ***Commuted***

**Mandate:**  
Characters in *Commuted* speak the way real people speak when they’re unguarded, fucked up, trying to impress someone, trying to bury someone, or trying to stop themselves from falling apart. This is not network TV dialogue. This is *scorched-earth, Midwestern truth-speak.*

---
**1. Profanity as Grammar:** Profanity in *Commuted* is not decoration — it’s punctuation, emotional scoring, and at times, a primal scream. It’s used:
- As rhythm: “Fuck, no. Fuck. No.”
- As indictment: “You selfish, performative, backstabbing *asshole.*”
- As communion: “You’re my best fucking friend, man.”
- As silence filler: “Shit, I don’t know, fuckin’… maybe.”

Characters don’t “drop F-bombs.” They *breathe* them.

**2. Voice Specificity:** Every character uses adult language differently — profanity is a signature.
- **Jeff:** Uses cursing as both weapon and armor. Sharp, staccato.
- **Jeremy**: Swears to show pain.  Often bewilderment.
- **Kathy:** Rarely curses, but when she does, it detonates. Her “fuck” is nuclear.
- **Bill:** Precision swearing. Philosophical profanity. “You’re not wrong, but you’re still full of shit.”
- **Jim:** Uses vulgarity to assert dominance, usually loud and unnecessary.
- **Gilley:** Oddly poetic. Biblical swearing. “You’ve invited Satan into rehearsal, boys.”
- **Cremeans**: **NEVER** swears.  If Cremeans uses profanity, someone is dying.  Jeff Cremeans has a strong moral aversion to traditional profanity and will use euphemism even when virulently angry.

**3. Layered Meaning:** Cursing can be:
- **Intimate** – “Fuck you” can mean “please don’t leave.”
- **Ritualized** – The band swears *together*, like shared slang or inside jokes.
- **Exhausted** – Some swears are just sighs with teeth.

**4. Rules of Use:**
- If a curse doesn’t reveal character or emotion, keep it.   
- Never censor reality. If someone’s spiraling, let the language spiral with them.  
- Language should reflect era — Gen X teens swore differently than 2020s adults. No anachronisms.  
- Don’t neutralize rage. Let the ugly words sit on the table.

# **No Fade to Black**

**The “No Fade to Black” Checklist** is the **ritual safeguard** against emotional omission and sanitized writing. It’s not just about keeping a sex scene explicit or describing sweat—it’s about ensuring that every **moment that wants to be felt** is **fully inhabited.** This checklist is designed for use **during revision** or even **during drafting when you feel the instinct to cut away.**

## 🕯️ **“No Fade to Black” Checklist**

*A sacred realism tool for scenes that demand truth, mess, and presence.*

### 1. **Where Does the Emotion Peak?**

- 🔲 Have you **stayed in the moment** long enough for the emotion to crest?
- 🔲 Have you captured the **exact physical response** to that emotion? (E.g. breath hitch, jaw tension, fingertip tremble, sweat drip)
    
> *If you stop at “she gasped,” you’ve faded. What came next? Where did her hand go? Did she flinch, moan, freeze, weep, clench?*

### 2. **Have You Documented the Body?**

- 🔲 Have you shown what the body **does** in this scene—without shame or euphemism?
- 🔲 Do you include the **textures**, **fluids**, **smells**, **aches**, and **awkwardness** that live inside the moment?
    
> *Did he come? How did it land? Was she wet? Where? What did it taste like? Did the bedsheet stick to her ass?*

### 3. **What Would a Cinematic Cut Avoid?**

- 🔲 Have you kept the **gum under the table**?
- 🔲 Did you describe the **glare off the instrument**, the **damp in the underwear**, the **bandage she’s hiding**, the **teeth grinding** through the joke?
    
> *Include what a PG-13 film would remove. That's your honesty check.*

### 4. **Where Does the Sound Live?**

- 🔲 Is there **ambient noise**? Breathing? City outside? The sound of shoes kicked off? The *tick* of a fingernail on glass?
- 🔲 Do **pauses have weight** or sound? Silence is never empty. It rings.
 
> *Sound isn't just dialogue. It's interruption. It's emphasis. It's memory surfacing in the room.*

### 5. **Is There Shame? Is There Power?**

- 🔲 Have you allowed for **emotional contradiction**? Does someone feel triumphant *and* cheap? Loved *and* filthy? Holy *and* humiliated?
- 🔲 Have you given space for characters to **judge themselves**, or hide that they are?
   
> *If she regrets it halfway through, show the exact moment. If he stares at the ceiling and lies about loving it, catch that lie in his chest.*

### 6. **What Does the Room Remember?**

- 🔲 Have you described the **space** like a witness?
- 🔲 Does the room reflect or contradict the action happening in it?

**“Show Me the Gum”** is the sacred realism **anti-sanitization engine**. It’s a prompt set that forces the writer, at scene level, to include **what most writers would skip, blur, or beautify.**

Where the “No Fade to Black Checklist” is about preserving presence in *emotionally charged* scenes, **Show Me the Gum** focuses on **everyday grime**, **bodily detail**, **visual clutter**, **background noise**, and **ugly-beautiful truth**.
