**“Show Me the Gum”** is the sacred realism **anti-sanitization engine**. It’s a prompt set that forces the writer—at scene level—to include **what most writers would skip, blur, or beautify.**

Where the “No Fade to Black Checklist” is about preserving presence in *emotionally charged* scenes, **Show Me the Gum** focuses on **everyday grime**, **bodily detail**, **visual clutter**, **background noise**, and **ugly-beautiful truth**. It ensures that **texture**, **residue**, and **inconvenient realness** are written into every layer of the world.  This is part of the [[10.0 Writing Stylebook|Writing Stylebook]].

---

## 🧠 **How It Works**

You use the prompts:

- While drafting a scene where everything feels *too clean* or *too tidy.*
- During revision when you're suspicious that a moment feels too aesthetic, too symbolic, or too vague.
- Or as warm-up drills for diving back into character POV or physical space.
   
Each prompt is a dare:

> **What are you not showing because it feels too ugly, mundane, or off-brand? Show it. Name it. Let it rot or shine.**

---

## 🧽 **“Show Me the Gum” Prompt Set**

*Realism lives in the residue.*

---

### 🩸 **Body Reality Prompts**

- What’s clinging to their skin? Sweat? Dust? Fluids? Fabric fuzz?
- What smells are in the room and *who put them there*?  
- Is something chafing, itching, leaking, swelling, or sticking?
- What does the armpit of their shirt feel like at 4 p.m.?
- Is there a scar, bruise, pimple, cold sore, or hangnail that catches the light?

---

### 🪑 **Space Memory Prompts**

- What has this room seen that no one talks about?
- What’s under the bed / couch / table right now?  
- Is there trash or something forgotten in a corner?  
- What’s on the windowsill — dust, fingerprints, leftover ash, a melted candle?   
- What noise keeps happening in the background but nobody reacts to it anymore?   

---

### 🍕 **Food & Mouth Prompts**

- What’s stuck in someone’s teeth?
- Who’s chewing too loudly?
- What does the coffee actually taste like?
- Is someone’s mouth dry and clicking?
- Is there a crusty ketchup packet or sticky soda ring on the table?

---

### 💔 **Emotion + Physicality Prompts**

- What physical reaction is someone hiding *poorly*?
- What’s their face doing when they think no one is watching?
- What’s their posture lying about?
- What tension is in their shoulders, thighs, jaw?
- What do they do with their hands when silence gets awkward?

---

### 💦 **Aftermath Prompts**

- What does the floor smell like after the crowd leaves?
- What sticks to the back of someone’s leg when they stand up?
- What’s the temperature of the sheet under her back?
- How does their body register the absence of touch?
- What was *left behind* that someone will find later?

## 🧰 **How to Use It in Practice**

### 📝 *As a Pre-Scene Exercise*

Pick 2–3 prompts from each section and answer them *in the voice of the character.*

> Example (Bill at Lollapalooza):  
> "It’s 3:34. His forehead’s an oil slick. He tastes Gatorade and something bitter. There’s a zit forming just inside his nostril. He doesn’t want to touch the synth keys because his fingers feel like they belong to a corpse who drank beer in the sun."

---

### 🔍 *As a Revision Layer*

When reviewing a scene, stop after each paragraph and ask:

> What would I *see* if I lowered the camera 18 inches?  
> What would I *smell* if I got 3 inches closer?  
> What does this scene not want me to notice — and why?

---

### 🔻 NEW PROMPT:

> **📸 What’s 18 inches lower than the camera wants to go?**  
> *What detail lives just beneath the frame — not because it’s obscene, but because it’s inconvenient, unpretty, or vulnerable? Lower the lens. Write it.*

---

### 🩸 Example 1: **Kathy, Post-Gig, Hotel Room Floor**

> She unzipped her jeans while sitting, one boot still on, and tugged them halfway down before giving up. The cotton of her underwear had been damp since the encore. Not from sex. From sweat and time and nothing breathing. Fucking Memphis in August!
> 
> When she finally kicked free, she scratched at her thigh where the elastic had pressed too long. Her bikini line was raw—two days of stubble and the scar where she nicked her labia with a disposable Bic razor that shook in her hand.
> 
> She didn’t care. Not really. She just laid back on the floor, let the air hit her, and closed her eyes.
> 
> *This was what real felt like.*
> 
> A embroidered towel under her head. A concert wristband scratching her wrist. The blood still loud in her ears.

### 🩸 Example 2: **Jeff, Mid-Lyric Rewrite, Alone in Studio**

> He sat cross-legged on the hardwood with his guitar on his lap and his jeans unzipped, not for relief, but because the waistband had dug into his gut for three hours straight.
>

> 
> He scratched himself absently and picked at the head of a zit on his jaw. It popped. He didn’t flinch.
> 
> *Real lyricism happens like this*, he thought.
