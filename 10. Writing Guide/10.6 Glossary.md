## 📚 **CRAFT TERMS FOR ADVANCED WRITERS**

### **A**

- **Act Structure** – The shape of a narrative, often in 3 or 5 parts (setup, conflict, rising action, climax, resolution). More jazz than formula. Useful when tracking thematic pivots.
- **Anaphora** – The repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses for emphasis.  
    *E.g.: “She moaned. She arched. She begged.”*


### **B**

- **Beats** – The smallest unit of storytelling. A shift, reaction, or moment of meaning. Can be emotional, physical, or tonal.
- **Beat Sheet** – A barebones outline of major narrative beats. Not the plot itself, but the emotional architecture.
- **Blocking** – The choreography of character movement, especially in scene. Helps control pacing and subtext.

### **C**

- **Chekhov’s Gun** – The principle that everything introduced in a story must serve a purpose.  
    *If there’s a loaded revolver on the nightstand in Act I, it better go off by Act III.*
- **Compression** – Packing dense meaning into fewer words. A vital revision tool.
- **Cross-cutting** – Alternating between two scenes occurring simultaneously to increase tension, rhythm, or thematic echo (which you *excel* at).
- **Control the Camera** – A directing metaphor: choose what’s “in frame.” What are we seeing? What’s withheld?

### **D**

- **Dramatic Irony** – The audience knows something the characters don’t. Fuels dread, humor, and heartbreak.
- **Diegesis** – Everything that exists within the narrative world (diegetic music vs. background score). Useful for scenes with environmental texture.

### **E**

- **Emotional Logic** – Events that make sense not because they are rational but because they feel true to the character’s psyche.
- **Echo** – Repetition of theme, language, or imagery across a scene or book. Think of it like a harmony line.

### **F**

- **Focalization** – Whose eyes we’re seeing through at any moment. Not the narrator—*the consciousness inside the prose*.
- **Free Indirect Discourse** – Blending third-person narration with a character’s internal voice.  
    *E.g.: “He was late. Of course he was late. That damn alarm…”*

### **G**

- **Goal-Obstacle Stakes** – The basic engine of scene.  
    Character wants X → something stops them → tension escalates.

### **I**

- **Interior Monologue** – The character’s unspoken thoughts. Can be italicized or embedded in prose (you often embed, and do it well).
- **In Medias Res** – Starting in the middle of the action, bypassing setup. A pacing trick.

### **M**

- **Modulation** – A tonal shift within a scene: humorous → dangerous, flirty → confessional. Adds complexity and rhythm.

### **O**

- **On-the-Nose Dialogue** – Dialogue that says exactly what the character means. Often a problem unless used ironically.
- **Objective Correlative** – A physical object that reflects a character’s emotional state.  
    *E.g.: a cracked guitar mirror reflecting Jeff’s internal damage.*

### **P**

- **Pacing** – The rhythm of a scene. Controlled by sentence length, paragraph breaks, action density.
- **Point of View (POV)** – Who’s experiencing the scene? Controlled tightly, it builds tension and intimacy.  
    *You use tight 3rd-person with remarkable consistency.*

### **R**

- **Resonance** – When an idea, image, or phrase lingers or echoes between scenes. The *aftertaste* of good prose.
- **Rhetorical Rhythm** – The musicality of your sentence structure. Balanced clauses, rising and falling action in the line.

### **S**

- **Subtext** – What’s not said but is felt.  
    *E.g.: “You’re late again,” might mean “I’m scared you’ll leave me.”*
- **Scene/Sequel Structure** – An old school breakdown:  
    **Scene =** goal → conflict → disaster.  
    **Sequel =** reaction → dilemma → decision.  
    You often intuit this already, especially in sex scenes.
- **Stakes** – What the character stands to lose. Good scenes always cost something.

### **T**

- **Tone Control** – Keeping your emotional register consistent within a scene—or deliberately shifting it with purpose.
- **Turn** – A sudden shift in direction: in emotion, topic, power, or truth. Shakespearean sonnets do it. So does great prose.

### **U**

- **Unreliable Narrator** – A voice that can’t be fully trusted. In third-person, this can bleed through internal thoughts, contradiction, or omissions.

# **D Standard**

![D Standard](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=22dBq--nvF0)

D Standard refers to ==a guitar tuning where all strings are lowered by a whole step compared to standard E tuning, resulting in the notes D-G-C-F-A-D from the lowest to the highest string==. This alternate tuning provides a lower, heavier sound and looser strings, which can make the guitar easier to play and allow for different chord shapes and melodic ideas, though it changes the feel and response of the instrument. 

**How it compares to Standard Tuning** 

- **Standard Tuning (E Standard):** E-A-D-G-B-E
- **D Standard Tuning:** D-G-C-F-A-D

**What it does**

- **Lower Pitch:**  The most obvious change is that the entire guitar is pitched lower, giving a thicker, darker, and more "beefy" tone, according to [Riffhard](https://www.riffhard.com/how-to-tune-to-d-standard-on-guitar-tuna/). 

- **Looser Strings:** The strings have less tension, making them easier to bend and fret, which can be beneficial for certain playing styles and for reducing hand strain. 

- **New Sonic Possibilities:** D Standard opens up a new range of chord voicings and melodic ideas that aren't easily accessible in standard tuning. 

## **Who uses it**

D Standard tuning is popular in heavy music genres like metal, stoner rock, and doom metal, where the lower, heavier sound is desirable. Bands like Mastodon, Metallica, Dream Theater, and Judas Priest have used this tuning. 

## **Why use it**

- **Heavier Sound:** It's a great tuning for achieving a powerful and crushing tone, perfect for heavy riffs. 
- **Flexibility:** You retain the relative intervals of standard tuning, but at a lower pitch, making it easy to use standard chord shapes. 
- **Experimentation:** It can help break a guitarist out of a creative rut by providing a different way to approach the instrument.

# **Snake Case**

Snake case is a naming convention that separates words in a variable, function, or file name using underscores (_) and writes all letters in lowercase. It is widely used in programming languages like Python and for database table names to improve readability and consistency. A common variation, screaming snake case, uses all capital letters for constants, such as MAX_VALUE. 
 
## **How to Use Snake Case**

1. Lowercase: All letters in the name should be in lowercase.
2. Underscore Separators: Use an underscore (_) character to separate words. 
3. No Leading/Trailing Underscores: Do not start or end the name with an underscore.

## **Examples** 

- Variables: user_name, total_count
- Functions: calculate_average, find_all_users
- Filenames: my_script.py
- Screaming Snake Case (for constants): MAX_BUFFER_SIZE, PI_VALUE

## **Common Use Cases**

- Python: It is the standard convention for variables and functions according to the Python style guide, PEP 8. 
- Databases: Used for naming tables and columns.
- Constants: Screaming snake case is often used for defining constants in various programming languages, as shown in C, Java, and JavaScript. 

## **Advantages**

- Readability: Underscores clearly show word boundaries, making names easier to read and understand.
- Consistency: Promotes a consistent style across a project or team.
