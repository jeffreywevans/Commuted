**Actor Note from Jennifer Connolly on** **Sarah** **Malcolm** 

Setting: Jennifer Connolly sits in a dimly lit, intimate behind the scenes interview space, wearing a simple sweater and jeans. She’s thoughtful, her hands gesturing lightly as she talks. The tone is conversational but intense, like she’s deeply invested in her character.

"Alright, let me break **Sarah** **Malcolm** down for you. **Sarah** is… complicated, and I love her for it. She’s a character who is, on the surface, the epitome of strength and professionalism. You see her walking into boardrooms, absolutely owning them, wielding her intelligence and charisma like weapons. She’s the person you want on your side because, if she isn’t, you don’t stand a chance. 

But underneath that polished, badass exterior? She’s a mosaic of cracks, just barely holding together. And those cracks are where the story lives. 

**Sarah** started at Island Records as this fresh-faced, data-savvy Columbia graduate in the early ‘90s. She was brilliant, hungry, and, like so many women in male-dominated industries, constantly underestimated. But she thrived, not just because she was smarter than everyone else—though she absolutely was—but because she learned to navigate the egos and politics of the music business without losing her soul. 

Except… maybe she did lose pieces of it along the way. 

The affair with **Gilley** is a perfect example. It’s not just a tawdry secret; it’s this metaphor for how **Sarah** tried to reconcile her personal desires with her professional ambitions. She truly loved him, but the affair was also her way of accessing the band’s inner world, understanding their dynamics in ways no one else could. It gave her this edge, this insight, but it came at a cost. She’s always balancing on this moral tightrope, and sometimes she falls off. 

Fast forward to now, in 2025. **Sarah**’s at the pinnacle of her career, but the cancer diagnosis flips everything on its head. She’s spent decades controlling her narrative, controlling her environment, and now she’s faced with something she can’t control. It’s devastating, but it also forces her to confront what really matters. She’s grappling with legacy—both her own and the band’s—and it’s excruciating to watch her realize she won’t be around to see the impact of her life’s work. 

As an actor, playing **Sarah** is like a gift wrapped in barbed wire. I get to explore her as this powerhouse, but also as this deeply flawed, deeply human woman. She’s fiercely protective of the band, even when they drive her insane, and she’s desperate to ensure their success outlasts her. Her relationship with **Madeline** is fascinating because **Madeline** is basically a younger version of **Sarah** and seeing how that dynamic plays out is both heartbreaking and, honestly, kind of scary. 

One of the most gut-wrenching things about **Sarah** is how much she sacrifices for the band, for Island Records, and for her vision of what music can mean to people. She’s the kind of character who makes you question what you’d be willing to give up for greatness—and whether it’s worth it. 

I’ll be honest, there are moments in this show that are almost too raw to play. The scene where **Sarah** receives her hospice recommendation? I had to walk away from the set afterward and just breathe. But that’s the beauty of **Sarah**. She’s real, she’s messy, and she’s unforgettable. 

And let’s not forget her wit. She’s got this razor-sharp sense of humor that she uses like armor. Even when she’s crumbling inside, she’ll hit you with a one-liner that leaves you reeling. It’s this mix of brilliance and vulnerability that makes her so compelling—and so relatable. 

**Sarah**’s story is a tragedy, yeah, but it’s also a celebration of resilience, of fighting for what you believe in even when the odds are stacked against you. And honestly, I think she’d be okay with that. Because if there’s one thing **Sarah** **Malcolm** knows how to do, it’s leaving an indelible mark."
