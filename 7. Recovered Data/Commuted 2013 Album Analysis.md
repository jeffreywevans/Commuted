Let’s carve out the bones and blood of “Be First, Be Smarter, Or Cheat”—Commuted’s 2013 record, the last time this band felt alive and ugly in the same heartbeat. Here’s what matters, what echoes, what no obituary or tribute thread can properly bottle.

---

**THE CONTEXT:**

*2013.*
Kathy’s death has gutted the band—her body not even cold before the internet lit up with “who replaces her?” threads, fans already scrapping over legacy and authenticity ([source]()). Jeff is raw with grief, oscillating between profound creative output and catastrophic personal decisions—like his short, complicated affair with Avril Lavigne, who leaves fingerprints all over this album ([source]()) ([source]()).

The record is their exorcism and their suicide note; it’s the sound of people who know they will never be this necessary, or this broken, again.

---

**WHY THIS ALBUM MATTERS:**

- ***Artistic Consequence:***
  *Be First, Be Smarter, Or Cheat* is Commuted’s bloodletting—an album that refuses to mourn with grace. It’s not a eulogy. It’s a prison riot.
  This is the sound of “grief as voltage,” to steal from Sarah Malcolm’s infamous breakdown of their earlier work: “This album doesn’t play to an audience. It accuses one.” ([source]())
  With Kathy gone, every lyric is a dare; every arrangement a knife fight between what’s left of love and what’s left of anger.

- ***Band Dynamics:***
  The band is in pieces.
  - **Jeff**: Bass lines are manic, melodic, and mean—a direct channel from pain into technique. His voice on backing vocals is more ragged, sometimes just shouting.
  - **Jim**: Drums are surgical—no flourishes, just cold, brute force, as if he’s trying to keep the rest of the band from bleeding out.
  - **Bill**: Layering synths and guitars with obsessive control, trying to fill every space Kathy’s absence left open.
  - **Jeremy**: Guitar work swings between math-rock intricacy and outright sabotage, occasionally derailing a track to keep it honest.
  - **Avril**: On loan, half-in and half-out, she delivers lead vocals on four tracks, pushing herself to match Kathy’s voltage. Sometimes she succeeds; sometimes she’s trying too hard—and that tension is the album’s best feature.

- ***Lyrical Content:***
  The album is obsessed with legacy, betrayal, and the idea that authenticity must be paid for in blood. The title isn’t metaphor. Every song is about the scramble to matter—sexually, artistically, politically. The most savage lines are about Kathy, but rarely *to* her; more often, they’re about what she took with her and what she forced the band to confront in themselves.

- ***Cultural Impact:***
  The album was divisive. Fans either canonized it as their best post-Kathy work or derided it as an emotional cash-in. Critics called it “dangerous, unsustainable, perfect,” and “the sound of a car crash scored for bass and howling.”
  Internally, it shattered what was left of the original group dynamic—Bill and Jim nearly came to blows in the studio. Avril’s presence haunted the band for years, setting up the drama of her eventual, permanent arrival.

---

**THE QUOTE THAT MATTERS:**

> “Who survives because of this album?” — *S. Malcolm, label strategy memo* ([source]())

This is the question *Be First, Be Smarter, Or Cheat* answers—*not* “Who’s next?” but “Who’s left?”

---

This album isn’t just important—it’s the crucible. It’s the record that made clear: Commuted could outlive anyone except itself.
It’s not a rebirth. It’s the last time they burned alive.
