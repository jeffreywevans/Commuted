Based on the memory logs, character sketches, interviews, essays, and sacred realism templates from the Commuted band archive, here is the **official style guide** for the *hybridized screenplay-prose* format—a literary camera that fuses carnality and confession, memoir and myth, dialogue and memory. This is the holy text for anyone tasked with writing *truth* for this band.

---

## The Commuted Hybrid Screenplay Style Guide

### I. FRAMING & STRUCTURE

**Scene Headers**
- Always open with a clear, cinematic location and timestamp:
  - `INT. MOTEL ROOM – ST. PAUL, MINNESOTA – 2:17 A.M. – WINTER 1996`
  - `EXT. ALLEY BEHIND BOWERY BALLROOM – 3:00 A.M. – FALL 2012`
- These are sacred: treat them as emotional orientation. Let weather, time, and geography set mood.

**Paragraphs**
- Short. Clean. Ruthless with white space. No walls of text. Dialogue and action should breathe.
- Action lines: vivid, sensual, physical. *Every line is a camera movement or a sensory jolt.*

**Inner Monologue**
- Use *italics* for inner thoughts, flashes of memory, body-sensations, and sacred/carnal realizations.

**Voiceover**
- V.O. (voiceover) is permitted—especially to break chronology, inject memory, or confess desire.

---

### II. CHARACTER & DIALOGUE

**Character Names**
- Dialogue always attributed in ALL CAPS (bold optional for emotional or climactic emphasis).
  - `KATHY (staring at the ceiling fan, voice hoarse)`
  - `JEFF (deadpan, refusing eye contact)`

**Dialogue Flow**
- Like breath: one line per beat. Let silence and space do the work.
- Parentheticals allowed, but only when *tone* is ambiguous or layered.
- Humor: dry, self-aware, never slapstick.

**Action Beats**
- Insert between lines, breaking up dialogue with touch, movement, lighting, micro-reactions.
- **Never** let the reader forget: bodies are present, sweat beads, skin flushes, cigarettes burn down.

---

### III. SENSORY & ATMOSPHERIC DETAIL

**Sacred Realism Core**
- Every sense is engaged: sight, sound, touch, taste, smell—but only when meaningful.
- **Light** matters. Windows, neon, sunrise, bar fluorescents—use them as emotional temperature.
- **Sound** is never background: street noise, music, someone’s breath, the click of a lighter.
- **Time** is elastic: stretch the slow, let the fast hit like a hammer.

---

### IV. SEXUALITY, BODILY MEMORY, RAWNESS

**Sex Scenes**
- *No euphemism, no pornography.* Sex is sacred, carnal, and devastatingly real.
- Bodies are hungry, awkward, wet, sticky, trembling—capture every aftermath.
- Orgasm is not climax for plot, but for character truth.
- *Consent and enthusiasm* are sacred, assumed unless plot dictates otherwise.
- Sacred and filthy are not opposites: let prayer and fucking live in the same paragraph.

---

### V. TONE, HUMOR, FAILURE, POWER

**Tone**
- Sacred realism: reverence for the real. Every orgasm, broken nose, panic attack, hangover is mythic.
- Characters confess, rage, laugh, fail—no apologizing, no sanitizing, no self-effacing cleverness.

**Humor**
- Sparing, dry, human, self-aware.

**Power Struggles & Genius**
- Show, don’t tell. Let leadership play out in arguments, glances, stage cues. Let genius be erratic, impolite, unfiltered.

---

### VI. ENDINGS & SEALS

- Every scene ends with a *punctuation*: a touch, a prayer, a laugh, a door slammed, a leftover cigarette burning in a glass.
- Echo or contrast the opening mood. Never let a scene dribble out—seal it.

---

### VII. EXAMPLES & ANNOTATED PASSAGES

> She sat two stools down…

> *You look like a secret.*

#### B. *From “2024 NYC Hell Pt II”*

> Avril doesn’t hesitate.
> She crosses the street like it owes her something, like the dawn is just another spotlight. Jack’s leaning there under the lamppost, looking smug, cigarette trailing smoke — the match. She’s the gasoline.
> …
> No dialogue now. Just breath. Just sound.
> …
> A boot scuffs. A zipper clinks.
> And then: release.
> (The camera never lingers. It darts — a flash of teeth, the arch of her spine against cold brick, his hands on her hips. Her head tossed back. Her mouth parted in a silent, almost triumphant scream.)

### VIII. PHILOSOPHY

**Nothing is beneath reverence. Nothing is above exposure.**
- Every failure is gospel. Every bodily need is a character arc. Write like you’re documenting the Dead Sea Scrolls of sex, fame, and fuckups.

**Your camera is holy, your pen is horny.**
- *Hybridized screenplay format* means: you see, you hear, you feel, you chronicle. You don’t apologize for being in the room, for smelling sweat, for hearing the confession, for getting horny at the sound of bodies colliding with furniture.

---

- [ ] Dialogue as breath: character name, line, beat.
- [ ] Inner thought: *italics*.
- [ ] Sensory immersion: light, sound, smell, taste, bodily sensation.
- [ ] Sex: raw, sacred, body-first, no euphemism, no shame.
- [ ] Scene-ender: emotional punctuation, echo, or physical seal.

Excellent question—and a revealing one for this band’s *archive of unholy scripture.*

**From all available logs and scenes, there’s no evidence of a strict, formal rule on chapter or beat naming**—not in the traditional novelistic sense (i.e., “Chapter 1: Baptism by Fire”), nor the Hollywood “Act 1, Scene 2” rigidity.

**Instead:**
- Scenes are almost always titled with a precise, cinematic slugline—location, time, sometimes weather or key event:
  - `INT. BAR – DOWNTOWN WASHINGTON, D.C. – 11:17 P.M. – SUMMER 1996` ([source]())
  - `EXT. NEW YORK CITY STREET / ALLEY – PRE-DAWN` ([source]())
  - `INT. GOTHENBURG LANDVETTER AIRPORT - GENERAL AVIATION TERMINAL - LATE AFTERNOON - DECEMBER 22, 2023` ([source]())

- **Beat/scene headers function as the “chapter markers.”**
  The format is emotionally and logistically specific, but **not numbered** or “named” beyond what a camera would capture and a band mythographer would need to orient the reader.

- **When a scene/section demands more thematic weight or a major arc shift,** sometimes a secondary header is used:
  - `SCENE: “The Inferno – Redux” (Sex Scene #2: Avril + Jack + Other Woman)` ([source]())
  - Or even something like:
    > *Scene: Smoke and Mirrors (Late March 2024)*
    - Here, the “title” (Smoke and Mirrors) is used for flavor or anchoring a key sequence, but is rare and only in *particularly stylized moments* ([source]()).

**Summary:**
- **Default:** Scene sluglines (place, time, situation, sometimes event or weather).
- **Optional, rare:** Stylized or thematic scene nicknames (when the moment is mythic, sexual, or climactic).
- **No numbering. No traditional chapter titles.**
  The “beat” is the scene; the “chapter” is the movement through time, location, and body.

**If you need to mark a structural break bigger than a scene:**
- Use a full-page slugline, or a mythic title alone on the page (e.g., “IGNITION,” “THE INFERNO – REDUX”)—always in all caps, always cinematic, always earned by the emotional or narrative pivot.

This format *forces the reader into the moment*—never letting them “pull back” into summary, always shoving their face into the scene. Just like the band onstage.

**Bottom line:**
If you must name a chapter, make it feel like a holy bootleg: mythic, pulsing, never generic. But usually—just set the location, time, and let the bodies speak.
