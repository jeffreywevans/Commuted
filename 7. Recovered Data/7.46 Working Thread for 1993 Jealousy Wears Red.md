Neon smears across wet pavement. Jer and Gilley tramp down the sidewalk, guitars still buzzing in their bones, the warehouse’s glow behind them fading into the late-night hush of nowhere Ohio.

Gilley’s grin is wide, almost conspiratorial as he shoulders Jeremy sideways, already plotting.

**GILLEY**
Hell of a day, huh? Band’s actually *working*. I say we get drunk and talk some shit before the universe takes it back.

Jeremy laughs, relief in his voice.

**JEREMY**
It did go long, man. But yeah, it felt—right. Like we finally remembered what the fuck we’re doing.

They duck into a dive bar, lights flickering, locals hunched over the bar. Gilley drops onto a cracked vinyl stool, signals for beers.

**GILLEY**
Alright. New idea.
(slaps the bar, eyes wild)
You write the next song. Me and you, Jer. We’ll show Kath and Jeff what’s what.

Jeremy lifts his glass, wary.

**JEREMY**
It’s not that simple, man. Not black and white. I like figuring out chords, melodies, the puzzle of it. It’s math, not poetry. I say let Kath and Jeff hash out the drama—they’re good at it.

Gilley won’t let go. He leans in, determined, half-drunken prophet.

**GILLEY**
We gotta play our part, man! If it *was* black and white—yes or no, do or die—you’re gonna do it for me.
(beat, grinning like a hyena)
Besides, I got the name. Monochrome. Like the silver and black! Raiders, baby.

Jeremy just blinks at him, trying to process the manic energy.

**JEREMY**
Gilley, you sound like you’re freebasing powdered prune juice.

Gilley just shrugs, pleased with himself, lost in his own mythology.

**GILLEY**
Silver and black, man. You bring the math, I’ll bring the madness.
(raises his glass, toasts the future)
To *Monochrome*—whatever the hell that ends up being.

Jeremy laughs, shakes his head, but somewhere between the skepticism and the friendship, the seed of a new riff takes root.

The beers clink. The night outside is neither black nor white—just blurred lines and possibility.

**EXT. THE POUR HOUSE – VANDALIA – LATE NIGHT**

Beer bottles litter the sticky table, the jukebox mumbles in the corner, and two stools down, a couple of regulars are busy losing at Keno. Jeremy and Gilley are deep in, spirits high and egos bruised by the local cold shoulder. It’s hard to be an anonymous rock star when you’re drinking three miles from your old high school.

Jeremy sighs, surveying the bar—no one gives a shit, and the pickup game is running ice-cold.

**JEREMY**
You know what’s worse than being ignored by women in your own home town?
(shrugs, half-laughs)
Nothing. Absolutely nothing.

Gilley laughs, thumps his bottle against Jeremy’s, leans back with that wild grin.

**GILLEY**
Speak for yourself. I came for the beer. Women are just an extra credit assignment.

Jeremy shakes his head, then starts in, the kind of story you only tell at the bottom of the night.

**JEREMY**
Hey, remember last winter? My dad—old man Evans—got caught up in that “Coors Force One” mess? He’s making a keg run to Greenville and gets stuck on the wrong side of a tornado warning.

Gilley perks up, eyes wide.

**GILLEY**
That was a real thing? Shit, I just thought your dad was full of it.

Jeremy grins, warmed up now.

**JEREMY**
I swear to god, Gilley, the sky went pitch gray, rain like fucking silver bullets. And then there’s this black snake—an F3 tornado in November, for fuck’s sake!—twisting down and dragging half of Darke County to Oz.

Gilley’s eyes go round, and then suddenly, he sits up, inspired, smacks the bar.

**GILLEY**
That’s it!
(points at Jeremy, almost giddy)
I am *so* glad I put myself in charge. You are writing my song, and it’s about—
(leans in, solemn, ridiculous)
—the sky trying to kill a beer deliveryman. Forget Monochrome, man, we’re writing the Midwest apocalypse.

Jeremy tries to look annoyed but cracks up, the image too good, too real.

**JEREMY**
What the hell do we call that? “Twister on Tap?” “Barroom Rapture?”

Gilley raises his beer, mock heroic.

**GILLEY**
“Coors Force One (Flyover Doom).” Or, hell, just “Snake in the Sky.”
(grins, nudging Jer)
Don’t tell Jeff or Kath. They’ll try to make it poetic. I want death, beer, and weather.

Jeremy lifts his glass, resigned but grinning.

**JEREMY**
Deal. Tomorrow, we write about tornadoes and beer runs. Tonight, we survive one more round.

They drink, two lost sons of Ohio—safe inside, for now, as the sky outside just threatens rain.

**INT. COMMUTED BAND WAREHOUSE – VANDALIA – AFTERNOON**

Sunlight cuts through the dust. The band circles up, post-lunch, too full for tension but not so tired they can’t be annoyed. Gilley stands before them, reading from his battered notebook with the drama of a man who has declared himself “in charge.” His song, “Coors Force One,” gets its full, epic delivery: tornadoes, beer trucks, God’s wrath, and midwestern doom.

Bill, Jim, and Kathy exchange synchronized, almost balletic eye rolls. Kathy leans into Jeff, whispering, “I can’t believe we’re giving the floor to Beer Moses.”

Jeremy slouches, hands in pockets, face frozen in a neutral “I did not write this” expression.

Gilley’s vision peters out. The room goes quiet, polite in the way only musicians can be when someone’s just played them three minutes of tornado-ballad about his buddy’s dad.

Jeff breaks the silence with his signature lazy confidence.

**JEFF**
You were onto something with “Monochrome,” though. All that dread, watching the radar, colors sliding over Ohio like the world’s most aggressive bruise. The red isn’t just a warning—it’s an ulcer, trying to bleed out the county line.

Jim perks up, tapping his sticks absently on his knee.

**JIM**
Like a gutshot.
(beat)
You see that line move closer, you just know it’s gonna ruin your night.

Jeff glances at Kathy, voice sly but real.

**JEFF**
I’ll work something out, if Kathy’s willing to be flexible.

Kathy stretches, throws her arms up behind her head, arching her back. She flashes Jeff a look, all challenge and innuendo.

**KATHY**
Jeff, I’m so flexible I can touch the floor with my left foot and the ceiling of your bedroom with my right.

The band howls. Bill is nearly on the floor, Jim cackles, Jeremy finally cracks a grin. Gilley just beams—chaos was the point all along.

Jeff gives Kathy a long, bewildered stare, like a Great Dane seeing a poodle in heat for the first time: all lust, all confusion, no earthly clue how to bridge the distance.

**JEFF**
How the hell am I supposed to fuck that tonight?

Kathy winks, bends over, and plants a hand on the projector cart.

**KATHY**
Better eat your Wheaties, Evans.

The band’s laughter echoes off the warehouse walls, rolling over them in bright waves. For a moment, even dread feels like something you can laugh your way through.

**INT. JEFF’S CONDO – TIPP CITY – EVENING**

The kitchen smells like sautéed onions and garlic, plates licked clean, wine glasses tracing lazy constellations on the table. The windows are open, letting in that spring air with the first hints of storm in it. Kathy’s got her feet on Jeff’s lap, her notebook on the armrest, hair tumbling, gaze fierce and alive. Jeff—shirt gone, socks mismatched, appetite undiminished—leans in, all attention.

Kathy taps the notebook, working it out for herself as much as for him.

**KATHY**
Look, we’ve actually got two ideas. Not one. We gotta sever them or they’re both gonna be a mess. First: the storm. That’s its own beast.
(drawing a finger through condensation on her wine glass)
It’s a radar-indicated tornado, right? The kind where you’re watching TV, hearing the warning, but you’re also out on the porch, watching the sky, feeling that dread—wondering if the world’s about to get ripped open.

Jeff nods, lazy, eyes locked on her, his hand tracing slow circles on her ankle.

**JEFF**
Yeah. That’s got teeth. I can feel that song clawing at the window.

Kathy shifts, a smile ghosting her lips, the tip of her tongue on her lower lip as the second idea arrives, sharp and sad.

**KATHY**
But you want something like “Monochrome”?
(looks up, a dare)
That’s not the storm. That’s… two colors, dull and lifeless.
(she leans in, voice dropping)
Why not make that about the real monochrome?
(points at herself, at him)
A dull, lifeless, nine-to-five job. No stakes, no wild sky. Just gray, and gray, and more gray. Every morning’s the same. Every night, a little smaller.

Jeff’s face lights up, grin wolfish and hungry, hands sliding up her calves. The world has narrowed to the kitchen light, the hum of her vision, the way her ideas make him burn.

**JEFF**
Screw edibles, Espich—I’m gonna eat you alive. That’s the clearest vision of an amazing path forward I’ve ever heard.

Kathy laughs—low, delighted, her foot pressing hard into his thigh, a dare and a challenge.

**KATHY**
You better, Evans.
(leans forward, breath hot on his jaw)
But after I get these ideas down. Otherwise, you’ll be fucking a ghost.

He kisses her ankle, voice muffled but reverent.

**JEFF**
Deal. But after, you’re not going anywhere until I’ve had seconds.

She scribbles as he slides closer, and outside, thunder rolls on the horizon—two storms, both waiting to break.

**INT. JEFF’S CONDO – TIPP CITY – NIGHT**

The post-dinner hush is broken only by the tick of an old wall clock and Kathy’s pen moving across her notebook. Jeff sprawls on the couch, feet up, watching her with that unhurried curiosity only a lover and collaborator can afford.

Kathy sets the scene, voice half-playful, half-confessional, the past still real in her mouth.

**KATHY**
You want monochrome? Try being a bank teller in Tipp City. Boredom isn’t the word. Some days the highlight was a dog at the drive-thru.
(smirks)
Mr. Stevenson used to bring me a Diet Mt Dew, every Tuesday. Most excitement I got all month.

She grins at her memory, then flashes him a sly look.

**KATHY** (cont’d)
I’m not sad to say I make a *lot* more than seventeen grand a year now.

Jeff barks out a laugh, shaking his head.

**JEFF**
How the hell did you survive that?

Kathy’s fingers fidget with the pen, eyes wandering to the window.

**KATHY**
I wrote songs and stories in my head. Daydreams. Most of the good ones—
(a little proud, a little sad)
—went into 69 Reasons. All that pent-up mess. That’s how you get an album.

Jeff tips his head, watching her, softening.

**JEFF**
What else did you do to get through?

She hesitates, pen stopping. Her voice is low, not quite embarrassed, but circumspect.

**KATHY**
Sometimes?
(beat)
I’d have sexual fantasies about other men.

Jeff’s eyes glint, eyebrows arched in mock offense.

**JEFF**
Gilley?

Kathy bites her lip, then nods, just a flicker.

**KATHY**
On occasion, yeah. You spend enough hours bored out of your mind, your imagination goes to weird places.

Jeff grins, rolling his eyes, relishing the confession.

**JEFF**
Me?

Kathy leans in, flicking her gaze over his bare chest, her grin growing lopsided.

**KATHY**
Well, we weren’t exactly humping like the Dumpty Family three years ago, so…
(shrugs, smiling, honest)
Rarely. You were still a puzzle then. I hadn’t figured out if you were the answer or the problem.

Jeff just shakes his head, fake wounded, grinning like he won the lottery anyway.

**JEFF**
And now?

She shrugs, coy, thumb tracing a circle on his thigh.

**KATHY**
Now you’re both.
(leans in, kisses him)
And I like my stories with complicated endings.

He laughs, hands finding her waist, and in that old living room with a little night left to burn, boredom has never felt so far away.

**INT. JEFF’S CONDO – TIPP CITY – DAWN BLEEDING IN**

Coffee is cold. Notebook pages are everywhere—on the table, under the couch, between the pillows, some already torn and balled up, others scrawled top to bottom with verses. Kathy is giddy, eyes wild, hair unwashed, jeans half-buttoned from when she crawled into Jeff’s lap three hours ago only to hop up and change a stanza. Jeff is sprawled backwards on the carpet, one arm flung over his eyes, more stunned by fatigue than by lust.

He looks at her—really looks at her, the way a man does when he’s been awake too long and surprised to find himself, again, in awe.

**JEFF**
You know, I still don’t see what reason you got for being here. You could be anywhere, Espich.

Kathy is radiant, weird, exhausted, legs tangled under her, grinning like a little kid.

**KATHY**
That’s the beauty of it, Evans. I could be. But I’m not.

Jeff sits up, brushing his hair out of his eyes, a plan blooming slow and real.

**JEFF**
Alright. If you can’t remember a fantasy…
(he gestures at her, at the room, at all of it)
Then use that as the theme. Not the specifics—just the ache. Build the song out of that.

Kathy bites the cap off a pen, furrows her brow, then starts writing furiously, all hesitation burned away by hours of confession, too much coffee, and whatever chemical makes artists forget to sleep or screw when the words are right.

**CUT TO: EIGHT HOURS LATER**

Sun is up, birds are fighting over the gutters. They’re both in the same clothes as last night. Neither remembers the moment sex became less important than getting the bridge right. Kathy finally sits back, blinking, hands shaking, giddy as hell.

**KATHY**
Evans—I’ve been up for 24 hours, I’m practically in your lap, and somehow I haven’t fucked you yet. What the hell is wrong with us?

Jeff laughs, the sound rough and warm, and looks at the mess they’ve made: a song, complete, impossible, sweaty with longing and regret.

**JEFF**
It’s the words, Espich. The words always win.

Kathy looks down at the notebook, reads their work out loud, almost not believing it’s real. For a moment, they don’t need sleep or sex—just the work, the air electric, the thrill of knowing the song *is* the fulfillment.

The pages lay open, breathless, daring the world to try and be bored again.

**INT. JEFF’S CONDO – TIPP CITY – MID-MORNING, BARELY STANDING**

The night’s mania has ebbed. Kathy sits cross-legged on the carpet, still in his shirt, eyes rimmed red but bright. Jeff, half-delirious, sips burnt coffee and surveys the carnage: pages, guitar picks, half a pizza. He looks at her like she’s the last real thing on earth.

**JEFF**
So—now that we wrote our brains out… wanna fuck?

Kathy, honest as ever, doesn’t even blink.

**KATHY**
Honestly? Badly.
(rubs her eyes, sheepish but grinning)
And that’s why I’m not going to. Not today. I need a day to reboot—otherwise, I’ll just black out in your lap and drool on the best part.

**JEFF**
Gotta keep up appearances, I guess. Wouldn’t want the guys thinking I wore you out.

Kathy stretches, arms overhead, a tired cat in borrowed clothes.

**KATHY**
Please. I did sideboob all day yesterday, and after the boys got their objectification out in the first thirty seconds, they treated me like an older brother.
(smiles, fond but a little rueful)
Not the older sister, Evans—the *older brother.* That’s a promotion.

Jeff considers, watching her with that forensic tenderness that drives her mad.

**JEFF**
Your public and private personas are crystal clear, Kath. You neuter yourself on purpose. You get to be the dangerous one.
(leans in, softer, voice dropping)
But I know better. I know you’re female—because I can make you come so hard you see God, and only a woman can pull off that much red nail polish on her left hand and have a cult following on three continents.

Kathy wiggles her fingers, shows off the chipped scarlet lacquer, grins.

**KATHY**
*The Left Hand.* Still the best merch decision we ever made.

**JEFF**
You think the fan group would riot if they found out you’re secretly a big softie?

She snorts, crawling over to collapse beside him.

**KATHY**
They’d never believe it.
(pauses, gentle)
Don’t worry. Tomorrow, you’re getting laid so well you’ll forget your own lyrics.

Jeff laughs, warm and wrecked, and lets her head fall into his lap, content—for once—to wait for the storm.
