It’s a nice internal callback to earlier in the album, turning the tables on the sky as something to be conquered by ingenuity rather than endured. Throughout the song, **metonymy** is used: *“Ohio to the moon”* is repeated ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/13HXP07A-hcCDLmxDIdtwBJYSgJTfPEPb)) ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/13HXP07A-hcCDLmxDIdtwBJYSgJTfPEPb)), letting *Ohio* stand for the people and spirit that launched Armstrong. In Verse 2, the lyrics lament how *“The papers forgot / But we remember well”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/13HXP07A-hcCDLmxDIdtwBJYSgJTfPEPb)), evoking the **collective voice** of the Midwest determined to keep its heroes alive in memory. There’s also a clever line, *“Because space ain’t new to a land that grieves”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/13HXP07A-hcCDLmxDIdtwBJYSgJTfPEPb)), which is both a **metaphor** and a commentary – space (emptiness) isn’t new to a region familiar with loss and vacancy (perhaps referring to depopulation or the vast open farmland and the void left by industry’s decline). The structure of “Exit 111” is conventional (verse/pre-chorus/chorus), fitting its role as a narrative tribute. The rhyme schemes are softer, more **ballad-like** than the rest of the album – appropriate to its reflective mood. As a thematic piece, it ties into the Great Lakes/Midwest emphasis by honoring a native son. It also subtly critiques how quickly grand achievements are forgotten (underscoring a theme of **public memory and oblivion** that first appeared in “We Put a Man on the Moon First”). Deviations from carnality are total – this is pure homage and historical reflection, with zero reference to love or lust. Instead of bedroom or barroom, the stage is the cosmos and a small Ohio town. The literary and emotional ambition of this track is impressive; Commuted uses Armstrong’s story to elevate the album’s scope to *“From Wapakoneta… to forever”* as the bridge quietly puts it ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/13HXP07A-hcCDLmxDIdtwBJYSgJTfPEPb)). The result is an *innovative rock eulogy* of sorts, unlike anything the band had attempted before. It invites the listener to consider legacy and pride beyond oneself, in stark contrast to the self-indulgence of the band’s 1990s output.

### Track 9: “Heaven Turned Its Back on the Water”
The ninth track plunges into the eerie depths of Great Lakes lore. **“Heaven Turned Its Back on the Water”** is a slow, heavy waltz of a song that grapples with the wrath of Lake Superior – particularly the infamous shipwrecks that have earned Superior a reputation (it recalls the legend of the Edmund Fitzgerald and the notion that Lake Superior “never gives up her dead”). The lyrics are drenched in dark spirituality and dread. In the chorus, a potent image appears:

> *“God is angry at Whitefish Point / You can hear it in the waves / The bell tolls not for mercy / But for names no one saves / He don’t speak in thunder / He just drags the light / Into a lake that forgets every fight”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1KJAcl0-VbaQagGakqYcYqYVYmTHqgqod)).

The rhyme scheme here has a ghostly quality – **Point/point** is repeated (beginning and end of the stanza), giving a cyclic feel. **“Waves/saves”** rhyme, and **“light/fight”** form another pair ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1KJAcl0-VbaQagGakqYcYqYVYmTHqgqod)). These flowing rhymes are coupled with heavy, dragging rhythm in delivery, mimicking waves crashing. The literary allusions are rich: “the bell tolls” evokes John Donne’s famous *“for whom the bell tolls”*, but here *“not for mercy but for names”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1KJAcl0-VbaQagGakqYcYqYVYmTHqgqod)) – a haunting line that references the bell at Whitefish Point (the site of a lighthouse and shipwreck museum) ringing for lost sailors. The song uses **personification** and **prosopopoeia**: *God* is portrayed as actively *angry*, but in a cold way, and Lake Superior itself is depicted as a sentient force that “forgets” and “takes.” The line *“Heaven turned its back on the water”* (the title) suggests even God/Heaven refuses to look at the lake’s lethal fury – an almost Miltonian image of a forsaken place. In Verse 2, the narrator speaks in first person: *“I saw a freighter split like a truth… nothing heals”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1KJAcl0-VbaQagGakqYcYqYVYmTHqgqod)), grounding the song in witnessed tragedy. There’s a clear **Great Lakes regional reference**: Whitefish Point (in Michigan’s Upper Peninsula) is specifically cited, anchoring the song in a real geography. Rhyme-wise, verses often use half-rhymes and consonance to avoid too much sing-song – appropriate for the somber tone. For example, **“shine/away/place”** in the pre-chorus ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1KJAcl0-VbaQagGakqYcYqYVYmTHqgqod)) don’t exactly rhyme but share vowel sound adjacency that feels unsettled. This lack of perfect rhyme in sections creates a sense of **unease**. The bridge is especially poetic: *“He carved this lake with his fingernails / Filled it with bones and called them sails”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1KJAcl0-VbaQagGakqYcYqYVYmTHqgqod)) – a chilling bit of mythology that gives Lake Superior a creation story out of a horror movie (here God or some primordial force literally digs out the lake and populates it with shipwrecked bones). This is **metaphor and imagery** of a high order, almost metal in its darkness. Thematically, this track stays on the Midwest/Great Lakes focus, dealing with *nature’s carnage and the spiritual despair it can bring*. There’s an undercurrent of **moral reckoning** – lines about *“Too many men with too many lies… You wanna meet God? Sail here at night”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1KJAcl0-VbaQagGakqYcYqYVYmTHqgqod)) imply that perhaps hubris or sin led to these disasters. Such moralizing would have once, in Commuted’s hands, been directed at depraved human behavior (like on earlier erotic/excess songs), but now the lens is turned to human insignificance in the face of nature. *Carnality is entirely absent; spirituality and fatalism take center stage.* The song’s title phrase suggests that even salvation (*Heaven*) has withdrawn from these waters – a stark statement that lingers. In terms of rhyme and structure, the measured, almost ballad-like stanzas and the recurring refrain about God’s anger give it a folk-song quality, albeit wrapped in grungy distortion. It’s the album’s longest track and feels like a dark epic poem set to music. This ambitious storytelling through song demonstrates Commuted’s growth as lyricists; they’re confident enough to invoke God and fate without irony, in service of a regional tale.

### Track 10: “Superior Is Hungry”
If track 9 addressed Lake Superior from the human perspective (and a bit of God’s), **“Superior Is Hungry”** flips the perspective: the lake itself seems to speak. This song is essentially **Lake Superior personified as a character**, delivering a monologue about its eternal hunger. The mood is foreboding – a distant drum pulse and bass groans open the track ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1nDIz9DD-F8PvzUA2rrgIrlMCL0TJ2AIa)). When the voice enters, it’s low, almost detached, as if the lake is ancient and indifferent. The chorus is a chilling proclamation:

> *“The Lake is hungry / It doesn’t need to hate / It just waits / And waits / And takes / The Lake is hungry / And it never sleeps / It holds your wrecks / It counts your grief”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1nDIz9DD-F8PvzUA2rrgIrlMCL0TJ2AIa)).

This is delivered in a slow, heavy cadence with a simple **ABCCDEEF** rhyme pattern (note the repetition of “waits… and waits… and takes” – the internal rhyme/repetition of *-ates* gives a sense of an endless cycle ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1nDIz9DD-F8PvzUA2rrgIrlMCL0TJ2AIa))). The short phrases (**monosyllabic words** like “waits / takes / sleeps”) land like waves or the ticking of a clock – very appropriate to the theme of patient inevitability. The band uses **anaphora** in repeating “The Lake is hungry” at the start of sections, making it a menacing mantra. Lyrically, it’s rife with references to actual shipwrecks: *“You give me names – Fitzgerald, Cyprus, Ironton – but they’re just crumbs in my mouth of iron”* the Lake says in Verse 2 ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1nDIz9DD-F8PvzUA2rrgIrlMCL0TJ2AIa)). These are names of ships lost in the Great Lakes (the Edmund Fitzgerald being the most famous on Superior). Calling them *“crumbs”* is a darkly clever metaphor – to the lake, these grand ships are mere trifles, and *“mouth of iron”* not only alliterates but evokes both the iron ore these ships carried and the iron-grey maw of the lake. We see **alliteration** throughout, like *“More tide than tomb / More truth than sky / More god than moon”* in the second chorus ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1nDIz9DD-F8PvzUA2rrgIrlMCL0TJ2AIa)) – the *t* and *m* sounds make those comparisons memorable and incantatory. The rhyme scheme loosens in verses to allow these names and images to stand out without the constraint of rhyme, then tightens again in choruses for emphasis. **Personification** is obviously the key device (the lake speaking). But Commuted goes further, giving the lake a personality: arrogant, vengeful in a quiet way, viewing itself as an ancient deity. *“No prayer will float / No rope will save / This is not death / It’s return to cave,”* the lake warns in the pre-chorus ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1nDIz9DD-F8PvzUA2rrgIrlMCL0TJ2AIa)), using metaphor of death as a return to primordial darkness. The bridge in this song is whispered, almost ritualistic: *“Tell your children not to sing near my shore… I don’t forget, I don’t stop”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1nDIz9DD-F8PvzUA2rrgIrlMCL0TJ2AIa)). It sounds like a curse or a folklore warning. Thematically, “Superior Is Hungry” continues the album’s journey through Midwest tribulations, focusing on the Great Lakes’ **historical tragedies**. By giving voice to the lake, Commuted remove any trace of human lust or sin – this is nature’s perspective, cold and unsympathetic. It’s as far removed from a love song as one can imagine, yet it holds a strange dark seduction in its imagery. The **deviation from earlier carnality** could not be more stark: where once Commuted might have sung “I am hungry for you” in a sexual sense, now *the only hunger is that of a lake for souls*. One can’t help but note how the word “hungry” appears here and in the next track’s title – a motif of insatiability that used to apply to sexual appetite in their older songs, now repurposed to symbolize nature and toil. “Superior Is Hungry” is thus a critical piece of the album’s conceptual fabric, reinforcing the idea that some forces (*time, nature, history*) cannot be appeased or tamed – a thematic echo of the album title’s suggestion that some things are raw and unrefinable.

### Track 11: “A Prayer Upon Dying at Work (Hungry)”
This penultimate track brings the album’s thematic threads together in a harrowing, heartfelt way. **“A Prayer Upon Dying at Work (Hungry)”** is written from the perspective of a blue-collar worker contemplating the very real possibility of dying on the job. It’s effectively a workers’ hymn – solemn, with a pacing kick drum like a heartbeat and mournful guitars. The parenthetical “Hungry” in the title links it conceptually to “Superior Is Hungry,” but here the hunger is that of the working class soul yearning for meaning and recognition. The song’s structure is hymn-like: each chorus begins with the titular *“If I die at work…”* and proceeds to a set of wishes. One chorus reads:

> *“If I die at work / Let the machines slow down / Let the lake hold my name / Don’t let me drown / In silence, in debt / In the hum of regret / Let me go hungry / But don’t let them forget”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FGJdSnoIP4SpJDGkHUUfMvVgv1vWzpNH)).

We see a **pleading tone** and a simple rhyme scheme: **ABCBDEEF** (with *down/drown* and *debt/forget* as the key rhymes or near-rhymes) ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FGJdSnoIP4SpJDGkHUUfMvVgv1vWzpNH)). The repetition of the “If I die at work” line at the start of each chorus acts as a somber refrain, much like a prayer or psalm refrain – anaphora that reinforces the song’s title and concept. Poetic devices are deeply woven in: **metaphor** (the “floor’s my altar” appears in verse lyrics, casting the factory floor as a sacred place of sacrifice), **alliteration** (“silence… in debt… hum of regret” – the “s” and “h” sounds create a hushed whisper effect ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FGJdSnoIP4SpJDGkHUUfMvVgv1vWzpNH))), and especially **symbolism**. The line *“Let the lake hold my name”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FGJdSnoIP4SpJDGkHUUfMvVgv1vWzpNH)) is symbolic – tying the individual’s legacy to the Great Lake (perhaps Lake Michigan or Erie, given many Midwest factory towns hug these waters). It’s as if asking the mighty lakes not to forget the worker, just as we name ships lost at sea. This recalls the previous two tracks’ theme of the lakes holding onto the lost, bridging the natural with the human. The song employs **apostrophe** – directly addressing forces (machines, lake, an unnamed “them” who might forget). In Verse 2, the imagery of daily grind is vividly drawn with a sort of grim rhyme: *“Lunch break on the line / Half a sandwich, full of ache / Counting hours like rosary beads / Each one a breath I take”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FGJdSnoIP4SpJDGkHUUfMvVgv1vWzpNH)). There’s a nice internal half-rhyme of **“ache/take”** and the powerful simile **“hours like rosary beads”**, which frames factory work in religious terms, each hour a prayer or penance. This extends the album’s motif of quasi-spiritual redemption in mundane places (here, the assembly line is almost a church, much as the dive bar was in track 12). As the song builds, the vocalist’s delivery intensifies, almost cracking with emotion – you can hear the **strain** (the notation *“voice fights through”* in the chorus indicates how it’s sung ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FGJdSnoIP4SpJDGkHUUfMvVgv1vWzpNH))). By the final chorus, much of the instrumentation drops to leave piano and voice, as if the prayer is now solitary and bare, then it ends on the repeated plea *“Don’t you forget”* fading into silence ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FGJdSnoIP4SpJDGkHUUfMvVgv1vWzpNH)). Thematically, this track hits at **the value of a human life** in the working class context, and the fear of dying for nothing. It’s a dramatic turn from songs about erotic desire – here the deepest desire is *to be remembered and to have meant something*. The hunger referenced is perhaps the unfinished longing (the narrator says “I left here hungry” in the final chorus, implying unfulfilled needs or dreams ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FGJdSnoIP4SpJDGkHUUfMvVgv1vWzpNH))). By invoking hunger, Commuted subtly nods to how previous albums’ characters were “hungry” for pleasure, yet now that word is imbued with existential weight – hungry for dignity, for life itself. The **Great Lakes region** appears not just in the mention of the lake, but in the unspoken backdrop: factories by the water, the Rust Belt routine. As a poetic piece, “A Prayer Upon Dying at Work” stands among Commuted’s most sophisticated songs. It reads almost like a Bruce Springsteen or John Prine lyric in its empathy and detail, but delivered with Commuted’s grunge dynamics and female vocals that crackle with sorrow (notably, a female voice leads parts of this song, which adds a layer of lamentation – perhaps embodying a widow or a collective motherly perspective). The track brings the album’s thematic arc to a human level after the cosmic/natural forces of tracks 8, 9, 10. It serves as a penultimate emotional crest, trading overt rage for deep lament. By now, any vestiges of Commuted’s former focus on sex or rock excess have been washed away in favor of this powerful humanist statement.

### Track 12: “This Is My Church”
Closing out the album on a bittersweet note, **“This Is My Church”** takes us to an unexpected sanctuary: a seedy Midwestern **dive bar**. It’s a soulful, gritty rock song that fuses gospel influences (handclaps, a hint of organ, and backing “choir” harmonies) with barroom blues. The title itself is a declaration – reclaiming the idea of *church* for those lost souls who congregate under buzzing neon signs instead of stained glass. The chorus testifies with tongue-in-cheek reverence:

> *“Raise your glass (to the broken saints…) / Every cracked booth (every faded name…) / This is my church! (With a bourbon choir…) / Where regrets burn souls and the truth’s on ice / Amen to the lost, in the neon glow / Find salvation in the dive bar glow”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1d-Zg7yAVprDrGO74AMSQ9lLAv6XREcGP)).

The call-and-response structure (lead vocal followed by parenthetical responses) is a clear nod to gospel church services, cleverly subverted. The rhyme scheme in the chorus is loose but present: **saints/name** (near rhyme), **choir/ice** (not rhyming, but “choir” conceptually ties to “Amen” in the next line), **glow/hoe** (original lyrics say “dive bar hoe” in the last line – a provocatively crude way to refer to someone at the bar – rhyming with glow) ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1d-Zg7yAVprDrGO74AMSQ9lLAv6XREcGP)). The album’s last printed word is literally “hoe,” a final cheeky flash of Commuted’s irreverence; it’s arguably the only direct sexual reference on the record, and even here it’s used more as gritty scene-setting than titillation. Throughout the song, the band employs **irony and metaphor** with gusto. The **bar as church** is the central metaphor, and they explore it fully: bar patrons are “broken saints,” the bartender’s jokes and the jukebox songs are the “gospel,” the act of raising a glass is akin to a prayerful offering. The lyric *“Where regrets burn souls and the truth’s on ice”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1d-Zg7yAVprDrGO74AMSQ9lLAv6XREcGP)) is especially potent – conflating the **fire-and-brimstone** concept (burning souls) with the mundane image of whiskey on ice. There’s an internal rhyme of **“souls”** with **“glow”** that ties those lines together, and **consonance** of “s” sounds that snake through the chorus (saints, souls, ice, lost, salvation). In the verses, we meet characters: a woman at the bar with *“Eyes stained in whiskey, shadowed and glassed”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1d-Zg7yAVprDrGO74AMSQ9lLAv6XREcGP)), a heartbroken man strumming one chord by the pool table ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1d-Zg7yAVprDrGO74AMSQ9lLAv6XREcGP)). These detailed vignettes use **imagery** and some alliteration (e.g., “beat-up guitar… half-tanked car” gives a little rhythmic bounce with the hard “k” sounds, even as it describes despair ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1d-Zg7yAVprDrGO74AMSQ9lLAv6XREcGP))). The song’s bridge even has a mock-choir humming and a last-call announcement, leaning into the theme. In terms of rhyme and structure, “This Is My Church” uses a traditional verse-chorus form, fitting for a closing anthem. The rhyme schemes are not overly strict, allowing the narrative to flow. The chorus, however, with its repeated **“This is my church!”** line, provides a strong refrain – one that invites audience sing-along, much like a congregation responding “Amen.” As the final track, this song carries a lot of weight: it humanizes and localizes the album’s journey. After tales of storms, shipwrecks, and cosmic achievements, we end up back with regular people seeking comfort in a bar. It’s as if Commuted is saying, *after all the grand narratives, our real church is each other – the community of the flawed and weary.* Thematically, it connects to the Midwest by portraying a **small-town bar culture** (something very familiar in Great Lakes and rust-belt towns where the corner bar is a social hub). It also echoes earlier themes: the notion of finding the sacred in the profane (also touched on in track 11’s factory-as-altar and track 9’s forsaken heaven). And notably, while it flirts with lustful imagery (the “dive bar **hoe**” and the shadows dancing seductively to a song without a chorus), it’s framed not as glamorized sin but as part of the tapestry of brokenness that the song lovingly embraces. Thus, even when Commuted returns to a **sexualized word** here, it’s undercut by melancholy and empathy – a far cry from the celebratory carnality of their early “sex, drugs, & rock’n’roll” anthems. Musically, the blend of **gospel and rock** also symbolizes refinement of their sound – ironically, by *not* refining away their roots but by embracing them, mixing the church music of their youth (perhaps) with the dive bars of their adulthood. “This Is My Church” ends with a repeated whispered *“Amen…”* ([source](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1d-Zg7yAVprDrGO74AMSQ9lLAv6XREcGP)), bringing the album to a solemn close. In doing so, Commuted effectively **consecrates the album’s journey** – from the instrumental opener’s abstract baptism, through trials of nature and society, and finally to this humble congregation of the disillusioned. It’s an ending that invites reflection, and it solidifies *You Cannot Refine It* as a concept album: one that finds divinity not in the sky or heaven (since both turned their back) but in the resilience and camaraderie of common people.

### Style, Tone & Innovation – Comparing *You Cannot Refine It* to 2009’s Rock Landscape
Released in 2009, *You Cannot Refine It* arrived in a **banner year for diverse rock music**, and it stood out for its gritty regional focus and conceptual ambition. While many peers leaned into slicker production or experimental sounds, Commuted dug into their roots and came up with something both old-school and fresh.

To put it in context: 2009 saw albums like **Phoenix’s *Wolfgang Amadeus Phoenix***, which embraced polished indie-pop hooks and cosmopolitan cool, and **Yeah Yeah Yeahs’ *It’s Blitz!***, which shocked fans by incorporating synth-pop sheen into a formerly garage-rock band. Compared to those, *You Cannot Refine It* is **decidedly rougher and earthier**. Commuted wasn’t chasing the dance-floor (despite that one EDM-flavored intro on track 1); instead, they doubled down on fuzz guitars, lo-fi aesthetics, and live, unvarnished vocal takes. In tone, Phoenix’s work was breezy and urbane, with lyrics about romantic and existential ennui in global cities, whereas Commuted’s album is stark and parochial – all about crumbling towns and nature’s wrath. Yet, interestingly, both albums share an emotional directness: Phoenix’s “Lisztomania” and “1901” are bright and catchy but tinged with longing, while Commuted’s songs like “Nice Try, Sky” are loud and aggressive but born from vulnerability and pain. One could say Commuted brought the **heart of Heartland rock** into dialogue with the indie-rock trends of the late 2000s, implicitly countering the cosmopolitan romance of Phoenix with their own **Midwestern gothic romance** (romance in the literary sense of dramatic storytelling).

Then there was **Animal Collective’s *Merriweather Post Pavilion*** and **Dirty Projectors’ *Bitte Orca***, two albums that exemplified the experimental, arty side of 2009’s music. Merriweather was lush, psychedelic, and built on loops and samples, while Bitte Orca featured complex rhythms, unconventional song structures, and quirky vocal harmonies. By contrast, *You Cannot Refine It* seems, on the surface, more traditional – many songs follow verse-chorus forms and the instrument palette is standard rock (guitars, bass, drums, with the occasional piano). However, Commuted’s innovation was not in electronic experimentation or avant-garde composition; it was in **lyrical concept and thematic coherence**. In a year when indie-rock was pushing sonic boundaries, Commuted pushed narrative boundaries for a rock band. They created an album-length **concept narrative about the Midwest**, a sort of rock opera without the rock-opera tropes. This was relatively rare in 2009 – concept albums had been around for decades, but few bands of Commuted’s generation were doing deeply region-specific, story-rich albums at the time. In that sense, *You Cannot Refine It* was as ambitious as Animal Collective’s sonic collage or Dirty Projectors’ genre-bending – just in a different dimension. It’s noteworthy that critics in 2009 often praised AC or DP for “reinventing what a rock song can be” with sounds; similarly, critics praised Commuted for reinventing what their **songs could talk about** – shifting from hedonistic tales to near-literary explorations of place and identity (some reviews called it “the *Grapes of Wrath* of concept albums,” half-jokingly comparing the band’s lyrical journey to Steinbeck’s social commentary).

On the heavier end of the spectrum, 2009 gave us **Mastodon’s *Crack the Skye*** – a progressive metal odyssey with a cosmic storyline – and **Them Crooked Vultures’ self-titled** debut – a hard rock record by a supergroup indulging in Zeppelin-esque riffs. Commuted’s album shares some DNA with these in terms of **muscular sound**. There are moments on *You Cannot Refine It* (“Go For Three,” “Superior Is Hungry”) that have the sludgy riffs or thunderous drums that wouldn’t feel out of place on a Mastodon track, albeit less technical. Like *Crack the Skye*, Commuted’s album is conceptual, though Mastodon’s was a fantastical tale of astral travel and Rasputin, whereas Commuted kept the fantasy earth-bound in real Rust Belt issues. One might say *You Cannot Refine It* is almost **“post-metal” in spirit**: it has a unifying theme and uses recurring motifs (hunger, weather, machines) much like Mastodon used recurring musical motifs. And just as Mastodon’s album honored a lost loved one (the drummer’s sister, Skye) through its concept, Commuted’s album in many ways honors the **lost souls of the Midwest**. Meanwhile, Them Crooked Vultures and even **Alice in Chains’ *Black Gives Way to Blue*** (a comeback album in 2009) were more about revitalizing classic hard rock and grunge sounds. Commuted, who themselves emerged in the early ’90s grunge era, likewise revitalized their sound by infusing it with authenticity and maturity. *Black Gives Way to Blue* dealt with Alice in Chains’ grief and personal darkness (after losing their original singer), whereas Commuted dealt with collective grief and societal darkness. In tone, Commuted’s work is closer to AIC’s somber, heavy mood than to the cheeky bombast of Them Crooked Vultures. Yet, Commuted always offers a glimmer of hope or defiance (even if bitter or wry) – this balances the album’s tone so it’s not unrelentingly bleak. For example, track 5 “Nice Try, Sky” turns trauma into triumph, and track 12 “This Is My Church” finds warmth in community. This tonal balance of darkness and hope made *You Cannot Refine It* accessible and cathartic, much like the broad appeal of Green Day’s rock operas that mix despair with resolve.

Speaking of **Green Day’s *21st Century Breakdown*** and **Muse’s *The Resistance***, those were two 2009 albums that also had big themes and concepts. Green Day continued the rock-opera format of *American Idiot*, addressing America in the Bush era, and Muse went full sci-fi dystopia with symphonic flair. Commuted’s album, while not explicitly a “rock opera” with characters, does feel like a song cycle about a particular world. In a way, it’s the *Midwest’s answer* to those more globally-minded records. Instead of broad political strokes or Orwellian narratives, Commuted zeroed in on what 21st-century breakdown felt like in a small town, or what resistance means in the context of resisting oblivion and complacency. Where Muse layered Queen-like harmonies and classical piano interludes to evoke rebellion on a grand scale, Commuted layered raw feedback and heartfelt pleas to evoke a *ground-level rebellion*: farmers, factory workers, and ordinary folks refusing to give in. There is innovation in that perspective. Also, interestingly, Commuted’s willingness to incorporate non-rock elements (the electronic intro, the gospel touches in “This Is My Church,” the spoken word bits in “Nice Try, Sky” and “Go For Three”) mirrors how Muse incorporated symphonics or how Green Day incorporated theatrics. It shows Commuted stretching their sonic palette modestly to serve the songs – they innovated just enough to refresh their sound without losing their identity.

Finally, consider **The xx’s *xx***, another acclaimed 2009 debut known for its minimalist, intimate approach – essentially the polar opposite of Commuted’s maximalist, collective ethos. The xx made whispery confessionals about young love in London over sparse beats; Commuted made roaring testimonies about communities in Ohio over dense guitars. Yet both albums share an honesty and a clarity of vision that critics loved. *You Cannot Refine It* did not chase any trend – much like The xx carved out a unique space with hushed simplicity, Commuted carved out a space with unfiltered grit.
