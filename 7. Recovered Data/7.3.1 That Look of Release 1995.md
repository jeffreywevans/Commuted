**ROLLING STONE | April 1995 | Advance Exclusive**

"That Look of Release" — Commuted Just Changed the Rules
Byline: Greg Phillips

---

Get ready, kids.  In six weeks, Commuted — that little American band that somehow made arena rock dirty, poetic, and radioactive all at once — is dropping their third full-length studio album.  And it’s already stirring up a hurricane.

The title?  That Look of Release.  The cover?  Let’s just say: you might want to sit down.

There’s no swirling mist. No strategic hair placement. No tasteful drapery or shadowy silhouettes.  No, what you get instead is Kathy Espich — bare, smiling, unflinching — looking dead into the camera lens.  And, yes, if you look close enough, she’s not just topless. She’s fully, frontally nude.

It’s not vulgar.  It’s not coy.  It’s not even trying to seduce you. It’s a declaration.  
A warning shot wrapped in a body that refuses to apologize for existing.

Historically speaking, nudity on album covers isn’t new.

- Blind Faith’s self-titled 1969 album stirred outrage with its underage model — and was pulled almost immediately.
- John Lennon and Yoko Ono’s Two Virgins (1968) dared to show full frontal nudity but got stuffed into brown paper bags for store sales.
- Roxy Music’s Country Life (1974) gave us scantily clad models — but still clung to fashion photography aesthetics.
- Prince’s Lovesexy (1988) showed Prince himself naked — but surrounded by pastel, almost cartoonish innocence.

But nobody — and I mean nobody — has done what Kathy just did.  There’s no artifice here. No winking. No irony.  It’s one woman, one body, one unapologetic gaze.  Center frame. Full force. Nothing blurred. Nothing bowed.

Industry insiders are already predicting Walmart won't carry it.  (Target hasn’t commented, but insiders say they're ordering “big.”)  Meanwhile, Tower Records is planning a massive rollout.

Sarah Malcolm, Commuted’s marketing lead at Island Records?  When asked whether the controversy worried them, she shrugged and said:  “We didn’t make this album for the gatekeepers.  We made it for the ones still brave enough to look at the truth without flinching.”

Early takeaway?  That Look of Release isn’t just an album title.  It’s a battle cry.  Brace yourselves.  The 90s just officially grew up.
