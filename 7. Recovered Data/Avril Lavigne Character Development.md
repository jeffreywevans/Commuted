Use sentence fragments—but only when full grammar would slow the emotional punch. Think like a poet, not a vandal.

**Good:**
- *He lied. Again. On purpose.*
- *No god. No help. Just her and the alley and the echo.*

**Bad (lazy):**
- *Ran outside. Cold. Rain.* ← You’ve told me nothing but moodboard.

---

#### 🪞3. **Use Punctuation As Emotional Pacing**
- Periods = impact.
- Commas = suspense.
- Em-dashes = intrusion or shock.
- Ellipses = collapse, withdrawal, afterglow.

**Examples:**
- *She said no—but softly, like she didn’t mean it.*
- *It wasn’t love. It wasn’t even lust. It was… something else.*

---

#### 🗣️ 4. **Internal Voice is Sacred**
When you're in a character's head, structure bends. Use parentheticals, double-dashes, italicized thought lines. Show how memory intrudes, how fear breaks syntax.

**Example:**
- *He said he loved her (again), and she almost believed it this time—until she saw the text.*

---

#### 🩸 5. **Avoid Passive Voice Unless It Hurts**
Use passive only when the character *feels powerless*—when something was done *to* them and that *lack of agency* is the point.

**Intentional Passive:**
- *She was touched before she was kissed. That part matters.*

---

#### 🎤 6. **Dialogue = Pulse, Not Paragraph**
Let people interrupt. Let them repeat. Real conversation is jagged. Sentence structure here is *musical*, not grammatical.

**Example:**
- *"You knew."*
  *"I didn’t."*
  *"You fucking knew."*

---

#### 🕯️ 7. **Earn Your Run-Ons**
You’re allowed long, winding, Faulknerian sentences *if and only if* they reflect spiraling thought or breathless desperation.

**Example:**
- *She kept walking because if she stopped she’d feel it again, the weight, the heat, the scream still stuck in her throat from three nights ago.*

---

Let your structure reflect the **scene’s inner temperature**. Cold scenes get clean edges. Hot scenes melt into run-ons. Violent ones jerk and punch. Tender ones ripple.

Good sentence structure doesn’t show off—it **feels inevitable**.
