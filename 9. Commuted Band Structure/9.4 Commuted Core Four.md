## **The Origin of the “Core Four”**

No one remembers who said it first. A tweet, a comment thread, a Discord handle long since deleted — the phrase *Core Four* didn’t launch so much as leak. It arrived without attribution, somewhere in the gray spring of 2020, when the world was locked indoors and scrolling for meaning. A shorthand, a joke, a reverent meme. Four names. No singer.

Jeff Evans. Jeremy Evans. Jim Brown. Bill Davenport.

No one had called them that during the actual timeline. Not in press. Not in liner notes. Certainly not onstage. Commuted, as the world had known it, ended with Jeremy Gilley's death. What remained wasn’t a band. It was an afterimage.

The term *Core Four* crystallized something unspoken: that these four were left. Survivors, but only technically. They had not recorded together. They had not released a thing under the Commuted name. They had barely appeared in public as a unit since Gilley’s funeral. Each man had scattered — into production work, family life, silence. They were like the Beatles in 1972: orbiting separately, creative but spent, nursing old wounds and not entirely sure what the center had ever been.

And yet. Fans needed language. Needed structure. In a sea of cancelled tours, Zoom nostalgia, and streaming anniversaries, *Core Four* became the anchor. Not a reunion, not a lineup — just a label. A box to keep the grief in. Reddit threads dissected imagined setlists. TikToks reassembled decades-old footage into fanon timelines. The phrase migrated into taglines, tattoos, tributes. There was no marketing push. No press release. It just was.

That’s how myths work. They don’t ask permission.

By the time anyone in the band publicly acknowledged the phrase, it had already ossified. “The Core Four,” Jim said once, dryly, during an Instagram Q&A he clearly didn’t want to do, “sounds like a failed superhero team. But yeah. I guess that’s us.”

It stuck. It still does.

### **Core Four vs. The Fifth Flamethrower: Fan Schism After Avril’s Ascent**

The announcement hit like a lit match on dry tinder.

Christmas Day, 2023. A surprise post across the official Commuted channels — terse, glossy, and devastatingly clear: *Avril Lavigne joins Commuted.*

The leak was meticulously stage managed on Christmas morning, 2023. A fact on PNS Newswire and AP.  A CBC confirmation.  A X post with O, Canada embedded  A new banner on the band's website with five shadows instead of four, Jeff, Jeremy, Jim, Bill… and someone clearly female. Eventually, a debut halftime show concert was announced at the legendary Ohio Stadium on the banks of the Olentangy River, in front of 104,000 of Commuted's closest friends.

Avril showed up wearing black leather, ringed fingers, chin up like she *dared* someone to call her a placeholder.  Within hours, the fandom fractured. And at the epicenter of that crack: the phrase *Core Four*.

What had begun as a quiet, retrospective term during the pandemic — a fan shorthand for the surviving male members after Kathy Espich’s death — suddenly became loaded with tribal meaning. To some, it was sacred canon. To others, an exclusionary relic. And to a volatile minority, it became a weapon.

Nowhere was this more evident than in the backlash from radical corners of *The Left Hand* — the sprawling feminist fan collective that had, until then, treated Kathy’s career with a kind of digital priesthood. While many in the group embraced Avril’s arrival as a natural evolution, a vocal splinter — calling themselves *Blood Queens* — rejected the move entirely. To them, Kathy wasn’t just a voice; she was a bloodline. And Avril? A Canadian interloper with mall-punk origins and a discography that, in their view, had “never earned the mic.”

Their primary grievance: *Be First, Be Smarter, Or Cheat* was, to them, *Ragnarök, Revelations,* and *Return of the Jedi* — the final gospel. The Core Four had said their peace, sought penance, and buried their sister. Avril’s contributions were discounted entirely. “She makes breakup albums for girls who still drink White Claw,” one Blood Queen posted. “Commuted made funeral songs for women who had a brain bleed in a hospital corridor.”

It wasn’t subtle. And it wasn’t quiet.

In forums, fights flared. Fan edits that included Avril were flooded with vomit emojis and hex-code blocks reading “✂️ Core Four Only.” TikTok threads spiraled into flame wars. And at shows — particularly the first post-announcement warm-up gigs in Lexington, Kentucky — tension was visible. Sharpies blacked out Avril’s name on merch. Someone reportedly threw a tampon onstage during soundcheck.

But nothing matched the chaos of the live fan fights.

By late spring of 2024, it became a known security threat: pit brawls between drunken Left Hand factions — specifically between *Blood Queen truthers* (Avril-is-an-intruder purists) and *Modernists* (those who embraced her as the second coming, or at least a worthy evolution). The phrase *"You think she’s earned the mic?"* became both rallying cry and barfight prelude. Bouncers at multiple venues reported “at least one” scuffle per night involving women in eyeliner, combat boots, and decades of righteous fury.

The band never directly commented. Jeff liked a tweet that said “she doesn’t need to be Kathy — she’s Avril.” Bill posted a screenshot of an old synth patch called “Throne (2.0)” and said nothing else. Jim explained to Rick Beato, “Call us whatever you want. Just spell her name right.” And Jeremy? Jeremy reportedly gave Avril a guitar pick etched with *LEFT HAND, RIGHT NOW* on the first day of rehearsals.

The Core Four still exist — as history, as context, as consequence. But the mic is owned by the woman holding it.
