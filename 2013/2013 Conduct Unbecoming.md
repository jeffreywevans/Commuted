“I’m afraid…” His voice cracked, and he shook his head. “I’m afraid that letting her go means I failed. That I wasn’t enough to keep her here. That I’m going to screw everything up—again.”

Avril didn’t speak this time. She just stayed there, her hand steady on his shoulder, letting the silence hold space for him.

Jeff turned his head slightly, his eyes meeting hers. “Why are you even here?” he asked, his voice hoarse. “Why do you care?”

“Because,” she said simply, “sometimes the world is shit, and no one listens. But tonight, I’m listening.”

And for the first time in what felt like years, Jeff let himself believe it.

### **Room 517: Red Pill**  

Avril stayed perched on the arm of the chair, her hand steady on Jeff’s shoulder. His words came in stuttering bursts, like a dam cracking under pressure.  

“She hated me for so long,” Jeff whispered, his voice a rasp, his throat raw from the whiskey and the flood of emotions clawing their way out. “And the thing is, she was right to. I wasn’t there when she needed me. I wanted her to be more, to live up to this… this version of her I’d built in my head. But all she ever wanted was to be Kathy. And I couldn’t let her.”  

Avril stayed silent, letting him keep going. She saw his hands shake as he gripped the bottle, the tension in his jaw as he fought to keep it together.  

“Bill said I was gonna fuck this up again,” Jeff muttered bitterly. “And the thing is… I probably will. That’s what I do. I ruin things. Kathy, the band, Bill, myself… everything I touch turns to ash.”  

“You don’t believe that,” Avril said softly.  

Jeff laughed, sharp and humorless. “Don’t I? Look around, Avril. You think I’m here because I’ve got it all figured out? I’m here because I’ve burned every bridge I’ve ever built. You’re just the last one standing.”  

Avril didn’t flinch. “So burn me, too.”  

The words hit like a slap. Jeff’s head snapped up, his eyes searching hers, looking for the trap. But there wasn’t one. She was calm, steady, unshaken.  

“What are you talking about?” he asked, his voice barely above a whisper.  

“I’m saying, if you’re so set on destroying everything, go ahead. Take me down with you,” she said, her tone daring, but not unkind. “Because if that’s what it takes for you to see that you’re not just soot and smoke, I’ll take the hit.”  

Jeff blinked, stunned into silence.  

Avril leaned in closer, her voice low and even. “But here’s the thing, Jeff. You’re not gonna do it. You know why? Because deep down, in that fucked-up, brilliant head of yours, you know you’re more than the mess you think you are. And you’re scared shitless of proving it.”  

The air between them crackled with tension, the weight of her words settling over him like a challenge he couldn’t ignore.  

“I don’t need your pity,” Jeff said, but the words lacked bite.  

“It’s not pity,” Avril replied. “It’s a mirror. And you’re not ready to look, but you will. Maybe not tonight. Maybe not tomorrow. But one day, you’re gonna see yourself the way the rest of us do. And when that happens, you’ll realize you’ve been wrong about yourself this whole damn time.”  

The room fell silent, the hum of the hotel’s air conditioning the only sound. Jeff stared at her, his chest rising and falling as if he’d run a marathon.  

Finally, he stood, the bottle dangling loosely from his fingers. “I can’t do this,” he said, his voice shaky but resolute. “Not here. Not now.”  

Avril nodded, her expression unreadable. “Then I’ll follow,” she said simply.  

Jeff frowned. “Follow where?”  

“Wherever you’re going next,” she said, rising to meet him. “I’ve got nothing better to do, and you look like someone who could use a co-pilot.”  

For a moment, Jeff just stared at her, the enormity of her words sinking in. Then he sighed, a deep, weary sound, and turned toward the door.  

“Suit yourself,” he muttered, the faintest flicker of something—gratitude? Relief?—crossing his face before he pushed it away.
