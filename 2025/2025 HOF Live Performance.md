“They Killed the Hall”: Commuted’s 16-Minute Magnum Opus Turns Induction into Apocalypse

By Rhett Collier, Senior Music Writer

CLEVELAND — Public Auditorium on induction night is a fortress. Polished wood underfoot, history hanging from the rafters, the audience a living museum of road scars and gold records. These are people who’ve survived blown monitors, bottle fights, guitar heroes melting down in real time. They’ve been drenched in feedback and beer, kissed by the famous and spit on by the same. They thought nothing could really hit them anymore.

They hadn’t met this.

Avril Lavigne rolled in on a Harley like a lit match in a gasoline church. No teleprompter, no script, no patience. She grinned like she had a secret worth ruining you over and took the mic without so much as a breath. The induction “speech” was a slash-and-burn. She carved Kathy Espich’s name into the night like a gravestone rubbing, spit a filthy one-liner about Jeremy Evans’ tuning that left the front row blushing, and fired shots so personal you could hear the sharp inhale from the label tables.

Then Jeff and Bill hit the first chord — low, hot, seismic. The kind of note that doesn’t just shake the floor, it shifts your organs. Jeremy and Avril stepped forward, bodies angled toward each other, and the solos began. This wasn’t your tidy, polite call-and-response; this was foreplay you could smell in the air. Jeremy’s lines sliced like serrated steel, jagged and insistent. Avril’s came back slow and deliberate, bending strings until they whimpered, letting the tone bloom into something wet and aching before letting it drop. They passed the lead like lovers swapping control — thirteen, fourteen times — each handoff dirtier than the last.

No count-off. No cue. Just the sudden mechanical churn of a Harley engine through the PA, run through so much fuzz and filter it was both beast and ghost. Low idle turning into throttle bursts, gears clunking like chains dropping, harmonics screaming like mating whales under gunfire. It didn’t just fill the room; it filled you. By minute three it was in your bones. By minute five, in your pulse. By minute seven, you weren’t hearing a motorcycle — you were seeing her. Leather hot against bare skin, wrists straining, eyes locked on the Devil in a room that smelled like sweat and motel dust, every lyric daring him to break her and every chorus proving he couldn’t.

And then the song took her out of there. She hit the badlands somewhere around minute eight, riding flat-out under a low Jersey sun, the asphalt shimmering ahead, grit and salt air mixing in her teeth. By minute twelve she was rolling past shuttered diners and rusted boardwalk rides, the ocean flashing between buildings like it was keeping pace. By the final minute she was in Cape May, boots off, toes buried in sand, sipping a cold lemonade while behind her the guitars burned themselves into a final, impossibly sweet harmony.

The crowd wasn’t moving. Eyes fixed, jaws slack, half-empty drinks sweating on the tables. Two label presidents stood, turned, and walked into the Cleveland night without a word. Bruce Springsteen — who’d lived those roads she sang about — muttered “Well, fuck” and sat down like he’d been knocked sideways.

When it ended, there was no applause. Just breathing — ragged, uneven, like the room had been underwater. Then the noise came, not loud so much as desperate. Survival instinct.

Backstage, someone said it, not smiling, not joking: “They killed the Hall.” And in that moment, with the Jersey coast still ringing in your ears, you knew it was the truth.
