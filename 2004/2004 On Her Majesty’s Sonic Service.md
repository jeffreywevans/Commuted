# **On Her Majesty’s Sonic Service**
## **INT./EXT. TWICKENHAM STADIUM – LONDON – DAWN – SUMMER 2004**

Cold light, flat and English. The kind that makes metal look honest.  Fourteen articulated lorries idle along the service road, coughing diesel breath into the ribs of the stadium. Forklifts beep, reverse, pivot, dance. It’s a ballet for brutes.

JIM LAWSON (48), Nottingham-born, crow’s-feet cut into his eyes like knife work, stands with a clipboard and a Motorola pinned to his shoulder. Stopwatch on his wrist. Cigarette tucked behind his ear like a carpenter’s pencil. He surveys the pitch the way generals used to measure a killing field.

On the clipboard: a grid sketched in ballpoint. MOTHER GRID scrawled at center. Around it: PA MAINS, SUB ARCS, VIDEO WALL A/B, PYRO LINES—NO CREW WITHIN 6M DURING HOT. In the margins: math. Capacities, loads, angles. Roman numerals for the truss runs. He likes the Roman numerals. London likes its ruins polished.

He clicks the radio.

**LAWSON** (quiet, even)
Riggers, you’re up. Eight on the steel. Three on each V-DOSC hang. Winds are friendly. Let’s not make them interesting.

Up in the nosebleeds, black dots become men. Riggers move like spiders, slinging one-ton motors on shackles, feeding chain. Down on the pitch, Stageco carpenters unlock the first truss carts, steel humming as it rolls. A steward in a hi-vis vest, **UNION STEWARD**, grizzled, amused, watches with arms folded and eyes like calculators.
  
**LAWSON** (to Steward, nodding)
We’ll keep to the call sheet. Ten o’clock tea. Twelve-thirty lunch. Six o’clock smoke break whether you need it or not.

**STEWARD** (dry)
You know the religion.

**LAWSON**
I tither regular.

He signs the Permit to Work with a stubby pencil. It’s a gorgeous, stupid ritual. The Romans would’ve stamped a wax seal and bled a goat. Here they initial boxes.

Forklifts peel a flight case labeled FOH—MIDAS H3000 off Truck 2. The audio chief, a Scot with a martyr’s bun, barks to his lads. They peel open 19-pin Socapex tails and snake them toward dimmer world. Lawson steps over coils like they’re sleeping dogs.

**LAWSON** (into radio)
Audio, you’re clear to hang. Mains first. Sides after carp, do not swing that motor over my lads. Repeat: no swinging steel over the carpenters. I don’t fancy writing poetry to their mums.

He checks the anemometer taped to the mother grid callout. 6.2 m/s. Friendly. He writes it down anyway. Paper remembers things radios forget.

Two Martin MAC2000 lamps sit on a carpet of bubble wrap like eggs. Lighting crew crack cases, pull cable, count out Powercon jumpers with a gambler’s mouth—silent math, lucky totals. A kid tries to help push a video case and gets scolded by a lampy with a cigarette.

**LAMPY**
That’s not your bloody guild, son. Smoke if you must, but don’t touch her.

The kid steps back. Lights a cigarette. Lawson half-smiles. England is efficient until you ask it to sin against a boundary, and then it lights a smoke and waits for an Act of Parliament. He respects it. He hates it. Both truths live fine in his chest.

On the pitch, the stage grows ribs. Black aluminum vertebrae bolt to legs, then accept a skin of non-slip deck. The lifts rise, hydraulic whine like whalesong. Lawson paces the perimeter, tapping the deck boots with the toe of his steel-caps. He likes the thud. The thud tells him how the weight’s talking to the ground.

**LAWSON** (into radio)
Pyro, you’re on my hip in five for safety. Bring the Le Maitre paperwork and your “sorry we set Wembley on fire that one time” apology face.

A **PYRO TECH** appears like a conjurer, arms full of binders. Lawson flips, scans, caps a box with a palm.

**LAWSON** (cont.)
This line here, six meters standoff. Make it eight. We’re in Britain. Insurance men here sleep with calculators.

**PYRO TECH**
Eight it is.

Lawson moves. He never runs. Running says you don’t know your own plan. He walks fast enough to draft breeze behind him.

If you stood in the center of the pitch and looked up, you’d think a cathedral wanted to be born but forgot the stone. Black truss spine, V-DOSC arrays like wings. Video walls, Barco ILite, still skeletal, modules in crates labeled by row and column like a librarian’s private code. He hears the LEDs click as they wake on test power. It’s a tiny ticking sound, like rain on hot tin.

He pauses at Powerworld. Rows of Powerlock tails in red, yellow, blue, green, black, fat like pythons. Camlok adapters in tidy coils. An electrician, a **SPARK**, leans into a 400A distribution rack, checking torque on a lug.

**LAWSON**
Not too dainty. Not too eager. I want forty-five Newton-meters and no heroics.

**SPARK**
Aye.

He checks the schedule in his head. 06:00 steel on the ground, 07:00 rig in, 08:30 power live to dimmers, 09:30 line-check, 10:00 tea, 11:15 video walls full-tilt, 12:30 lunch, 13:30 trim heights final, 15:00 pyro hot, 16:30 doors. It’s the same song in every city with different vowels. London’s vowels are rounded, efficient. He likes football grounds; he likes the way sound bends and comes back honest. Anfield breathes. Old Trafford sulks. Twickenham carries like a chapel.

**LAWSON** (into radio)
FOH, drop that H3 in her bunker. Keep the snake off the thoroughfare; stewards will have my head if a punter trips over your ego.

He cuts across the service alley. A catering trolley rattles past: beans, toast, beige. He scowls at it like it owes him money. Food is the only religion he refuses.

**UNION STEWARD** (ghosting him)
Beans are protein.

**LAWSON**
So’s regret.

He sticks the cigarette in his mouth and doesn’t light it. Habit, not hunger.  A forklift beeps and a truss segment slips a fraction when a green lad throws the pin too slow. Lawson’s voice doesn’t rise; it narrows.

**LAWSON**
Stop.

(beat)
Wind the chain back half a meter. Pin on the ground. Not in the air. Gravity is not your mate. Again.

They do it again. Better. He nods once. The nod is a currency; he spends it rarely. The lads beam anyway.

On the far side, the PA flies—left hang up, then right, each array a curved black jaw. The sub arc grows along the pit rail, SB218s like coffins laid end-to-end. A system tech walks the line with a tape measure and a laser, muttering distances that mean cardioid magic later. Lawson clocks the numbers. Thirteen meters center-to-center. Good. Tonight the low end will feel like a leviathan rolling in the Thames.

A band member, hood up, sunglasses, moving like a rumor, drifts along the tunnel mouth, peering out at the skeleton of his own evening. Lawson meets his eye, gives him a grunt that means I see you; I’ve accounted for you; you do not belong here yet. The figure nods and vanishes. That’s the extent of it. That’s enough.

He checks his watch. Flicks the stopwatch. Ten o’clock. The air changes like the tide turning. Bodies slow. Tools get set down. A collective invisible chain yanks this entire apparatus into Tea.

He hates it. He adores it. It keeps men alive.

LAWSON (into radio)
Tea. Ten minutes. Smoke what you must. When we come back, we hang the video skins and I want that mother grid trimmed to ten point eight. Don’t make me miss you.

He steps onto the stage. Runs a palm along the deck seam. It’s flush. He hears the stadium waking up properly now, stewards arguing about barricade shape, a distant compressor coughing, gulls. Always gulls in London, screaming like they saw God and didn’t like the haircut.

The union lads form small circles, lighters clicking like castanets. Someone tells a joke. Someone else lies about a footballer he once saw at a petrol station. Lawson leans on a road case, flips the clipboard, checks the Costs page. Overtime multipliers glare at him like little red devils. Time-and-a-half after eight. Double after ten. The numbers don’t scare him; they offend his sense of rhythm.

He looks at the stadium and sees the Colosseum. If Rome had had LED, they would’ve sold different sins. The Romans understood labor: the craft of dying for spectacle. His lads don’t die. That’s the point. They make a living raising a thing that looks like death with lights.

Tea ends. The pitch inhales.

**LAWSON** (into radio)
Video, build me a sky. Row A through M, left to right, with the ladders. Check your burn-in. If I see a dead pixel the size of a sixpence, I’ll feed it to you. LX, power test on the MACs. No pan until I say. Audio, pink the left hang slow. Sub delay at nine meters. Start there, we’ll season later.

Modules rise. Hands slap home plastic tabs in a rhythm. Click, click, click, like bones snapping into place on a kindly skeleton. The video op calls a test pattern and a barber pole floods the wall. Red, green, blue. All there. He watches for the cancerous black, doesn’t see it. Good.

A gust runs its fingers over the hangs. The anemometer ticks up. 8.1 m/s. He makes a mark. He remembers a different ground in a different country where that number went wrong and a mother kept his name in a drawer for ten years before forgiving herself. He never runs. He just pays attention.

**LAWSON** (into radio, softer)
Riggers, secure your tails. Weather’s feeling philosophical.

The pyro line goes down—tape, cones, signs that lie about being friendly. The pyro tech mounts flame projectors on black plates and clips a lanyard to his belt with a little flourish, showman even when no one’s looking. Lawson reads the distances again. Eight meters. He knows what eight meters looks like even in his sleep.

At FOH, a tarp comes off the MIDAS like a reverent unveiling. The surface gleams, a promise of faders and absolution. A tech tickles it, then pulls his hand back like he’s touched a reliquary. Lawson allows himself one private thought: analog boards sound like sin forgiven in warm wood. He’ll never say it out loud. The lads would never let him live it down.

He moves to Barricade World, measures the pit lane with a tape he’s had since ’91. It’s four millimeters short of his mark and it annoys him in a way only professionals understand. He squints down the line and sees the Roman curve in it, the arc that keeps a crowd from breaking its own ribs on joy.

A stadium manager appears with a sheaf of forms; he signs with a flick. Insurance, noise curfew, prohibited pyrotechnics. The curfew is a joke that always ends with a wink. He hates winks. He plans around them anyway.

**LAWSON** (into radio)
Dimmer check. Run the chase. Give me something vulgar.

The rig blushes alive. VL3000s wink. MACs swing like planets; a crude chase runs the trusses, a carnival in grayscale. For a second the whole stadium is a lighthouse. A gull screams approval. Somewhere deep in the bones of the place, a drunk will later swear he saw a god wave.

**LAWSON**
Monitors, ring out the wedges gentle. We’re not summoning whales; just the good ghosts.

Feedback yawns and gets strangled. A man in monitor world makes a face like he’s smelled something personal. He tweaks. The face smooths. Lawson nods again. Another coin spent.

He checks the time. 13:12. He could write the rest of the day without looking, but that’s where hubris lives, and he’s outlived too many men who thought they married perfection. He lives with almost. He worships enough.

A golf cart buzzes near the tunnel—management traffic. Lawson lifts two fingers in a wave as it passes, neither deferent nor warm. The cart vanishes. The wave returns to his pocket, unremarked.

**LAWSON** (into radio)
Trim to ten point eight. Lock it.

(to himself)
Give the sky a ceiling.

Motors whine. Chain feeds. The mother grid rises that last small, holy distance, then stops obediently where his pencil said it would. The pitch looks suddenly inevitable. He loves that feeling. When a day of belligerent parts collapses into fate.

He lets himself light the cigarette. First drag all day. It tastes like victory and someone else’s lungs. He stares at the stage and sees the ghost of night inside it. The legion will come—stewards, punters, kids in shirts older than they are—and the lads will step aside, invisible artists of gravity.

The radio kisses his shoulder.

**PYRO TECH** (V.O.)
Pyro hot in thirty. You want a test burp?

**LAWSON**
Make it a whisper. Health & Safety get hives if you shout.

A gout of fire licks the air like a pleased cat. Even in daylight, people turn. It’s Rome for a breath. He marks the time in his head: thirty-one minutes to doors.

He crushes the cigarette under his boot, lines up the last three boxes on his clipboard. Doors. Changeover. House to black. The kind of silence that isn’t. The kind that’s an inhalation.

He looks out over the pitch and hears the old words as if a thousand road crew in tunics were muttering it in bad Latin behind him.

We who are about to die salute you.

No one dies. Not on his deck. They just build a thing that makes mortals feel immortal for ninety minutes. He taps the stopwatch.  The day obeys.

## **INT. TWICKENHAM STADIUM – SITE OFFICE PORTACABIN – LATE MORNING – SUMMER 2004**

A kettle clicks off. The window hums with distant forklifts. The whiteboard is a thicket of arrows: **RIG, PA, VIDEO, PYRO, BARRICADE**. On the desk: a laminated license, a stack of **RAMS** (Risk Assessment & Method Statement), two high-vis vests neatly folded.

**JIM LAWSON** stands with a clipboard, stopwatch on his wrist, pencil tucked behind his ear. **CREMEANS** sits opposite, back straight, hands clean, eyes taking everything in like scripture. Outside, London is surprisingly kind: blue sky, a light southerly, the pitch a green plate.

**LAWSON**
Right. Let’s make it plain. Weather first. High pressure. Light wind out of the south, five meters a second at trim, gusts have been shy. No rain. Dry deck. That’s a blessing and a hazard; sound’s going to carry like gossip at a funeral.

He taps the anemometer readout clipped to the clipboard.

**LAWSON** (cont.)
We’ll stay under ten meters a second on the grid for pyro. If it spikes, I kill flame. No appeals. We’ve got CO₂ jets as a pretty understudy if we have to sack the fire.

**CREMEANS**
Understood. Reliability beats drama. If you cut flame, you cut flame. I’ll handle the artist conversation.

Lawson nods, appreciative: a manager who speaks fluent consequence.

**LAWSON**
Local law and licence next. Council’s noise conditions are attached to the premises license. Limits measured at front-of-house: we’ve got a LAeq window they care about over fifteen minutes, and a low-frequency admonishment that isn’t written like a law but feels like one when the complaints line starts glowing. The neighbors in St Margarets will ring if their teacups march off the shelves.

He flips the laminated sheet.

**LAWSON** (cont.)
So: house wants a ninety-eight to one-hundred dB A-weighted average at FOH over fifteen. They’ll tolerate short peaks. They don’t love sub-bass peaks. They’ve warned us about boundary monitors. There’s a mic in a garden three streets over that sends red lights to a man with a clipboard. I don’t scare easy, but I respect a man with a clipboard who can end a show.

**CREMEANS**
Then we tailor the array. Down-tilt the hangs, narrow the throw. Cardioid the sub arc, point the anger into the floor, not the borough. Jeff’s preference is robust in the forty-to-sixty-three band. He will call it “honest.” The license will call it “a nuisance.” I vote we give the license a minor victory.

Lawson’s mouth twitches. In his language, that’s a grin.

**LAWSON**
We’ll steer the arc. I’ve got the system tech plotting a broadside cardioid across the pit with end-fire on the wings. That’ll carve the backside a good twelve, fourteen dB. We’ll taper the low end after ten p.m., keep the song list from stacking sub-heavy numbers at the back half.

He underlines <u>CURFEW</u> on the whiteboard.

**LAWSON** (cont.)
Curfew is 22:30. Not “about half ten.” Ten-thirty. After that, the fine schedule starts behaving like a slot machine. They can and will pull mains at eleven. House lights become our judge and jury. So we build a show that lands at 22:27 with an encore that can be cut like a ribbon if we’re long. I want three versions of the last quarter: Green, Amber, Red. Green gets the poetry. Red gets the exit wound.

**CREMEANS**
Sure. I’ll brief set-caller. We’ll keep the banter lean. If we slip behind, we’ll cut transitions, not players. No speeches about destiny. No thank-yous with zip codes.

He places a finger on the license.

**CREMEANS** (cont.)
I need your eyes on workforce safety. You know my threshold. If we save money and lose a man, we failed the only test that matters.

Lawson’s pencil stops. A shadow like the memory of a dropped shackle crosses his face; then it’s gone.

**LAWSON**
Noise at Work regs are the same hymn across the island. We’ve got Class 4 plugs issued at the door. Muffs available for anyone on deck. Rotations in PA world so nobody lives under the arrays for an hour straight during pink-noise hell. Hot-work permit is live only during pyro mount with CO₂ and extinguishers on every corner of the deck. Fall protection on the mother grid. One-ton motors double-checked, redundancies on anything that could decide to become a guillotine if it feels neglected.

He sets down the clipboard, looks Cremeans in the eye.

**LAWSON** (cont.)
No running. No heroics. If I call a stop, it stays stopped until the reason changes its mind.

**CREMEANS**
If you call a stop, I’m your megaphone. No questions asked on the floor. We can argue later when the building is empty and everyone is breathing.

Lawson flips to Pyro & Special Effects.

**LAWSON**

Flame height capped at what we filed. Eight-meter standoff from audience. No ignition during confetti, no confetti during ignition. Haze is water-based; we’ve done the COSHH; respiratory flags are up for the asthmatics. Stage exits clear. Cable runs taped and bridged. Barricade is a proper C-rail, not that portable letter to the coroner. Pits staffed every four meters. Crowd surf protocol rehearsed, two hands up, head over rail, feet down, escort to release. If the pit boss says we’re at capacity on a pocket, we feather the lights and lengthen changeovers to bleed pressure.

**CREMEANS**
Good. I prefer to disappoint sound men before I disappoint mothers.

He taps the Volume note.

**CREMEANS** (cont.)
About the band’s preferences—Jeff’s bass, Jim’s snare, Kathy’s… voice. For tonight: Jim will push his kick. It will taste like thunder. If House starts blinking at FOH, I want the low-shelf contingency rehearsed. We will not chase approval; we will honor the license.

**LAWSON**
FOH engineer’s got a traffic-light box—green, amber, red tied to the council feed. He’ll mix to the colors. I’ve told him if it goes red during the big number, we ride the master like a gentleman and don’t make me choose between art and the mains breaker.

He scribbles CAMERA LINES on the whiteboard.

**LAWSON** (cont.)
Media: seven cameras, four jibs, two dollies. I’ve put no-go lines on the deck for camera ops—nobody crosses pyro troughs. Lens caps on until I say hot; no surprises for flame sensors.

**CREMEANS**
Thank you. I like my surprises in the encore, not the coroner’s report.

A gull cackles somewhere above the roofline. The kettle ticks in its cooling.

**LAWSON**
Last bits. Emergency e-stops are here, here, and FOH. Security gets the map, not just the story. Medical is staged Stage Left with a proper ALS kit, not just plasters and hope. Evac routes are printed and taped by the vomitories. If we need to empty fifty thousand, we’ll do it without practising chaos theory.

**CREMEANS**
If we empty fifty thousand, I’ll be at the head of the line with a torch and a prayer, and you’ll have the radio. We’ll keep it orderly. We’ll keep it human.

Lawson inhales, considers, then adds the thing that matters to him most.

**LAWSON**
One more rule. It sounds like poetry. It’s not. If a man’s tired, he sits. I don’t pay for bravery. I pay for tomorrow.

Cremeans folds his hands.

**CREMEANS**
You’ll have no argument from me. Glory costs more than it’s worth. We’re here to deliver a show and return the crew in the same number we borrowed them.

Lawson signs the Method Statement with a blunt pencil. Slides it across. Cremeans signs with a tidy pen stroke that looks like a promise.

**LAWSON**
Right, then. We’ll set FOH at ninety-eight to start. Keep the subs honest until the sun slides down. After twenty-two hundred, we’ll lean the low end ten percent. If the garden mic starts tattling, we’ll notch a whisker at fifty-seven and sixty-three and let the guitars do a bit of the shouting.

**CREMEANS**
I’ll prime the set-caller. I’ll tell Jeff the same thing a kinder man would tell the ocean: you are beautiful; you are powerful; and today, you will be measured.

That earns a proper smile.

**LAWSON**
Doors in thirty-five. One last thing, if the wind decides to find Christ and starts preaching at the grid, I’ll kill pyro mid-song. Crowd won’t like it.

**CREMEANS**
Then we’ll give them a different miracle. Light instead of fire. Harmony instead of heat. I’ll warn comms.

They both stand. Two men who don’t need to shake hands to know a covenant has been made. Outside, the day holds. The sky is English and impossibly blue. The stadium waits like a tame colosseum, hungry for safe thunder.

**LAWSON**
We who are about to work… intend to go home.

**CREMEANS**
That’s the only encore I’m unwilling to miss.

## **INT. TWICKENHAM STADIUM – PORTACABIN / SITE OFFICE – LATE AFTERNOON – SUMMER 2004**

The window hums with England. Blue sky that looks borrowed. Flags barely stirring. A gull cackles like it knows the setlist.

On the table: laminate badges, a pot of pens, a Method Statement fat with tabs. On the whiteboard: CURFEW 22:30 in block letters, and under it, LAeq(15) WATCH and a crude heart monitor drawn through the word BASS.

**JIM LAWSON** stands with a clipboard and a stopwatch. **CREMEANS** sits opposite, posture straight as a hymn, hands folded like a man who respects both prayer and paperwork.

**LAWSON**
Let’s talk about the ending. *Come Inside Me*. Eleven and a half at one-twenty. That’s one thousand three hundred and eighty impacts to the building. The last eleven minutes are not a song so much as an engineering test.

**CREMEANS**
It’s our ritual. We will honor it and send everyone home happy.

**LAWSON**
We’ll do both, provided we don’t make the borough glow. Cleveland, you had an empty bowl, highs ran riot and the low end felt theatrical. Here we’ve got seventy thousand bodies. Meat eats the treble; bass walks through bone like it paid for a ticket. What felt “loud” there will feel “biblical” here.

He flips open the clipboard, reveals a laminated sheet: LOCAL CONDITIONS & NOISE LICENCE.

**LAWSON** (cont.)

Council cares about two things: the LAeq over fifteen and the low-frequency nasties that spill into St Margarets. There’s a boundary mic in a garden that sends a Christmas tree of alerts to a man with a clipboard. He can’t cut the mains himself, but he can make the house do it. So: we mix to our art until that man’s lights say we’re sinning.

**CREMEANS** (smiling)
We will not lead the venue into temptation.
 
**LAWSON**
Amen. So our final sequence goes like this.

(he writes FINALE: <font color="#205867">GREEN</font> / <font color="#f79646">AMBER</font> / <font color="#ff0000">RED</font> on the board)

Green is the dream—full show file, subs honest, pyro live, eleven thirty of thunder. Amber trims the sub-arc three dB and nudges the masters one decibel below where pride lives. Red is the lifeboat: high-pass the 808 at thirty-eight Hertz, multiband clamps at fifty-seven and sixty-three, psychoacoustic juice around one-twenty and two-forty to keep the feeling of bass while the neighbors keep their crockery.

**CREMEANS**
Please articulate who calls the color.

**LAWSON**
I do, with FOH as my echo. FOH has a traffic-light tied to the council feed on the doghouse: green/amber/red. If we see red for more than five seconds inside any sixty, we are not having a conversation; we are changing colors. No heroics. You’ll back me on comms?

**CREMEANS**
Without reservation.

**LAWSON**
Good. Now, the physics of your finale: Jim’s got an 808 and a kit. That’s two engines pushing the same wall. We’ll sidechain the 808 to the kick on a short leash so they don’t sum into a medieval siege. Jeff is the bigger problem—he’s disciplined, and discipline at one-twenty is a pile driver. We’ll print a parallel saturator to throw second- and third-order harmonics into the one-to-two hundred band. That keeps it huge to the human ear when I ask FOH to lean the subs ten percent after 22:10.

**CREMEANS**
Jeff prefers honesty in the forty-to-sixty-three band. He will not enjoy this.

**LAWSON**
He’ll still feel unbeatable on deck. The punters will still feel the room breathe. The borough will not feel its windows reconsider their vows. That’s the triangle we’re solving.

> [!caution]
> He circles CURFEW 22:30.

**LAWSON** (cont.)
And we’re not blowing curfew. Doors were kind. Weather’s kind. The fines are not kind. I want the downbeat of the finale at 22:16 latest. If we hit 22:24 and we’re still somewhere between verse and delirium, set-caller gets in your ear: Lose the call-and-response. Skip the false ending. Straight to coda. We land this aircraft at 22:27, gratefully.

**CREMEANS**
The encore is not a sacrament. The curfew is a covenant. I will prepare the band accordingly.

**LAWSON**
Brilliant. Now crowd dynamics. That rhythm will turn the pit into a piston. Barricade’s a proper C-rail; we’ve doubled stewards in the corners and staged ALS medical Stage Left. Pit protocol is rehearsed—two hands up, head over rail, feet down, escort to release. If pit boss calls a pressure pocket, I’ll ask LX for a thirty-second cool wash—take the temperature down without insulting anyone’s evening.

**CREMEANS**
We will serve the room, not provoke it.

**LAWSON**
Exactly. Pyro: no ignition in the last minute if we’re on Amber or Red. Wind’s been five to seven today—friendly—but I don’t set fire to friendly. If the anemometer pushes past eight on the grid during the finale, flame is dead and CO₂ jets take the bow. I won’t be asking; I’ll be doing.

**CREMEANS**
You will have my voice on comms and my silence in your way.

Lawson pauses—two beats—somewhere behind his eyes a memory of falling steel and a mother in a dark coat, then he lets it go.

**LAWSON**
Last Cleveland note: empty bowls lie to you. You had ghosts for audience. Tonight you have lungs. The highs will soften; the lows won’t. So we rehearse the step-downs like music cues.

(he writes them down as if they were lyrics)
> [!important]
> Step A at 02:00—subs drop 1 dB, 808 HPF to forty-two.
> Step B at 05:30—if the garden tattles, another 1 dB, saturator up a hair.
> Step C at 09:00—only if FOH sees sustained red—low-shelf –3 dB, spotlight on melody, let the crowd sing what the subs can’t.

**CREMEANS**
If the house must be appeased, we will give it something beautiful to remember.

**LAWSON**
That’s the job. Give them thunder, then send them home hearing. On the human side: earplugs on deck are issued, not optional. Rotations in PA world—no one stands under the hang for more than twenty during pink. E-stops: here, here, FOH. Evac routes taped by the vomitories. If I call Stop, it’s a full stop until the reason changes its mind.

**CREMEANS**
I will repeat that sentence to anyone who needs it.

Lawson underlines <u>WE GO HOME</u>.

**LAWSON**
Two more bits. First, optics: cameras have no-go lines marked around the pyro troughs. Nobody gets cinematic and steps into the dragon’s mouth. House lights at 22:28 like dawn in an old painting.

**CREMEANS**
Light instead of fire. Applause instead of fines. Understood.

The kettle clicks off. Somewhere outside a forklift beeps in polite English.

**LAWSON**
Right, then. Your band gets their proper ending. My crew keeps all their fingers. The council gets to sleep in the smug knowledge they saved a teacup.

**CREMEANS**
If a teacup is saved and seventy thousand souls feel briefly immortal, I will call it a good night.

We who are about to make a racket… have done our homework.

Then let us keep faith with the plan.

They stand. No handshake needed. Outside, Twickenham waits like a tamed colosseum, hungry for safe thunder. The finale already exists in the air—the boom boom boom of a thousand hearts practicing. The two men step into it like engineers walking onto a battlefield they intend to leave alive.

## **INT. TWICKENHAM – GREEN ROOM – 47 MINUTES TO DOORS – SUMMER 2004**

Concrete walls, scuffed couches, bottled water sweating on a folding table. You can feel the stadium breathing through the cinderblock.

**CREMEANS** steps in with a road-map brain and a messenger bag. He doesn’t raise his voice. He doesn’t need to. He sets a clipboard down; opens a neat folio; flips a small portable whiteboard to a page that already has three words on it:

<font color="#00b050">GREEN</font> — <font color="#f79646">AMBER</font> — <font color="#ff0000">RED</font>

Around the room: **JEFF** leans on his bass case, arms folded, the stubborn set of a man who trusts physics more than people.  **JIM** is pacing, drumsticks in his hoodie pocket like live ammo. **KATHY** has her water bottle and a look that says make it quick, make it real.
**GILLEY** sprawls on the couch, knee bouncing, eyes hot. **JEREMY** against the wall, calm, coiled.  **BILL** perched on a road case, fingers twitching like he’s already programming light.

Cremeans clicks a dry-erase.

**CREMEANS**
We’re talking about one song. “Come Inside Me.” Eleven minutes, thirty seconds. It’s the signature. It also interacts with London law in a way Cleveland did not.

**JEFF**
European Union fuckery.

**CREMEANS**
Different license, different neighborhood, same band. We respect all three.

He draws three horizontal bars beneath the colors.

**CREMEANS** (cont.)
Green is your Cleveland dream: full sub array, 808 and kick married, no taper until the coda. Amber trims low end two decibels after 22:10 and rides a parallel saturator so the perceived weight stays put. Red is the lifeboat: high-pass the 808 at thirty-eight hertz, multiband clamps at fifty-seven and sixty-three, psychoacoustic lift at one-twenty. Audience still feels huge. Neighbors keep their crockery.

**JIM**
That’s highly bullshit. The beat’s the point. You de-fang the low end, you neuter the ritual.

**CREMEANS**
The ritual is momentum, not a number. We’ll sidechain the 808 to your kick so they don’t sum into a pile driver. You will still feel like thunder. We’ll lose the damage, not the drive.

**JIM**
Sidechain’s fine if the room’s honest. Stadiums lie.

**CREMEANS**
Then we’ll make them tell the truth. FOH mixes to a traffic light tied to the council mic. If it glows red, we change color. No debate, no drama. We keep the show; we keep the mains.

He turns to **KATHY**.

**KATHY**
All I care about: does this kneecap my vocal? I am not fighting mud in the second half of the song.

**CREMEANS**
You won’t. Monitor world keeps your wedge bright in the two-to-four kilohertz band; sidefills get a small scoop in the three-hundred region when we go Amber, so your consonants cut and your chest stays clean. If we hit Red, LX gives you a cool white wash and FOH feathers guitars up a hair—your voice becomes the anchor the crowd sings to. You’ll own the room when the subs take a breath.

Kathy nods, not happy, but satisfied she won’t be swimming in soup.

**GILLEY**
The entire point is frenzy. We want seventy thousand people jumping a hundred times a minute until their Achilles snap and their grandchildren feel it. You start trimming at the waist, you kill the bounce.

**CREMEANS**
We’re not trimming the bounce. We’re steering it.

(he sketches a shallow arc under AMBER)
Array down-tilt and broadside cardioid mean the floor gets the punch and the roof gets the story. LX will run a vertical blade chase in the strobes on the quarter-notes, so eyes and legs agree. You’ll lead the chant at the bridge; crowd does the heavy lifting. Frenzy without fines.

**GILLEY**
I don’t want “polite London frenzy.” I want riot-in-a-stadium.

**CREMEANS**
You’ll get rapture. We’ll stop short of court.

**JEREMY**
This is the signature. We nailed it in Cleveland. Exact arrangement. No taper. It worked.

**CREMEANS**
Cleveland was an autopsy room. Empty bowl. Ghosts absorb treble; ghosts don’t call councils. London is lungs. Bodies damp highs and let bass walk through bone. You sounded loud there; you will feel biblical here. Biblical attracts inspectors. We answer with design, not apology.

A beat. Bill clears his throat.

Man, I just want to turn this into the biggest rave in London. Lights, haze, sequenced madness. Why can’t we?

**CREMEANS**
We can, by moving dopamine out of the subwoofers and into the sky.

(points at BILL)
You and LX build a quarter-note strobe ladder and a one-sixteenth shimmer on the truss edges for the last three minutes. Give me a cathedral tremor without asking the subs to do all the sinning. You can make ten-thousand cameras record the same rhythm. That’s a rave.

You’re telling me to be quiet in my own house.

I’m asking you to be heard in this one.

And when the traffic light sneezes because some guy in St. Margarets doesn’t like joy?
