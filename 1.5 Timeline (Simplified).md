# **The Timeline to the World of Commuted**

---

*Commuted* takes place in a heightened version of America from 1989 through the 2030s — a deeply textured, emotionally resonant parallel timeline that mirrors our world but tunes it to a different frequency. The characters don’t just live through history — they change its rhythm.

---
# **1. Setting Anchors – Time and Place**

## **1989–1991:** 

Rural and suburban Ohio. Rust Belt detritus. Unheated garages. Small-town oppressiveness contrasted with bursts of teenage audacity. Local malls, school dances, VHS rentals, the back rows of used record stores are all sacraments.  Commuted takes almost four years to become an overnight success.

---
## **1992–2005:** 

The road. Tour buses, motel parking lots, dingy green rooms. Industry cities,  L.A., Chicago, London, Berlin, home is where you sleep at night. Fame is isolating. Sex is currency. The sound is the only constant.

---
## **2006–2012:** 

The reckoning and mini-renaissance. [[4.4 JIM BROWN character bible|Jim]], [[Bill]], and [[4.2 JEREMY GILLEY character bible|Gilley]] begin to clean up their substance abuse issues and attempt to reforge personal identities. [[4.6 JEREMY EVANS character bible|Jeremy]]’s slow-burning alcoholism becomes impossible to ignore. [[4.5 KATHY ESPICH character bible|Kathy]] grapples with cocaine dependency during an intense creative period. [[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff]] endures a turbulent 2009 divorce after twelve years of marriage, marked by infidelity, artistic jealousy, and long absences. The band, unexpectedly, produces some of its most mature and introspective work during this time.

---
## **2012–2014:** 

Collapse and infamy. Kathy dies suddenly of a stroke shortly after voluntarily entering drug rehab, devastating the band. Jeff comes under intense legal scrutiny for multiple federal and state-level felony allegations in Massachusetts. A streaming biopic series, co-written and co-produced by Quentin Tarantino,  is announced, then collapses in the wake of Kathy’s death on October 31st, 2012. Jeff avoids indictment but spirals into a creative fugue state, resulting in the band's most ambitious and divisive record, **_Be First, Be Smarter or Cheat**._ The album wins three more Grammys but nearly destroys what’s left of Commuted.

---
## **2014–2019:**

**Commuted 2.0** emerges — not with fire, but with flickers. The band tours rarely and writes even less. They remain culturally visible, mostly coasting on legacy until the release of 2016’s ***You're Meant to Lose***, an emotionally bruising album penned largely by Gilley with lyrical refinements by Jeff. The record shocks fans and critics alike, revealing a version of Gilley the world hadn’t seen, serious, lyrical, and searing. The album reignites speculation about what Commuted 1.0 might have become had Gilley committed more to craft than chasing Hollywood starlets and nightclub shadows.

---
## **2020:**

Tragedy returns. Gilley dies on January 4th, 2020 at the very beginning of the COVID-19 pandemic, succumbing to a heart attack followed by a stroke. The band, already fragile, becomes even more fractured. With no one willing or able to take over vocal duties, and grief compounded by lockdown isolation, Commuted retreats from public life. The remaining "[[9.4 Commuted Core Four|Core Four]]" members raise their children, nurse their losses, and let the music go silent. Jeff turns to writing video game scores. Bill takes his EDM production seriously for the first time. The survivors, quietly and collectively, say: fuck it.

---
## **2021–2023:**

Interregnum. Commuted is famous for being famous, a band more myth than machine. After over 30 years in the public eye, the surviving members settle into quiet domesticity, collecting royalty checks, exploring personal interests, and tending long-term relationships. Cremeans' wife succumbs to breast cancer in 2022, prompting him to take time off to finish homeschooling his three daughters and grieve in private. The band's silence feels less like a breakup and more like the long exhale after an endless sprint.

---
## **2024–2032:** 

Reawakening and reinvention. Jeff, restless and full of unfinished business, declares he has things to do before he fades away. He engineers a catastrophically awkward interview on BBC2 that forces a dramatic intervention among the remaining band members. Out of that chaos, **Commuted 3.0** is born, a phoenix built from ashes, grief, and audacity. The surviving "Core Four" guys extend an invitation to Avril Lavigne to front the band. She says yes, and brings more chaos and change than they bargained for, sharp-edged charisma, commercial voltage, and a refusal to treat their legacy as inviolate. That same year, HBOMax finally greenlights the long-stalled streaming series. It premieres in 2025 to massive acclaim, hailed for its unflinching portrayal of the rock business. Less a nostalgic tribute, more a war chronicle, think *Band of Brothers* or *John Adams*, only with sex, betrayal, electric guitars, and blood on the snare drum. This coincides with the band's delayed induction into the Cleveland Rock Hall of Fame.  In 2032, the chaos takes an unexpected turn toward grace: Avril marries Cremeans in what becomes a heart-tugging, dramatic triumph, her third marriage, his first since widowhood. The band's collective pulse stabilizes. They take up residency in the newly refurbished Sphere in Las Vegas, falling in love with the rhythm of fans coming to them and the comfort of sleeping in their own beds before midnight. Jeff, restless and full of unfinished business, declares he has things to do before he fades away, like getting the world to remember their story properly.

# **2. The Industry as Battleground**  

Labels, managers, radio stations, awards shows are the arenas where power is brokered and souls are bartered. The music industry is portrayed with both archival realism and narrative exaggeration, contracts are cursed scrolls; studio execs are smiling vampires. Fame is never just given, it's sold.  Few dare pay with parts of their souls.

---

# **3. Cultural Context and Parallels**  

Every era within *Commuted* is written with deliberate echoes of the real world:
#### The ‘90s media obsession with “girl power” weaponized against Kathy’s creative control.
#### The Great Recession overlays the band’s private collapse and public reinvention.
#### The early 2010s reflect the rise of prestige streaming content and the emotional toll of commodified biography.
#### The late 2010s cultural longing for authenticity mirrors Gilley’s surprise reinvention as a serious artist.
#### The 2020 pandemic isolates the band permanently, ending a 30-year journey not with a bang, but with a stunned silence.
#### The 2020s nostalgia cycle commodifies their past and distorts their image for corporate gain.  Someone else remembers it for them, wholesale.

# **4. Fictional but Plausible World-Building**

**Venues, cities, record labels, magazines, podcasts, and producers** are invented but intricately connected to real-world equivalents. Every fictional location has a texture, a history, a smell.

**Commuted’s discography** has complete liner notes, tours, and feuds — woven into the broader fictional music landscape.

**Famous cameos and crossovers** are plausible but always tangential. A bitter SNL performance with Chris Gaines. A Rolling Stone cover. A thrown drink at a VH1 awards show. [Kurt Loder]() dishing industry news. [Ric Ocasek ]()producing *69 Reasons*.
