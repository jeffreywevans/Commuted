# **INT. BACK-OF-HOUSE – HOCKEY ARENA TURNED TEMPLE – AFTERNOON LOAD-IN**

He looks like a longshoreman who fell in love with lightning. Big shoulders under a black fire-retardant jacket, clipboard tattooed with scorch marks, hearing protection around his neck like a set of prayer beads. Boots clean (that matters), hair short (also matters), eyes always counting—sightlines, distances, ceiling truss, sprinkler heads, the distance from flesh to flame and back again. When he shakes my hand it’s not performative; he’s calibrating pressure, reading the metacarpals the way other men read tea leaves.

“Call me Mike,” he says. “Everyone does. The rest is for the paperwork.”

We stand beside a gray, dented road case stenciled in block letters: PYRO—NO TILT. The case smells like ocean air and pennies. The arena hums with forklifts and union jokes and the soft dread of a show that will either baptize or immolate the faithful. Upstage, carp is ratchet-strapping a hedge of strobes. Downstage, [[4.4 JIM BROWN character bible|Jim Brown]] ghosts past, counting something with his whole body. Later tonight someone will scream, someone will cry, and 18,000 strangers will believe they’ve survived something together. For now: tape marks. Velcro. OSHA placards. Mike’s kingdom.

“Origin story,” I say, flipping open my notebook. “Let’s go.”

He grins like a man with a secret exit.

## **THE GOSPEL ACCORDING TO PYRO MIKE**

He was born in the exhaust plume of other people’s weekends—county fair fireworks, drag strip pops, a father who worked three jobs and a mother who ran a cash-only stand that sold funnel cakes and illicit sparklers. “We were the unlicensed concession to licensed spectacle,” he says, wry. “I grew up under a sky that lied to me—made it look easy.” He learned early that _nothing_ about fire is easy. Fire is a union man with a grudge. Fire is undefeated.

At fourteen: volunteer with a municipal display crew, carrying sand buckets like communion. At sixteen: the school’s theater kid who refused to let Hamlet die under fluorescents, so he “borrowed” a fogger and made it snow on cue with a box fan and potato flakes. At eighteen: NFPA code in a binder and burn tests on Saturday mornings because a girl in chem class said, “You’re not careful,” and it lodged in his skull like shrapnel. At nineteen: a summer shadowing a licensed pyrotechnician who ran shows on river barges and required every assistant to recite distances before breakfast. “How far is a hundred feet?” the man would bark. “Eighty paces if you’re honest. Ninety if you’re a liar.”

By twenty-two he had the alphabet medals: OSHA trainings, site-safety certs, the NFPA scripture dog-eared and snot-green with highlighter, the permits for indoor flame effects and the sober relationship with the Bureau that separates artists from arsonists. “You don’t _own_ the show,” he tells me. “You rent permission. You return it unburnt.” He can talk romance about flame, sure, but the tone goes surgical when the letters come out: the codes, the filings, the distance tables that keep strangers intact.

“People think I’m a maniac,” he says. “I am the opposite of a maniac. I’m a librarian who can count to zero.”

## **HOW HE FOUND COMMUTED (OR HOW FIRE FOUND THEM)**

The first time he met them they were still a band that believed belief could outrun physics. Ohio kids with outlaw hearts, the whole lot of them. [[4.6 JEREMY EVANS character bible|Jeremy Evans]] wanted the “floor to open” under a downbeat; [[4.1 BILL DAVENPORT character bible|Bill]] wanted “flame like punctuation, not exposition”; [[4.5 KATHY ESPICH character bible|Kathy]] wanted _risk_ because risk tasted like agency; [[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff]] wanted control, because control was the only way he could stand to feel anything at all; Jim—God love him—wanted a riot you could write a setlist for.

Mike was working a festival—one of those humid Midwestern weekends where guitars warp and tempers short—and Commuted’s LD blew a universe mid-changeover. They needed “a moment” and were about to do something incredibly stupid with a stack of flash pots and a prayer. Mike intercepted with a look that said, you want to be legends or lawsuits?

“I gave them an ending,” he says, shrugging. _Blackout. Count of four. Blood-red wash. One chaser. One concussion. One three-second waterfall._ Clean, timed, survivable. Kathy stepped forward into that light like she was the weapon the flame had been waiting for. The crowd _stopped breathing_ and then howled. The band turned to look for the guy who did it. Mike was already packing his case. The manager—[[5.2. JEFF CREMEANS MODERN REVIEW|Cremeans]]—caught him in the tunnel and asked three questions in one breath: “Can you tour? Can you read a budget? Are you _perfect_ with OSHA?” Mike said yes, yes, and _yes_ and that was the altar call.

Lisbon? That was his first stadium with them, the one where the silhouette of Kathy in field-fire made a nation behave like a church. “Do you know what a hundred thousand people sound like when they think they’ve seen God?” he asks, deadpan. “They sound like compliance paperwork if you’re me.” He means: the louder it gets, the more he hears the quiet math underneath. (By the time the band is screaming at Giants Stadium years later, Jim is tossing off, “I already talked to Pyro Mike,” like ordering napalm via takeout—because once Mike is on a thing, the thing burns on _purpose_.)

## **WHO HE ANSWERS TO (AND WHY HE SLEEPS AT ALL)**

“Look,” he says, “anybody with a credit card can buy flash and call it art. I work because Cremeans will feed me to wolves if I miss a dot on an i.” He taps the clipboard. “Everything ladders. The band dreams it. I translate it. Cremeans owns it. The venue blesses it. A fire marshal either eats a hotdog in peace or sends me home. If a sprinkler head so much as _thinks_ about me, I’m wrong.”

The romantic version of his job is _bang on the downbeat_. The adult version is _permission acquisition_. International? Double it—temporary import permits, carnets, translators who can say “flash duration” in languages you only know from menus, local pyro chiefs who assume you’re a cowboy until you out-bureaucrat them with a smile. “There’s a reason I dress like an accountant during advances,” he says. “It disarms the cynics and terrifies the amateurs.”

He keeps two bibles: the one with the distances and the one with the names—hotel engineers who can tell you which ballroom suppresses with water and which with halon; stage managers who will die for a clock; forklift operators who will move a case just because you said please like a human being.

## **THE WORK (THE REAL WORK)**

He shows me the show the way a surgeon shows you a scar. Not the secrets—those stay locked, because craft without consequence becomes theft—but the shape.

Morning walk: count heads, walk exits, greet the house crew by name. E-stop check, twice. Talk to lighting like allies, not rivals. Talk to audio like diplomats, because nothing ruins a show like the low end hating the flame. “Lasers and pyro argue,” he says, amused. “It’s just physics wearing two different colognes.”

Afternoon: build cues to the body language of the drummer, because drummers run hot and _hot_ changes micro-timing. He watches Jim breathe. “I don’t chase metronomes,” he says. “I chase lungs.” He builds a show that tolerates chaos at the edge without ever letting chaos through the door. The band thinks it’s improvising; Mike is running an air-traffic pattern with flare and mercy.

Evening: the ruin and the redemption. He stands with the dead-man switch like a priest holding a detonator to your confession. One cue is a love letter, the next is a warning shot, the next is a funeral pyre on a timer that stops exactly where skin begins. He does not blink when the downbeat hits; he’s counting frames in his head like a watchmaker.

And when it lands? When the crowd’s scream falls down the bowl like weather and the band looks taller than they are and nobody is bleeding? He writes it down. Notes it. Fixes it. Packs it. Tomorrow they do it better.

---

## **WHY HE DOES IT**

“Because it’s the only honest metaphor left,” he says, scratching a note on his clipboard. “Music pretends to be forever. Fire does not pretend. You see it, you feel it, you _remember_ it. You can’t stream it in the morning. It leaves you changed or it leaves you alone, and if I’ve done my job—safely, precisely, boringly on paper first—then for four seconds everyone in that room is the same animal again.”

He doesn’t drink on show days. He doesn’t date crew. He doesn’t bring friends backstage. “This is church,” he says. “Church with insurance.”

He laughs when I ask about fear. “I’m not afraid of fire,” he says. “I’m afraid of slop. Slop gets people hurt. Slop is the devil.” Then he shows me the little talisman in his jacket: a quarter from the first gig where he ran a button and the band got a bonus in coins. He rubs it without looking, like a superstition he refuses to name.

---

## **THE FIGHTS (LASERS, LIGHT, AND THE HUMAN FACTOR)**

Yes, he argues with laser techs. Yes, he’ll take a punchline over a compromise. He believes light is narrative and heat is punctuation; lasers believe light _is_ punctuation and heat is a bouncer who talks too loud. “We’re both right,” he admits, “but only one of us sets the sprinklers off by accident.” He shows me a cue list with polite little skulls drawn where sensors live. He is not above theater. He is not above cartoons.

Bill Davenport once pitched opening with “Sirius” and Mike made a fist like the Bulls had just checked in. Of course he did. He understands _sports myth_ and he understands _American hunger_. He’ll burn for it if you give him the run. He’ll also put the whole thing in a tamper-sealed box if the house says _no._ He is both outlaw and clerk, which is the only sustainable form of outlawry.

---

## **HOW EARLY HE CAME ABOARD (AND WHY THEY NEVER LET HIM GO)**

He got there before the money should have afforded him, because Cremeans is a futurist with a calculator and the band’s appetite outpaced their insurance. When your singer wants to spit fire and your drummer insists that a set should start like “a hostage situation with rhythm,” you hire the man who can make a riot confess to the fire marshal. From early club runs to national TV to stadiums, he’s the calm heat in their chaos. And when Jim barks “I already talked to Pyro Mike,” that’s a shibboleth: the grown-ups are in the room; the danger will be gorgeous _and_ it will be survived.

---

## **EPILOGUE: THE INTERVIEW INSIDE THE INTERVIEW**

“Last question,” I say. “When you’re gone someday, what should your obituary say?”

He slings the hearing protection back around his ears, looks past me through the tunnel toward the empty seats that will soon turn into a single animal.

“That he kept his distances,” he says. “That he made loud things precise. That he lit saints without making martyrs.”

He taps the case. ***“And that he never once set off the sprinklers by accident.”***

Then he’s moving, making the rounds, the accountant of combustion, humming something that sounds suspiciously like a lullaby. Later tonight, when the room goes blood-red and the air warms and 18,000 people think about calling their mothers after, I’ll look stage left and see him: still, wired, counting the beat of other people’s hearts and giving it back as heat.

God damn, but Pyro Mike is good at blowing shit up—_professionally_.
