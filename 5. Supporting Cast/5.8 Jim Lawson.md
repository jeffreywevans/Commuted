**Jim Lawson (b. 1956, Nottingham, UK)**

Lawson came up the hard way, hauling cables and stacking trusses in the Midlands club circuit in the late ’70s. He wasn’t a musician, though he always carried a battered notebook where he jotted down lighting cues and cash totals with the precision of a bookie. His first “big break” was working logistics for a Judas Priest tour—when half the crew OD’d and Jim was the only sober one who knew which road case had the pyro triggers. From there he became one of those **grizzled lifers**, the men who make stadiums breathe and who treat time as the only sacred resource.

By 2004, Cremeans had him on retainer. Lawson was **a decade and a half older than Commuted**, which gave him authority they begrudgingly respected. He could wrangle stadium unions like a warlord, balancing awe at their efficiency with contempt for their obstinacy. “You ask ‘em to move a chair that isn’t in their guild contract, and twenty blokes light up Marlboros like it’s bloody tea break,” he once growled to Cremeans in Wembley’s bowels. But he also knew the value of those crews: load-in complete in record time, rigging immaculate, no crushed hands or dropped amps.

**His quirks:**

- Wore a wristwatch and actually used it—on a band where “time” was a loose metaphor.
- Could quote the electrical load capacity of every major football stadium in England.
- Despised English catering: “If I see one more tray of beans on toast, I’ll torch this stadium myself.”  
- Loved football itself, though—he’d wax poetic about Anfield or Old Trafford acoustics with the same reverence Jeff reserved for bass tones.


Lawson’s **Cremeans dynamic** was pure friction: Cremeans ran tight, military-style operations, and here was a tour manager who *already thought like a general*. Cremeans would snap an order, Lawson would nod once, and 300 stagehands would move like a single body. The band rarely noticed him—just that when Lawson was around, their chaos somehow landed on stage at the exact second the lights hit.

He wasn’t warm. He wasn’t part of the inner circle. But Lawson was a **linchpin**—a guy who could turn a half-drunk band and a million-dollar stage into a working miracle night after night. He chain-smoked, cursed the food, praised the stadium acoustics, and made sure Commuted looked like gods when they walked out into 70,000 screaming voices.
