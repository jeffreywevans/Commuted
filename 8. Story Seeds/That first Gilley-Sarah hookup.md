# **THE FIRST HIT IS ALWAYS FREE**  

**Act I, Scene I – Indianapolis – April 1991**  
*An origin myth in slow motion.*

---

## **INT. THE MELT – INDIANAPOLIS – NIGHT**

It’s not a club. It’s a basement wearing eyeliner.

Ceiling too low for confidence. Walls so close you feel like you’re being judged by brick. The bar’s a plank nailed to cinderblock. The floor’s wet but not from rain. This is where Midwestern music goes to either *ignite* or *implode*. And tonight, the air is heavy with the smell of one last chance.

Sarah Malcolm stands back against the far wall, notebook open, red pen dancing even before the music starts. She’s twenty-three, freshly shipped out of NYU with a minor in ethnomusicology and a major in surviving assholes. Hair pinned with surgical precision. Blazer sharp enough to kill a trend. She’s holding a half-full plastic cup of room-temperature merlot and glaring at the stage like she already knows she’s going to hate this.

She does not hate it.

Not yet.

---

## **ANGLE: STAGE**

Commuted is already two songs in. The first was a false start—too fast, too loose. But the second catches. Kathy’s voice wraps around the room like a switchblade dipped in honey. Jeff and Jeremy lock into something tribal and breathless. Bill’s synth pulses like a hangover in Morse code. And then—

**Gilley.**

No warning. No subtlety. Just *him*, lunging forward like a guy trying to win a bet with God. Hair everywhere. Sweat on his collarbone. Guitar slung low, like he’s trying to fuck the floor. He hits his solo and the crowd stops breathing. He doesn’t play like he’s practiced. He plays like he’s *dared*.

Sarah lowers her pen.

He hits a harmonic that shouldn’t be legal and then slides down the fretboard with a feral grin that suggests either genius or severe emotional damage.

Nick Stewart, posted up against the other wall, smirks into his beer. He watches Sarah without watching her, like a man taking inventory of future regret.

**SARAH (V.O.):**

He’s not good.  He’s *unreasonable.*  Which is worse.  Which is better.

The song ends. The room bursts. Not cheers—*relief.* Like the air’s been punched full of static and the crowd’s finally allowed to exhale. Sarah scribbles:

> “Guitarist = chaos, no filter. Wildly undisciplined. Impossible to ignore.”  
> “Band is hungry. All edge, no polish.”  
> “Potential: radioactive.”

She glances at Nick. He nods, just once, like he knows she’s not wrong.

The next song starts. Gilley doesn’t play the same solo twice. He looks like he’s chasing something only he can hear. His mouth curls when he plays, a little half-smile like he’s listening to a private joke being whispered by God.

## **INT. BACK WALL – MIDWAY THROUGH THE SET**

Sarah leans over to Nick, raises her voice above the noise:

**SARAH**  
“He’s either brilliant…or stoned.”

**NICK**  
“Not mutually exclusive.”

They both laugh, but she’s already writing again.

She sips her wine. It's warm now. She doesn’t care.  There’s a moment—just a flicker—where Gilley glances out into the dark. Right toward her. Just for a second.  He smiles. Like he *knows* who she is.  And then he turns back to his amp and kicks it hard enough to shake dust out of the rafters.

## **INT. GREEN ROOM – POST-SET – LATER**

The band filters in laughing, breathless, high on their own feedback. Gilley grabs a beer, peels off his shirt, wipes his face with it, and throws it into the corner like it insulted him.  Sarah walks in without knocking.  Gilley sees her.  Raises one eyebrow.  And smirks.

## **INT. GREEN ROOM – POST-SET – THE MELT, INDIANAPOLIS – NIGHT**

It smells like achievement and armpits. One flickering fluorescent bulb above the mirror, casting enough shadow to flatter the bruises. The floor is a sacrificial laminate offering to the gods of spilled beer and bad decisions. Gilley has commandeered the only clean towel and is using it to dry his hair with one hand while fishing in the cooler with the other.

Sarah doesn’t knock. She’s already decided not to play the game like a groupie or a girl. She walks in like she owns the building, or might repossess it.

Gilley doesn’t flinch. He just grins around the lip of his bottle like he’s already memorized her measurements and opinions.

**GILLEY**  
So. What’s my verdict?

**SARAH**  
You play like you’re trying to start a fire with your teeth.

**GILLEY**  
Better than playing like I know what I’m doing.

**SARAH**  
Debatable. You know you blew three transitions?

**GILLEY**  
Only three? Shit, must be my birthday.

**SARAH (eyeing his beer)**  
You’re drinking cheap domestic like you earned it.

**GILLEY (tilting it toward her)**  
I *always* earn it. Even when I don’t.

She leans against the cracked makeup counter, notebook still in hand but closed now. She doesn’t offer her name. He doesn’t ask. It’s like they’re skipping courtship entirely and skipping to post-mortem.

**SARAH**  
Your band’s got something. Not a clue what it is yet. But it’s loud, and people feel it.

**GILLEY**  
That’s all sound is, right? Math you can fuck to.

**SARAH**  
You think in slogans?

**GILLEY**  
I *live* in slogans. Slogans and handjobs. Everything else is taxes.

He sits on the beat-up couch and gestures vaguely to the spot beside him.

She doesn’t move.

**SARAH**  
Your lead singer has star quality. Your drummer’s invisible. Your bassist might be smarter than all of you combined.

**GILLEY**  
She is.

**SARAH**  
And you’re the chaos engine. You drag the whole show off a cliff and somehow make them thank you for the ride.

**GILLEY**  
That a compliment?

**SARAH**  
That’s an *observation*.

Beat.

**GILLEY (smiling now, lazy, crooked)**  
So what does the clipboard queen want from the loudest boy in Indiana?

**SARAH**  
Nothing. That’s the dangerous part.

She walks to the cooler, grabs a bottle, cracks it without asking. Sips.

**GILLEY**  
Let me guess. NYU. Two internships. Thinks music is numbers and fashion is math.

**SARAH (deadpan)**  
And let me guess. Local dropout. Body by Marlboro. Can’t keep time but can make girls cry in two verses or less.

He raises his beer.

**GILLEY**  
Guilty.

She clicks her bottle against his.

**SARAH**  
I’m here because someone said you were interesting. I’m still deciding if they were wrong.

**GILLEY**  
You’re here because you like watching people come unglued. You just usually don’t get wet watching it.

Beat. Her eyes narrow.

**SARAH (smiling, like a guillotine blade)**  
You ever consider how many men have said that line to me before?

**GILLEY**  
Sure. But none of them *meant* it.

He leans back, arms spread like he’s offering her both his pulse and his worst idea. She doesn’t flinch. She leans against the counter again and stares at him like she’s tracing the outline of a problem she hasn’t decided to solve.

**SOUND:** The rest of the band outside the door, laughing, voices rising with the hiss of another beer can cracked.

Inside, it’s quiet. And hot. The kind of hot that makes smart people do reckless things.

**SARAH (softly)**  
Don’t make me regret this.

**GILLEY**  
I won’t.

**SARAH**  
That’s what worries me.

She turns on her heel and leaves without another word. Door half-open, trailing the scent of notebook paper and expensive shampoo.

Gilley watches the door, grinning like a thief who just found out the vault was unlocked.

## **EXT. BACK ALLEY – THE MELT – NIGHT**

The door swings shut behind her with a slap, like it knows she’s leaving too soon. The alley is slick from rain or someone’s spilled guts. It smells like beer, piss, and ozone. Cigarette butts cluster near the drain like shriveled punctuation marks.

Sarah leans against the brick wall, exhales hard through her nose like she’s trying to blow the scent of him out of her bloodstream.

She’s not drunk. She never drinks enough to lose control. That’s for other girls—girls who think charisma is a currency. Girls who’d line up backstage for a wink and a pull.

But her knees are *soft*.

Not weak. Not trembling. Just softened. Like dough set too long in the sun.

She *hates* that.

### **SARAH (V.O.):**
He’s a fucking mess.  He plays like he doesn’t care.  He talks like he’s allergic to silence.  He *smiles* like it’s a sin, and he wants witnesses.

She watches a drunk couple stumble past the mouth of the alley. The girl’s laughing too hard. The guy’s pretending not to notice her tits. It’s probably real love. Or something close.

Sarah digs her thumbnail into the edge of her notebook and opens to the last page.  The page she didn’t mean to write on.

> GILLEY:  
> Solo at 9:42 – unnecessary, arrogant, electric  
> Shirt = security blanket / striptease combo  
> Audience response: irrational. Hormonal. Cult-like.  
> *I felt it too.*

She stares at that last line. Blinks once. Then underlines it.  Not for emphasis.  For evidence.

### **INT. HER HEAD – TIMESTAMPED MEMORIES – RAPID-FIRE:**

- Gilley’s fingers tracing the rim of his beer bottle.
- Gilley tilting his head when she insulted him, like a dog hearing a song only he understands.
- Gilley, shirtless, arms spread, looking like *fuck you made flesh*.
 
She closes the notebook. Snaps the elastic band so hard it slaps.  Dials her voicemail.

**VOICEMAIL:**  
*“You have one new message.”*

It’s her mother, from six hours ago.

> **MOM (cheerful, oblivious):**  
> “Honey, just checking in. Hope the gig goes well. Don’t let them treat you like a secretary. Remember what you’re worth. Love you.”

She deletes it mid-way. Not because it’s wrong — but because it’s *too right*. She’s not supposed to be flustered. She’s *Sarah fucking Malcolm*. She graduated cum laude. She led three campaigns. She once made a rock legend cry in an exit interview.

And now she’s in an alley in Indianapolis, clutching her own wrist like it might bolt from her.

---

### **SARAH (V.O.):**

He’s not my type.  
He’s not a threat.  
He’s not even *interested*.

So why the hell do I feel like I just inhaled him through my skin?

She looks up at the sky — no stars, just haze. A city sky. Always blank. Just like her face needs to be tomorrow.  She tucks the notebook into her bag, pulls out a lip balm, reapplies with mechanical precision.  Not for him.  Not for anyone.  Just to get her mouth back under control.

### **INT. HOTEL BAR – INDIANAPOLIS – LATER THAT NIGHT**

The bar is attached to the kind of budget hotel where everything smells faintly of lemon cleaner and compromise. Fake wood paneling. Tables just sticky enough to remind you not to stay long. But the bar itself is quiet, dim, and crucially: open late.

Nick Stewart is already there, seated at a two-top with a scotch and a pen. He doesn’t look like a scout. He looks like a man who could either close a million-dollar deal or fix your carburetor. The sleeves of his oxford shirt are rolled up. The notepad in front of him is already half-full.

He glances up as Sarah walks in.

**NICK**  
Well, well. The girl who made me get on a plane.

Sarah cocks an eyebrow. She doesn’t sit yet. She wants to see if he’s bluffing.

**SARAH**  
You must be Nick Stewart.

**NICK (smiling)**  
I am. And you’re the analyst with the red pen and the scary math who wrote that memo in June.

**SARAH (casually)**  
Which memo?

**NICK**  
Don’t be coy. You know the one. Island’s LA office forwarded it up like it was a goddamn prophecy. “Emergent regional act with nonstandard pacing structures, disruptive crowd syncopation, and multi-axis charisma signatures.” That was your phrase, right?

Sarah finally sits. Orders a glass of red without looking at the menu. Takes out her laptop like a priest removing scripture.

**SARAH**  
I stand by it.

**NICK**  
I’ve been in this business twenty-five years. I don’t know what “multi-axis charisma signatures” are, but I saw them at Dyche. Forty-eight minutes, no break, and the whole crowd was moving like they'd been rehearsing it for weeks. I watched a sophomore in a Goo Goo Dolls shirt cry during “*Payment in Kind*.”

He raises his glass.

**NICK**  
That’s your math?

**SARAH (smiling now)**  
Part of it. The rest is timing patterns. Regional attendance spikes. Turnover frequency. Velocity of merch resales. First-order emotional retention.

**NICK (grinning)**  
You’re gonna have to slow down. I’m an analog guy in a digital century.

**SARAH**  
You don’t have to understand the formula. You just need to know when the math is right.

She opens the folder. Spins it to face him. A graph blooms across the green paper — jagged, ascending, impossible to ignore.

> **LABEL: “COMMUTED – EMOTIONAL IMPACT CURVE VS. MARKET FRICTION”**  
> Y-axis: “Intensity of Post-Show Sentiment”  
> X-axis: “Region, Venue Size, and Tempo Divergence”

**SARAH**  
This is why they work. The emotion hits just after the second chorus, not the bridge. It’s counterintuitive. They break you early, then rebuild you during the hook.

Nick squints at the data like it’s a love letter in a language he doesn’t speak.

**NICK**  
Jesus.

**SARAH (gently)**  
You don't have to memorize it. Just trust it.

He chuckles, folds his arms, leans back.

**NICK**  
I trust what I see. And what I saw at Dyche was a band that doesn’t know how to behave like one yet — but they’ve already got a following that acts like they’re famous.

**SARAH**  
That’s what I flagged. The discrepancy. Fan devotion without mass exposure. That’s not normal. That’s… dangerous.

**NICK (nodding)**  
Yeah. It is.

They sit in silence for a moment. Her wine arrives. She doesn’t touch it yet.

**NICK**  
Can I ask something?

**SARAH**  
Shoot.

**NICK**  
How’d you find them? Before Chicago. Before the college stations started spinning “*Not Taken Kindly*.” Before *anyone* noticed.

She pauses. Closes the laptop. Takes a sip of wine.

**SARAH**  
I read seventeen local zines a week. Built search queries on crowd retention phrases. Isolated clubs with more than one band injury report per quarter. Then I found a photo. Gilley was bleeding from the face but still playing. The crowd was smiling. *That* wasn’t right. So I flew out.

**NICK (impressed)**  
Jesus. That’s…ten levels beyond diligence.

**SARAH**  
It’s not diligence. It’s instinct, filtered through math.

**NICK**  
We’re not that different, you know.

**SARAH (smirking)**  
Except I’ve never had to expense a bourbon before breakfast.

**NICK (raising his glass again)**  
Give it time.

They clink glasses. A quiet truce. Old school and new blood. Vinyl and waveform. Between them, an impossible band rising like smoke from a broken amp.

## **INT. THE ORION ROOM – COLUMBUS, OH – NIGHT**

Polish. That’s the first thing Sarah notices.

Soft lights, golden and clean. Ceiling-high velvet curtains, real ones, not the fire-hazard burlap that passed for stage dressing in Indianapolis. Tables with unchipped candles. Waitstaff with combed hair. A venue with a budget. A stage like a lacquered altar.

This is where grown-up bands play. Jazz residencies. Chamber pop acts. Major-label showcase nights with champagne wristbands and preloaded swag bags.

Commuted looks like they *broke in*.

---

## **ANGLE: STAGE**

They’re tight. That much is clear.

Jeff’s tempo is laser-stable. Jeremy’s fills don’t just land — they *unfold*. Kathy’s voice doesn’t so much float as *cut*. She’s learned how to work the mic since last week, holding it away from her mouth on the belt notes, then sliding back in like she’s whispering threats to the front row.

And Gilley — Gilley is behaving.

He’s pulling back. Letting the others have room. No guitar gymnastics, no fucked-up feedback dives. His hair is tied back with a thrifted satin ribbon that reads *WISH YOU WERE HERE* in faded pink embroidery.

It’s probably stolen.

Sarah scribbles furiously.

> “Tightest they’ve ever sounded.
> Kathy: 8.5/10.
> Set pacing: correct.
> Gilley: minimal chaos — elegant, even.
> BUT…”

She flips the page.

> “Fashion: thrift-store vomiting contest.
> Hair: haunted prom date.
> Presence: invisible.
> Connection: zero.
> Brand potential: buried under ego and laundry.”

She glances at Nick.

He’s not taking notes. He’s *watching*.

Not the band. Not the crowd.

*Her.*

He leans in, just enough to murmur.

**NICK**
You’re not wrong.

**SARAH (without looking up)**
But?

**NICK**
You’re missing the forest.

**SARAH (arch, still writing)**
I’m counting the trees. You said that’s how you win.

**NICK**
Sometimes. Other times, you just get lost.

Gilley’s solo on *No Candy for Cannibals* is *measured*. No theatrics. Just tone. Taste. A climb that resolves like it’s been holding its breath for three days.

The room goes still.

And *then* he looks up.

### **ANGLE: GILLEY’S POV – CROWD**

He scans the room like a wolf checking the tree line.  Kathy’s in full command, the spotlight flaring across her cheekbone as she sings into the bridge.  But Gilley isn’t looking at her.  He’s looking *dead at Sarah*.

### **ANGLE: SARAH – FREEZE FRAME MOMENT**

Her pen stops.  Just a flicker of eye contact.  His mouth moves around a word she can’t hear.  Maybe it’s *you.*  Maybe it’s *now.*  Maybe it’s *again.*

And then he looks away, back to the frets, as if nothing ever happened.

**SARAH (V.O.):**
You’re not allowed to flirt with me using tonal phrasing and eye contact.  You’re not allowed to *control a room* and then pretend you didn’t.  You’re not allowed to *know* what I know and still make me question it.

## **BACK TO: NICK – OBSERVING**

He doesn’t miss it.  He sees her pause. The breath that didn’t quite finish leaving her body. The little crack in her posture.  He takes one sip of scotch, savoring it like a punchline.

**NICK (V.O.):**
Well, shit.  Here we go.

## **STAGE – FINAL SONG BEGINS**

They close with *Come Inside Me*, and this time the breakdown hits *exactly* where it should. Gilley lets the amp ring out just long enough for Sarah’s spine to catch the vibration.

After the applause, Sarah claps. Twice. Controlled. Meaningful.  Then she closes the notebook.  
She’s seen enough.  But she doesn’t *understand* enough. Not yet.  Not yet.

## **INT. THE ORION ROOM – MERCH TABLE – POST-SHOW**

The crowd is still buzzing, pockets of conversation popping like kernels left in a microwave too long. The scent of sweat, citrus hairspray, and anticipation mingles as bodies drift toward the exit—but many cluster here, by the rickety folding table stacked with tapes, pins, and a velvet-lined cash box that probably contains more IOUs than bills.

Sarah lingers at the edge of the scene, notebook closed but mind still running numbers. She’s not here to buy a shirt. She’s here to *watch*.

---

**Gilley is surrounded.**

Girls in cheap lipstick and guys in bootcut jeans hang on his every word. He signs a bootleg cassette case with a flourish. Someone hands him a beer, and he accepts it with the easy grace of someone who always has something in both hands: a drink and someone else’s attention.

He talks like a campfire. Warm. Dangerous if you get too close and confess your sins.

He touches arms. Hugs strangers. Makes jokes like he’s known them forever. Somehow, he means it every time.

---

**Jeremy is all sincerity.**

He’s got a tiny flashlight looped to his belt and he’s helping someone count out change for a sticker. He thanks people by name—*even when he’s clearly guessing.* He adjusts the angle of a T-shirt display with the care of someone who once worked retail and never got over it.

---

**Jim’s cracking jokes.**

Not loud ones. Dry ones. The kind you have to lean in to catch. But he draws laughter like a magician pulls scarves. He’s got that dad-at-a-barbecue energy—approachable, just dangerous enough to make the beer taste better.

---

**Then there’s Kathy.**

Back turned. Sitting on the amp case. Hoodie up, hair damp, shoulders hunched like a warning sign. Someone tries to talk to her—an earnest boy in a Cranberries shirt—and she mumbles something that sends him walking backwards, confused and a little hurt.

---

### **SARAH (V.O.):**
This is the storefront. The band is the product.  And the frontwoman is hiding.

Kathy looks like she dressed in the dark while bleeding. Her lipstick is smudged like she forgot she was wearing it. The ends of her hair are stringy, wet with sweat and resignation.

Sarah’s pencil flies:

> “Kathy = frontwoman in name only.  
> On-stage vocal delivery: A-.  
> Off-stage charisma: D.  
> Style: Dorm room laundry basket.  
> Affect: Chew toy with a hangover.”

And then, scrawled in the margin, too furious to be filtered:

> “This is your *brand*?”

Sarah glares at the scene as Gilley throws an arm around a coed and gets her number with his eyes closed. Jim sells a tape by promising it cures hangovers. Jeremy gives away a pin to a kid who says he drove two hours.

**Kathy checks her watch.**

**SARAH (V.O.):**
You’re closing with a song called *Come Inside Me*.  You make people feel like their skin could catch fire if they dance the right way.  You conjure orgasm and euphoria and basement rebellion in one chorus.  And *this* is your face in the crowd?

Sarah clenches her jaw. Reaches for her bag. Starts walking toward the table, not for an autograph or a handshake—but to remind herself that *none of them* know yet.  She’s the only one here who sees the disconnect.  And it’s eating her alive.

## **INT. THE ORION ROOM – MERCH TABLE – NIGHT CONTINUES**

Sarah doesn’t mean to walk toward the table. Her body just moves. It’s the aftershock of *Come Inside Me* — a song so brazen, so luridly precise in its intent that it short-circuited the part of her brain responsible for careerist distance. It was a dare, that track. A challenge issued in half-whispers and full-throated crescendo.

The last note is still thrumming in her hips. Not desire. *Recognition.*

**They pulled it off.** She hates them a little for it.

She stops ten feet from the folding table. Watches.

**Gilley’s working the crowd like he’s running for prom king and dealer of the year.** His shirt’s only half-buttoned now, sweat dried in the creases of his collarbone. A girl with a nose ring gives him a sticker to sign and he asks for her address. He’s not joking.

**Jeremy’s helping a stoner count quarters.** Jim’s leaning over the table, performing unlicensed psychological evaluations and getting laughs that sound too loud to be polite. It’s working.

**Kathy’s gone grey.**

She’s there—physically. But she’s retreating inward, shrinking beneath a hoodie that should’ve been incinerated for crimes against elastic. She sits with her knees pulled up on the amp case like a sulking kid at the DMV. When a fan offers her a cassette insert to sign, she looks at it like it’s a subpoena and scrawls a single letter before turning away.

Sarah’s lip curls.

> “Charisma: reverse osmosis.  
> Style: a beige apology.  
> Appeal: Kraft Mac & Cheese — comforting if you’re six, but try serving it at a wedding.”

She flips the notebook closed before it combusts in her hand.  And then — **Gilley spots her.**

Mid-laugh. Mid-beer. Mid-sentence. His gaze locks onto hers with the clean violence of a power chord. He doesn’t pause. Doesn’t gesture. He just **beams**, like she’s the punchline he’s been working toward all night.

He walks over.

**GILLEY**  
You again. Still counting sins?

**SARAH**  
You just added three.

**GILLEY**  
Only three? Shit. I must be slippin’.

That last song. That’s the one you close with?

**GILLEY**  
Always.

**SARAH**  
It’s obscene.

It worked.

**GILLEY**  
You felt it.

**SARAH**  
You *meant* it.

**GILLEY**  
It’s not a gimmick if you believe it. We believe it. That’s the difference.

That, and your restraint.

**SARAH**  
Your frontwoman’s allergic to eye contact.

She doesn’t like being looked at. She only likes being heard.

**SARAH**  
Then she’s in the wrong fucking business.

**GILLEY**  
No. She’s the reason we’re in it.

He says it without theater. Without poetry. Just fact. Like gravity.  That answer stops Sarah cold — not because she agrees, but because he *does*. Completely. Stupidly. Irrevocably.  That kind of conviction can’t be taught.

Only *followed*.

**SARAH (after a long pause)**  
You know you’re going to have to dress better if you want to be famous, right?

**GILLEY**  
I’m not trying to be famous. I’m trying to be *mythic*.

**SARAH**  
Mythic people have stylists.

**GILLEY**  
Then hire me one.

She lets out a laugh that betrays nothing. Just tension expelled, clean and discreet.

**SARAH**  
You’re not as dumb as you act.

**GILLEY**  
And you’re not as detached as you pretend.

She walks away.  He watches her go.

## **INT. HOTEL BAR – COLUMBUS – LATER THAT NIGHT**

There’s barely anyone here. A TV flickers above the bar showing a college football replay. The bartender’s reading a *Time* magazine with the cover folded over. The decor is unambitious: wood veneer, fake brass, an earnest but ineffective attempt to feel more like “lounge” than “last call.”

Sarah’s already seated, wine in front of her, notebook nowhere in sight.

Gilley walks in like he was invited—because of course he was. No one said the words, but the air between them was heavy with *next*. He slides into the seat beside her without waiting for a nod.

**GILLEY**  
So… about that song.

Good. That wasn’t the goal.

He taps his glass, thinking.

**GILLEY**  
U2 turned themselves into a religion by accident. Bono's a cult leader who never filed the paperwork. But *Appetite for Destruction*? That wasn’t a band, that was a *home invasion*. Slash didn’t care if you liked it. He just needed to make sure you didn’t forget.

Sarah’s trying not to be charmed.  It’s not working.  He’s rambling, yes, but with purpose. He’s not a savant, not a poser — he’s a *theorist*. A sinner who quotes scripture.

**SARAH**  
And where do you fall? Between priest and burglar?

**GILLEY (grinning)**  
I’m Mephistopheles with a tape deck.

**SARAH**  
Marlowe or Goethe?

**GILLEY (without hesitation)**  
Marlowe. Always. More blood. Less moralizing.

She stares at him, a touch stunned.  Then leans back, lets out a breath like surrender.

**SARAH**  
You talk like this all the time?

**GILLEY**  
Only when I want to be remembered.

**SARAH**  
You’re not going to let anyone forget you, are you?

**GILLEY (softer now)**  
Isn’t that the point?

Silence. The kind that tastes like sex and danger, even if it’s never named. She looks at him—truly looks—and for the first time since the club in Indianapolis, she understands why the *Come Inside Me* lyric didn’t offend her.  It *fit* him.  Because the sex in that song wasn’t about permission. It was about **witnessing**.

**SARAH**  
You’re not famous.

**GILLEY**  
Not yet.

**SARAH**  
But you think you will be?

**GILLEY**  
If I’m lucky? No.

She blinks.

**GILLEY (CONT'D)**  
Fame’s a lie. People thinking they know you. *Myth*—that’s what lasts. That’s the *work*.

He leans in, eyes locked.

**GILLEY (soft, deliberate)**  
You’re here because you saw the myth starting. And you wanted to see if it was real.

And there it is.  The power shift.  The lights buzz overhead. The bar’s still half-empty. The world hasn’t changed. But Sarah Malcolm’s been seen in a way she didn’t ask for.  And—worse—she *likes it*.
