**WHY THIS ALBUM MATTERS:**

- ***Artistic Consequence:***  
    *Be First, Be Smarter, Or Cheat* is Commuted’s bloodletting—an album that refuses to mourn with grace. It’s not a eulogy. It’s a prison riot.  
    This is the sound of “grief as voltage,” to steal from Sarah Malcolm’s infamous breakdown of their earlier work: “This album doesn’t play to an audience. It accuses one.”  
    With Kathy gone, every lyric is a dare; every arrangement a knife fight between what’s left of love and what’s left of anger.
- ***Band Dynamics:***  
    The band is in pieces.

- **Jeff**: Bass lines are manic, melodic, and mean—a direct channel from pain into technique. His voice on backing vocals is more ragged, sometimes just shouting.
- **Jim**: Drums are surgical—no flourishes, just cold, brute force, as if he’s trying to keep the rest of the band from bleeding out.
- **Bill**: Layering synths and guitars with obsessive control, trying to fill every space Kathy’s absence left open.
- **Jeremy**: Guitar work swings between math-rock intricacy and outright sabotage, occasionally derailing a track to keep it honest.
- **Avril**: On loan, half-in and half-out, she delivers lead vocals on four tracks, pushing herself to match Kathy’s voltage. Sometimes she succeeds; sometimes she’s trying too hard—and that tension is the album’s best feature.

- ***Lyrical Content:***  
    The album is obsessed with legacy, betrayal, and the idea that authenticity must be paid for in blood. The title isn’t metaphor. Every song is about the scramble to matter—sexually, artistically, politically. The most savage lines are about Kathy, but rarely *to* her; more often, they’re about what she took with her and what she forced the band to confront in themselves.
- ***Cultural Impact:***  
    The album was divisive. Fans either canonized it as their best post-Kathy work or derided it as an emotional cash-in. Critics called it “dangerous, unsustainable, perfect,” and “the sound of a car crash scored for bass and howling.”  
    Internally, it shattered what was left of the original group dynamic—Bill and Jim nearly came to blows in the studio. Avril’s presence haunted the band for years, setting up the drama of her eventual, permanent arrival.

**THE QUOTE THAT MATTERS:**

“Who survives because of this album?” — *S. Malcolm, label strategy memo*

This is the question *Be First, Be Smarter, Or Cheat* answers—*not* “Who’s next?” but “Who’s left?”

---

This album isn’t just important—it’s the crucible. It’s the record that made clear: Commuted could outlive anyone except itself.  
It’s not a rebirth. It’s the last time they burned alive.

**WHAT’S WORKING (and why):**

- **Every character has a wound, a talent, and a moral horizon.** Gilley is guilt and hunger wrapped around a bottle and a punchline; Bill is craft and quiet dread; Jeff is unmovable, not unbreakable—“grief cooled into concrete.” Jim’s steadiness is not piety, but dogged contract fulfillment. Even Sarah Malcolm and Cremeans—outsiders, suits, lovers, truthers—are just as bruised, horny, exhausted as the artists.
- **No one is let off the hook.** Every single scene implicates someone: sexually, emotionally, legally, spiritually. No room for heroes or martyrs. You captured “power struggle without polish,” *sex without euphemism,* *failure without apology.*
- **Sex and death are the same breath.** This is crucial for Commuted. You show how every character negotiates loss through appetite—fucking, eating, arguing, making music, making toast at dawn. It’s all coping, all sacred, all desperate.
- **You didn’t let the plot settle for melodrama.** Every scene does at least two things—moves the narrative and builds (or tears down) the myth. Even the small ones—Irish butter, dawn sausages, birria tacos—*each is a fight for intimacy or distance*.

**WHERE YOU CAN PUSH EVEN HARDER:**

- **Give us *Kathy* as a haunting, not just a loss.**  
    She’s the sunken center of this album. I want to see moments where her *absence* warps the physical space—her perfume still lingering on an old coat in the studio, a lipstick mark on a forgotten whiskey glass, a voicemail no one will delete.  
    *Example:*

- **Jeff** picks up a pair of headphones, only to find a strand of Kathy’s hair twisted in the cable. He freezes, does not breathe, and then wraps it around his pinky and tucks it in his pocket. Never speaks of it.
- **Gilley** calls her old number just to hear the “number disconnected” message, then leaves a voicemail anyway—never mentioning it to anyone.

- **Let sex get dangerous again.**  
    The encounters are perfect—Jen and Cremeans afterglow, Gilley and the screenwriter’s banter—but you know Commuted’s real sex scenes are always one breath away from violence, breakdown, or revelation. Don’t let the reader feel safe; make them want, ache, fear, and judge.
- **Don’t forget the sound.**  
    When you describe a track, do it with the authority of a music critic *and* a lover. What does the bass do to your ribs? What does the synth sound like in a panic attack? Does Bill’s guitar on Track 5 sound like a warning or a seduction?  
    *Scene idea:*

- Let someone listen to a final mix alone, weeping.
- Or, two characters have sex to a rough mix—one hears only Kathy, the other only Jeff.
- Sound is the ghost. Sound is the betrayal. Sound is the reason they’re not dead.

**WHAT TO DO NEXT:**

- *Flashback Interludes*: Drop in flashbacks as *audio hallucinations*—a rehearsal where Kathy rips into Gilley for missing a cue, a drunken afterparty where she dares Bill to play synth topless, a hotel room in Boston the morning after rehab.
- *Public Reaction*: Give us a slice of the internet the morning “Chemical Saints” drops. The best/worst tweet. A Reddit thread titled “Did Jeff Evans kill Kathy?” The private DM from a fan who *knows* the truth, or thinks they do.
- *Final confrontation*: End with Jeff in the studio alone, listening to the mastered album. He plays the last note, then sits in silence, hearing only Kathy’s voice in his memory, the one nobody else will ever hear again.

**The Sound of “Terrified” (For Commuted, 2013)**

---

**You want music journalism? No. You want a memory log from inside the sound booth, every hair on your body raised, every window in the Over-the-Rhine studio trembling, Kathy’s ghost at your back, the stench of her old perfume pooling in the shower tile grout.**

This is what “Terrified” is:

- **Intro:**  
    *Field recording of the actual St. Eligius lobby at midnight—buzzing ballast, a janitor mopping, distant elevator bell. This is Bill’s idea; he wants to “haunt” the stems. Layered with a vintage Mellotron run through a tube amp, barely in tune, warbling like your stomach after four days awake.*
- **Drums:**  
    *Jim comes in with a kit tuned down and gated so hard you feel it in your teeth, not your ears. He’s not playing a beat; he’s simulating heart palpitations, forty seconds of arrhythmic terror before any vocal enters. He demands you hear your own mortality.*
- **Bass:**  
    *Jeff, channeling the entire Book of Job in twelve notes per bar, slides the neck in microtonal increments—no fixed root, just dread and a kind of sacred yearning. Every fill a question, never an answer.*
- **Guitar:**  
    *Jeremy Evans, barely present in the room (physically), but his parts slice like cauterized nerve endings: delays stacked into infinity, pitch-shifted so the attack sounds like shattered glass, decay like a siren in the snow. He only plays the chorus. On the bridge, he throws his pick across the room and mutters, “That’s enough.”*
- **Synths & Collage:**  
    *Bill’s the architect: layers in a found-sound collage—samples of church bells, reversed ER intercoms, processed to sound like digital wolves. Hidden in the left channel, he embeds Kathy’s laugh from a 2010 outtake. It’s not a sample, it’s a haunting. Only people who know her will recognize it, and it never appears in the stem notes.*
- **Vocals:**  
    *Two male voices—recorded in separate rooms, at separate times, never hearing each other. Awe (Jeff) is a high, cracked falsetto, run through a spring reverb with all the high end rolled off. Terror (guest vocalist, John Legend if he shows, or some local gospel king if not) is dry, chest, close-miked, almost spoken, with distortion added only in the final mix. Their lines never quite overlap—each one chasing the other, like Jacob and the Angel in the dark.*
- **The Bridge:**  
    *A field recording from inside St. Eligius chapel, with real hospital monitors beeping. The priest’s voice is an actual voicemail from Kathy’s old confessor, digitally mangled, repeating, “You are forgiven, you are forgiven.” This was Gilley’s idea, and Bill almost cut it, but Jeff insisted. In the mix, it sits just under the sub-bass, so you only catch it if you’re listening on real speakers.*
- **Outro:**  
    *The track fades not to silence, but to the sound of hospital HVAC and distant weeping, run through a stereo tremolo until it resolves into pure sine wave: a musical flatline. Then, with a final cruel joke, Bill adds one synth note—A natural—because, as he says in the log, “There’s no closure. Only resolution.”*

---

**How “Terrified” Feels in the Room:**

- **Bill, after the first playthrough:**  
    *“It’s like being skinned alive by grace. You think you want the truth, but your bones are shaking by the time you get it.”*
- **Jeff, at the console, eyes red but unblinking:**  
    *“I wanted the call and response to sound like a priest and a sinner taking turns at confession, but neither one is sure which is which. The only thing I know is you’re supposed to leave the tape rolling until somebody collapses.”*
- **Jim, packing up his sticks:**  
    *“We did it right if you’re not sure who’s still alive by the end.”*
- **Jeremy Evans, standing at the window, voice low:**  
    *“I played the chorus like I was holding her hand. Everything else is noise.”*

**Physical Truth:**

No one uses the shower anymore—not out of superstition, but because after the first mix session, someone (nobody admits who) left a post-it on the door:  
**“Sacred Ground. Do Not Enter. —K”**  
It’s still there.  
Her towel, still hung over the rod. Her lipstick, fused to the edge of the sink. No one’s cleaned up. No one will.

Kathy is gone, but she’s also the reason no one moves forward. She is the sacred, the absence, the presence, the ache in the studio, the joke nobody dares tell out loud.

---

**That’s what “Terrified” is.**

One track out of nine.  
Every other track is just as haunted, just as sacramental, just as savage.  
You don’t just listen—you survive it.

---

You see it exactly—**the whole band is in existential limbo**, not just from Kathy’s death, but from the gnawing, cellular guilt that *they* abandoned Jeff at his hour of need, and that, worst of all, Jeff is surviving without them. *Maybe even thriving.* That is the tension at the marrow of this entire album cycle, and you’re capturing it with surgical clarity.

The agony is precisely that:

- **They bailed, and they know it.**
- **Jeff might have outgrown needing any of them.**
- **If he did?**  
    It means he was always the center, and their contributions were just orbit.
- **If he didn’t?**  
    Then he’s either dead or dying, and they are complicit.

And no one—**not Gilley, not Bill, not Jim, not Sarah, not even Kendall with her holy pen and trembling thighs—wants to speak it out loud.** So instead they obsess over production notes, fight over vocals, sabotage their own affairs, and turn Kathy’s old workspace into a shrine no one can enter.

**That’s the truth nobody wants to confess.**  
And it’s also why the music is so dangerous, so unsparing, so *unlistenable* to anyone who isn’t willing to bleed with it.

**How to Push This Even Further:**

- **Let the silences expand.**  
    In scenes, use the space between what’s said and what’s avoided. People come within a hair of apology or confession, then veer off—change the subject, start a fight, fuck the wrong person, or, worst, crack a joke.
- **Let them self-sabotage.**  
    Someone (maybe Bill) tries to reach out to Jeff—gets as far as “Hey, I’ve been thinking about you” and then veers into a passive-aggressive dig about a bassline. Jeff sees through it. Responds with calm competence. The conversation dies.
- **Let Jeff haunt them.**  
    Not with rage, not even with absence, but with competence. With peace. With *the fact he doesn’t demand anything anymore*.
- **Let the music be the battleground.**  
    Track revisions become code for emotional reconciliation, or for pushing someone out. Session files left open on the desktop; “accidentally” deleted stems; notes written in the margins of lyrics that are clearly for one person only.
- **Kathy’s presence as absolution and accusation.**  
    She’s the reason they come together, and the reason they’ll never forgive themselves for falling apart.

**How To Push It Farther (and Earn the Shock):**

**1. Jim’s Mission: Kill Bill (with Work)**

- **Jim orchestrates back-to-back 16-hour sessions.** He schedules Bill so tightly there’s no time to sleep, eat, or even text his partner. Bill’s playing suffers, but Jim only cares about the *perfection* of the sound, not the people.
- **Scene:** Bill, hands shaking, tries to confide in Jim, only for Jim to hand him another rewrite or a plugin to install. Jim never breaks—he only doubles down, because that’s his only way to stave off grief: relentless competence, zero empathy.
- **Endgame:** Bill starts to *hate* Jim, but cannot walk away because, in the end, he fears what happens if he stops moving.
- **Physical symbol:** Bill starts bleeding from his cuticles on the keys, leaves blood on the synth, wipes it with his sleeve, and keeps going.

**2. Avril, the Nuclear Bomb of Joy**

- **Avril’s voice is so bright it’s offensive.** She does not *mourn* Kathy; she brings cupcakes to the first session, jokes about “dying your hair pink for Jesus,” and records her vocals in roller skates, chewing gum.
- **The band is aghast—Jeff is nearly undone, but can’t look away.**
- **Scene:** Avril takes one look at the lyric sheet—*all blood, death, confession*—and chirps, “Let’s try it double-time! Maybe with handclaps?”
- **Gilley:** “She’s an alien.”
- **Bill, under his breath:** “She’s the antichrist.”
- **Jim, to himself:** “She might be the only person left in this room not haunted.”
- **Avril, in the booth:** “Let’s make this the happiest funeral ever!”

**3. The “Candy-Coated Guillotine” Track**

- **The song is called “Thirty-six Degrees Fahrenheit.”**
- **It starts with an infectious, major-key hook and Avril’s sugar-rush vocals. By the first chorus, Jeff’s harmony slips in—a minor key countermelody, trembling with despair. The production is so slick it’s *nauseating*—and yet the lyrics, if you read them, are about holding the hand of someone dead, deep in the morgue of St. Eligius.**
- **Session Note:** Avril ad-libs a bridge where she invites everyone to “pour one out for Kathy”—and nobody knows if she’s kidding. It’s so wrong it’s *perfect.*

**4. The Aftermath**

- **The band is fractured.** Bill is wrecked, Jim is unrepentant, Gilley is in awe/disgust, Jeff is silent—but the song works. It’s horrifying, beautiful, anthemic, and will haunt every listener who isn’t already dead inside.
- **Avril, after tracking, leaves to go get tattoos with her bassist. The band sits in silence. No one knows what they’ve just made, but they know it’s nuclear.**
- **Jim, looking at Bill:** “Next session starts at eight. Don’t be late.”
- **Bill doesn’t answer. He just stares at his hands, bandaged and bloody, and whispers, “If you need a keyboardist, hire Avril’s drummer. I’m out.”**

---

**You’re writing the story of the last holy act in rock and roll: bringing pop into the chapel and letting it break everything you built.**  
Avril is the end and the beginning—a survivor, a joy terrorist, the only one in the room who isn’t ashamed to live.  
*And Commuted will never be the same.*

---

Oh, **I know it’s Avril Lavigne.** That’s what makes it so *fucking* delicious. Not a knockoff, not a no-name—**the** Avril. The *actual* bouncy, bratty, anti-bro, pink-tornado Avril Lavigne, 2000s Canadian princess of mall-punk, here to crash the funeral and redecorate it with bubblegum and razorblades.

**She’s a symbol and a weapon.**  
She is every anxiety the band has about “selling out,” about hope, about survival, about what it means to *actually* live past the pain—made flesh and fishnets.

**For Commuted? She’s the Antichrist, the Holy Fool, the disruptor.**  
For the narrative, she’s a reckoning:

- *How dare she be this alive?*
- *How dare she not know or care who Kathy was, and still show up and sing her face off?*
- *How dare she be immune to despair, even if she’s only faking it?*

But here’s the secret (and Kendall would whisper it into the mic, her thighs sticky under the desk, breathless with forbidden joy):  
**Avril is haunted, too.**  
She just refuses to let the ghosts run the setlist. She’s every bit as broken, but she weaponizes her sunshine and punk irreverence. She’s the only one with the balls to walk into a Commuted session and ask, “What if we didn’t make this about death?”

She’s the *mirror* the band never asked for—and the only one not afraid to leave when the mirror cracks.
