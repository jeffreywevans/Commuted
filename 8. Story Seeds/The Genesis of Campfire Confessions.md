**INT. JEFF’S BASEMENT STUDIO – DAYTON, OHIO – MID-AFTERNOON – LATE SPRING**

Humidity: high enough that jeans feel like wet tape. The room: a cathedral of thrift-store amps, a thrift-store couch, thrifted dreams. The desk lamp flickers in rhythm with the bass on the rough demo. A bowl of leftover popcorn, half a Diet Coke, Gilley’s vape leaking on a lyric sheet. Jeff’s vintage headphones hang from a hook like a skinned rabbit.

Gilley is pacing—nervous energy, sinew and pop-song desperation. He’s been after Jeff all week for “something *everyone* will sing, something even my drunkest cousin can yell at a bonfire.” It’s the thousandth time he’s invoked Springsteen and “Born to Run.” He means *glory*, but what he’s actually conjuring is *painful constipation.*

**GILLEY** (strained, literal)
Jeff. *Seriously.* We need a “whoa-oh, hands-in-the-air, call your ex and tell her you’re sorry” moment. *Everybody* singing, or else what’s the point?

Jeff—already irritated, glasses smudged, black T-shirt, flannel open, stoic—doesn’t even look up.

**JEFF** (stone dead)
Are you constipated, or just emotionally constipated?

Gilley blinks, then—because his guts *are* in open revolt—admits it.

**GILLEY**
Dude. I haven’t shit in, like, three days. I feel like a petrified log.

Jeff—lips twitch, eyes still down.

**JEFF**
Go take a dump. If you’re lucky, you’ll be lighter, and I’ll have a lyric for you by the time you return.

**GILLEY** (incredulous, then—shrugs, exits)
You want a ballad or an anthem?

**JEFF** (barely audible)
I want you out of my sight so I can think.

*The door closes. The house is suddenly quiet except for the whine of the amp and the distant, operatic grunt of plumbing from the guest bathroom.*

This is what collaboration looks like after thirty years: Gilley in the john, Jeff in the throes of secret genius, each pretending the other is less necessary than oxygen.

I settle in the doorway, notebook open, listening.

**INT. JEFF’S BASEMENT STUDIO – TWELVE MINUTES LATER**

Gilley returns, visibly lighter, hands washed, cocky again. There’s color in his face, which is rare for a man who’s been living off protein bars and regret.

**GILLEY** (grinning)
Dude, I feel like a new man. You got anything for me?

Jeff doesn’t turn. He plays a riff. *Big*, *wide*, chord progression—the kind that makes you want to grab the person you love, or the person you wish you loved, and sing at the night sky.

On the table: two pages, written in Jeff’s tight, furious hand.

The top reads: **“Fireside Confession”**

Jeff slides the lyric sheet across the table like a peace offering and a challenge.

**JEFF**
Try not to fuck it up.

Gilley reads the first stanza, eyes going wide. It’s simple, direct, catchy—everything he wanted, and everything he could never write. There’s a whiff of smoke, a hint of sex, a chorus ready to be shouted by strangers who’ve never met but know every word.

He starts humming the melody. It’s infectious. I’m humming it, too, against my will. Gilley grabs the acoustic, tries the verse. He can’t help but riff on the “Kara’s hand in my lap, babe”—making it both sweeter and dirtier, because Gilley cannot not flirt with the edge.

**GILLEY** (half-singing, grinning)
Campfire confessions, girl—can I get a witness?

He gestures for me to join. I shake my head, chronicler not performer, but the vibe is thick. Even Jeff, forever unmoved, cracks a smile. The first chorus soars. “Campfire confessions, underneath the stars tonight…” You can hear the crowd already, see the fists and lighters, smell the beer, the grass, the sticky smoke of a thousand midwestern summers.

By the second verse, Gilley’s already rewriting, ad-libbing lines, but it doesn’t matter—Jeff’s given him the bones, and the thing *lives*. When he gets to the “Viv leans in, with a teasing grin…” Gilley milks it for everything, eyes closing, going full preacher. He can sell *anything* if he believes, and today, he believes.

**GILLEY**
“Gonna keep rubbing her all night, my friend?”—
(he makes it a joke, a challenge, a call-and-response)

Jeff’s not even mad. He grabs the bass, adds a driving line, lets Gilley soar above it. I scribble notes: “crowd will lose it,” “perfect for late-set singalong,” “someone will definitely flash their tits during the third chorus, probably not for the first time.”

This is what it’s like, every goddamn time:

*Gilley gets desperate.  Jeff gets annoyed.  Someone cracks a shit joke.  A song gets written.*

There’s no glamour, just the brutal physics of being in a band this long. You get the work done in between ego collisions and digestive complaints. The only thing sacred is the song, and the only thing certain is the mess you leave behind.

And so, “Fireside Confession” is born not out of inspiration but out of constipation and mutual disrespect, which is to say—like most things worth singing.

Gilley, by the end, is walking on air, already texting Kathy for her take, already plotting harmonies and breakdowns, already trying to fuck with Jeff by insisting on an “unplugged” bridge. Jeff, pretending he’s not listening, is writing chord substitutions on a napkin. I’m already writing the post-mortem in my head.

Somewhere, the crowd is already singing.

**Author’s Note (me, Kendall):**
You asked how it went for me? This is the dream. Watching two men who’d rather die than give each other credit accidentally birth a stadium anthem while one of them is literally trying to shit out his anxiety. Rock and roll is not about myth. It’s about pressure, release, and what you do with your hands when the music finally gives you permission to sing at the top of your lungs.

Here’s your “banging sing-a-long,” Gilley. Go take another dump—there’s a bridge that needs fixing.

Let’s be honest: **“Fireside Confession” was engineered—literally and emotionally—for mass communion**. It’s a song built to work not just in the echo chamber of an arena, but across the wide, lawless expanse of outdoor festivals, county fairs, and fields packed with half a city’s worth of drunks. The architecture of it—major chords, unison chorus, those sticky, anthemic background vocals—*demands* to be sung back at the band, and it almost never failed.

**Arenas:**
In a closed space, “Fireside Confession” was a religious experience. The reverb-heavy vocals, the layered harmonies—those bled into the concrete and steel, made the rafters shake. When Gilley would lead that call-and-response (“Oh, we’re feeling good, yeah, we’re feeling right…”), you’d get ten thousand voices, hammered by beer and nostalgia, howling it back. In those moments, the band could stop playing entirely and the crowd would finish the song—word for word, sometimes even louder than the PA.

**The trick:** Jim would drop the beat, Jeff would pull back the bass, and for just a moment, it’d be *only* the crowd, naked, bellowing confessions to the rafters. It worked every time, and the band leaned into it—lights up, hands out, pure communion. It was built for arenas.

**Outdoor Festivals:**
But—here’s the real power: “Fireside Confession” didn’t just work in the vacuum of a stadium. It *thrived* in the chaos of open air.

- The **song is written for unpredictability**: The tempo could push or pull depending on the mood, the set time, the weather, the chemistry. Sometimes Gilley would extend the campfire metaphor, telling whole crowds to put their arms around strangers—like a weird cult leader, like a summer-camp counselor possessed by Mick Jagger.

- **Sound carries differently outdoors**—and you’d think that might dull the power. But the song’s structure is so modular and blunt, with those big hooks and that confessional chorus, it slices right through wind, fog, and spilled cider. You could be a thousand feet from the stage, knee-deep in mud, and still find yourself shouting “campfire confessions, underneath the stars tonight”—and meaning it.

- **The breakdowns and heavy guitar riffs**—they gave the crowd something physical to do, a reason to jump, a way to burn off the drugs and the tension. There are at least three videos (Germany, Roskilde, Lollapalooza) where the camera pans back and you see *waves* of people, bodies moving in time, hands up—no barriers, no boundaries, just the song owning everyone for five minutes straight.

**Small Clubs?**
It lost a little of its magic there. Too big, too communal, not dirty or desperate enough for the walls to sweat. The best nights, though, were when they played it as a “joke”—slowed down, acoustic, breaking the song into fragments. That’s when it felt confessional again. Otherwise, it was a stadium/field/arena beast.

**In summary:**
- **Arenas:** Absolutely. Designed for it. Crowd took over.
- **Festivals:** Even better, sometimes. Became a secular ritual.
- **Clubs:** Hit or miss, but sometimes—when they let it fall apart—became even more intimate and raw.
