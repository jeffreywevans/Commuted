## **EXT. RESIDENTIAL DADE COUNTY DIVE BAR – EVENING – SEPTEMBER 1992**

*Outside: the night is thick with swamp heat and the promise of slow violence. Suburban Dade is a sprawl of tan stucco, wilting palms, and plastic pink flamingos faded by hurricane sun. Inside: the air conditioner’s fighting a losing battle against the weather. Beer is cold, air is wet, everybody’s skin glows with sweat.*

**INT. DIVE BAR – MID-EVENING**

The bar’s a horseshoe of Formica chipped down to the plywood. Ceiling fans spin above, moving nothing but the illusion of air. A neon Bud Light sign flickers in one corner like a distress signal. Latin pop leaking out of the kitchen, mixing with the sticky-fingered drone of televised sports.

**JEFF EVANS** (bass: emotionally inaccessible, built like a barn with his soul locked inside) is hunched over a sweating Yuengling. Wire frames. Hair wild with humidity. His shirt’s plastered to his back, and he’s already marked the coasters with small, geometric patterns—something to do with nervousness, or maybe just math.

**BILL DAVENPORT** (keyboards: stocky, tanned, runs on citrus and vengeance) slumps beside him, looking for a breeze and finding only resignation. Bill’s got that look: the one he gets before he lectures an engineer, or a bartender, or Jeff himself.

**[[4.1 BILL DAVENPORT character bible|BILL]]**
You ever notice how this city just… stops? Like they ran out of blueprint when they hit the swamp.

**[[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|JEFF]]** (grunts, not looking up)
It’s not a city. It’s a quarantine. Every neighborhood is a different strain of the same disease.

**BILL** (smiles, teeth sharp, voice a soft threat)
You get poetic when you sweat. You want another?

Jeff lifts his glass, swirls the melted ice. He shakes his head.

**JEFF**
Gonna hydrate. Kathy’ll kill me if I show up hung over. Again.

Bill signals the bartender anyway, two more Yuenglings. Bartender’s a guy who looks like he’s lost a bet with God. Nobody says thank you.

**BILL**
Joe Robbie’s worse than this place.

**JEFF**
Joe Robbie’s a fucking spaceship. You seen the parking lot? It’s like being dropped off on the moon and then told to find your way back using only broken English and cab fare.

**BILL** (laughs, pulls a cocktail napkin closer)
I timed it. Seventeen minutes from our hotel. Five more to get out of the parking lot if we don’t get stabbed by a tailgate dad. There’s nowhere to go after load-out but here. Or the ocean.

**JEFF** (gazes into the foam, voice low)
That’s the point. They don’t want the city mixing with the band. Stadiums are built like prisons. All exits face away from life.

Beat. A couple at the bar are arguing in Spanish about infidelity or soccer—maybe both.

**BILL**
You know what I hate about Florida?

**JEFF**
Everything?

**BILL**
You can drown in your own sweat just standing still. And every street is named after a dead real estate agent.

Jeff smiles—barely. A twitch at the corner of his mouth. He reaches for a peanut from the bowl, considers it, drops it back.

**JEFF**
Miami’s like a fever. You hallucinate, you sweat, you lose three pounds and a band member every time you play here.

**BILL**
Last time was worse. You remember Gilley tried to sneak out after load-out? Wound up in a backyard pool party with a priest, a stripper, and the mayor’s nephew.

**JEFF**
He didn’t even take his guitar. Left it on the field. Who does that?

**BILL**
Somebody who knows he’s not carrying the set.

Jeff snorts. It’s the closest he’ll get to affection tonight.

**JEFF**
You ever feel like we’re the only ones who care how this sounds?

**BILL**
Every night. That’s why we’re the ones drinking in bars with no names, talking about the soundcheck like it’s a confessional.

Beat. The heat presses in, the condensation running down the glass like a threat.

**JEFF**
I’d rather play three nights at Churchill’s than headline Joe Robbie again. At least at Churchill’s, if the crowd hates you, they’ll throw something you can dodge.

**BILL** (smiles, sharp, almost fond)
You can’t dodge sound. Or heat. Or bad decisions.

They drink. The TV flashes a shot of the stadium. Jeff looks away, suddenly younger. Bill just watches him—calculating, always.

**BILL**
You want out?

**JEFF**
You know the rule. We go until we can’t. Then we play one more.

The couple at the bar leaves. Their argument trails out into the night like music.

**BILL**
You wanna play the setlist straight or you wanna blow it up?

Jeff’s eyes sharpen, all calculation, no mercy.

**JEFF**
Let’s blow it up.

And the neon flickers again, and the sweat runs, and the city stays outside, baking and silent, while two men in a nameless bar decide—for tonight, at least—to let the stadium burn.

## **EXT. PARKING LOT OUTSIDE JOE ROBBIE STADIUM – NIGHT – LATE SEPTEMBER 1992**

Miami’s heat doesn’t break after sunset. The stadium is a slab of floodlit concrete, floating in a swamp of its own exhaust fumes. Tailgates echo with the noise of a thousand rented cars and a single truck-mounted subwoofer blasting “Under the Bridge.” The air tastes like diesel and disappointment.

**INT. DIVE BAR – CONTINUOUS**

Bill wipes the sweat from his brow, studies Jeff over the rim of his glass. The *Chili Peppers* are printed on half the tickets out there, but Commuted’s name is on the flyer—smaller, a footnote, but present. Stadium gigs are still as foreign as palm trees.

**JEFF**
You realize we’re opening for a band that sells socks and calls it a philosophy?

**BILL**
Only difference is, their socks have more fans than our first pressing.

He laughs, but it’s not funny, not really. Stadium math is brutal: twenty-one tracks, one record. No one here for you. They’re here for the headliners, for the bouncers, for the bragging rights.

**JEFF** (recalculating, always)
We got fifty-seven minutes. Not a second more. I asked. They’ll cut the power if we try an encore.

**BILL** (dry)
Perfect. They can hear us die in real time.

He draws a setlist on a napkin—black ink bleeding in the humidity. Eleven songs circled. The rest—ballads, weird experiments, the deep cuts—fall away like dead skin.

**JEFF**
We don’t have time for weird. We barely have time to be ourselves.

**BILL**
So what? We walk out, play “Sixty Nine Reasons,” then run?

**JEFF**
We play every song like it’s a threat. No banter. No solos. You fuck up a cue, you keep moving. You finish a verse, you’re already starting the next one.

Bill nods, drawing a line under the last track.

**BILL**
We ever play stadiums again, you think?

Jeff doesn’t answer. Stadiums are a lie—the sound disappears into space, into the humid void. You play for ghosts and for drunks waiting for a band that isn’t you.

**JEFF**
They’ll forget us by the third bar of “Give It Away.”

**BILL**
So let’s give them something to remember. We play for the five kids in row fourteen who know our lyrics, not for the rest.

He lifts his glass—another toast in a long line of toasts to minor victories, to survival, to irrelevance.

**BILL** (cont’d)
To new ground.

Jeff clinks his glass—ceremonial, bone-tired, no smile.

**JEFF**
And to burning it down before the Chili Peppers get the fire.

The bar’s silent for a second, except the sound of the ice melting, and outside, the echo of distant, stadium-sized music that isn’t theirs—yet.

And for one hell-lit moment in a Miami bar, the impossible is just stupid enough to try.
