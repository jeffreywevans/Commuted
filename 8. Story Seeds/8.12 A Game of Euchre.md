# **A GAME OF EUCHERE**

---

# **CHAPTER ONE**

---

## **INT. BOSTON CLERK OF COURT’S OFFICE – NOVEMBER 12, 2012 – 11:21 A.M.**

Ten days after death. Autumn's dead sun slices through dusty glass, landing on a box stamped:

**“ESPICH, KATHERINE M. – EFFECTS, ST. ELIGUS HOSPITAL”**

A box like so many after a life ends.  Nobody wants it.  The world has moved on, except for the few still circling her shadow.

The hospital has tagged everything:
- one battered leather jacket, still smelling like cigarette smoke and hotel shampoo
- three spiral-bound notebooks, lyrics and scribbles and grocery lists and poems
- a guitar pick in the side pocket
- one pair of muddy boots
- and, tucked in a paperback copy of *Jane Eyre* with the spine half-broken—an envelope marked, only, “In case of.”

They release the effects to the the band's legal team: the paralegal Lisa-Ava Kelly, who’s never understood a damn thing about [[4.5 KATHY ESPICH character bible|Kathy]] but is too dutiful to say no.  Lisa-Ava is efficient, uncomfortable. She inventories, she signs the form, she puts the box in her trunk. She is about to drive away and donate it all when she pauses. *Something about the envelope…*

She pulls over in the parking lot behind Kroger. Opens it. Inside:  **A final letter.  Not to family.  Not to any bandmate.  But to Jim.**

The handwriting is sharp, decisive, aching.

> [!note] **HANDWRITTEN NOTE**
> **KATHY’S LAST LETTER (excerpt):**
> 
> Jim,
> 
> If you’re reading this, I’m already gone. You always said you’d outlive me. I never believed you, but here we are. I don’t have wisdom for you. I have regrets, and you know them all.  You kept me alive longer than anyone else. Not with love, not with hope, not even with music—With chaos. With the promise that something wild and bright might still be waiting.  I wanted to say I forgive you, but that’s not honest. I wanted to say I loved you, but that’s not enough, is it? 
> 
> Here’s the truth: I always thought we were on the same edge. But you never fell. If you find this, do me one favor: Don’t let them mythologize me. ***Tell the truth. Even if it’s ugly.***
> 
> And if there’s anything after this—I hope it’s noisy.
> 
> *KE*

Lisa-Ava doesn’t know what to do.  She brings the letter, days later, to the band’s manager—hands trembling. She doesn’t want to be involved. She just wants the story to end.

---

### **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – EVENING**

[[4.4 JIM BROWN character bible|Jim]] gets the letter, sealed in a plain envelope.  No ceremony. No condolences.

He reads it alone, knees against his chest, the world suddenly too quiet.  And for the first time since the crash cart failed and the chapel emptied, Jim weeps—not for the legend, But for the girl he never could save.

He will not mythologize her.  He will tell the truth.  And [[5.5 Kendall Weatherby|Kendall]]—me—will write it all, exactly as it happened.

### **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – OVER-THE-RHINE, CINCINNATI – NOVEMBER 2012 – NIGHT**

The studio is dim, golden light bleeding across stacked cables and empty beer bottles.  Jim sits on the floor, knees to chest, back pressed against the battered Rhodes.  The envelope is in his lap—*her handwriting, alive even now*.

[[4.1 BILL DAVENPORT character bible|Bill]] is at the mixing desk, headphones on, pretending not to notice.  Somewhere, a phone buzzes—a text from [[4.2 JEREMY GILLEY character bible|Gilley]], something obscene and idiotic, a distraction from the hole in the world.

Cremeans and Jeremy have gone home hours ago, too numb for words.

**Jim waits to be alone.**  He holds the letter for minutes, maybe hours, staring at the name on the front—“***Jim***.”  

It looks like an accusation.

He rips the seal with his thumbnail, hands shaking harder than he’ll ever admit.

---

He reads.  He reads again.  The words hit like a car crash, no airbags.  The letter is everything he feared and everything he needs: raw, unfinished, not an apology, not a love note—just Kathy, ferocious and unredeemed, telling him to *tell the truth, even if it’s ugly*.

He tries to breathe.  He can’t.  He doesn’t scream.  He *collapses*—quiet, folded in, the way a man does when there’s no one left to impress.

He presses the paper to his chest, then to his forehead, as if it could resurrect her, as if prayer were still possible.  He wants to call someone, anyone—Bill, or [[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff]], or even Gilley.  But all he can do is sob: gut-deep, silent, shuddering.  Not for the legend.  For the friend, the adversary, the holy disaster.

Bill glances back—removes his headphones, sees Jim shaking, and, for a second, doesn’t know what to do.  He just stands there, in the hush, and lets Jim break.

After a long, impossible minute, the studio door flies open—Gilley, wild-eyed, still dressed for the night, half-drunk, smelling like perfume and smoke and every bar south of 8th Street.

He takes in the scene: Jim on the floor, Bill frozen, the letter in Jim’s fist.

**GILLEY** (voice unsteady, bravado just for show)
What the fuck happened?

Bill just gestures: It’s Kathy.

Gilley drops to his knees next to Jim, wraps an arm around his friend, and for once, there are no jokes, no dirty stories, no sex or swagger—just the two of them, breathing together in the ruin.

**Jim can’t speak. He doesn’t need to.  He just lets himself lean into Gilley’s chest,
and Gilley holds him, tight, trembling, eyes wet but refusing to blink.**

There is nothing mythic here.  Just grief.

Gilley is alive, holding Jim up, and Bill is witness, and the night is endless, and the truth is uglier and more sacred than any song.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – NIGHT – MID-NOVEMBER 2012**

It’s late. Rain hits the Cincinnati windows.  Jim’s at the kit, eyes red, letter folded tight and pinned above the snare.  Bill hunches at the mixing board, chain-smoking Camels, jaw locked.  Jeff sits by the amp stack, silent, tapping a pencil until the wood snaps.  Jeremy is absent—couldn’t face it tonight.

Gilley storms in—late, drunk, hair wild, eyes bright with a grief he’ll never admit.
The door slams, making everyone jump.

**GILLEY**
You gonna keep crying or are we gonna do something useful?

Bill’s eyes flash—he’s had enough.  He stands, voice quiet but edged like a blade.

**BILL**
Fuck you, Gilley.  You weren’t even here when it happened.  You were on some bar’s floor.

Gilley shrugs—trying for cool, failing.

**GILLEY**
You want a medal for watching her die?  Nobody saved her. Not me, not you, not him.  Don’t pretend you’re cleaner.

Jim stands, fists clenched.  His voice—wrecked, barely above a whisper:

**JIM**
You don’t get to say her name like that.  You don’t get to make it about you.

Gilley advances—dangerous, desperate, needing a fight to feel something other than loss.

**GILLEY**
I loved her, asshole.  Maybe the **only** one who ever did without making it a goddamn tragedy.

Jeff explodes—voice sharp, bitter, the most anyone’s heard from him in months.

**JEFF**
You call that love?  You fucked her, you fucked all of us, and then you fucked off every time it got meaningful.

Bill puts a hand on Jim’s shoulder, steady, grounding.  Gilley’s bravado cracks—voice rough, almost pleading.

**GILLEY**
You all want a villain?  Fine. I’ll be the villain.  But don’t you dare tell me I didn’t care.

There’s a pause.  Heavy.  Sacred.

Jim, finally, voice hollow:

**JIM**
She asked me not to make her a legend.  She asked me to tell the truth, even if it’s ugly.

He looks at the letter, pinned and trembling.

**JIM (cont’d)**
The truth is—we all broke her.  Every one of us.

Nobody breathes.  Nobody argues.  Gilley sits, suddenly small.  Bill lights another cigarette.  Jeff stares at the floor.

And for the first time since Kathy’s heart stopped, They all occupy the same silence—A silence so heavy, it nearly breaks the building in two.

**Contention, confession, collapse.  That’s how it happens, after the song ends.**

# **CHAPTER TWO**

---

## ** INT. FORT ANCIENT MANSION — DAY/NIGHT — JANUARY 2013**

The river wore a brown belt and moved as if it had somewhere better to be. From the back terrace you could smell thawed mud, the kind that remembered boots and fights, the kind that let you sink a little, like it had a kink for weight. Sunlight barged into the rooms without knocking. The house was stone and timber, an expensive hush that made every sound—pick scrape, chair leg, ice in a glass—register like evidence. It should have been winter: bone-chime branches, honest breath you could see. Instead the air kept coming back soft and damp, shirtsleeve weather on days when the calendar insisted on penitence. Home flirted with April temperatures in mid-month and again near the end; a day even taunted with sixty-nine, the sky bright and unserious, like the world had forgotten how to dress for the dead.

Jeff tried to hate the sun and found he could. Rage was the only thing that behaved itself—showing up exactly when he summoned it, staying late, doing the dishes of his thoughts. He turned it into chores: make coffee, curse the sun, lock the gate, unplug the intercom, curse the sun again for the way it laid a hand on the piano as if it had earned that presence. He moved gear into the great room and declared it a monastery: Euphonix board under a wool blanket, Telecasters like rifles on a rack, the Jazzmaster lying faceup with its chrome grinning like a bad idea. He gaff-taped the floor with a footprint map: here for the vocal stand, here for the drum machine, here for the pedalboard that would become a small nation-state. He dragged a quilt over the grand’s harp to make it breathe shorter, more obedient.

He didn’t answer the door. He wouldn’t answer the phone. The house had a landline like old money keeps a bloodline, and it rang with the idiot persistence of a machine that still believed in the nineteenth century. He let it ring. When it stopped, he pictured messages blooming in the dark like mold, and when that calmed him, he let it ring again.

Grief loves a loop. Jeff built loops. That was the truce.

## **MONTAGE — THE FIRST WEEK**

- He lays down a click at 41 bpm, obscene and church-quiet, and lets it thud for twelve minutes while he hunts a chord that feels like the word *after*.  
- He rewires the patch bay until it looks like a city under siege and gives the kick drum a little god to worship—one that thunders when spoken to.  
- He turns off the house heat and opens the windows because if the world wants to impersonate spring, he will punish it by letting in its own lie. Outside, a January day lands in the mid-60s and has the nerve to be humid.  
- He writes four lines about a hospital corridor. Then he crosses out the word “corridor” and snarls *hall*, then crosses that out and writes *hallway*, which makes everything weaker, so he swallows the paper, actually swallows it, a ritual he invented that morning and will keep using until it starts working.

He ate so many drafts that week he started craving salt.

Day three, Jim texted something that tried to pass for care and couldn’t help auditioning for authority: *When you’re ready, I’ve got a better bridge for the one with the trumpet image.* Jeff stared at the blue speech bubble so long the phone dimmed, then he typed back *Delete yourself* and never hit send. He set the phone face-down on the piano as if he’d put a lid on a box of copyright strikes.

The work was a private crime spree: he broke into every room of his own head and took back the evidence he’d left there for other people to identify. If a melody he loved had the fingerprints of someone else’s approval, he broke it into untraceable pieces. If a chorus tried to solve a line with a crowd-pleasing vowel, he sent it to stand in the yard. He distrusted anything that arrived without a fight. He distrusted fights that ended too quickly. He distrusted Jim’s notes, Jeremy’s diplomacy, Bill’s alleged taste—especially Bill’s taste, which had the reliable sheen of a knife someone cleaned too often.

He watched the river through the big windows. Once, a hawk crossed the water with the theatrical confidence of a man who’d just paid cash for his car. Jeff wished it would circle back and take his phone.

### **“TERRIFIED” (A WORK TAPE)**

The room had found its key: E minor, sullen and roomy enough to move a couch around in. He hit record and didn’t breathe. The first verse shook out with the simple dignity of a confession: the pews were humid with coats, the candles performed their little hypocrisies, and the priest’s voice warmed the room like a radiator that only knew one setting. He coached himself through the take like a hostile witness: keep the vowels clean, keep the consonants on a leash. When the chorus arrived, it didn’t introduce itself; it just stood up and sang, the way a brave person stands up at a city meeting and says *my name is* and then tells the truth.

After, he listened back and hated the last four seconds, the way his last breath tried to be pretty. He recorded it again with a little sand in his throat, like he’d gargled old coins.

The machine caught everything: the click of the pick against the guard, the chair protesting his sway, the glass settling on the table as if the bourbon were rearranging itself into a better argument. The little red light on the preamp—how do you not imagine that it’s the heart of the building, and that you are either saving it or finishing the job?

### **WEATHER BREAKS THE FOURTH WALL**

Day eight, the false winter doubled down. A front leaned in, wind came on with a polite little cough, and then the afternoon turned green-edged and strange. By dusk it was sixty again, a shirt-sleeve evening in a month that owed him hard frost. He went outside without a coat, feeling personally mocked by the air. Fort Ancient capped January with a freak high near sixty-eight; the weather sensor by the kitchen window confirmed its betrayal.

He opened every door. He wanted the house to sweat. He wanted the river’s breath in the sockets, the warm wrongness to get inside his machines and make them sloppy. He recorded the room tone of his disobedient house and fed it under a snare, then muted it, then fed it again under a vocal, then took it out because the idea of weather as harmony was too on-the-nose even for a month doing kabuki with the seasons.

### **“SNOWGLOBE”**

He wrote it to punish the weather for lying. Every line had glass in it. Glass floors, glass lungs, glass that didn’t break until you looked away. He stacked harmonies like plates in a restaurant that doesn’t think you’re coming back. When the bridge came to ask for a favor, he gave it a minor third and nothing else. He set the drum machine to a humanly impossible pattern and refused to apologize.

Jeremy wrote three times, a festival of ellipses and patience: *If you want ears…* *Heard the scratch. One thing I’d push…* *You can tell me to fuck off.* Jeff did not tell him to fuck off because that would require acknowledging receipt. He turned the phone face-up, watched the messages appear, and then put the phone in the freezer for an hour, a trick he learned from a DJ who swore cold canceled need.

### **“GABRIEL’S MODERN TRUMPET”**

A guitar fed back in a way that sounded like a siren a block away. He took the emergency and put it in 3/4. He stood up while he played to make the blood move faster, and the riff answered by showing teeth. He named the track. He didn’t care if it was pious or blasphemous; it had the exact metal taste of the night that made it necessary.

Jim called. *Got a bigger chorus if you want it*, said the voicemail, then sang something with the syrup of a thousand stages. Jeff listened twice and admired the competence, the way you admire an enemy’s aim. He deleted it. He kept the silence after the delete, the blue hush of it, and sampled that. He tucked the hush under a high harmony because grief, like reverb, makes a note sound more true than it actually is.

### **“TIME OF DEATH”**

He did the research he’d been refusing to do and gave the line a timestamp he could live with. He put the time in the second verse, not the first, so the song wouldn’t open with an obituary; he wanted the news to arrive like a nurse who already knows your name and does not look at the floor when she says it. The chords stayed stubbornly simple, like two people who’ve decided not to argue in front of the kids. The last chorus refused to lift, so he let it sit in the room like a load-bearing beam.

# **INTERLUDE — THE HOUSE TALKS BACK**

At night the house popped and sighed, ancient timbers telling each other the weather. The bedroom faced north and hoarded its darkness. He slept like a man negotiating with a creditor: short, false, and quite transactional. Sometimes he woke convinced he’d just conducted an orchestra in a field; sometimes he woke having fought men he loved in a bar that no longer existed.

On the worst night he dreamed a studio where every fader was a headstone with a name he refused to read. He pushed them all up until the lights in the room flickered and the sound made a wall that might hold. He woke punching air. He apologized to the air.  The last time those mixed gasses were so surprised, the *Hindenburg* blew up.

---
### **“SIN AND DEATH SITTING TILL THEN AT THE GATES OF HELL”**

Milton came off the shelf because anything newer felt like asking for friendship, and he didn’t trust friendship anymore. He thumbed through lines that had learned to fight in harsher climates. He stole five words and admitted the theft to no one but the song.  After all, his mate Bono once opined that every artist was a cannibal and every poet a thief.  He put a chain on the kick drum, not metaphorical but actual chain, the metal clicking against the shell so each hit sounded like a prison door being polite. He played a single-note line across the neck with a slide until it moaned like a hinge.  Just like she did, when he carelessly remembered each night.

Bill texted: *If you want bass on any of this, I’m here. No ego.* The generosity landed like a salesman’s hand on a shoulder you were trying to keep to yourself. Jeff typed *no thanks* and deleted the space because it looked like the beginning of a compromise. He did his own bass and made it primitive on purpose, letting the D string ring like a bell only he could hear.

### **“THIRTY-SIX DEGREES FAHRENHEIT”**

He kept that one nearly instrumental. The temperature mattered. He lay a murmured recitation under the bridge—nothing you could parse, just breath with edges—while an organ did the work of keeping the lights on. He held the last note until it started to sound like somebody’s appliance giving up the ghost and then cut it dead. He wanted a song that felt like a room with a drawer in it, the metal silent because sound would be a betrayal.  Then he betrayed himself and all concerned parties changing it again, but in the key of hope.

### **“WHAT THEY SAY ABOUT PAYBACK (BATT A, 1-134 FA)”**

He wrote it as if percussion were artillery and the melody could wear camo without irony. He gave the chorus a parade step and then slurred it, like soldiers marching after the bar closed. He sang it hoarse. When his voice broke in the tag he kept the take; you keep the tattoo that tells the tale.

Jim called again. *I hear a hole in verse two. Let me say the thing you don’t want: you can’t do this alone.* Jeff stared at the ceiling and pictured the plaster as ocean and himself a mile below. He threw the phone into a pile of towels in the laundry room, missed, hit tile, and didn’t pick it up until the washing machine beeped to tell him the cycle was complete. He liked the beep. It was honest about limits.

### **WEATHER, AGAIN, BECAUSE SOMEONE HAS TO BE THE VILLAIN**

Another warm evening. He grilled chicken on the patio in the middle of January, because if the month wanted to impersonate July, he would humiliate it by agreeing. The thermometer nudged into the 60s more than once; a week earlier, a day had slipped up into the mid-60s and stayed there until night remembered what it was supposed to do. He stood in sock feet on stone, the heat lamp throwing a cone of summer onto his face, and he let himself think an unthinkably small thought: *maybe I’m not writing songs; maybe I’m writing the only version of the truth that doesn’t make me scream.* 

### **A PHONE CALL HE ANSWERS**

Jeremy again. *It’s me. I’m not pushing. Just… if you want me to listen, I’ll say nothing until you ask.* Jeff let the silence drag long enough to hear the line hum, the electricity of it. He pictured Jeremy in some room lit by a single lamp, doing his professional tenderness like a man threading a needle with one eye closed.

**JEFF**
“Not yet.”

**JEREMY**
Okay.

He did not fill the air with furniture. 

**JEREMY**
Bro, I love you.

Jeff ended the thought like you end a prayer you don’t believe in—politely, as if the Alpha and the Omega had good manners.

### **THE LAST WEEK**

He worked as if someone had put a meter on time. He scrubbed the songs with steel wool until they shone in places shining was an insult. He played the acoustic so gently the pick left no marks and then so savagely it threw splinters. He double-tracked a whisper. He sang a vowel from the back of his throat until it felt like a held plank pose in a class he despised. He got the chorus of “Terrified” to sit right by erasing the harmony he was proudest of; the deletion was physical, a warmth in his chest like the body saying *good—fewer lies to maintain*. He sent a single bounce to himself and didn’t open it, like a letter from the dead you decide to keep sealed because the handwriting is enough.

He went for a drive at midnight. The town had the polite emptiness of a hotel hallway. He put the window down to smell the sin of the river and the heat of a month that owed him cold. On the radio, some station had made a church of the seventies, and a song with leather in its DNA promised love as a kind of weather system. He laughed, the sound startling in the car, and turned it off. The laugh stayed in the air like a moth, uncatchable and faintly embarrassing.

### **DELIVERY**

He named the folder ***BE FIRST BE SMARTER OR CHEAT*** because putting the dare in the title meant he didn’t have to hear it from anyone else. He printed a tracklist in blunt capitals and taped it to the inside of the guitar case he touched most: seven titles in a column, no punctuation, like tombstones aligned in a shining city on a hill.

He sent a single email at 3:11 a.m. to the band account with no greeting and the subject line “**Demos**.” There were no notes, no instructions, no invitations to discuss. There was a sentence at the bottom that said “I don’t need parts,” which meant ***I don’t need you,*** which meant ***I need you so badly I can’t risk what happens if you arrive and speak.*** He hit send and unplugged the router with the ceremony of a man taking himself off life support.

He slept for twenty-two minutes, woke with hands that thought they were still playing, and went downstairs to watch the morning crawl in like a bartender who’d been told he was late. He made Vietnamese coffee that tasted industrious. He sat on the floor between the speakers and let silence assert dominion. He waited for the phone to ring and did not want it to. He wanted the weather to misbehave again so he could pin his anger to a thing that didn’t cry.

It was, in the end, just a January that refused to be January and a man who refused to be a band. The river kept its brown belt. The hawk did not come back. The house did the good house thing and held. The songs played inside the computer, tiny live animals with perfect obedience, waiting for a hand to open the cage and tell them what to be when they grew up.

In a city that shouldn’t have let him walk outside without a coat, in a month that smirked while it warmed his cheek, he wrote the ledger that grief demanded. It felt like stealing. It felt like prayer. It felt like he had told the truth and would never, ever be forgiven for it.

# **CHAPTER THREE**

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – JANUARY 2013 – 10:47 P.M.**

The fluorescent buzz is relentless, the room choked with cigarette haze and nerves.  Jeff is pacing, guitar swinging at his hip, eyes wild and rimmed with exhaustion—weeks without real sleep, days without a real meal.

Bill is slumped behind the console, a tangle of headphone cables and Red Bull cans at his feet.
Gilley sprawls on a battered amp, legs open, arms crossed, chewing a toothpick with fake disinterest.  Jim sits on a road case, tapping his sticks, the metronome of impending violence.

**JEFF** (pacing, urgent, voice already fraying)
Look. We keep circling the same old shit. We talk about a tribute, a comeback, a healing record—whatever you want to call it. But that’s not what this is. Not for me. Not for any of you if you’re honest.

He stops, eyes hard on each of them.

**JEFF (CONT’D)**
You want to make a record for Kathy? Write a “safe” record? Go do it. But that’s not what I’m here for.

Bill stares, arms crossed.

**BILL**
Then what are you here for, Jeff? To bleed on tape? To drag us through hell again?

**JEFF** 
Yes. Exactly.

(he points, almost laughing)
We’re not the fucking survivors, Bill. We’re the wreckage. So let’s make the album that sounds like that. The ugliest, truest, meanest fucking thing we’ve ever done.

Jim’s eyebrows go up. Gilley stops chewing his toothpick.

**JIM**
That’s a death wish, man. Nobody wants to hear that much truth.

**JEFF**
That’s exactly why we do it. We stop pretending. We tell the truth.

(voice rising)
You want a concept? Fine. Here it is:  You win, or outwit Death, or you cheat Saint Peter himself.  The way you survive isn’t pretty, and it’s never fair. It’s just the way it is.

Gilley, fresh from his flight from his private sojourn in northwest of Fountain and Fairfax grins, suddenly animated.

**GILLEY**
I **fucking** love it. You want the sound of a band tearing itself apart on tape? Let’s go.  But you better be ready to lose everything.  

Jeff stares him down, jaw clenched.

**JEFF** (chagrined)
I already have.

Silence. Bill looks at Jim, who shrugs.

**BILL** (sighs)
You know this will kill us, right?

**JEFF**
So? At least it’ll mean something. For once.

Jim stands up, moves behind his kit, spins a stick in his fingers.

**JIM**
You want ugly? You want broken?  Let’s bleed, motherfuckers.

Gilley whoops. Bill just sighs, rubs his eyes.

**BILL**
Alright. We do it your way.  But don’t say I didn’t warn you.

Jeff’s hand shakes as he plugs in his guitar, but his voice is steady, full of venom and resolve.

**JEFF**
Hit record.

The first chords are raw, violent—**a warning, a dare, a promise**.  The album will nearly destroy them—wreck friendships, shatter egos, break bones and hearts—but in this moment, it feels like the only honest thing they’ve ever done.

**Kendall’s Note:**
I watched them that night, knowing what it would cost.  And I wrote, in the margin: *Some albums are made in love. Some are made in grief. The best are made in rage and end in silence.*

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – WINTER BLEEDING INTO SPRING, 2013 – LATE**

You want truth about *Be First, Be Smarter, Or Cheat?*  Here’s how it really went down: Jeff took control.  Euchre without the left bower.

**Lyrics?** All Jeff.  **Melodies?** Jeff, alone, headphones clamped, hunched over the battered Wurlitzer, muttering lines, scratching verses out until his hands cramped.  **Arrangement?** He mapped every crescendo, every chord change, every breath between syllables.  For once—**no democracy, no edits, no input.**  He locked the rest of the band out. They were performers, not partners.

Gilley called it a coup, “the coldest fucking mutiny I’ve ever seen.”  Bill fumed, “He wants to bleed alone? Let him.”  Jim, even Jim, just shrugged and counted off—“He’s got the vision. I just play.”

**They played because Jeff demanded it.**  It wasn’t love.  It wasn’t tribute.  It was compulsion—his, theirs, all of it wrung out on tape.

**In the studio:**  Jeff handed out charts, no discussion.  He’d snarl if Gilley tried a vocal ad lib.  He’d hiss if Bill added a synth swell not in the notes.  He’d glare when Jim improvised a fill.
The message was clear: *This is mine.*

Resentment?  It ate the room alive.  Bill nearly quit twice.  Gilley drank himself to sleep in the vocal booth.  Jim punched a hole in the bathroom door.  Jeremy spent the whole session playing like a ghost, barely there, watching his brother burn.

**But the result?**  **Diamonds.**  No hyperbole.  The record sounds like the inside of a head shot wound—clean, clear, brilliant, sharp enough to see gray matter.

*Be First, Be Smarter, or Cheat* drops like a fist.  Every critic calls it “singular,” “visionary,” “pain made manifest.”  The band initially called it a Jeff Evans solo album in all but name.  The band?  They'll play the release show.  They'll do the interviews.  They'll pose for the photos.  They'll smile through their teeth, but with their dicks caught in their zipper.

Everyone who was there remembers: **This was Jeff’s.**  The only question is if it saved him—or broke the rest of them forever.

**Kendall’s Archive, margin note:**
*The cost of genius is always paid by someone else.  Diamonds are made under pressure.  But sometimes the mine never recovers.*

## **INT. BILL’S OTR STUDIO – MARCH 2013 – 2:37 A.M.**

The studio is empty except for Jeff Evans, hunched over the console, mumbling lyrics to himself, reworking a bridge for the seventh time, headphones slung around his neck. Bill has gone home angry, Gilley is missing (again), Jim texted “fuck this” hours ago, and Jeremy won’t even answer the phone.

Outside, the Cincinnati rain slashes the windows.

**Enter Jodi**—not with drama, not with threat, but with the steel-cold certainty of someone who knows exactly where the bodies are buried and is not impressed by any of them.  She doesn’t knock. She doesn’t need to.

**JODI**
You’re making history, Jeff.

Jeff doesn’t look up. “Finally, someone who gets it.”  She just stares until he feels it.

**JODI (CONT’D)**
You think you’re Moses coming down from the mountain.  You’re actually an asshole marooned on it.

He turns, half-offended, half-confused. 

**JEFF**
What the hell are you talking about?

Jodi crosses the studio, stands between him and the console.  She folds her arms. She makes herself **immovable**.

**JODI**
You don’t see what you’re doing to them, do you?  You think this is leadership? Genius? It’s not.
You’re burning everyone out. Bill, Jim, Jeremy, even Gilley—They’d rather set themselves on fire than spend another week letting you run them like machines.

Jeff bristles. 

**JEFF**
Someone has to hold this shit together. Someone has to finish what we started. That’s what Kathy would have wanted.

Jodi’s face hardens—her voice, ice over gasoline.

**JODI**
Don’t you dare use her as a shield.  She loved you because you could *feel*.  But right now, you don’t even feel yourself.  You’re a bully with a vision and no heart.

Silence. He doesn’t respond. He honestly doesn’t know how.  Jodi leans in, close enough for him to smell her perfume and fury.

**JODI (CONT’D)**
You’re losing them, Jeff.  Not just as a band. As friends. As family.  You’ll win this album and lose everything else.

She lets it sit. She watches him try to process it—the flicker of confusion, the denial, the anger, the panic that follows in its wake.  He tries to brush it off, but he can’t. She’s the only one he can’t shout down.

**JODI**
You need to fix this.  Or when it’s all over, you’ll be king of ashes.

She turns to go, stops at the door.

**JODI (CONT’D)**
Ask yourself, Jeff—if no one’s left when you’re done, was it really worth it?

She leaves him alone with the rain and his diamonds, the word “king” ringing in his ears like a curse.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – THE DAY AFTER JODI’S INTERVENTION – 1:13 P.M.**

The light is grey and thin, the room hungover, yesterday’s ghosts curling around the battered gear.
Bill arrives first, still raw.  Gilley blows in, wrung out, acting cool but eyes nervous.  Jeremy stays close to the wall, eyeing his brother.  Jodi lingers by the door, unreadable.

Jeff Evans sits at the console—gaunt, haunted, hollowed out from the truth Jodi poured into him the night before. He doesn’t meet anyone’s eyes. He waits.  Jim arrives last—jacket slung, brow furrowed, already expecting another round of creative dictatorship.

But Jeff stands.  He puts his hands in his pockets, clears his throat.

**JEFF**
So, uh.  I think I’ve hit my limit.

Bill’s head snaps up. Gilley laughs—a dry, disbelieving snort.

**JEFF (CONT’D)**
I’ve been running this like a solo record.  I know it.  I’m not… I’m not really proud of it.  I want it to be more than just me.  I need it to be.  So I’m stepping aside.  I want Jim to lead.  He’s the one who holds us together.  Always has.

**SILENCE.**

Nobody moves.  Nobody breathes.

**Jim stares.**  His mouth works for a second—like a fish plucked from the water, trying to form words in a new, impossible element.

**JIM**
Wait—You want me to…Lead the record?  Like, run the sessions?

Jeff nods, almost embarrassed.

**JEFF**
Yeah.  I need help, man.  And you’re the only one I trust to do it.

It’s like **water asking the sand how to be hard**—Unnatural. Impossible.  For 22 years, Jeff’s been the axis, the sun.  Now he’s circling Jim for gravity.

**Bill lets out a sound—half laughter, half disbelief. Gilley’s mouth hangs open. Jeremy’s eyes go wide.**

Jim sits, hard, on the edge of the amp.  He runs a hand through his hair.

**JIM**
Shit, Jeff.  You never ask for help.  Not once.  Not even that time in Dayton when your hand was literally broken.  Not even in fuckin' Baltimore, neither!

**JEFF** (sheepish)
Guess I’m finally learning.

Jim is still staring, but something in him softens—Like a muscle unclenching after a lifetime of tension.

**JIM**
You actually sure about this?

**JEFF**
I need you.  We all do.  I need to be a part of the band again, not the whole fucking band.

A long, slow breath from Jim.  He nods—stunned, but honored, and suddenly very aware of the weight.

**JIM** (lips pursed)
Alright.  Let’s make this a fucking band record, then.

Bill finally grins, Gilley pounds the table, Jeremy gives a quiet “yes” under his breath.  Even Jodi lets herself smile—mission accomplished.

**In that moment, the air clears.**  For the first time in months, they’re a band.  And the record that follows will have blood, but also laughter.  Diamonds—but with the warmth of hands holding them, not just the cold flash of the cut.

A different energy—uncertain, raw, a little afraid. The band circles Jim now, but every glance keeps drifting back to Jeff Evans, sitting on the floor with a guitar in his lap, for once **not** barking orders, just quietly, maybe even shyly, playing whatever Jim asks for.

**GILLEY**
You think he’s going to walk out?  Or worse?  He’s acting like he’s already a ghost.

Others make you share the burden.  Jeff’s not letting go of life.  He’s letting go of the myth that he has to carry it alone.

## **INT. KENDALL AND JIM’S BEDROOM – 2:02 A.M. – SPRING 2013**

Rain ticks on the window. Kendall—archivist, lover, chronicler of chaos—lies curled on her side of the bed, notebook resting on the nightstand, mind still chewing the day’s session.  Jim is beside her, sprawled in an exhausted heap, eyes half-closed, the outline of a new bruise ghosting his forearm from drumming too hard (again).

Kendall whispers into the hush, voice raw with worry she can’t quite write away:

**KENDALL**
Jim, are you sure he’s okay?  Jeff.  I keep watching him—like I’m waiting for the bass to just… stop.

Jim rolls closer, his chest warm against her back, breath slow and patient. He pulls her in, presses his lips to the space behind her ear.

**JIM**
You’re not the only one, babe.  We all worry.  But he’s not done.  Not yet.  He’s too stubborn for oblivion.  And if you ask him, he’ll tell you: As long as he’s got a song, he’s got a reason.

Kendall sighs, tension leaving her shoulders but not quite her mind.

**KENDALL**
I keep writing it like a disaster is coming.  Like the archives are a warning sign.

Jim’s hands drift up, gentle, fingers tracing circles over her mons, grounding her.

**JIM**
Don’t write him off yet.  He’s grieving in music, same as always.  He’ll never ask for comfort, but he’ll take the shape of it.  From you, from us.  Hell, from the band—even if he never says thank you.

Kendall laughs, low and a little sad.

**KENDALL**
You’re too kind to him.

Jim shrugs, grins against her skin.

**JIM**
Someone has to be.  You’re the one who writes what hurts.  I’m the one who drums the pain out.

Kendall turns, facing him, studying the worn lines of his face—her anchor, her witness.

**KENDALL**
If he goes off the rails—If he disappears again—We find him, right?

Jim nods, solemn.

**JIM**
We find him.  And we drag him back.  Like always.

He kisses her forehead, and Kendall lets herself soften, lets the day slip away.


It is easier to write tragedy than to trust in survival.  But some nights, you let yourself hope anyway.  Sleep comes.  The band is still whole—broken, bleeding, but whole.  And Kendall, mythographer, chronicler, lover, lies awake a minute longer, praying the song keeps playing, just a little while more.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – LATE NIGHT, SPRING 2013**

It’s a strange, fragile peace.  Jeff Evans—once tyrant, once visionary, once storm center—now just sits on the amp, hands folded in his lap, eyes half-lidded but watching, waiting.

The band sprawls through the space.  Jim is at the kit, rolling sticks through a gentle, syncopated groove.  Bill hovers over the keys, layering in a synth patch, frowning in concentration but open, for once, to mistakes.  Gilley leans in the vocal booth, humming to himself, running lyrics under his breath, the devil-may-care grin returned.  Jeremy, guitar in hand, follows every chord Bill throws at him, grinning shyly whenever Jim shouts “Yeah, that’s it!”

And Jeff?  He isn’t driving.  He isn’t controlling.

He’s listening.  He’s catching the melody where Gilley falters and offering a riff, gentle and new.
He’s tuning Jeremy’s guitar between takes, not because he has to, but because it’s what Jeremy forgot to do.  He writes chord changes on a notepad, but keeps it on the floor—never thrusts it in anyone’s face.

Every so often, Bill glances over, waiting for an argument that never comes.  Jim looks back, waiting for Jeff’s infamous critique. Instead, Jeff just nods, “Sounds good, man.”  Gilley catches his eye, teases, “Evans, you getting soft?” and Jeff shrugs, “Maybe I was just hard for too long.”

**He’s not absent. He’s not lost.  He’s simply—present.  He’s doing all the little things the rest of them have always needed, but never asked for.**

When someone suggests a change—he plays it, no questions.  When the groove falls apart—he’s the first to laugh, not the first to blame.  When the room fills with silence, he lets it breathe, doesn’t stomp it dead with his own ideas.

**Kendall writes in the archive:**

*This is what hope looks like, not a victory march, not an anthem.  A quiet surrender to the group,
a willingness to be what they need, not what he demands. Jeff Evans, at last, is a bandmate—not a king.  Sometimes, that’s the bravest song you’ll ever play.*

## **INT. BILL’S OTR STUDIO – LATE MAY 2013 – 11:54 P.M.**

Studio lights hum. Drums still echo off the insulation. Coffee cups and lyric sheets litter every surface.

Jeff Evans sits on the battered leather couch, hunched forward, elbows on knees, picking a callus on his thumb. Jim, in a sweaty T-shirt, settles across from him, towel around his neck, still catching his breath.

**JEFF** (confidential, tense)
We’re gonna have a problem, Jim.  As soon as these tracks are done, we’re putting everything in Bill’s hands to mix and master. You know what that means, right?

Jim closes his eyes, already tired of this song.

**JIM** (correct)
He’s meticulous. He’ll get it right.

**JEFF** (true)
He’ll take a fucking year.

(leans in, counting off on his fingers)
We’ve got—what, six weeks after tracking to get everything done if we want a December drop? Christmas market, man. Anything later, we’re dead.  And if Bill spends half that time A/B testing kick drums, we’ll miss it.

Jim looks at him, jaw tense, but not angry—just weary.

**JIM**
That’s not our only problem.

**JEFF**
What, you think I don’t see how everyone’s playing?  The last three takes—nobody’s even close to what they can do.  It’s like the air’s gone heavy.  Half the time I look over and Gilley’s just mouthing lyrics, not singing.  Jeremy keeps staring at the wall.  Bill’s not even arguing with me anymore.  Even you—

(gestures, small smile)
—your fills are off. You never miss.

Jim sighs. He grips the towel tight, voice low:

**JIM**
It’s not suboptimal, Jeff.  It’s depression.  We’re dragging a stone through every fucking song.  This isn’t catharsis—it’s just heavy.  Too heavy, maybe.

Jeff sits back, shaking his head.

**JEFF**
That’s what the record is.  It’s honest.  It’s not supposed to feel good.  It’s supposed to be true.

Jim looks at him—afraid, honest.

**JIM**
What if you’re right?  What if this is what it’s supposed to sound like, and none of us can take it?
What if we put everything in and all we get is more pain?  What then?

Jeff doesn’t worry. He doesn’t apologize. He just looks back, certain:

**JEFF**
Then we did our job.  I’m not here to make anybody comfortable.  If this album hurts, maybe it should.

A silence hangs—too long.

**JIM**
You’re not wrong.  But I wish you were.

He stands, wipes his face.  Jeff just watches him go, jaw set, eyes cold, convinced to his bones that he’s right—even if it means every single one of them comes out broken.

**Kendall’s margin, archive:** *Sometimes the right song is the one that breaks you open.
Sometimes, the hardest truth is that it’s worth it.*

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – 2:17 A.M. – THREE DAYS LATER**

The clock blinks red. Empty pizza boxes and spilled coffee mark the hours.  The band’s just wrapped another late-night tracking session—energy spent, nerves frayed, silence brittle as glass.

Jeff waits until the others are gone.  Jim is half-packed, but Jeff grabs his arm, voice low, shaking a little.

**JEFF**
Jim.  I just—I need to say it.

Jim looks at him, patient, ready for another technical debate.

**JEFF (CONT’D)**
I love you, man.  Not just bandmate, not just friend.  Like a brother.  You’re the only reason I’ve made it through any of this.  But I need you to do something for me when the time comes.

Jim is caught off guard by the softness—so rare, so unguarded in Jeff.

**JIM**
Name it.

**JEFF**
When we turn these tracks over to Bill—If he starts in with the endless tinkering, the tweaks, the perfectionism—If he starts dragging his feet or makes it about what *he* needs instead of what the band needs—I need you to ram it down his fucking throat.  No *“please.”*  No compromise.  We have *six weeks*, Jim.  If Bill makes us miss the deadline because he wants to chase *“perfection”* for another year—It’ll kill this record, and maybe the band.

Jim studies him. Sees the fear, the determination, the unspoken love.

**JIM**
You got it.  I’ll keep him on task.  Even if I have to threaten to mix the record myself.

Jeff finally smiles, a weak thing, almost a laugh.

**JEFF**
I’d pay money to see that.

Jim claps him on the back, fierce, brotherly.

**JIM**
We’re getting this out, Jeff.  All of it.  Even if it kills us.

Jeff nods, relief mixing with old grief.

**JEFF**
Thanks, man.  I mean it.

Love, for these men, is a blood pact—more command than comfort, more promise than plea.
The only way forward is through each other.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – EARLY MORNING – WEEK SEVEN OF TRACKING**

It’s past sunrise, the windows pale and fogged.  Takeout boxes, chewed pens, crushed cans—evidence of creative attrition.  Jeff Evans sits slumped at the battered Fender, shoulders hunched, eyes rimmed raw.  Jeremy stands by the door, arms folded, guitar strap still around his neck. He’s tired. He’s pissed. He’s family.

**JEREMY** (pushing, voice low but urgent)
You done, Jeff?  ‘Cause I think you’re out of gas.  We’ve got eight tracks—barely.  You think we’re really gonna scrape up more?  Feels like I’m playing on your solo project anyway, but—

(pauses, something softer)
You got anything left up there?  Anything after “Sin and Death Standing at the Gates of Hell”?

Jeff doesn’t answer.  He just stares at the fretboard, jaw clenched.  Jeremy waits—used to Jeff stonewalling, never admitting anything, always pushing through.  But this time, it’s different.

Finally, Jeff lifts his head.  Voice thin, shaking, words sharp and helpless:

**JEFF**
I’m empty, Jer.  That’s it.  That’s the last song in my brain.  I can’t…I can’t even write a fucking chord right now.

It’s the first time in Jeremy’s life—decades of brotherhood, bandhood, war—that Jeff admits he’s failed.  Jeff’s hands shake. He sets the guitar down, hard.  He covers his face, the sob fighting out of his throat.

**JEFF (CONT’D)**
I got nothing.  I thought if I kept pushing, if I just…But it’s gone.  I’m gone.

Jeremy stands frozen—resentment melting, replaced by something jagged and ugly: empathy.
He crosses the room, kneels by his brother.

**JEREMY** (quiet, finally gentle)
It’s okay, Jeff.  Eight tracks is enough.  Let someone else finish the story for once.

Jeff shakes, shoulders convulsing.  Jeremy just stays there, not hugging, not fixing, just *being*.  The silence is thick—Not defeat, not yet, but a moment when even the architect admits the house has to stand as it is.

**Kendall’s Archive, margin:** *No heroics.  No myth left.  Just a man, and his brother, and the music that’s left when everything else falls away.*

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – BAND MEETING – MIDDAY, JUNE 2013**

Twenty-nine minutes. Eight tracks.  I can’t sell that, not in 2013.  Not unless you want to be a punchline in every review.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – TWILIGHT, JUNE 2013**

The air is dead with exhaustion and disappointment.  Cremeans is gone, muttering about contracts and sales quotas.  Nobody talks, nobody tunes.  Just a slow collapse, eight tracks and thirty-three minutes hanging in the air.

Then—Jim, leaning back on the drum throne, twirling a stick, stares at the ceiling like he’s watching fate crawl across the paint.

**JIM**
Or—

(soft, dangerous, impossible)
We could cover something.

**JEREMY**
Dylan. Or Suicide.  God, what if we did a fucking Pet Shop Boys song?

**GILLEY**

**JEFF** (not angry, not harsh, just empty)
You guys do what you want.

**Kendall’s Archive:**
*Some ideas are lifelines. Some are salt in the wound.  For a band running on fumes, the chance to steal someone else’s fire is a relief—unless you’re the guy who set himself on fire first.*

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – NIGHT, JUNE 2013 – THE LAST SESSION**

The clock is racing. The room is ragged, everyone at the edge—throats raw, eyes burning, patience spent.  Bill’s pacing in the control room, a synth loop ticking in the background, half-dream, half-nightmare.

Jeff finally breaks the lull, voice gravelly, old smoke and stubbornness.

**JEFF**
I’ve got one idea.  “See the Lights.” Simple Minds.  It’s been, what, twenty-two years since that came out?  Nobody’s done it justice.  We need a dirge to close this thing out.  Pet Shop Boys? Hard pass.

Bill explodes, hands flying, almost shrieking:

**BILL**
Oh, but *“It’s a Sin”* on the B-side of a limited vinyl? That’s history! That’s drama!  It’s a B-side—make it fucking *camp*, do a dance remix, do whatever—just *give them something to talk about*.

Gilley groans, dropping his head in his hands.

**GILLEY**
Sure. Whatever.  I’ll record “It’s a Sin,” but after that I’m done.  I mean it, man.  I’m ready to walk out and never look back.

Jeremy’s picking out chords on the Gretsch, voice soft, exhausted.

**JEREMY**
How much longer can we keep at this?  Feels like we’ve been here forever.

Jim glances around, weary but resolute.

**JIM**
Tomorrow’s it.  That’s the deal.  We get the last takes done and we call it.

**Nods all around. Everyone wants out.**

But Jeff, now perched on the edge of the amp, finally looks up, and there’s that dangerous glint—the old flame, the leadership nobody asked for but always needed.

**JEFF**
We don’t have the right vocals for any of this.  Not for “See the Lights.”  Not for “It’s a Sin.”
Hell, not for the mood piece, not even for my own stuff.  But I’ve got an idea to fix it.  If you want to listen.

**GILLEY**
We’re listening, chief.  Just don’t say “gospel choir” or I’ll start screaming.

**JEFF**
Not gospel. Not choir.  But if you’ll let me try something…I think I can make it work.

**He doesn’t say more. Not yet.  The night is alive with nerves—one last chance to save the record, or at least save themselves.**

**Kendall’s Archive:** *It’s not just the end of an album.  It’s the end of the myth that you can do it all alone.  Jeff’s idea is the last bridge out of hell—if anyone’s willing to cross it.*

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – FINAL SESSION, JUNE 2013 – MIDNIGHT**

The air tastes like old sweat, burnt coffee, and finality.  Everyone’s packing up, bodies slumped and spent, nothing left to squeeze out.  Jeff stands by the glass, guitar case in hand, but he’s not storming out, not sulking—he’s calm, decided, almost gentle.

**JEFF**
Look, I’m done.  Gilley’s done.  But we don’t have to finish this thing alone.  If “Terrified” needs vocals, let’s get a guest.  Someone who can give it what we can’t.

Bill perks up, hopeful for the first time in hours.

**BILL**
You serious?  Like a feature?  Who?

**JEFF**
Doesn’t matter if it’s someone nobody expects.  Someone with a voice that scares people.  Call and response—us, then them, right?  That’s how “Terrified” works in my head.  We need outside blood.

Jim sits up, grinning, clapping his sticks.

**JIM**
That could work.  Could be the best thing on the record.

Jeremy is already digging through his phone, scrolling playlists.

**JEREMY**
Alright, what about “Thirty-six Degrees”?  If we’re going outside, let’s go wild.  Who’s the most sublime, most subversive female artist we could dream of?  Hell, let’s make it a duet, or more.

Gilley, legs up on the console, almost laughs.

**GILLEY**
Name your poison.  PJ Harvey? Santigold? Siouxsie?  Who do you want?  Fuck it, call all of them.

Jeff—who’s barely listened to anyone all year—finally, finally looks around the room.
