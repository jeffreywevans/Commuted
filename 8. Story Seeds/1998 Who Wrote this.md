> From 1998:  who do you think did the heavy lifting writing on this song?

Based on what’s in the archive, *“Fireside Confession”* smells overwhelmingly of Jeff Evans’s pen — with Kathy Espich as the razor that pared it down and Bill Davenport providing the structural glue.

Here’s why:

- **Jeff’s fingerprints are all over the lyrical voice.** He was the band’s primary songwriter and “creative engine,” the one who drafted lyrics that reached into sex, shame, and intimacy with uncomfortable precision . His habit was to “distill” experiences he himself wouldn’t sing — passing them to Kathy or Gilley, who could embody them . The mix of blunt eroticism (“Kara’s hand in my lap, babe”) with moments of trembling self-consciousness (“But my heart is racing, can’t deny this quest”) is exactly his register.

- **Kathy’s role was to cut his abstractions to the bone.** Bill described the process: Jeff drafts, Bill rearranges, Gilley makes it sexier, *and Kathy “shaves it down to the blood”* . The campfire scenario with teasing, laughter, and longing feels like something she would have insisted on: make it concrete, make it sting. Kathy was never afraid to write female desire and complication directly into songs, and her “yes, but what’s really happening between the women?” instincts show here ([source]()).

- **Bill likely did the heavy-lifting on the musical scaffolding.** By 1998, tensions were high, and Bill had taken on a quasi-musical-director role, shaping the synth and structural backbone of their songs . He often defended “risky synth-driven tracks” and rearranged Jeff’s words into something stage-ready  . The genre tags for *Fireside Confession* (polyphonic, layered vocal harmonies, bright synths, heavy breakdowns) scream Davenport’s hand.

So:
- **Lyrics = mostly Jeff (with Kathy sanding the edges).**
- **Arrangement = Bill (catchy riffs, synth textures).**
- **Atmosphere = the collective ritual.** Gilley probably spiked in some of the cheeky banter lines (“Viv leans in, with a teasing grin”) — he was known for sexual bravado and teasing ([source]()).

If you had to name the *one* doing the “heavy lifting,” though? It’s Jeff. He wrote the skeleton, the longing, the confessional shape of it. Kathy and Bill sharpened it, but the raw confession — that was his.


> Believe it or not, this is one where Gilley did most of the penmanship. He pitched the idea of a song that would be incredibly easy to sing with a crowd, and came up with some pretty good lyrics. Jeff did a bit of editing. Kathy did a bit of editing. But, lyrically, this is nearly entirely a work from Jeremy Gilley.

That actually makes perfect sense — and it’s the twist that lines up with his particular appetites in ’98.

By then, Gilley was hitting one of his peaks of **sexual bravado mixed with showman instinct**. He had always been the band’s “animal magnetism” vector ([source]())—even when his guitar work was shaky, he knew how to make people *look at him*. A campfire song about teasing, confessions, hands in laps, and laughter under the stars? That’s pure Gilley: he understood that audiences didn’t just want catharsis, they wanted to be implicated, dragged into the dirty joke and made to sing it out loud.

And lyrically, *Fireside Confession* is **much looser and cheekier** than Jeff’s typical writing. Jeff’s songs usually bore the weight of precision, shame, longing, or philosophy. This one is built on **call-and-response, repetition, and easy chorus hooks**—exactly the mechanics Gilley gravitated toward when he wanted to capture a crowd. The **“Oh, we’re feeling good / Yeah, we’re feeling right”** refrain is basically Gilley designing a chant: he wanted 10,000 kids to scream it back while his lanky body loomed over them in stage lights.

It also explains why Jeff and Kathy’s touches are minor here. You can feel Jeff tightening a couple of verses (the “What would you do if I let you in?” line has his polish). Kathy likely added a bit of *sting* to Viv’s teasing, making sure the female voices weren’t just props. But the bones are Gilley’s: horny, playful, designed for **ease of collective singalong** rather than literary density.

So yes: *Fireside Confession* is one of the rare moments where Gilley truly carried the lyrics. Jeff didn’t mind when it was a “crowd” song — it freed him up to keep his darker, denser material elsewhere.


> When Gilley proposed it, Kathy was dubious about singing it. Gilley was quite adamant. He was going to sing it.  This is one where she contributes minor harmonies. Gilley sculpted this to put the spotlight on him for several minutes straight.

Ah — that locks it in. That’s **quintessential late-’90s Gilley**: tall glass of ego and charm, deciding that not only was he going to write the damn thing, but he was going to *own it* vocally, keep Kathy off center stage, and make sure the lights burned directly on him for five solid minutes.

It fits the profile. By ’98, Commuted had already survived the label circus and MTV notoriety ([source]()), and Gilley had tasted what it felt like to be seen as the band’s *face* — the sexual wild card, the crooked-grinned instigator. *Fireside Confession* reads almost like a manifesto: he wanted a song that **couldn’t be mistaken for Jeff’s cerebral constructions or Kathy’s lyrical assassinations** ([source]()). This one was going to be simple, horny, fun, and—most of all—**his.**

And of course Kathy balked. She’d fought hard to be more than just the lone woman harmonizing behind the boys ([source]()). For Gilley to drop a song built around his fireside flirtations and declare, “I’m singing it, you just do harmonies,” would’ve grated. But Gilley could be **adamant to the point of bulldozing** — smooth-talking, cajoling, or just sheer refusing to back down ([source]()). And when he wanted the spotlight, the rest of them usually relented, if only because the bastard could pull it off in front of an audience.

So yes: *Fireside Confession* is the rare Gilley-fronted set piece — written, sung, and staged to showcase his charisma, with Kathy relegated to a shimmer of harmony while he basked in his own storytelling.

> Keep it in mind that  Gilley only seems dimwitted as an act. His laziness is in comparison to the other five workaholics, all of whom Gilley is familiar to their flaws that he seamlessly manages to exploit.  He works very hard so that he can be lazy.


Yes — exactly. That’s the secret of Gilley, isn’t it? The surface-level clown, the overgrown kid with Coke-bottle glasses who leans back like nothing matters — but underneath, he’s a **studied manipulator of tempo, attention, and human weakness**.

It’s not dimwittedness. It’s theater. Gilley performs laziness so the others will underestimate him, so Jeff thinks he’s the unserious one, so Jim rolls his eyes, so Kathy snarls — and then, when the moment comes, he **slips in the move that gets him what he wanted all along.** It’s the same with women, with producers, with fans. He reads flaws like scripture and pushes buttons like he’s playing the organ.

And you’re right — he works hard at it. That kind of “effortless” charisma costs hours of thought. He watches, listens, catalogues every tic of his friends: Jeff’s obsessive precision, Jeremy’s insecurity about his baldness, Jim’s volatility, Kathy’s fury at being sidelined, Bill’s perfectionist need to arrange. Then he sets little traps — not cruel, but designed — so that when he insists “I’m singing this one,” he knows exactly what arguments will fall flat and which ones will collapse under their own weight.

*Fireside Confession* is a perfect case study. Gilley knew:
- Jeff wouldn’t fight too hard if the song was “dumb fun” — Jeff could keep his gravitas elsewhere.
- Kathy would bristle, but if she had a harmony part and the song clearly didn’t cut into her signature space, she’d begrudgingly let him have it.
- Bill would play along if it meant layered harmonies and catchy riffs.
- Jim liked anything rowdy and chantable.
- Jeremy just wanted to prove himself on guitar, so he didn’t care who sang as long as he got the riffs.

So Gilley carved out five minutes in the setlist where it was **all him, all spotlight**, under the guise of “it’ll be easy for the crowd to sing along.” Lazy on the face of it — but underneath, a perfectly engineered machine to guarantee him ownership of a song.
