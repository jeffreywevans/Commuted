# Sarah's Senior Year at NYU

- **Early context**: Sarah in her last months at NYU. We see her alien among peers—where they’re drunk or chasing internships at Condé Nast, she’s building weird frequency maps on a janky Macintosh SE/30. She’s obsessed with patterns in how audiences respond to sound. She can’t shut her brain off.

- **First arena of conflict**: Make her go up against a professor, an internship boss, or a smug industry exec who doesn’t “get” her data-driven vision of music scouting. He calls her “too academic.” She bites back with a proto-version of the speech she’ll later give about Commuted: this isn’t about preference, it’s about *voltage*.

- **Personal detail**: Give her something sensual but understated—a relationship or fling that reveals how she compartmentalizes. Maybe she ends a hookup early to jot notes on how people react to live music. She’s ruthless with herself, but also young, not yet armoured in blazers and precision.

- **The test**: She lands a last-minute chance to pitch herself to Island Records A&R. Frame it like a hostile job interview: gray suits, dismissive laughter, someone skimming her résumé too quickly. She survives it not by humility but by throwing down something undeniable—her model, her theory, or just her verbal fire.

- **The payoff**: Instead of “thank you, we’ll be in touch,” the room goes silent. Then: *“Congratulations, you’re hired.”* No explanation, no hand-holding, just the sudden snap into a new life.


That ending, if you set it up right, doesn’t just feel like a career break. It feels like a supervillain origin reveal—the moment Sarah becomes Sarah Malcolm, the strategist who will eventually gamble her job on a band from Ohio.
