Bill Davenport peered through his sunglasses at the blazing Atlanta sun. It was July 1996, and the Olympic Games had lit the city with a hum of energy that matched his mood. He and Jeremy Evans were both relieved to be free for an afternoon—no long studio hours, no band drama, no baggage. Well, almost no baggage. Jeremy had just weathered a disastrous divorce two years ago, and Bill was determined to help his friend feel human again.

They’d snagged seats in the upper stands of the soccer stadium, waiting for a qualifying match to begin. The distinct hum of different languages mingled in the hot summer air. Fans from around the world waved flags and chattered excitedly. Bill grinned, passing Jeremy a tall soda.

“Atlanta’s wild this summer,” he said, taking a sip of his own drink. “How’d we get roped into soccer, anyway?”

Jeremy shrugged. “Less crowded than gymnastics or track and field. And hey, it’s not like we had time to buy tickets to the Dream Team basketball games.”

“True enough.” Bill stretched out, feeling the warm bleachers under his arms. “So… how’s life in your apartment?” He tried to keep his tone light, recalling how Jeremy’s ex-wife had left him in a tangle of resentment and heartbreak.

Jeremy exhaled, pushing that memory aside. “Quiet. Surprisingly quiet. I’m still adjusting to the hush when I walk in the door. But you know, I’m glad you’re here. Doing normal stuff like this—it helps.”

Bill clapped his shoulder. “Been meaning to hang out more. You’ve been… well, not yourself.”

“Yeah,” Jeremy admitted, then forced a smile. “But it’s good to be out in the world again. Even if it’s a hundred degrees in the shade.”

They both laughed, then turned to watch some players jogging across the field. The warm-up was already entertaining—bright jerseys glinting in the sun, flags snapping in the breeze. Bill and Jeremy basked in the moment, the simplest of pleasures: good company and the promise of a thrilling match.

Suddenly, Jeremy’s phone buzzed in his pocket. He fished it out, skimmed the message, and shook his head in disbelief.

“What's up?” Bill asked.

Jeremy held the phone so Bill could see the text. It was from Kathy Espich— bandmate, longtime friend, once so laid-back and tomboyish she’d hardly touched lipstick. Now her messages read like a page ripped from a fashion magazine. She was apparently in Atlanta for a few days, too.

“Read that,” Jeremy laughed. “She wants us to meet her at some fancy boutique after the game. Says she found ‘the perfect runway attire for the next Commuted photo shoot.’ Runway attire. Kathy. The same girl who used to wear flannel and Doc Martens year-round.”

Bill raised an eyebrow. “I heard she’d gone all vamp and fashion plate. Guess she’s doubling down.”

Jeremy pressed his phone to his chest. “She’s the same Kathy, right? Or did aliens replace her?”

They shared a grin. It was surreal. Kathy had always been the raw nerve in the band—sassy, brash, definitely not a glamour queen. She’d cut her hair with a pair of scissors in a hotel bathroom more than once. Now, from the tone of her texts, she was stalking high-end stores in stiletto heels, trying on designs that practically sparkled.

“Think about it, though,” Bill said. “This might be her big reinvention. If she wants to push the band’s look or her own persona, let her. She was always the one unafraid to pivot.”

“Yeah, I just… never pictured her in a slinky black dress with a matching Gucci purse.” Jeremy shook his head, still amused. “But if that’s what she wants, I’m game. I guess plain old Kathy isn’t so plain anymore.”

Bill chuckled, glancing at the field as the teams took their positions. “If you’d told me two years ago she’d be dragging us to designer boutiques and sipping champagne while wearing five-inch heels—”

Jeremy nodded. “I’d have called you insane. That was the girl who refused to comb her hair for photo shoots!”

They laughed heartily. A few fans on the bleachers behind them glanced over, curious at what was so funny. Bill raised a hand in a friendly wave, then turned his attention to the center circle. The referee was about to blow the whistle to start the match.

As the game kicked off, Bill felt a subtle joy: the Olympics in full swing, a hush of excitement in the stands, and his friend by his side, healing from heartbreak. For a moment, they weren’t two men in a globally famous rock band. They were just Bill and Jeremy, buds who’d grown up messing around with guitars and drums in a dingy garage, now watching world-class athletes chase a ball across the grass.

During the second half, Jeremy’s phone buzzed again. A tiny pixelated image this time—a snapshot from Kathy, wearing a sleek purple satin dress that clung to her in ways that made her look absolutely nothing like the sweatshirts-and-boots Kathy of old. Big sunglasses, glossy black pumps, hair done in glamorous waves. If Bill and Jeremy hadn’t known her all those years, they might have mistaken her for a model off the runway.

Jeremy let out a low whistle and tilted the phone for Bill to see. “Okay… so she’s definitely changed. I guess we owe it to her to see what that’s about in person.”

Bill smirked. “You game to go after the match?”

Jeremy hesitated, his expression serious for a moment. “Thanks for not letting me wallow in the divorce. I really appreciate it.”

Bill gave a half-grin, as if to say, “That’s what friends do,” then nodded. “Sure. Let’s see what kind of ‘runway attire’ Ms. Espich is trying to rope us into.”

They rose to cheer for a sudden near-goal, adrenaline rushing through them. The crowd roared its disappointment when the ball went just wide. As the energy of the stadium soared, Bill glanced at Jeremy, who was finally smiling without that hint of sadness in his eyes. Kathy might have turned into a glam vamp these days, but for all her theatrics, she’d probably be thrilled just to see her friends doing better—maybe that new image was her own path to being happy.

“Here’s to new starts,” Bill said under the roar of the crowd, patting Jeremy on the shoulder. The whistle blew, marking halftime, and the two men took in the scene—Olympic fever shining across the stands, bright summer sun casting golden edges on every silhouette. It felt like another chapter was beginning for all of them, a chance to reconnect beyond the heartbreak and transformations. And if that meant braving a designer boutique with vamp-Kathy in a city pulsing with the spirit of the Games, so be it.

“Let’s see if she’s changed as much on the inside as out,” Jeremy said, joking, but with a hint of genuine curiosity.

Bill laughed. “One way to find out, man. One way to find out.”

They high-fived, turned back to watch the second half, and let the electric spirit of the Atlanta Olympics carry them into the next adventure—together, just like old times.

Jeremy Evans paused at the entrance to the Michelin-starred sushi restaurant, the quiet hush of voices inside a stark contrast to the bright Atlanta hustle just outside. Bill Davenport gave him a little nudge—an unspoken reminder that even though the prices were going to be outrageous, Jeremy could foot the bill. After all, millionaire status, even after a brutal divorce, still meant security.

They spotted Kathy Espich at a corner table, perched elegantly on the plush banquette. She’d slipped out of her towering heels, and now her bare feet rested on the soft tatami mat. At her side sat a glossy, black binder—thick as a dictionary and firmly closed. The moment they approached, Kathy stood (barefoot though she was) and leaned in to greet them with a brilliant smile.

“Welcome to heaven on earth, gentlemen,” she said, gesturing around. The subtle hush of the refined dining room, the glint of gold-leaf décor, and the discreet staff whisking away empty plates gave the place an otherworldly sheen.

Jeremy tried not to grimace as he flipped through the menu. A California roll for one hundred and twenty dollars. He glanced at Bill, who offered a roguish grin. If Bill was fazed by the cost, he didn’t show it. Kathy, well… it was clear she was right at home.

They settled down, cross-legged. Kathy’s toes flexed against the tatami as she kicked her shoes a bit farther away. She motioned for the nearest server and discreetly ordered a round of chilled sake. Then, with mischief in her eyes, she patted the glossy binder.

“I’ve been working on something,” she said in a low voice, “and I want you both in on it. So… let’s order first, then I’ll share.” She drummed her fingers on the binder’s cover, black nail polish glinting under the subdued lights.

Bill studied the menu, eyebrows rising at the chef’s tasting set that cost as much as a decent used car. He cleared his throat. “Right. Maybe…we’ll split a few signature rolls? I trust you, Kathy.”

Jeremy gave a half chuckle, half sigh. “Sure,” he said. “At these prices, we’d better trust her.”

Kathy flashed them a grin. “Good call. You guys handle the fish. I’m going for the omakase. The chef can just do his thing.” She set her menu aside, then placed her hand on the binder. “Now, about this.”

Bill gave her a curious look. “Something to do with the next Commuted press shoot?”

Kathy nodded, eyes agleam. “And more. I’ve got color swatches, mock-up sketches, even a list of designers who are begging to style us. All top-tier, fancy stuff. We are going to look—” She paused, letting the silence hang with dramatic flair. “Let’s just say it’s a far cry from flannel shirts and ratty jeans.”

Jeremy smirked. “Yeah, I still can’t picture you in five-inch heels, though I see evidence to the contrary.” He flicked his gaze toward her discarded shoes.

She leaned forward, letting a wave of her newly glossy, styled hair slip over her shoulder. “I’m telling you, the transformation is real. And if we’re stepping into a new era, let’s do it with the best the fashion world has to offer.”

The server glided over, poured sake into petite ceramic cups, and whisked away to place their order. Kathy lifted her glass. “To reinvention,” she said, waiting for them to join her.

Bill and Jeremy clinked cups with her. “To reinvention.”

The first sip was clean and smooth, cutting the edge of tension at the table. Jeremy wondered when exactly Kathy’s pivot had happened—somewhere between dog-eared grunge and sudden haute couture. But the sake was good, the atmosphere was luxurious, and, well, it was good to see her bright-eyed and confident.

Kathy finally flipped open the massive binder. Inside were meticulously arranged pages of glossy prints, design mock-ups, and pinned swatches of decadent fabrics—satins, velvets, elaborate prints. Bill let out a low whistle at the artistry, and Jeremy’s eyebrows rose at the sight of a few sketches that resembled runway pieces rather than rock-band attire.

“You weren’t kidding,” Bill said, tapping a photo of some avant-garde jacket with spiked shoulders. “This is wild.”

Kathy leaned in, practically thrumming with excitement. “We can ramp up the edge, go full vamp, or keep it classy—whatever we want. But the key is: no more half-measures. We can’t keep dressing like it’s still 1992. The press tours, the interviews… let’s wow them.”

Jeremy nodded slowly, taking it in. “You do realize the fans might be a bit shocked, right?”

She shrugged. “They’ll come around, or they won’t. But I’d rather push boundaries than stay stuck. I did it for myself; why not for all of us, too?”

Bill raised his sake cup. “Hear, hear.” A slight grin curved his lips as he turned to Jeremy. “Better to wow them than let them think we’ve got nothing new to say.”

The server returned, depositing porcelain plates of artfully arranged sashimi and the shimmering hundred-twenty-dollar California roll. Jeremy eyed the garnish of gold flakes on the rice—excess, sure, but it fit the mood.

“Hope it’s worth the price,” Jeremy murmured, lifting a piece with his chopsticks.

Kathy smirked. “Indulgence, my friend. It’s part of the package.”

They dug in, flavors bursting bright on their tongues. Bill closed his eyes, savoring a bit of uni nigiri. Jeremy took a breath, decided that for once, he wouldn’t sweat the cost or the transformation. Tonight, he had enough. More than enough.

And across the table, Kathy was in her element: shining, confident, binder brimming with ideas. She was already flipping to another page, this time something with a fierce, modern cut that actually had Bill nodding approval.

They might have started as a bunch of kids in flannel, but here, in a hushed, candlelit Michelin-star sushi haven, with ephemeral sake and a grand binder of future visions, it felt right to be a little over the top. They would figure out how to present their new look to the world—just like they always did: together.

Jeremy raised his glass again, feeling a subtle grin tug at the corner of his mouth. “To the next phase,” he said softly, and the three clinked their sake cups under the glow of the paper lanterns.

In that moment, it didn’t matter how extravagantly expensive the meal was, or how shocking Kathy’s style pivot might be. All that mattered was that they were there, alive and well in a city buzzing with Olympic energy, forging the next chapter of Commuted—and doing it on their own terms.

Kathy’s hand froze on the binder’s glossy pages. The flickering candlelight danced across the black bunny logo—its iconic silhouette partially hidden behind a vellum overlay. She’d passed over it quickly, but Bill had caught her movement.

She sighed softly, still barefoot in the elegant sushi restaurant, idly flexing her toes beneath the table. Across from her, Bill placed his chopsticks aside. Jeremy watched them both, brow arched in curiosity.

“I saw you skip that Playboy tab and pages,” Bill remarked gently, gesturing to the binder. “What’s that about?”

Kathy’s lips twisted in an uncertain half-smile. “It’s… not quite part of the fashion plan. More of a personal side project.” She tapped the edge of the bunny logo, the corner of her mouth betraying a nervous twitch. “I’m, uh… not sure I want to bring that out into the open. Not yet.”

Jeremy, halfway through a piece of sashimi, let out a short laugh. “C’mon, man. Quit messing. You wouldn’t pose for them. Right?”

Kathy met his eyes, then dropped her gaze, pushing a stray lock of hair behind her ear. She didn’t look amused. “I’m serious. I did a…a test shoot in Bora Bora last month. It’s not locked down or anything, but they might run it in a special spread.” She swallowed. “It’s glamorous, tasteful, but definitely Playboy.”

A flicker of something—anxiety or pride—crossed her face. Jeremy, who’d been about to scoff again, paused. “Wait. You’re not pulling my leg?”

She shook her head. “No jokes. It was a legit test shoot.” She lifted a piece of sashimi to her lips, but seemed too distracted to actually eat. “I’m just… I’m not sure how to go public with it, especially with all the new direction we’re taking. The band, the style… I don’t want fans thinking I’m jumping off the deep end.”

Bill leaned forward, voice calm and measured. “But you must have thought this through. You obviously flew all the way to Bora Bora. That’s not just a whim.”

“Exactly,” Jeremy added, crossing his arms. “So… what gives?”

Kathy exhaled, setting down her uneaten sashimi. “I wanted to do something… iconic, I guess. Something edgy and powerful on my own terms. I didn’t realize until I was actually on the beach, under those lights, that it was bigger than just a photoshoot. It was…vulnerable.”

Bill nodded slowly. “So it’s the vulnerability that has you hesitating now?”

“Among other things,” Kathy admitted. She thumbed the corner of the bunny logo page as if deciding whether to reveal it. “Jeff’s not around to weigh in—he’s over in England. Jim’s off the grid on his own stuff. It’s just you guys here. I don’t know how it’ll land if we suddenly announce, ‘Hey, Kathy’s in Playboy. Also, check out our new style for the next album cycle.’”

She tried to laugh it off, but her eyes glinted with concern. “Feels like I’m piling on a midlife crisis or something. I just wanted an artistic challenge. Now I’m worried it’s too… sensational.”

Jeremy set his chopsticks down and raked a hand over his bald head, a habit he’d had since he was a teenager. “Honestly, I bet the last woman who can pull off a tasteful Playboy thing, it’d be you. Hell, you always had that raw confidence.  But, damn."

Kathy let out a breath that was halfway relieved. “Thanks,” she murmured.

“But,” Bill interjected, softly, “the question is—what are you not telling us? There’s clearly something else on your mind.”

Kathy’s eyes flicked from Bill to Jeremy and back again. The hush of the Michelin-starred room swirled around them, the gentle clink of porcelain and whispered conversations. “I… might have already heard from the magazine’s staff. They love the shots. They asked if I’d do a follow-up, maybe an interview or—” She shrugged, clearly nervous about how it would sound. “A cover.”

“A cover?” Jeremy repeated, eyebrows jumping. “Of Playboy?”

She nodded. “And I’d have full creative control. Which is huge. I could tie it in with a new band aesthetic. Or highlight some personal cause. But it’s big. And I know how the public can be. Fans can be weird about this stuff. Critics will say I’m overselling sex or overshadowing the music. I’m torn.”

Bill lifted his glass of sake in a small salute. “Kathy, it’s bold. But if it’s truly what you want—”

“I think it is. Or at least, I want the option. I never thought I’d see that side of me, you know? The vamp, the runway star, the potential Playboy cover girl. Yet here we are.”

Jeremy blew out a breath, seeming to process the thought of teenage-Kathy in flannel morphing into polished, fearless Kathy who might grace a magazine cover. “Look, we’re your friends. We want you to do what’s good for you. If it’s weird for the band PR, we’ll figure out how to manage it. Hell, maybe we even spin it to our advantage.”

“Exactly,” Bill agreed. “I just don’t want you going through all this alone—especially if you feel torn. You should talk it out with us, with Jeff, with whoever else you trust.”

Kathy stared at the binder, the bunny logo now in plain sight. She let her fingers trace over it. “Thanks,” she said quietly. “I really appreciate the support. Even if I decide to go for it… I want the band behind me. It’d feel lousy doing it if everyone’s bracing for backlash.”

Their conversation paused as a waiter gracefully placed more sushi at the center of the table. A quiet hum of activity flowed through the restaurant, but to the three of them, the world felt narrowed to a hush around Kathy’s revelation.

“Well,” Bill said, clearing his throat. “We’re here, we’re listening. If it’s a matter of ‘how’ and not ‘if,’ you’ve got at least two votes in your corner. Right, Jer?”

Jeremy nodded, picking up another piece of sashimi. “For sure. Look, you’re a grown woman. If it’s on your terms, you do it. Just let us know how we can help.”

Her shoulders relaxed visibly, and a small, genuine smile found its way to her face. “I’m glad I told you guys, seriously.” She sipped her sake, eyes flicking over the binder again. “Maybe once I talk to Jeff and Jim, it’ll feel less like a secret ticking time bomb.”

Bill and Jeremy exchanged glances—nothing about the band’s future was ever boring, that much was certain. And if Kathy was forging a new path—well, it was just another turn in their ever-evolving story.

“Whatever you do,” Bill said, leaning back with a hint of a grin, “we know it’ll be an adventure.”

Kathy exhaled, half laughter, half relief. “That’s for damn sure.” She shut the binder softly. “Alright, let’s tackle the rest of this fancy sushi before the sake makes me spill more secrets.”

Jeremy lifted his glass again, and they toasted—this time not only to reinvention, but to taking risks, owning choices, and forging ahead, no matter how daring it all seemed. And in that dimly lit corner of an Atlanta sushi paradise, the trio felt that old, unbreakable camaraderie—knowing they had each other’s backs in whatever came next.

Kathy fiddled with the edge of the black binder, a ripple of amused disbelief curving her lips. Across from her, Bill and Jeremy both froze, sake cups suspended in midair.

“I guess I forgot to mention Cremeans,” she said with a small shrug, trying not to laugh. “I told him about the Playboy test shoot when I was still feeling it out. He, uh…” She paused, eyes darting between the two men. “He prayed over it.”

Bill’s eyebrows shot up. “You’re telling me that Cremeans—our devout, no-nonsense manager—gave you the green light?”

Kathy lifted a hand. “He did more than that. He got me in contact with the best agent and made sure I’d have total control of the final images if it goes forward. He basically said, ‘Do what you need to do, Kathy. Oh, and if you do it, I’d love a signed copy.’”

For a heartbeat, silence reigned at the table. Jeremy set down his sake so hard it nearly toppled. “What?” he barked, glancing from Bill to Kathy. “I’m sorry, but that’s— What?”

Kathy bit her lip, trying not to giggle at their expressions. “He said he’d appreciate an autograph on the magazine. For his private collection, I guess?”

Bill’s eyes widened in mock horror. “Dude did not tell you to drop your jeans.” He shot Jeremy a glance, as if to verify they were hearing this right.

Jeremy ran a hand over his bald head, still processing. “I mean, I figured Cremeans might… I dunno, oppose it? Worry about the brand or something. But wow.”

Kathy sighed, closing the binder gently. “He’s more progressive than you think. Or he’s just looking for ways to expand the Commuted ‘brand’ in new markets. In any case, he told me it’s my call and that he’s behind me. Figuratively,” she added with a smirk. “God forbid he tries to micromanage that shoot.”

Bill leaned forward, still amazed. “So the one person we assumed might have a meltdown over the moral implications is—basically giving you a thumbs-up?”

Kathy shrugged again, trying to look blasé though amusement tugged at her. “He said he prayed, and this is how God answered, apparently. Who am I to question that?”

Jeremy snorted. “Remind me never to assume anything about Cremeans again.”

They all laughed, tension swirling into bemusement at the bizarre twist: the famously straight-laced manager not only embracing Kathy’s potentially controversial move, but actively helping her navigate it. Bill downed the rest of his sake in one gulp, letting it burn away any leftover shock.

“I guess we should never underestimate the power of prayer,” he deadpanned.

Kathy snickered, tapping the Playboy page in her binder. “Nor the power of a limited-edition signature for a manager who definitely wants to see ‘his star’ in a different light.”

Jeremy coughed, “I’m never looking Cremeans in the eye the same way again.” But he said it with a grin, realizing that maybe—just maybe—this crazy new era for Kathy wasn’t the nightmare scenario he’d worried about.

And for a moment, the three of them sat in a sort of incredulous camaraderie, wondering what else could possibly happen next in the ever-unpredictable life of Commuted.

Kathy narrowed her eyes as she flipped the binder shut once again, her mouth set in a line. “Well,” she said dryly, “seems like the prude back in Dayton is giving me more credit for my so-called ‘artistic choices’ than you two bozos are.”

At the word “bozos,” Jeremy winced as though he’d been physically jabbed. “Hey, c’mon—” he started, but Kathy waved a hand, cutting him off.

“What?” she snapped, voice low. “Is it so hard to accept that maybe Cremeans respects my vision enough not to make jokes about me ‘dropping my jeans’ or—” She threw Jeremy a withering look, “or basically laughing me out of the room?”

Jeremy’s cheeks colored. “I’m not laughing at you,” he insisted, though he couldn’t quite meet her eyes. “I—I was just shocked. It’s… big news, that’s all.”

Beside him, Bill Davenport straightened, posture rigid. “Wait,” he said carefully, “are you accusing us of disrespecting you? Because I think we’ve been on your side, Kathy.”

Her foot, still bare under the table, tapped against the tatami. She looked ready to spit fire. “I said the prude from Dayton’s showing me more respect. He’s the last person I expected to be so supportive. Meanwhile, you two…” She trailed off, sending out a seething glare that felt as sharp as broken glass. “You keep defaulting to jokes, or acting like I’m nuts for wanting this.”

Jeremy opened his mouth to respond but closed it again—he saw how her shoulders tensed, how her knuckles whitened around the sake glass. Kathy was sending out bad vibes all right, and the smoky undercurrent of frustration practically filled the air around them.

Bill cleared his throat. “We’re not mocking you,” he said firmly, controlling his voice. “We might be surprised, but we respect your decisions. So… what did we do wrong this time? Because from where I’m sitting, we’ve been supportive.”

Kathy shot him a withering look. “It’s the way you say it. The constant cracks about dropping jeans, the wide-eyed shock. Even if it’s a joke, it feels like you’re—” She paused, wrestling with the right words. “—not taking me seriously. This is huge for me. A real artistic pivot. I can’t have my closest friends acting like it’s just a punchline.”

A beat of silence passed, the quiet sushi restaurant still humming around them with the gentle clink of plates and hushed conversation. Jeremy exhaled, turning the sake cup between his fingers. “You’re right,” he admitted, his tone subdued. “I guess we’re used to seeing you as… you know, the old version of Kathy. We kinda forgot how big a deal it might be for you, stepping into the spotlight like this in such a different way.”

Kathy’s stare softened, just a fraction.

Bill nodded. “So, sorry if our shock turned into wisecracks. We do take you seriously. You want to do Playboy or anything else, we’ll back you up. Just… let us catch up to how fast you’re changing.”

She pressed her lips together, then let out a slow breath, tension draining from her shoulders. “All right,” she said quietly, “just… next time, try not to make me feel like the punchline. Fair?”

“Fair,” Bill said, offering a small, placating smile. “We’ll do better.”

Jeremy nodded too. “Definitely,” he murmured. “We’re on your side, Kathy. Always.”

Some of the storm passed from her expression. She lifted her sake glass and took a calm sip. “Thanks,” she said, looking between the two. “I appreciate that.”

And the unsettled energy between them eased, replaced by a fragile but genuine understanding—enough to get them through the next round of sake and the next chapter of whatever reinvention Kathy had in store.

Bill let out a low whistle and leaned back. “Jeff’s gonna be pissed,” he muttered, still processing the idea of Kathy posing for Playboy—especially without Jeff having any input.

Kathy shot him a sharp look over the rim of her sake cup. “Well, that’s tough for him,” she said bluntly. “He can waltz into a bookstore, drop five bucks, and buy the issue if he’s so curious. I’m not losing sleep over your dear brother right now.”

Jeremy gave a faint wince at her dismissive tone. He knew how much Jeff loved his creative control—and how easily he might blow a gasket if he felt cut out of big decisions. “I get it,” Jeremy said quietly, “but he’s still part of the band. Kinda a major part.”

“Sure, in London. Or Manchester. Or wherever the hell he’s sorting out the business with that side project,” she snapped, impatience edging into her voice. “Look, it’s my body, my shoot, and my career move. If Jeff can’t handle it, that’s on him. Let him buy a copy like everyone else.”

A beat of tense silence passed. Jeremy opened his mouth to protest, then closed it again. Bill cast a wary glance from Kathy to Jeremy, gauging how close they were to a real argument.

“Jeff’s your biggest cheerleader when it comes to your voice, your style,” Bill ventured, choosing his words carefully. “It’s just… you know how he is. He’ll think we all kept him out of the loop.”

Kathy’s shoulders rolled in a slow shrug. “He had his chance to be here—he’s the one who took off to England.” She drummed her nails against the table. “Besides, this is personal. I don’t need all of Commuted signing off on a decision about my body. If I do the shoot, it doesn’t automatically become a band problem. Not unless we make it one.”

Her gaze flicked to Jeremy, daring him to disagree. But he just exhaled, resting his elbows on the table, half in surrender. “Hey, I’m not saying you owe him anything. Just… be ready for his reaction, okay?”

“I am,” Kathy replied, softer now. “He’ll have to deal with it, just like you two.”

She glanced at Bill and Jeremy again, eyes steely but less confrontational. “I appreciate you hearing me out tonight. But I can’t keep tiptoeing around Jeff’s potential meltdown. If he’s mad, he’s mad.”

Bill nodded slowly. “We’ll handle him if it comes to that,” he offered, an olive branch in his voice.

Jeremy held her stare, then gave a small, reluctant nod. “All right. I just hope it doesn’t blow up bigger than it needs to.”

Kathy’s mouth curved into a thin smile. “That part’s out of my hands,” she said, draining the last of her sake. “But trust me, five bucks at the bookstore won’t kill him.”

And with that, she signaled the waiter for another round, determined to carry on her plan with or without the absent bassist’s blessing—leaving Bill and Jeremy to brace themselves for whatever fiery reaction Jeff might bring back stateside.

Jeremy leaned forward, the flush of too much sake coloring his cheeks. “So,” he slurred a bit, pointing at the binder on the table, “riddle me this…why aren’t you doing this—this Playboy thing—under the aegis of the band? I mean—” he hiccupped lightly, “—we’ve always packaged everything as Commuted, right? Why not keep it all in-house?”

Kathy sat back, surprise flickering across her face. “You saying I should plaster the Commuted logo on my Playboy spread?” She half-laughed, half-scoffed. “I’m not sure that’d go over well, Jeremy. This is my personal project.”

“But,” Jeremy persisted, tapping the table for emphasis, “we have the brand name. Name recognition. People would eat it up. We’d tie it in, like…like a cross-promo. The band’s next era. Y’know?” He threw a quick, awkward glance at Bill, looking for backup.

Bill grimaced and shrugged, unwilling to jump into the fray. He sipped his water instead, well aware Jeremy was teetering on the edge of tipsy logic.

Kathy exhaled, crossing her arms over her chest. “Look, Commuted is a rock band. This shoot—” She paused, searching for the right words. “It’s me stepping out of that role for a minute. Doing something that’s entirely about how I want to present myself. The second I stamp the Commuted name on it, it becomes a band agenda. PR reps, album synergy, blah, blah, blah. And then I lose control.”

Jeremy blinked, trying to process her reasoning. “But we’ve always done everything together,” he mumbled.
