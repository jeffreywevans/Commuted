## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – CINCINNATI – TUESDAY AFTERNOON – LATE SUMMER 2027**

Golden light slants through the cracked blinds, dust motes hanging like cigarette ghosts. The air is a swamp of old coffee, tangled cables, and lamb grease from the kebabs Kendall dragged in from some hole-in-the-wall joint across the street. The studio is cluttered but holy: racks of synths like Cold War control panels, a Rhodes piano sagging under stacks of marked-up sheet music, a couch with stuffing peeking through cigarette burns.

Kendall sits on that couch, kebab stick in hand, chewing slow like an executioner. Sauce stains her wrist, glistening. She doesn’t wipe it away. She savors it.

The door *slams*.

**AVRIL** storms in. She doesn’t glide or enter. She *erupts*. Leather jacket like armor, black boots hammering the floorboards, sunglasses still on as though she’s hiding from a sun only she can see.

**AVRIL** (furious, breathless)
They fucking sued me. Again.

Kendall doesn’t move. Just tilts her head, still chewing, eyes calm. A scientist watching a volcano form.

**KENDALL**
How awful is it this time?

Avril rips the sunglasses off, hurls them onto the Rhodes. They skid, nearly topple, saved by the grit of dried dust. She’s pacing like a caged wolf, voice cracking with rage.

**AVRIL**
All I did—*all I did*—was transfer the goddamn domain name. My old legacy site. Dead thing. Nobody used it. I moved it to the one Tim Wright likes, made it cleaner, cheaper, smarter. His LLC handles the backend, saves me money. I thought—cut costs, honor a small request, *be efficient*. And now? Federal fucking case.

She stops, jabs a finger at the ceiling like God Himself is the plaintiff.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
They’re treating it like I broke into Fort Knox and pissed on the Constitution.

She throws her arms wide, jacket creaking like old leather armor.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
For a *domain name*!

The word hangs in the air, ridiculous and devastating at once.

Kendall licks tzatziki off her thumb, smirks.

**KENDALL**
You sleeping with one of these lawyers at least? Get the friends-and-family discount?

Avril freezes mid-stride, whip-cracks her glare onto Kendall. Sunglasses off, her eyes burn—green, venomous, exhausted. She doesn’t rise to the joke. She *leaps over it*.

**AVRIL**
No. No, no, no. You know what? Shakespeare was right. *The first thing we do, let’s kill all the lawyers.*

Her voice booms in the studio, echo ricocheting off steel and wood. It’s half-bellow, half-incantation. She spits the line like scripture she finally understands.

Bill’s synth wall hums faintly, as if the circuits are vibrating with her fury.

Kendall sets down the kebab stick, finally meeting her at eye level. Calm, steady.

**KENDALL**
Welcome to the family, Av. You wanted Commuted? This is what it costs. Blood. Sweat. And apparently subpoenas.

Avril collapses onto the couch beside her, leather creaking, shoulders trembling under fury and fatigue. She buries her face in her hands. The kebab wrapper crinkles under her thigh, greasy and loud.

**AVRIL (muffled, raw)**
Goddamn leech fuckers.

Silence. Only the hum of the amps, the faint hiss of dust settling in sunlight.

Then Avril lifts her head. Her mascara smudged, her hair sticking in damp strands. She looks less like a superstar and more like a soldier left bleeding in the mud after a battle she didn’t start.

**AVRIL**
I didn’t come here for this. I came here for fucking *music*.

Her voice cracks on *music*. The word is a plea, a scream, a prayer.

Kendall watches her with the steady patience of someone who’s seen too many wars—political, musical, legal—and knows the battlefield is always papered with contracts. She picks at the last shred of meat on the skewer, sucks the salt and fat.

**KENDALL**
Music doesn’t save you from the machinery, Av. It just makes the machinery hungrier.

**AVRIL** (snarling)
Then fuck the machine. Let it starve.

The door opens again, creaking like it hasn’t been oiled in years. **BILL DAVENPORT** steps in, sleeves rolled, carrying a half-drained Gatorade. He takes in the sight: Kendall with her kebabs, Avril half-collapsed on the couch, rage crackling in the air like live wire.

**BILL**
…what did I miss?

**KENDALL**
Av got sued for existing. Again.

Bill doesn’t laugh. He just mutters something under his breath about the inevitability of entropy, sets the Gatorade down, and starts fiddling with the synth rack.

**AVRIL** (erupts)
Don’t *shrug* at me, Bill! Do you know what it feels like to be sued for doing the *right* thing? To try to make something easier, cheaper, cleaner—just to be hammered into the dirt because some asshole wants to prove they still own me?

Bill doesn’t look up.

**BILL**
Yeah. I know exactly what it feels like.

That shuts her up. For a second.

Avril presses her fists into her thighs, hard enough to leave marks.

**AVRIL**
I swear to God, this world doesn’t want me free. They don’t want me in Commuted. They want me branded, barcoded, shoved back into some pop princess box I outgrew fifteen years ago.

Kendall leans forward, low, voice sharp enough to cut.

**KENDALL**
Then burn the box.

Avril’s eyes flash. She looks at Kendall like she’s been handed a weapon.

**AVRIL**
I could. I fucking could.

Her grin is savage now. The lawsuit hasn’t vanished, but the anger has transmuted—alchemy from despair to violence, from fatigue to fight.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
If they want war, they picked the wrong bitch to corner.

---

The kebab grease stains the console. The lawsuit papers wait in some New York office, fat with signatures and threats. But in that golden, dust-thick studio, something else lives: a girl from Napanee, furious and small and unstoppable, ready to scream the lawyers into silence with the only thing that has ever been hers—her voice.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – CINCINNATI – LATE SUMMER 2027 – TUESDAY AFTERNOON**

The kebab wrappers are wilted now, grease soaking into console paper. Avril still vibrates with rage, perched on the couch like a coiled spring. Kendall leans back, arms crossed, skewers like cigarette ends between her fingers. Bill has retreated into his modular rack, patch cables slung like veins.

The door bangs open again.

**JEREMY EVANS** lopes in, wiry, bald, dressed in his perpetual flannel-with-sleeves-rolled. He’s got a sweat sheen—he’s been running, as always. He catches the tail end of Avril’s threat.

**JEREMY** (flat, dry)
You’re Canadian. You can’t burn your Constitution. You don’t have a written one.

Behind him, **JEFF EVANS** shuffles in, carrying a bass case like it’s an anvil. He doesn’t look up from the floor, just drops the rebuttal as if compelled.

**JEFF** (muttering, professorial)
That’s a misconception. Canada does have a written constitution. The Constitution Act, 1867, and the Charter of Rights and Freedoms, 1982, among other statutes and conventions—

He pauses, sees the faces around the room glazing over, realizes his mistake. His glasses slide a little down his nose.

**JEFF (cont’d)**
Never mind. If I explain, it’ll bore everyone. I’ll stop.

There’s a beat.

Then, in perfect unison, *all three—Avril, Kendall, Bill*—say it:

**ALL**
Thank you.

Jeff nods once. Accepted. He sets down his bass and goes back to checking cables, as though silence is the natural end of any sentence.

Avril takes a breath. Her anger twists into a new shape: mischief. Her lips curl, and suddenly she detonates her Tsar Bomba.

**AVRIL**
Actually… I’m studying for American citizenship.

The room *erupts*.

Kendall chokes on air, coughing into her hand like she swallowed a chili pepper. Bill’s head jerks up from the synth rack, eyes wide like a cat hearing thunder. Jeremy freezes mid-step, then barks out a disbelieving laugh. Even Jeff looks up, which for Jeff is basically a scream.

**JEREMY**
Say *what*?

**BILL** (half-laugh, half-groan)
Oh, Jesus Christ.

**KENDALL** (leaning forward, incredulous)
You? An American? You hate half the states and mock the other half in interviews!

**AVRIL** (grinning, defiant)
Yeah. And I’ve got the study guide. Civics questions. Preamble. Federalist Papers. All of it. I’ve been sneaking flashcards between rehearsals.

**JEREMY**
That’s like a dog deciding it wants to be a cat.

**JEFF** (murmuring, sharp)
Or maybe it’s like a wolf deciding it wants to live in the city.

Everyone turns to look at Jeff.

**JEFF** (quiet, matter-of-fact)
You want the passport, Av, you’re saying you want to belong here. In this mess. With us. With the lawsuits, the contracts, the—noise.

**AVRIL**
Exactly.

Silence swells. The dust motes hang in amber light. It’s not just a joke anymore—it’s a vow.

Avril sits straighter. The rage that dragged her in has cracked open, and underneath it burns something harder, cleaner: resolve.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
I’ve been Canadian my whole damn life. But Commuted isn’t Canadian. It’s American. It’s Ohio. It’s lawsuits and sweat and stadiums and all-night diners. If I’m gonna bleed for this band, I might as well take the oath.

Kendall whistles low, half-impressed, half-terrified.

**KENDALL**
You’re gonna have to swear to defend the Constitution you just threatened to burn.

**AVRIL** (smiling wolfishly)
Yeah. And I’ll mean every word.

---

The kebabs are gone. The lawsuits still circle like vultures. But in this moment, Avril Lavigne doesn’t look like the pop-punk princess dragged into another band’s mythology. She looks like a soldier crossing the border by choice, ready to plant her flag in Commuted’s war-torn soil.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – CINCINNATI – LATE SUMMER 2027 – TUESDAY AFTERNOON**

The bomb still hangs in the room: Avril, studying for American citizenship. Everyone’s eyes are on her, the air charged, kebab grease and disbelief mingling into something combustible.

Kendall leans forward, skewers dangling between her fingers like cigarettes, eyes narrowed—not buying it.

**KENDALL**
What’s the *real* reason, Av?

Avril stiffens.

**KENDALL (cont’d)**
I saw your testimony on Parliament Hill. The hearings. Your whole song and dance about therapeutic use. This isn’t about stars and stripes. It’s about MDMA access.

Bill’s head jerks up, caught between shock and a smirk. Jeremy raises his brows, impressed. Jeff just exhales through his nose like he’s been expecting this reveal.

Avril glares, defensive, a flush creeping up her neck.

**AVRIL** (flat, sharp)
It’s not about taxes.

**KENDALL**
I didn’t say it was.

**AVRIL**
Yeah, well, Ohio’s taxes, the IRS—they hammer me just like they hammer anyone stupid enough to get famous here. I can live with that. But don’t pretend this is about me running from a tax bill.

(beat—her voice drops, lower, raw)
It’s about medicine. About my right to not have to fly across the goddamn border every time I want treatment. Canada drags its feet. The U.S.—*your* U.S.—is moving faster.

She stops, realizing she just admitted it out loud.

Silence.

Even the amps seem to hum softer.

Kendall watches her carefully, like she’s peeling away the last protective layer.

**KENDALL**
You didn’t address the elephant in the room.

Avril clenches her fists, jaw tight. Then she finally exhales.

**AVRIL**
I want to belong to the place that doesn’t make me beg for my own sanity. If that means pledging allegiance, swearing the oath, paying more taxes than I should—fine. I’ll do it. I’m not fucking ashamed.

Her eyes glitter with something fiercer than rage now: clarity.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
This isn’t about a green card or a goddamn passport. It’s about *survival.*

---

Jeremy lets out a low whistle, muttering something about how Avril just declared war on both countries in one sentence. Bill mutters, *“Well, shit,”* and starts twisting knobs like he can process her confession through sound. Jeff, for once, doesn’t correct a single fact.

Avril just sits there, small and volcanic, daring anyone to argue.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – CINCINNATI – LATE SUMMER 2027 – TUESDAY AFTERNOON**

The studio feels smaller now, like the walls are inching inward with every word Avril spits. She’s perched on the couch, hair falling in messy strands around her face, cheeks blotched with fury. Kendall leans back with arms crossed, the kebab skewers finally abandoned. Bill sits rigid by the synth rack, pretending to adjust knobs but really just trying to breathe through the tension. Jeff leans against his amp stack, inscrutable. Jeremy lurks by the door, arms folded, watching like he’s at a prize fight.

Avril’s voice cracks open again, sharper, darker.

**AVRIL** (angry, gutted)
You want the truth? Canada *disdains* me. All those smug fuckers on Parliament Hill with their speeches about our “superior health care system.” Superior my ass. I begged. I testified. I laid my goddamn soul bare about what happened to me in New York. 2024 chewed me alive, and I stood there asking for help. And what did I get? Shrugs. Delays. A pat on the head and a “we’ll study it further.”

Her voice breaks, but she pushes through, fists pressing into her thighs.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
After what happened to me in New York, it’s betrayal. *Betrayal of my core identity.* They looked at my scars, my wreckage, and told me to wait my turn in line like I was asking for a fucking nose job.

She pulls a crumpled pack of gum from her jacket pocket, tosses it onto the console like evidence. One little foil square half-slid out.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
But one thing stays the nightmares. One thing keeps me from waking up screaming. A tab of MDMA. That’s it. That’s the medicine. Not their antidepressants that make me feel embalmed. Not their platitudes about counseling waitlists. Just that. One pressed pill. And my own country won’t fucking give it to me.

The words shiver through the air like electricity. Kendall doesn’t move. Bill stops fidgeting. Even Jeff looks up.

The door bangs again, and **JIM BROWN** barrels in, hoodie halfway unzipped, drumsticks jammed in his back pocket like spare weapons. He senses the heat instantly, grins crooked.

**JIM**
(trying to cut through)
Hey, hey. Don’t sweat it. Being an American’s fun. Free refills, loud parades, fireworks every goddamn week if you know the right county. You’ll get used to it.

Avril turns on him, eyes wet, voice breaking into a jagged edge.

**AVRIL**
Fun? FUN? Jim, I’m *heartbroken*. You think I wanted this? You think I wanted to torch the one thing that shaped me—my very Canadian sense of fairness—just to get a doctor to look me in the eye?

Her voice drops to a whisper, ragged.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
I grew up believing the system was supposed to catch you when you fall. That fairness wasn’t a myth. And now? Now I’m chasing pills across borders like some goddamn criminal.

Jim’s grin falters. He rubs the back of his neck, uneasy.

Avril looks around, her whole body shaking with exhaustion.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
And with these new goddamn lawsuits—it’s every *fucking* thing against me again. Always again. My label. My country. The law. Even my own goddamn body.

Silence falls. Heavy. The amps hum like distant thunder.

For once, nobody cracks a joke. Not Kendall, not Jim, not even Jeremy.

Avril sits there, hollowed out and furious, clutching the ghost of her country with one hand and the promise of another with the other—neither offering her mercy.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – CINCINNATI – LATE SUMMER 2027 – TUESDAY AFTERNOON**

The kebab wrappers are dead soldiers. The room is soaked in Avril’s fury, the smell of grease and sweat fusing with her confession about MDMA, about betrayal, about her homeland’s cruel delay. Nobody laughs now.

Jim shifts uncomfortably, scratching at his jaw. He’s still got drumsticks poking out of his hoodie pocket, like he’s armed but useless. His voice drops low, gentler than anyone expects.

**JIM**
Av… look, I don’t want to play dad here. But you’re spiraling again. We’ve seen this. You know when we tour, Cremeans always has a psychologist traveling with us. That’s not a coincidence. He keeps one in his back pocket like extra cymbals. Do you—

(beat)
Do you need help?

The room holds its breath. Bill’s hand hovers over the synth knobs. Jeff pauses in mid-adjustment of his strap. Kendall doesn’t even blink.

Avril wipes her eyes roughly with the heel of her palm. The mascara smears, but she doesn’t care. Her voice comes out ragged, defiant.

**AVRIL**
No. I don’t need a shrink. I don’t need another clipboard telling me I’m “processing trauma.”

(beat—suddenly sharp, almost comic in her fury)
I just need to figure out the difference between Madison and Monroe.

Everyone stares.

**JEREMY**
…what?

Avril slumps back into the couch, tugging her jacket tighter, half-laughing through the tears.

**AVRIL**
The civics exam. The goddamn citizenship test. They’re gonna ask me about dead presidents and I can’t keep them straight. Madison. Monroe. Who’s who? One’s short, one’s tall? One wrote papers, one just looked like a paperweight? Fuck if I know.

She lets out a manic laugh, then jabs a finger at the ceiling.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
And Jesus hell, if you’re gonna have two political parties, why name one the *Federalist* and the other the *Anti-Federalist*? What kind of masochist branding is that? You couldn’t think of a different word? You had to just tack *“anti”* on and call it a day? No wonder the whole system’s broken.

Her laugh skids into something harsher, a snarl.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
I just want the bitch, okay? The passport. The papers. I’ll memorize their founding fathers, their wigs, their stupid compromises. Just give me the bitch.

The silence that follows is thick, but not empty. It’s the silence of four Americans watching a Canadian tear herself apart to belong—half admiring, half horrified.

Jim finally sighs, shaking his head.

**JIM**
Only you, Av. Only you could make U.S. civics sound like a bar fight.

Avril smirks through the wreckage of her face, eyes glassy, jaw stubborn.

**AVRIL**
Then let’s fucking brawl.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – CINCINNATI – LATE SUMMER 2027 – TUESDAY AFTERNOON**

The air has shifted. The kebab grease, the lawsuits, the jokes about Federalists—they’re shadows now. What takes over is Jim’s voice, stripped of sarcasm, stripped of rhythm, bare like a drum with the skin peeled off.

**JIM** (flat, deadly serious)
No. I’m not laughing this off. Not this time.

He steps forward, plants himself in the center of the room like he’s about to call a set change. His eyes pin Avril to the couch.

**JIM (cont’d)**
Because we never got Kathy the help she needed. Not really. Not until it was already too late. Half a day into rehab and she was gone. That was our failure.

Avril blinks. Her smirk falters.

**JIM (cont’d)**
And Gilley? We didn’t watch him close enough. We let him grind himself down, night after night, until his body just quit. He worked himself to death under our noses.

Bill looks away. Jeff shifts his weight. The ghosts are here now.

**JIM (cont’d)**
And Jeff—hell, we spent twenty goddamn years calling it “eccentricity” and “temperament.” We didn’t know he was on the spectrum. Didn’t try to understand. Just put up with it. He lived in hell while we looked the other way.

Jeff’s jaw clenches. He doesn’t deny it.

**JIM (cont’d)**
And Jeremy’s drinking? Don’t talk to me about luck. That was dumb luck. Dumb fucking luck he didn’t drink himself into the grave before somebody pulled him out.

Jeremy looks at the floor. Silence.

Jim leans forward, voice cracking but hard as stone.

**JIM (cont’d)**
So no, Avril. We’re not gonna “wait and see” with you. We’re not gonna laugh about civics flashcards while you bleed out in the margins. You can either get the help you need, get the medication you need, or—

(beat, savage)
—or you can take a long walk off a short pier.

The words hit like a brick through glass. Nobody breathes.

Avril stares at him. Tears hover but don’t fall. She’s small in the corner of the couch, leather jacket swallowing her, but her eyes flare back with something volcanic.

**AVRIL** (low, trembling)
You’d throw me out like that? After everything?

Jim doesn’t blink.

**JIM**
I’d rather throw you out alive than bury you with a song on your lips.

The amps hum. Bill sets his hand on the Rhodes to steady himself. Jeff’s face is stone, unreadable, but his hands are shaking at his sides. Kendall doesn’t speak—her whole job is to chronicle, and she knows a moment when the band’s survival is on the knife’s edge.

Avril finally looks down, her knuckles white where they grip her thighs.

**AVRIL**
(quiet, broken)
I just wanted the bitch. The passport. The belonging. The fight.

**JIM**
Then fight smart. Not suicidal.

The silence after is unbearable. A silence so heavy it could crush the room.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – CINCINNATI – LATE SUMMER 2027 – TUESDAY AFTERNOON**

The silence holds, heavy as wet cement. Avril’s chest heaves. Jim’s ultimatum still hangs in the air like smoke from a blown speaker. Everyone waits for her to spit fire back, to snap, to deny. But instead—

She breaks.

Her voice cracks in half.

**AVRIL** (whispered, raw)
I can’t.

The word stuns the room.

**AVRIL (cont’d)** (louder, shaking)
I *can’t*. I’m broke. I’ve cracked. I’ve pushed my body as far as it can go, and past it. I’m at the line. Beyond the line is death and dismemberment.

Her face twists. She looks less like the Av who walked into the studio—leather, fire, fury—and more like someone stripped to the bone. The truth, naked and dangerous.

The four men—Jeff, Jim, Bill, Jeremy—stand frozen. Commuted has seen chaos, has seen overdoses, has seen one another bleed and burn, but *never this*. Never someone in their circle flat-out admitting the breaking point. Kathy never said it before she collapsed. Gilley laughed instead of confessing. Jeff buried it under intellect. Jeremy drowned it in alcohol. Jim spun it into rhythm.

But Avril lays it down like a weapon on the table: *I can’t.*

It shocks them.

Jeremy blinks like he’s been slapped. Bill stares at the floor, hands trembling, cables forgotten. Jeff’s jaw works, but no words come. Jim just breathes hard, shoulders rigid, eyes wide.

They *want* to help. Every cell in their bodies says: pull her back, keep her safe, fix it. But the truth is brutal—none of them can unless she lets them. Unless she actively chooses.

Kendall watches from the couch, skewers abandoned, pen in hand, knowing she is watching the most honest thing Commuted has ever witnessed.

**JEFF** (soft, almost ashamed)
We’ll help. But you’ve gotta ask.

**JEREMY** (quiet, nodding)
Yeah. You have to say it.

**BILL**
We can’t carry you unless you let us.

Finally, Jim again. Voice low, raw, without armor.

**JIM**
We’ll do whatever it takes, Av. But you’ve gotta choose it for sure.

The room is a prayer, the amps humming like a choir, the light turning gold as if it knows the weight of what’s been said.

Avril sits on the couch, head in her hands, breath hitching, the weight of every lawsuit, every nightmare, every pill denied pressing down. She’s cracked open. For once, Commuted doesn’t mock or dodge or disguise. They just wait.

Because the band can’t drag her over the line. She has to *walk back herself.*

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – CINCINNATI – LATE SUMMER 2027 – TUESDAY AFTERNOON**

The air is so still you can hear the faint buzz of a cable not quite plugged in all the way. Avril sits hunched forward on the couch, small in her leather jacket, knees knocking, mascara streaking in clumsy smudges. The fire is gone from her voice. What’s left is something nobody has ever heard from her—not on stage, not in interviews, not even in the late-night chaos of tour buses.

**AVRIL** (quiet, trembling)
I need help.

The words fall like bricks, heavy and undeniable.

Nobody moves. Jeff’s fingers twitch on his strap but he doesn’t look up. Bill swallows, his Adam’s apple bobbing like he’s choking on dust. Jeremy leans against the doorframe, his eyes wide, arms folded like he’s holding himself together. Jim, still in the center of the room, exhales slow, the tension in his jaw loosening just a hair.

**AVRIL (cont’d)** (voice breaking)
It’s been three years. Three years into this… whatever the hell you wanna call it. My reinvention. My second life. And I love it. I fucking love it. Being here, in this band, in this madness. It’s the first time since I was seventeen that music feels like mine again.

She presses her fists against her thighs, digging in like she wants to bruise.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
But my body doesn’t work half the time. My brain flickers out like a bad light bulb. Some days I can’t get out of bed. Some nights I can’t sleep without the pill. And I keep telling myself it’s fine, it’s manageable, that if I just… push harder, scream louder, it’ll sort itself out.

She shakes her head, finally looking up. Her eyes are wet, furious at herself, pleading at them.

**AVRIL (cont’d)**
But it doesn’t. And I can’t push anymore. I’ve cracked. And I don’t want to end up like—

Her voice cuts off. She doesn’t say Kathy’s name, doesn’t say Gilley’s. She doesn’t have to. The ghosts are already in the room.

Silence.

Then Jeff speaks, voice low, the closest he’s ever come to tender.

**JEFF**
You’re asking now. That’s the difference.

**BILL**
And we’ll back you. Whatever it takes.

Jeremy pushes off the doorframe, steps closer. His voice isn’t soft—it’s blunt, but steady.

**JEREMY**
You don’t have to carry it alone anymore.

Finally, Jim kneels in front of her, lowering himself so he’s eye to eye. No jokes. No bravado. Just the drummer, stripped raw.

**JIM**
We’ve lost too many. You’re not gonna be one of them. Not on my fucking watch.

Avril exhales a jagged breath, and for the first time since she stormed in about lawsuits and constitutions and medicine, her shoulders drop. She’s not cured, not whole, not fixed. But she’s *asking*.

And that is everything.

The amps hum like they’re bearing witness. Kendall watches with pen in hand, knowing this is history—not the kind that goes on posters, but the kind that saves lives.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – CINCINNATI – LATE SUMMER 2027 – TUESDAY AFTERNOON**

The amps hum, Avril’s breath stutters, the band stands frozen in the gravity of her words. She has *asked*. At last.

Jeremy paces once, then stops cold.

**JEREMY**
We need Cremeans.

**JEFF**

## **EXT. FOUNTAIN SQUARE – CINCINNATI – JULY 4, 2026 – MIDDAY**

Heat sheens off the granite like a fever. The sky is an enamel blue, cloudless, merciless. Volunteers in polo shirts ferry stacks of pamphlets that say *Oath of Allegiance* in a font designed to calm. Children wave paper flags on sticks that bend in the humidity. Microphones cough with test pops. The iconic bronze figures of the Tyler Davidson Fountain glisten as if sweating with everyone else.

There are maybe seven hundred of them, clustered in rows, passports tucked in plastic sleeves, faces lifted to the stage. A hundred languages riding the air like birds in their own thermal currents. A man in a linen suit dab-dabs his forehead. A woman in a hijab fans her grandmother with a program. Somewhere behind the soundboard, a baby laughs, or cries, or both. From the streets beyond the square: sirens, car horns, cicadas sawing the afternoon in half. America hums.

Avril is in the crowd, not on the stage. That’s by design. No eyeliner mask, no leather spikes; she’s wearing a simple blouse that sticks to her shoulder blades, black jeans, hair pulled low and loose. There’s a small blue ribbon tied around her wrist—something she found in a kitchen drawer this morning and looped on without thinking. Her palm is damp against her chest.

She does not look like a star. She looks like an applicant.

On stage, the magistrate clears his throat, a microphone squeals, and the ceremony begins. A short speech about journeys and courage, about all the places these voices have come from, about how the nation is an argument worth having. The words roll like warm beer—earnest, flat, necessary.

“Please raise your right hand.”

Arms lift: trembling, sure, arthritic, tattooed. Avril’s forearm gleams under the light. The square hushes. The magistrate begins the long, familiar incantation of belonging, and seven hundred mouths follow, braided into one sound.

Avril repeats each clause carefully. The consonants sit heavy in her mouth, as if they’ve been waiting for a bed to lie down in. She is not thinking about the tab she sometimes takes to keep the nightmares from detonating her out of sleep; not about the lawyers who tried to turn her name into a toll road; not about Canada’s shrug or the way she stood under the light of Parliament and was told to wait her turn for medicine. She is thinking about this sentence, then the next one, and how the shape of the vow feels like a door she can push open with both hands. She is thinking, for the first time in a long time: *I can stand here. I can say this out loud. I can belong without apologizing to the past.*

The magistrate reaches the end. There’s a tiny beat—silence inhaling—and then applause, real and muscular. A boy on someone’s shoulders shouts, “Happy birthday!” Someone laughs, a sound that cracks like lightning on stone. Paper flags flutter like a field of quick birds. A brass band in the corner hits two confused notes and stops; the heat has warped their sheet music.

Backstage, behind the municipal bunting and the rented risers, officials ripple with a different kind of energy. A whisper goes through the headset like a spark on dry grass: *She’s here.* It zigzags from a clipboard to a lanyard to a uniform, collects superlatives as it moves—*the* Avril, yes *that* one—and by the time it reaches the man with the schedule grid, the whisper is a plan. He points; the junior staffer sprints.

The staffer finds her in the second row of new citizens, cheeks flushed, eyes bright and rimmed by the kind of exhaustion that looks like victory. He does the smile that says both apology and opportunity.

“Ms. Lavigne—would you consider—maybe—singing the anthem? A verse would be—”

Avril laughs. It’s short, clean, a blade dulled by kindness.

“Let’s not turn this into a circus,” she says. She glances up at the stage—flags, microphones, the speechifying scaffolds of a country making itself up again, always again. “I just got here. Today isn't about my face.”

She watches his face fall, then catches it before it hits the floor. She is kind. She has learned the cost of spectacle, and where spectacle saves.

“But one of us can play it,” she adds. “Clean. No fireworks. No diva moment. Just—us.”

Her eyes scan the crowd, the familiar gravity wells where her people stand. Jim—knee bouncing, jaw set, trying to look unimportant in a Dayton Dragons cap. Jeremy—flannel sleeves rolled, sweating, watching her like a brother at the edge of a cliff. Jeff—sturdy, still, hands clasped; he blinks toward the stage, the tiny in-ears hidden under his hair. Cremeans off to the side in a crisp white shirt, tie loosened, posture exact, the Psalm tucked inside his breast pocket like a talisman. Kendall back a few rows with a notebook and a face that says *remember everything.*

And Bill—sunglasses reflecting the fountain, arms folded, a black case in his hand like a promise. He doesn’t look surprised. He looks inevitable.

“Bill,” Avril says, and the staffer’s eyebrows climb. “He’ll do it.”

Bill unslings the case. He doesn’t rush. He doesn’t grin. He walks. People part without quite knowing why. He is a small, solid man carrying a black Stratocaster that the band barely lets him touch in public. In Commuted 3.0 he keeps keys like a cathedral, builds the scaffolds of sound the others rage against, and every once in a while—when they need mass, thickness, a shadow of thunder—he straps on this instrument and the room changes temperature.

He steps onto the portable stage. The riser shudders under his boots. A harried tech points at a small amp that looks like a toy next to the skyscrapers, flicks the switch, and the amp breathes to life with a mosquito hiss. Bill plugs in. Checks the cable. Taps the volume knob. No one needs to tell him the rules; the rules live in his hands.

He slings the Strat over his shoulder, lets it settle. Sun glances off the pickguard. He rolls his shoulders. He’s thinking about the curve of the melody the same way he thinks about signal flow: how to make what is obvious become new without showboating the trick. He palms the pickup selector—bridge for bite, tone down just a notch. He looks up at the sky as if asking permission.

And then he plays.

The first note is a bell struck under water—pure, clean, slightly overdriven, a strain of silver drawn across the square. Heads turn. Children stop fidgeting. A squirrel pauses on the lip of a planter like even animals know what a national moment sounds like. Bill walks the melody up its first small hill. He lets the second note lean into a bend that makes the air ache. He milks the vibrato exactly as long as memory does and no longer.

He is not doing celebrity. He is doing clarity.

People who think they hate the guitar lean forward. People who think the anthem is a fight lean toward the music instead. The amp breaks at the exact point where electricity turns honest. It is not polite. It is exact.

From the second row, Avril watches him and falls through time.

**FLASH – NYC, LATE SUMMER 2024 – ALLEY – NIGHT.** The camera at the edge of the dark. The breath in her throat like ash. Jack’s laugh, too loud, the smell of metal, the snap of a belt like a gun in a small room. Misdirection and neon. The paparazzi flash that gnaws your face into a headline. Her body making a sound she doesn’t recognize and refuses to narrate. Then—Bill’s hand. The shock of fingertips at her elbow. His voice with no microphone: *Move.* Not a question. A vector. Then his jacket around her shoulders like a roof thrown over a fire. The wound still raw, the night choosing not to eat her. Clarity, not rescue; direction, not pity. Out. Away. Breathing again.

**BACK TO FOUNTAIN SQUARE.** Bill drags a harmonic until it whistles and breaks. The crowd exhale-laughs at the audacity, and then goes quiet because this isn’t stuntwork, it’s prayer. Time bends around it.

Near the stage, a Sikh man slips off his sunglasses and patches his eyes with a handkerchief. A woman in scrubs, still in hospital clogs, clutches her toddler to her chest. An old Marine with a cane doesn’t salute; he just nods, once, to no one. A teenage boy in a Mexico jersey tries to pretend he isn’t choking up. Sound stitches them all into one shape.

Jim stands with his cap in his hands and his mouth tight. He’s heard a thousand national anthems in a thousand rooms, none of which mattered until now. Jeremy keeps wiping his forearms on his sleeves; he looks like a man who could take a punch again. Jeff watches Bill’s left hand and hears an entire syllabus about restraint; he has a small smile you’d need a magnifying glass to see. Cremeans’ eyes glisten—he will deny this later—and he rests a palm on the inside pocket where Psalms live.

Bill hits the middle section and lets the melody climb. He does not quote anyone, though history hums behind his left wrist like a living wire. He uses the bar once, gently, makes the notes breathe like lungs. The square feels the inhale, the exhale. The judges on the stages of his life—labels, gatekeepers, racetrack odds-makers—are nowhere. There is just Cincinnati, heat, steel, glass, bronze, the smell of sweat and sunscreen and street pretzels, the hum of air conditioning units, the human throat.

Avril presses her hand harder to her chest. She is not pledging to an abstraction. She is pledging to the possibility of repair. To the permission to be helped. To a band that will not let her vanish into glamorized destruction again. She thinks of the long office in Dayton, Mountain Dew lined up like a secular altar, Cremeans reading Romans under his breath after sending the headset contracts for Jeff, the way the word **clarity** has been circling them since January like a hawk waiting for a thermal. She thinks of waking up some nights and not knowing where she is until her palm finds the blue ribbon on her wrist. She thinks of saying out loud, *I can’t,* and not being punished for it.

Bill leans into the crescendo. The Strat is a blade, a siren, a bell thrown off a church tower and caught by a crowd that refuses to let it shatter. He rides the melody right up to the edge where sentimentality lives and then swerves. It becomes something furious and clean. A held note turns into light pouring off glass.

Avril sees the flashbulb again—the alley—and then she sees Bill’s face in profile, the jawline of a man who works instead of talking about working. She breathes as if the note is an oxygen mask.

At the lip of the stage, the official who brought her the ask is standing with both hands folded in front of his belly, no headset now, just eyes. He expected a famous person to do famous person things. He is getting an engineer priest playing a melody that makes the ground smarter.

The last phrase arrives.

Bill lifts his pick for the small, necessary cliff, lets the chord ring, coaxing feedback like a trained animal—dangerous, yes, but leashed. A shimmer of harmonic ghosts flickers around the fundamental like heat on asphalt. For a heartbeat, the square is only that sound, the way a room becomes only a face when you tell the truth.

He mutes the strings with the side of his hand.

Silence. Like a pin settling in the hinge of a door.

Then the crowd detonates. Clapping, shouting, a roar that bounces off the glass and comes back twice as loud. Flags whip. A toddler shrieks with delight because noise means we are alive. Someone yells “Bravooooo!” with too many o’s and nobody minds.

Bill does not bow. He unplugs, wraps the cable, tucks the guitar under his arm. He walks down the stairs as if returning from changing a lightbulb. He passes Avril and she catches his sleeve. Her mouth opens, but the word collapses in the heat before it leaves. She squeezes his arm instead. He nods. It is more than enough.

Cameras tilt toward her—someone has put her on a text chain—and she shakes her head, gentle, a hand up that says *Not today.* A reporter mouths *just one question* and she smiles like a teacher who knows you didn’t do the reading. She turns away, toward the stage, toward the judge shaking hands, toward the line where new citizens collect their small packets with forms and pamphlets and a flag stapled to a stick.

Cremeans materializes at her elbow. He doesn’t startle her. “There’s a tent with shade,” he says, quietly. “Water. Contracts can wait until Tuesday.” He says it like a joke but means it like a vow.

Jim appears on her other side, cap back on, tapping a rhythm into his thigh he hasn’t named yet. “You did it,” he says, which on paper is nothing, but in the mouth of a man who has dragged whole stadiums into rapture, it is almost a sacrament. Jeremy squeezes her shoulder with two fingers, careful and quick. Jeff extends a small U.S.-flag pin as if it were a pick to a student, and when she fumbles with the clasp he does not help; he waits until she gets it, then nods once, proud as steel.

Kendall scribbles two final lines and shuts the notebook. *Clarity is not quiet,* she will write later. *It is the exact opposite of noise.*

The brass band tries again and makes it eight bars before the conductor stops them with a hiss. The square begins to breathe like a city again. On a rooftop across the way a TV camera points its cyclops eye toward the stage and a talking head begins to speak in a tone calibrated to sell mattresses and joy. Down the block a vendor shouts about lemon ices in a voice meant for ballparks.

Bill is gone for a minute, then back, wiping his hands on a black cloth as if rinsing voltage off his skin. He looks a little shell-shocked, as anyone does when they’ve told the truth so loudly it rings for a while.

He and Avril share a look. It isn’t long. It is forever. It says: *Move. Keep moving. This is how we live.*

“Thank you,” she says this time, the word finally making it past the heat.

“Always,” he answers, and if you didn’t know the history, you’d think he was being polite. If you did, you’d know it is contract, credo, code.

The first fireworks later are just tests—white chrysanthemums that pop low and loud enough to rattle teeth. The kids cheer anyway. As dusk edges forward, the square will fill again and fill again, and the sky will be bullied into confession. But now, in the sweaty bright of afternoon, the victory is smaller and cleaner: a woman who almost didn’t make it is standing on a square in Ohio with a flag on a stick and a new name in the government’s mouth. A band that often set itself on fire is, for once, a bucket brigade. A country that argues with itself for sport has just grown a little nearer to what it says on the pamphlet.

The magistrate steps to the mic to say something about volunteer lawyers in the shade tent, and the sound fuzzes for a second as if the square itself is clearing its throat. Avril looks down at her blue ribbon, at the pin resting against her blouse. She imagines a rehearsal in three days where she doesn’t need to pretend she’s fine, where someone passes her a bottle of water and another someone says, “Take five,” and no one mistakes mercy for weakness. She imagines the new songs that don’t have to apologize to the old ones. She imagines sleep.

“Fountain Square,” the loudspeaker says, “please give another hand to—”

Nobody hears the rest. The applause crashes anyway. It isn’t for the name that follows. It is for the ordinary miracle of a vow kept, for the way a guitar can cut a hard day into digestible pieces, for the simple, indecently bold act of beginning again in public.

Avril lets the noise move through her like a tide she no longer fights. She is part of the crowd on purpose. No spotlight, no pose. Her chest rises and falls. The heat is awful. The air smells like sugar and metal and possibility. She smiles without performance and feels the muscles of her face remember how.

Clarity, at last. Not silence. Not purity. Just the right signal at the right volume, carrying across a square full of strangers who have just, for a few minutes, become a single instrument.

Bill holds the case against his thigh and watches the flags lift, lower, lift. He is not surprised by any of this. He had the guitar ready. He always does. He saved her once in an alley; today he saved nothing and no one, and because of that, everything. He will go back to the rehearsal room where cables knot like an argument and he will make the signal clean. That is his profession. That is his faith.

Avril tucks the pamphlet into the back pocket of her jeans and laughs when it crinkles. She looks up at the picture-book sky and murmurs something we cannot hear. It is not a prayer. It is not a curse. It is an answer.

The square swallows it and answers back with wind and light.

Somewhere in the distance, a first firework shouts to test the night. The crowd looks up as one organism, the way flocks turn. For a breath, everyone is quiet again, waiting for the sky to tell them the next part of the story.

It will, of course. But the plot has already shifted.

**FADE.**

## **INT. MINIVAN – I-75 NORTHBOUND – EARLY EVENING, JULY 4, 2026**

Avril looks at the Core Four and then at us. She sings it dead-eye true.

*Lately I’ve been lost, spinning in my own head,
Thought I needed love, but I just needed a friend instead…*

The plaza sways. People who’ve never met put shoulders together as if they have. When the first chorus hits, the crowd joins without asking permission:

*Hope is in the air, we’ll find our way tonight,
You said, “You’re better off with me, it’s gonna be alright.”*

She points her mic at the men behind her and they step in—rough harmony, perfect meaning. They don’t over-sing it. They just make the promise land.

*We build each other up, that’s how it’s meant to be—
In this crazy world, you’re the anchor holding me.*

Avril laughs through a tear, and there’s a moment—a small one, a knife’s width—where her mouth forms *thank you* without sound. They nod. All four. The last chorus is a citizen’s oath, not to a flag but to a practice:

*I’m not just surviving, I’m really living free—
With a friend like you… it’s where I wanna be.*

The note dies. The column stands. The crowd detonates: screams, sobs, the electric roar of strangers choosing to be a we.

Avril steps back from the mic and the four men step closer without moving. The plaza chants *Better off! Better off! Better off!* like they’re trying to rewire the country with one phrase.

Somewhere at the edge, Jeremy leans into her ear and murmurs the thing that’s been haunting the year: *Anthems are questions. You asked one. The answer came back.*

She nods once and lets the joy do its job.

They vanish as promised, cases into carts, carts into dark. The rangers watch, radios quiet, and do not decide to be villains.

**INT. FARMHOUSE – GREENE COUNTY – DAWN**

Twelve acres of silence. A kitchen so neat it looks ironed. An open Bible. A phone that’s a hive.

Cremeans hasn’t slept. He’s paced holes into the title of Romans. He’s watched #VictoryMonumentLiveShow fling sparks he can’t put out. He’s watched a flash of Avril’s chest multiply faster than lawsuits. He’s pulled up the map and seen all those tiny glowing pings sitting on an island that smells like beer and fried fish and bad ideas.

And one ping, alone, earlier, at a golf cart rental. He’d frowned at that. Then remembered whose phone that was and un-frowned.

He called Jeremy. The explanation tumbled out, sheepish and proud: not a spiral, a stunt; not destruction, communion; three songs and home. *Controlled chaos,* Jeremy called it. *A win, for once.*

Cremeans had not believed until the clip landed from a place he did not expect: the Secretary of the Interior boosting a shaky midnight video with a caption about *American music at American monuments.* It was obscene and perfect: someone had staged MMA at the White House this weekend. If absurd spectacle was the theme, then the republic would tolerate a hymn under a tower.

He sat. He breathed. He wrote.

> **Statement from Commuted**
>
> Last night, under Perry’s Victory and International Peace Memorial, we marked America’s 250th not with pageantry or politics, but with music. No stage, no tickets, no barricades—just a crowd that took care of a place while a band took care of a crowd.
>
> If you need a precedent, try Cana. Imagine less marble, more biker-bar reception: the party ran out of wine. Jesus didn’t scold it; he supplied it, so joy could aim higher. We tried to do the same. Three songs. No damage. Everyone home safe.
>
> In a season of absurd spectacles—even MMA in the Rose Garden—we’re grateful for the kind that ends with strangers carrying each other’s trash to the bin.
>
> Why close with *Better Off with a Friend*? Because that’s how we’ve survived twenty-five years of noise and grief. In 2026, clarity is saying it plain: we’re better off with friends.

He hit send. He folded his hands. He did not use his allotted profanity for the year. He watched the morning take the fields from black to gold and felt the absurd settle into useful.

**EXT. HOTEL PARKING LOT – SANDUSKY – DAWN**

The minivans glide in croaking and hot. Cedar Point’s coasters stand on the horizon like skeletons that forgot to lie down. The sun is up; which means the night is over; which means everyone is made of glass.

Doors open. Commuted spills out like laundry: straps bitten into shoulders, T-shirts stiff with sweat and lake air. There’s an argument brewing about whether Diet Coke counts as water, but nobody has the energy to prosecute it.

Jim does a headcount by instinct. Keys in hand, eyes on the lobby doors like salvation’s just behind a ficus.

**JIM**
All right. Rooms. How many we need? Four? Five?

Silence. Not a long one. Just long enough to show there’s a question under the question.

Then, sharp, simultaneous:

**AVRIL and JEFF**
Five.

It lands like a tiny gavel, final without explanation. Jeremy’s eyebrows climb and then he lets them fall. Bill tilts his sunglasses and doesn’t comment. Kendall writes it down like a weather event. Jim blinks because he had expected four and cannot say why out loud.

Nobody asks for why. Nobody offers. They pick up bags and go inside, the line they drew weeks ago still warm, still necessary, still the reason a night of risk ended in dawn instead of disaster.

They vanish into the elevator, one more anonymous band in one more anonymous hotel, the coasters throwing long bones of shadow over the parking lot. The sun climbs. The answer stands.

Five.

**END.**
