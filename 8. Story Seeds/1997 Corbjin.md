Kathy stood in the stark, industrial loft that served as Anton Corbijn’s makeshift studio for the day. It was 1997, and she was on the brink of releasing That Look of Release—an album that would become a lightning rod for controversy. She wore a plain black tank top, ratty jeans, and little else: no big hair, no flamboyant jewelry, just the raw, edgy confidence that had developed since her twenty-something reinvention. Corbijn didn’t waste time fussing—he had the camera in hand, brow furrowed, already picturing how he’d transform these shots into a legendary cover.

“Think you can give me that look, the one that says you’ve got nothing left to lose?” Corbijn asked in his understated, European-accented murmur.

Kathy offered a half-smile, stepping into the heavy floodlight near the battered cement wall. The bright beam bleached her features, etched every subtle contour. She might have felt naked without her usual stage gear, but the whole point of This Look, as the album’s concept teased, was to show the raw nerve behind the glamour. She let the weight of the past year—her near-scorched romances, her band’s internal drama—sink into her eyes.

A single click echoed in the hushed space.

Corbijn exhaled with something like admiration. “Yes,” he said simply, adjusting the camera and taking a few steps left, never looking away from her face. “Hold on to that.”

Click, click, click. Each shutter press felt like a little electric jolt. Kathy shifted, meeting Corbijn’s lens head-on—part defiance, part acceptance. She could guess what her bandmates would say: that it was too bold, that maybe they should keep it more radio-friendly. But it was 1997; she was tired of playing safe.

The two of them danced around that notion of “release,” in every sense. It was the name of the record, sure, but also a reflection of Kathy’s new creative hunger. She wanted more than guitars and standard shoots—she wanted an image that encapsulated how the album felt: unfiltered, messy, unashamed. Corbijn, famous for capturing artists’ souls in black-and-white, or in washed-out color that seemed to highlight every flaw and every triumph, was the perfect partner-in-crime.

He had her lean against the window, evening sun flaring behind. The studio’s tall, grimy panes threw mottled light over her skin. She arched an eyebrow when he asked her to climb onto a rusted metal stool in the corner. But she complied, hooking one foot on a rung and letting the other dangle, head tilted at an angle that gave Corbijn the moody shadow he craved.

“You’re about to spark a conversation,” he said, voice echoing slightly in the cavernous loft. “You ready for that?”

She laughed softly. “Conversations, controversies… I’m done apologizing for existing, if that’s what you mean.” She paused, letting a half-smile cross her lips. “So yeah, I’m ready.”

More clicks—rapid, decisive. Corbijn nodded. “That’s it.”

As the last frames reeled by, Kathy could almost see the eventual album cover: her posture tense, gaze unwavering, every muscle in her face telegraphing Don’t even think about telling me who I’m supposed to be. People would freak out at the boldness, the near-nudity in some promos, the half-lidded stare that refused to flinch. The band’s label might sweat. Her hometown might shake their heads. But it no longer mattered. She wanted honesty—actual, blistering honesty—in both the music and the image.

When the final shutter click echoed, Corbijn lowered his camera. “I think we’ve got it,” he murmured. He flipped through a couple of shots, a small, satisfied grin crossing his face. “This is going to stir them up.”

Kathy hopped off the stool, pushing damp hair from her forehead. “Yeah,” she said quietly. “Let them be stirred.”

They shared a conspiratorial smile before she grabbed a worn denim jacket and slung it over her shoulder. Outside, the Atlanta summer pressed like a furnace against the building’s corrugated walls, a reminder of how, in less than a month, That Look of Release would hit the public ear—and eye—like a flash bomb. Only later would Kathy learn just how uproarious the fans, the critics, and her own bandmates could be about that cover. But for now, in Corbijn’s battered studio, she was simply content knowing she’d etched this piece of herself into film forever, capturing a side she was no longer afraid to show.
