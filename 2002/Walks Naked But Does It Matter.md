# **The New York Times – Arts & Leisure, April 21, 2002**
## *“Commuted Walks Naked, But Does It Matter?”*

Byline: Jonathan Ellsworth

---
Commuted’s new album, *Face Down, Ass Up*, arrives in stores this week under the shadow of vulnerability and, perhaps inevitably, controversy. The cover image—a lone, nude Kathy Espich photographed from behind as she walks away from a hotel hallway—has already been described alternately as exploitative, brave, and mundane. One is tempted to note that network shampoo commercials reveal more skin.

Yet the cultural resonance seems less about anatomy than timing. In an America obsessed with Weapons of Mass Destruction, we have perhaps mistaken our own cultural fragility for something more sinister. *Face Down, Ass Up* is an album full of vulnerabilities that rarely detonate. Instead of dynamite, we get damp fuses. “*My Handcuffs*” lashes out with some vitality, but much of the record feels listless, as if the band were too drunk, too stoned, or too distracted by their expanding families to remember what urgency sounds like.

This is not the first time rock music has failed to live up to its provocative packaging. The Who once smashed guitars they couldn’t top on record. Nirvana’s famous baby-in-the-pool swam in deeper waters than its imitators could fathom. Commuted here delivers a cover that promises scandal, but contents that whisper.

Ironically, the stark cover photograph, which might have landed harder in 1992, today feels less like a bombshell than a melancholy shrug. Vulnerability is a powerful weapon, but in a world where we scour deserts for evidence of WMDs, it’s one the band seems unwilling, or unable, to deploy.
