# **ACT I SUMMARY: LEAVE YOUR KEY AND GO**

Spring, 1998. The world is at a hinge.

In Copenhagen, Commuted is on tour and unraveling at the edges. Kathy Espich—lead singer, public icon, center of every headline—is no longer playing coy with her sexual energy. She dives headfirst into a sapphic affair with rising European film star Elaina, in a hotel room that becomes a temple and a playground, a mirror and a stage. Their bodies do not meet gently—they crash. And Kathy, for once, lets herself be devoured.

Elaina shoots an erotic film scene, closed set, close scrutiny. Kathy observes. Fascinated. Inspired. Humbled. She’s never been on that side of the camera before—not only as subject, but as artist. It lights something under her skin.

Meanwhile, the band’s next album—*Leave Your Key and Go*—looms. It’s raw. Painful. Personal. The title, a direct reference to Kathy’s forced estrangement from Jeff Evans. A humiliation she’s still metabolizing in public.

The band’s handlers—Cremeans and Sarah—recognize the danger of Kathy’s power, of her volatility. They initiate a back-channel operation to develop alternate concepts for the album’s lead video. Cremeans contacts each band member in secret, instructing them to prepare their own pitches. Kathy must be challenged, not worshipped. They need art, not ego.

Sarah persuades Gilley—through sex, intimacy, and a hot shower—to take the challenge seriously. Gilley shocks her. He goes full machine. Five sheets of genius in two hours. It rattles her. The “slacker” is more dangerous than she thought.

Gilley’s concept: "This is what it looks like when fame eats its young."

The band is assembling. The fight is about to begin.  But across the ocean, another war ignites.

In Washington, D.C., Kendall Weatherby, political operative and feminist true believer, is jolted awake by a pager reading one word: “Drudge.” The Monica Lewinsky scandal has detonated again. This time, it’s unsalvageable.

Kendall was in the thick of it. She defended the administration. She wrote lies. She spun silence. And now she knows: she can’t do it anymore.  She flees to Knoxville, confesses to her father—her moral compass, her grounding wire. He doesn’t coddle her. He challenges her: resign with integrity or stay and rot.

Kendall makes her decision: not resignation. Detonation.

She returns to D.C., marches into her boss’s office—the Chief of Staff to the Vice President—and delivers a flaming, screaming resignation on principle. Two believers, two causes. One walks out. One clings to the wreckage.  Kendall’s future is uncertain. But her conscience is clean.  And something in the back of her mind whispers: Music. Truth. A different kind of war.

End of Act I.
