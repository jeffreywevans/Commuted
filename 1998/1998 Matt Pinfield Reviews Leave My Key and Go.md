# Commuted — *Leave My Key and Go* (advance listen, summer ’98)

They’ve always been a paradox band: Midwestern kids with downtown nerve, synth-shined and garage-scarred, carnal and cerebral. If *That Look of Release* (’95) was the coronation and *Flash Behind Her Eyes* (’97) the messy afterparty, *Leave My Key and Go* is the morning after that still smells like a club—only now the windows are open and the groove breathes. The record’s thesis is exhibition as absolution: bodies and souls shown in unforgiving light, daring the listener to flinch.

## The sound & the stance

Musically this folds their classic architecture—melodic bass as spine, precision drums, serrated guitar, and Bill’s chrome-bright synths—into a 1998 palette that’s flirting with club textures without surrendering the band’s punch. You hear the “hit-it-on-the-one” authority that runs from the Original James Brown through post-punk and into alt-rock: the rhythm section keeps the hips honest while the vocals drag confession into the spotlight. When they chase house/tech-house coloration, it’s used as *attitude* rather than costume; where they go live, it’s to remind you why the bootlegs traded hands like contraband.

## Track-by-track (the short tour)

**“The Forgotten Era.”** A curtain-raiser about being watched and choosing it anyway—arena guitars, shine-off synths, and a chorus that pivots from dread to liberation. It’s consent-as-crescendo, grinding forward like arena U2 with a nasty Stooges grin, and it nails the album’s gaze-and-grace obsession.

**“The Tailspin.”** Power-pop voltage at ~120 BPM, all summer air, beer-sweat memory, and a name—Allie—floating like a hook. The melody has that late-’90s radio lift, but the lyric’s fixation on the half-remembered body keeps it crooked enough to feel like them.

**“Holkham Bay.”** Here’s the shocker: deep/tech-house pulse over their guitar grammar. Think Balearic breeze over Rust Belt muscle. It’s about nakedness as reclamation—shame rinsed off by salt air—and the four-on-the-floor works because the band treats it like a motor, not a gimmick.

**“Mare Nubium.”** Moonlit dares and statuary witnesses—two lovers staging a private rebellion in public. The arrangement swells like a thriller theme, then snap-cuts to whispered asides; it’s their old cinema fetish re-weaponized. Velvet Underground nerve, modernized gear.

**“Fireside Confession.”** Campfire call-and-response—three voices circling a boundary they’re half-joking about crossing. Layered harmonies and bright synths sit over a stomping backbeat; the hook is pure communal singalong, the subtext pure mess. Think Replacements tenderness smuggled into a glossy alt-rock single.

**“A Charcoal Sketch.”** The keeper. Female-led vocal, blue-room quiet, a model daring the artist to *see* rather than consume. When the bridge asks to be drawn “whole,” and the breakdown admits she expected to be seen as ugly but wasn’t, the band finally trades spectacle for mercy. It’s the album’s heart.

**“Poe’s Big Bang.”** Punk-pop tempo, goth-romantic wordplay, and a raven perched on the headstock. Desire wrestles denial; an Edgar wink lands mid-bridge. It’s bratty and baroque at once—more *Faith No More thumbed through Hot Topic* than straight pop-punk—which is exactly why it scans as Commuted.

**“Better Off With a Friend (Live).”** The B-side that refused to die. Dragged into the set, it blooms—crowd-ready chorus, a lyric about choosing the anchor over the storm, and that mid-90s jangle turned into a shoulder-to-shoulder shout. You can hear why Gilley championed it—it’s the band’s communal grin.

**“The Hilton (Live from the Nutt House).”** Archive lightning: their industrial-flavored ’93 cover resurrected, now done as pastel trance with a female lead scything through it. It’s louche and gleeful and vulgar in a way only a great live band can justify—a palate-cleanser that tastes like battery acid and bubblegum.

## What ties it together

Nearly every song is about being *seen*: on a crowded night, on dunes, under the moon, by a friend, by the sketch artist. That’s not just sex—it’s surveillance and self-possession. The through-line is agency: choosing the gaze, or fighting it, or discovering your reflection inside it. Thematically, that’s a clever set after *That Look of Release*: if ’95 crowned them, ’98 asks what the crown *shows* you about yourself.

Historically, it fits the moment. Post-grunge is either going glossy or going numb; electronica is bleeding into rock; nu-metal is growling at the gate; trip-hop moodiness is tempting everyone into molasses. Commuted takes the path James Brown would respect: keep the pocket undeniable, keep the call-and-response alive (audience, self, and partner all answering the lead), and let the show be the sermon. When the house textures arrive (“Holkham Bay”), they feel like an invitation back to the dancefloor; when the live cuts punch in, they certify what the studio implies: this band still slays in front of bodies.

## Quibbles (because it’s my job)

Sometimes the lyrics bruise their own subtlety—there’s the odd wince-line in “Poe’s Big Bang” where lust gets… anatomically literal. The sequencing’s brave but slightly lurchy: dropping both live cuts late dents the studio arc’s spell, even if they’re individually killer. And a couple of rhyme choices lean notebook-first-draft when the arrangement deserves a scalpel.

## Verdict

No one else from their cohort could make a record this naked *and* this sturdy in ’98. It doesn’t eclipse *That Look of Release*—few things do—but it stands shoulder-to-shoulder with it, grinning. The groove ethic traces straight back to the Godfather; the exhibitionist theology is defiantly their own. Call it an A- minus if you need the letter. More usefully: it’s the sound of a great band choosing visibility over myth—and getting away with it.
