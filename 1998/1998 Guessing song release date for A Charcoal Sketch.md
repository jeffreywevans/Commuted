Based on all canon and surrounding context, here’s my best-educated guess about when Jeff wrote “A Charcoal Sketch,” even though it hasn’t been officially placed in a project file yet:

**Timeframe Guess:**
**2024 – early 2025.**

**Reasoning:**

- **Musical Style:** The blend of deep house/tech-house with power rock, double bass, and crowd-pleasing elements suggests a *post-2020* Commuted, when the band has fully embraced a hybrid live-electronic sound and large-venue arrangements (consistent with scenes of live comebacks and the Grammy years, or even the new NYC/2024 material).

- **Lyric Themes:** The self-revelatory, emotionally aware back-and-forth between male and female vocalists feels like post-Kathy, post-trauma writing: a band aware of vulnerability, longing for honest connection, and wrestling with visibility and shame. There is also a distinct *therapeutic* and *exposing* vibe, echoing Jeff’s “surgically precise” post-diagnosis lyrics seen in 2024-2025 canon.

- **Voice Distribution:** The structure—female verses, male choruses—reads as if written specifically to utilize a new *female* singer alongside Jeff’s more confident, exposed lead vocals, which matches the post-Kathy, Avril (or replacement vocalist) era, not the earlier 90s-00s years when Kathy led most songs.

- **Songcraft and Self-Reference:** The “charcoal sketch” as metaphor points directly at the band’s *visual iconography* (photography, album covers, self-portraiture), which becomes more pronounced in the 2024 era, especially in the aftermath of the band’s brutal public and private reckoning with their own image, trauma, and history ([source]()).

- **Production Detail:** The 120bpm, “fat bass,” and double bass drum are specifically called out in your notes as characteristics of the *2024 and beyond* live/studio crossover sound (not present in the early grunge years, nor during the stripped-down acoustic eras).

**Narrative Fit:**
It almost certainly *does not* originate in the early 90s (too modern and female-empowering in its gaze, too structurally open), nor does it fit the “broken” post-Kathy immediate years (2012-2014), which are sonically and lyrically much darker, more minimalist, and more grief-stricken.

The song is anthemic, empowering, and almost redemptive—qualities much more in line with Jeff’s post-therapy, post-autism diagnosis songwriting, when he’s “writing for the new lineup” and intentionally building crowd-ready, communal material for big stages and the streaming era.

**The “Avril Effect”:**
If Avril is still in the band at this point, she could easily be the vocalist on these verses, but it’s equally plausible that this was written as an “audition” piece for her *replacement* or as part of a set of new songs being demoed by Jeff to re-launch the band after all the 2024-2026 chaos.

**Why 1995 Makes Sense for “A Charcoal Sketch”**

- **Kathy’s Emotional Collapse:**
The lyrics center on themes of vulnerability, exposure, self-doubt, and the fraught gaze between artist and subject—all of which mirror Kathy’s well-documented struggles in 1995. This was the *exact* moment she was grappling with being “seen” (or not seen) as a woman in a male-dominated band, feeling both invisible and hyper-visible, and fighting internal battles with depression and body image.

- **The Move to Guitar:**
That subtle detail about her switching to guitar only four months prior makes the “face me, let your world fall away” chorus even sharper. It’s about daring herself to step into the spotlight—not just musically, but physically and emotionally.

- **Jeff Writing for Kathy:**
The gendered interplay in the lyrics (“Turn to face me…” sung by a male voice, with verses by the female) now reads as a literal dialogue between Jeff and Kathy, with Jeff coaxing her to accept herself, to let the light in, and to stand unapologetically as both woman and musician. This is consistent with your established dynamic: their best creative work often happened when they were pushing each other, sometimes painfully so.

- **Live Jam Origins:**
The fact that the first 3:15 is a live jam from Bill’s tape of Jeff and Jeremy at soundcheck is *chef’s kiss* level period accuracy. That’s classic mid-90s Commuted: spontaneous, guitar-forward, a bit messy but undeniably powerful. Jeremy’s demand for more solo space also fits the timeline—he was pushing his sound, and the band was letting him off the leash.

- **B-Side Status and Fan Love:**
Being a B-side but a fan favorite is so Commuted: these emotionally raw, less-commercial tracks would become cult classics, especially when performed live during long, cathartic sets.

- **Song as Communication:**

Her poor reaction is more than justified. It’s not just about the engagement—it’s about being sketched, observed, rendered into art instead of being embraced as a person. There’s both a yearning to be seen and a fear that being “seen” means being abandoned, catalogued, or judged.
