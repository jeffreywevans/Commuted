[[1998 Kendall's First Essay]]
## **Forged in Sound and Steel:** 

**Commuted, the Midwest, and the Quiet Legacy of the Northwest Ordinance**

Jeff—

You said 4,000 words.  You didn’t say they had to be sterile.

You didn’t say they had to be polite.  You didn’t say I had to pretend.

The first thing I turned in was 461 words long. You read it in under two minutes, looked at me for maybe five seconds, and said:  “You can either write the essay I asked for, or you can work the next three days and go home forever.”

I took the stairs to my car. I sat in the driver’s seat for 41 minutes. I didn’t cry. But I did write this sentence in the fog on the window:  *“He’s not wrong.”*

So I started over.

I started with the name—*Commuted*. Then I went to the garage. Then the gigs. The sound. The scar tissue. The Ordinance. (Thanks, Jeff Evans, for the lecture in the rain outside Independence Hall—your monologue about 1787 has a rhythm I still dream in.)

I wrote about Kathy like she’s a tectonic plate.  I wrote about Gilley like he’s a lit cigarette tossed near gasoline.  I wrote about Jeff like he’s a municipal surveyor trying to measure his own pain.  I wrote about Bill like he scares me—because he does.  And I wrote about Jim…

Well. You’ll see.

You asked if I could write a newsletter. What I gave you is closer to a personal theology. I don’t know if that disqualifies me. But I do know this: This isn’t just a band. It’s a fault line that keeps making art.

And I want in.

—Kendall Weatherby

# Forged in Sound Part 1

There are places in this country where the past still clings to the buildings. Ohio is one of them. The state is not a symbol—it is a crucible. And Commuted, born in its rusted lung, is less a band than a side effect.

You could argue that Commuted didn’t “form” so much as erupt, like steam through a cracked gasket. One by one, they found each other inside garages ringed with aluminum siding and regret. They tuned cheap guitars while trains screamed in the distance and fathers cursed at drywall. By 1990, the Midwest had stopped producing steel and started producing bands. Not to export. Just to survive.

Their name—Commuted—wasn’t ironic. It was a prayer disguised as a legal term. To commute a sentence is not to absolve it. It is to shorten it, soften it. They chose it as teenagers, not knowing how accurately it would one day describe their whole trajectory: not released from fate, just delayed. Just enough time to make noise.

But they were more than noise. From the beginning, Commuted carried the marks of the place that bore them. Not just the accents or the aggression, but the bone-deep logic of the Northwest Ordinance, the 1787 legislation that carved the industrial Midwest from wilderness. That Ordinance established more than geography—it instituted order. Rectilinear land parcels. Tax-supported education. A system of rights, theoretically available to all. A design.

And like that grid, Commuted had a structure.

You could hear it in the basslines—intellectually aggressive, never improvisational. You could hear it in the guitar solos—blistering, yes, but never chaotic. You could feel it in the drumming: not flourishes, but declarations. Even their lyrics, when at their rawest, betrayed a logic. A hard logic. Kathy Espich, their early core, once sang: “The law freed my hands, not my heart.” That wasn’t poetry. That was autobiography.

The Midwest invented rock and roll. And it perfected the silence that rock and roll answers. In that silence—post-shift, pre-bankruptcy, late afternoon haze where your dad is still wearing steel-toed boots inside the house—Commuted began to grow. And grow angry. And grow brilliant.

They weren’t the best musicians in Ohio in 1990\. They weren’t even the best in Clark County. But they were the most haunted. And the most hungry.

So they clawed their way onto stages. They punched through blown speakers. They earned bruises, lost jobs, skipped school, smashed microphones, and made enemies. In return, they got a sound. Something unique. Something structured and unhinged. A structure trembling at its edges—like the Midwest itself.

By 1998, they had tasted everything: acclaim, estrangement, loss, and platinum. But they still lived under the shadow of the sentence.

They weren’t free.

Only delayed.

# Forged in Sound Section 2

The name thing was functional at first. There were two Jeremys in the band. One had a severe buzz cut and spoke in chords. The other was 6’4”, had hair like a cartoon Viking, and was already trying to charm teachers out of giving him detentions. “Gilley” wasn’t a nickname—it was a survival mechanism. You couldn’t have two Jeremys in one van. The universe wouldn’t allow it.

And besides, the name *fit*. Gilley didn’t just act like a frontman—he radiated it. His guitar work was fine, if a little diffident, but when he stepped up to the mic, something clicked. He had the kind of animal magnetism that made bouncers nervous and booking agents giddy. If you gave him five minutes with a reporter, you got a feature story. If you gave him ten minutes with your girlfriend, you got a breakup.

Still, there are questions. Mostly whispered ones.

Is Gilley the horniest member of Commuted?

Jeff Evans, never one for public emotion, once muttered into his coffee during a radio interview:  
“It’s unbelievable. I’ve seen, with my own eyes, at least four different women come in and out of his hotel room in the same *afternoon*. One had a guitar. One had a grin. One had a goddamn PowerPoint. I don’t even know what was happening anymore.”

Kathy Espich, when pressed by a journalist, blinked once and said:  
“No comment.”  
Then she smiled so faintly it made the room go cold.

Jeremy Evans—the *other* Jeremy—has only ever responded to the topic by saying,  
“I’m the real Jeremy,”  
with the same tone one might use to contest a parking ticket.

But the best line belongs to Bill Davenport. When asked if Gilley had ever stayed over at his apartment, Bill stared for a long beat and replied:  
“Jeremy Gilley is a lifelong friend. And that’s why I will never, under any circumstances, allow him to use my bathroom.”

And the thing is—they’re all right. Gilley wasn’t just sexually active; he was socially radioactive. It wasn’t about conquest. It was about momentum. Gilley didn’t chase women—he just existed in a state of perpetual flirtation, like it was a natural byproduct of respiration.

But even this undersells him. Because while his libido was legendary, his emotional intelligence was… complicated.

He could hold court in a greenroom, disarm a label exec in under sixty seconds, and calm Kathy down mid-meltdown by complimenting her pedal configuration like it was a poem. And somehow, you never knew if it was genuine or strategic.

That was Gilley’s true power: ambiguity. He could be charming, loyal, absurd, infuriating—and it would all come wrapped in the same grin.

By 1998, he was still doing his thing. Touring. Flashing smiles. Leaving a trail of keycards and scented candles in his wake. But there were rumors that even he was getting tired.  Not of women, but of always needing to be *that guy*.

So is he the horniest?  Probably. But only because he’s the only one who *let it become a job*.  The others kept it private.  Gilley built a brand.

# Forged in Sound Section 3

Kathy Espich joined Commuted from the very beginning—though, of course, not as a founding member. No, that honor belonged to the boys, with their messy amplifiers, pizza-stained lyric sheets, and a deeply endearing refusal to read any music written after 1979\. Kathy came in later. She always did.

She wasn’t loud at first. You almost had to coax her out from under her oversized flannel, her guitar slung low like it was more shield than instrument. She had a way of making you work to hear her—and maybe that was the trick. Maybe that’s why people started listening.

Everyone agrees she was ‘gifted.’ That’s the word they always use. Never ‘visionary.’ Never ‘brilliant.’ Gifted. Like it was something she unwrapped, not something she fought for.

Her lyrics were confrontational, sure. Erotic. Violent. Almost… *brave*, some might say. But what do you expect when you’re the only woman in a room full of creatively stunted men? Of course she wrote about blood and power and breathless sex. Of course she painted her left hand red and dared the crowd to stare. One has to find ways to be memorable.

By 1994, she was unraveling—though nobody said so out loud. That’s the other thing about Kathy. She didn’t fall apart; she leaked. Drip by drip. Her contributions were quieter. Her solos shorter. Her backstage presence thinner. It wasn’t dramatic. It was… professional. She simply receded.

And yes, she had a temper. That was legendary. You could set your watch to her outbursts. But wasn’t that part of her charm? The rage? The fragility? It made her seem dangerous. And therefore desirable. The boys would never say that, but you could see it in their eyes. Especially Jeff, who fumbled around her like he was always on the verge of some important confession.

But let’s be honest.

Kathy was never the frontman. She was never the architect. She was an accelerant. A beautiful, combustible agent that turned a mid-tier Ohio grunge band into a cultural detonation. She made the chaos sound like a sermon. She made grief rhythmic.

And then she tried to leave. Before she faded. Or was pushed. It depends on who you ask.

By 1998, Kathy was already myth. Still breathing, still writing, still playing some nights—but the band had moved on. Jeremy got louder. Jeff got quieter. Bill went sideways. And the band got platinum thrice.

Kathy didn’t need a headline. She had liner notes. And stage scars. And a generation of girls who painted their hands red and started screaming.

Which is lovely, really. That’s a kind of legacy. Isn’t it?

# Forged in Sound Section 4

If the Northwest Ordinance had a favorite musician, it would be Jeff Evans.

He is, without exaggeration, the embodiment of that document’s logic—its sacred geometry of surveyed plots and moral stipulations. Sectioned. Structured. Sober. Land was to be rational. Education was to be guaranteed. Slavery was to be banned. Likewise, Jeff’s basslines were never improvised. His gaze never wandered. And his thoughts? Always calculated for maximum clarity, minimum exposure.

Jeff didn’t speak unless he needed to. Not because he was shy, exactly, but because language was imprecise. He preferred systems. Routines. He organized his notebooks chronologically and by mood. He refused to drink soda unless it was called pop. Once, on tour, he re-tuned a borrowed bass because the previous player had set it up “according to feel.” Jeff did not believe in feel. He believed in reproducibility.

In high school, he was the only student who cited historical land ordinances during a debate on zoning laws. That wasn’t a phase. It was a worldview.

He joined Commuted not to make noise, but to make sense of it. His basslines didn’t drive the band—they stabilized it. While Gilley riffed, Kathy howled, and Jim forgot what time signature they were in, Jeff was the one who snapped everything back into place. He was the silent hand on the grid, saying: *No. This goes here. That stays there. This is how we don’t fall apart*.

It would be romantic to say he was the band’s conscience. But no—Jeff was its scaffolding. Uncelebrated but essential. You could tear down everything else, and he’d still be there, blinking behind his wire-rimmed glasses, asking if the pedal board was alphabetized.

He was also, heartbreakingly, the one who *couldn’t adapt*. As the band swerved toward chaos—Kathy’s volatility, Gilley’s theater, Jeremy’s perfectionism—Jeff remained painfully committed to the idea that order could still save them. That if you just played the right notes, in the right order, with the right tone, the world might briefly make sense again.

When Kathy threatened to go solo in ’96, Jeff didn’t protest. He didn’t beg. He just quietly rewrote all the setlists to remove her verses—just in case. He told himself it was preparation, not pessimism. And when she didn’t leave, he didn’t celebrate. He updated the setlists again, without comment. As if admitting relief would destabilize the next show.

By 1998, Jeff Evans was still the first one to tune up and the last one to speak. He wore the same three shirts on rotation. He calibrated tone curves like other men adjusted their belts. He had nothing left but precision, and it was still enough.

The Northwest Ordinance promised order, progress, and moral geometry. Jeff believed it.  But the problem with surveyed land is that it doesn’t bend when the river moves.

# Forged in Sound Section 5

There are musicians who play, and there are musicians who build. Bill Davenport did both. And better than anyone else in Commuted, he understood that noise without structure was just chaos, and structure without imagination was a spreadsheet. He lived at the intersection of architecture and intuition. He didn’t just play music—he engineered it.

At first glance, Bill didn’t seem like much of a threat. He was the shortest guy in the band, though nobody ever joked about it. Something about the way he carried himself—calm, coiled, always processing—made people respect the silence around him. He was built like a weightlifter and moved like a software update: quietly, precisely, and with consequences.

By the time Commuted began recording demos in earnest, Bill had already reverse-engineered most of his synth rack and modified it beyond recognition. While the others were still arguing about song order or how to phrase a bridge, Bill was isolating frequency ranges and finding ways to bury subliminal tones underneath Kathy’s vocals—tones that made you clench your jaw without knowing why. He called it “emotional scaffolding.” No one else knew what it meant. They didn’t need to.

He had a collection of patch libraries he refused to share. He ran cables across the floor like nerves. He soldered his own loop controller out of garage sale parts and a stolen alarm clock. He once routed a guitar solo through a broken answering machine to see what would happen. What happened was Track 4 on the *Should I Press Pause* EP.

He was also the only member of Commuted who could disappear in a room and still leave fingerprints on every single thing you heard. Onstage, he was stoic. In the studio, he was elemental. He never raised his voice, never fought for screen time. He didn’t have to. When you listen to any track from 1991 to 1998 and feel your skin crawl during the transition? That was Bill. When the chorus hits harder than the decibel meter says it should? That’s Bill cheating physics.

The others knew. They didn’t say it, but they knew. Gilley was the face. Kathy was the fire. Jeff was the logic. But Bill was the machine. The black box. The vault. The reason you felt something instead of just hearing it.

And he never bragged. Not once.

If anything, he seemed a little embarrassed by how much he understood. Once, a journalist asked what it felt like to be the band’s technical backbone. Bill just shrugged and said, “I’d rather the work be clean than clever.”  That was a lie. Bill Davenport was clever as hell.  But his real genius was knowing how to bury it.

# Forged in Sound Section 6

It started with a joke. Or maybe a threat.

It was one of those endless, airless July nights in Bethel Township, the kind that make you forget the rest of the world exists. The band—though they didn’t yet call themselves that—had been rehearsing for four hours. Kathy was bleeding from her cuticle. Jim’s snare was slipping again. Jeff had stopped talking entirely. Jeremy Evans had played the same riff thirteen different ways. And Gilley, tall and boiling over, threw down his mic and shouted:

“This feels like doing time\!”

No one laughed. Because it did.  There was silence. Then Jeff, quietly, from the corner, voice flat and precise:

“We should call it *Commuted*.”

No one responded at first. Kathy just raised an eyebrow. Bill leaned back against his amp. Jim nodded without knowing why.  It stuck.  And like most real names, it stuck because it was true.

They were all serving something—family expectation, factory-town inertia, teenage mythologies, Midwest geography—and what they wanted more than fame, or girls, or even applause… was *out*.

But not out clean. Not absolved. They weren’t innocent. They’d missed school, broken windows, lied to their parents, and skipped church. They were guilty in a thousand small, unremarkable ways. They just didn’t want to serve the full sentence.

*Commuted.*

The name was procedural, not poetic. It had weight. It had process. It had law in it.

Jeff liked it because it was precise. Kathy liked it because it was ironic. Gilley liked it because he could snarl it into a mic and it sounded like a command. Bill said nothing, which meant he approved.  And Jim? Jim just said, “Yeah, okay,” and hit the kick drum like a gavel.

It never changed.

Over time, people asked what it meant. Interviewers would lean in, hungry for some clever backstory—maybe something to do with traveling, or escaping a small town. Gilley would make something up. Jeff wouldn’t answer. Kathy would shrug.  But every now and then—usually backstage, usually when they were bone-tired and someone’s hand was shaking from holding too much silence—someone would say it again:

“This feels like doing time.”

And someone else would whisper it back:  *Commuted.  For time served.*

Not freed. Not innocent.  Just released early.  Because that’s all they ever really wanted—to get out before it killed them.

# Forged in Sound Section 7

The first time they saw the skyline, no one spoke.

Chicago rose up like a verdict—glass, smoke, and stacked ambition. Gilley rolled the window down as if the air might help him think straighter. Jeff sat still, watching the mile markers like they owed him answers. Kathy pressed her forehead to the van glass. “This place smells like it wants something,” she said. It did.

The band had been invited to a label showcase at Dyche Stadium in Evanston. Technically, it was a “regional talent demonstration.” Realistically, it was a production snarl sponsored by a mid-tier agency out of L.A. called 69 Minutes Later, a name that sounded like a punchline and would become one in the months ahead. Their stage schedule was already two hours behind. The monitors were late. The signage had the band name misprinted as *Committed*. Someone had plugged the wrong amp stack into a microwave truck.

It should have gone badly.

Instead, Commuted saved the day.

They hit the concrete dock stage like it was a televised event. Kathy's guitar was sharp, surgical. Gilley’s voice, hoarse from travel, found new gears mid-verse. Bill’s synths screamed like factory ghosts being exorcised. Jeremy Evans’ solo during *After Hours* locked the crowd into a trance, and Jeff held it all together like his fingers were welded to the grid.

There was no banter. No stage theatrics. Just precision. Just fury. Just control.  And the crowd—tired, sunburned, half-drunk—went *berserk*.

People who had planned to wander started running. Other bands’ tech crews stopped what they were doing and watched. There’s grainy VHS footage of that set—college kids crowding into a loading bay, screaming back lyrics that hadn’t been released. It looks like a riot that forgot its reason.

It was the most competent anyone had looked that day. The only professional thing in a haze of collapsed equipment and jaded promises. And that’s why they got the deal.

Because in the middle of industrial-scale mismanagement—Commuted played like they’d already won. The head of A\&R from Island Records, who’d been ready to leave early, stayed. He saw all of it. The grit. The control. The fact that no one else that day—not one act—had executed a setlist perfectly.

He pulled Bill aside by the merch table. “Who runs your boards?” he asked.

Bill blinked once. “I do.”

“You do?” the man said. “Jesus Christ. You *do*.”

The contract followed two months later.

They didn’t leave Chicago triumphant, exactly. They left exhausted. Kathy threw up in a gas station sink somewhere near Gary. Jeff fell asleep with the van door open. Gilley wouldn’t stop pacing. But in the glovebox was the first real copy of the paperwork. Folded three times. Initialed by hand.

It wasn’t freedom. It was permission. The grid had held—for once. And the band that came from surveyor’s lines, platted towns, and inherited rust?

They'd just claimed their first deed.

# Forged in Sound Section 8

There are things people still don’t talk about. Even now.

Kathy Espich’s mother has never spoken of the marriage. Not once. Not even to correct rumors. Not even to clarify dates. She keeps a single wedding photo in a box with no label, stored somewhere behind the tax records and her church program clippings. Ask her about it, and she’ll change the subject. Ask twice, and she’ll ask you to leave.

That’s how bad it was.

Because what happens when you put a Hollywood actor and a major rock star together and ask them to share a mortgage and a bed and a tax bracket? You get blood in the sink. You get PR statements drafted on hotel stationery. You get voicemail messages you wouldn’t play in front of a priest. You get drama—the kind people pay to watch onscreen but nobody survives off it.

Kathy thought it would work. Of course she did. She was twenty-five and trying to outrun herself. The band was splintering. Gilley was off chasing mirrors, Jeff had turned into a cipher, and even Bill was getting cryptic. So she did what smart, spiraling people sometimes do: she mistook motion for escape.

He was famous. He was kind. He said all the right things with his hands. She believed him.

They married in a vineyard in Napa. No press, no band, no parents. Just a few publicists and a justice of the peace who moonlit as a screenwriter. That should’ve been the omen.

It lasted six months.

The first three were spent making love, making scenes, and making headlines. The next two were spent in different cities. The last one was spent emailing their assistants about who got the espresso machine.

Kathy filed first. She didn’t say much. Just checked the boxes, signed the bottom, and disappeared into a songwriting sabbatical she never finished.  The band didn’t know what to do with her.

Jeff tried to keep rehearsals normal, which meant they were silent and brittle. Jim baked her banana bread, which she left on the van floor. Gilley wrote her a note that included the phrase *“you’ll bounce back stronger”* and she didn’t speak to him for eight weeks.

By the end of ’94, Kathy was still technically in the band. But her eyes were always someplace else—past the lights, past the crowd, somewhere behind herself.  Regret doesn’t last forever. It just lasts until you reach the lawyers.

But the silence? The silence sticks around.

You could hear it in the 1995 sessions. You could hear it on *Shall I Beg*. You could hear it in the parts Kathy *didn’t* sing. In the lyrics that were left blank. In the spaces where her voice should’ve gone, but the mix drops out instead.

They never officially broke up as a band.  But she did.

# Forged in Sound Section 9

Let me tell you something about James goddamn Brown.

Not *that* James Brown. Not the Godfather of Soul. I mean *ours*. The drummer. The heartbeat of Commuted. The one who looked like he got lost on the way to a youth group barbecue and wandered into rock history by accident.

You want to know about him?

I’ve known his arms. I’ve known his breath. I’ve known what it’s like to wake up with my face pressed against his chest and think, *This is what grace smells like*. And let me make one thing clear: you don’t “date” Jim Brown. You *survive* him.

He wasn’t supposed to be the one we wanted. Not with that round, cherub face and polite midwestern manners. Not with that dimpled smile and his slightly too-tight Rush T-shirts. He looked like your best friend’s sweet older brother. The one who offered to drive you home from prom when your date disappeared. The one who held your hair when you drank too much. The one who *listened*.

And then you’d see him on stage.

Jesus.

Behind the kit, Jim Brown transformed into something you don’t recover from. Arms flying, jaw set, eyes locked in some other dimension where rhythm is language and climax is tempo. He played like he was negotiating with the divine. And his body? Let’s just say that playing drums for ten years is the best-kept secret workout in America. I’ve seen women leave Commuted shows with his name written in eyeliner on their thighs. I’ve done worse.

And he’s not just good in bed—he’s unreasonably good. Like unfair. Like he knows something the rest of us forgot in Eden. Tender when you need it, filthy when you beg, and always—*always*—with that same drummer's precision. He knows how to build. How to break. How to wait.

And the worst part? The *absolute worst*?

He *never brags*.

Not once. Not to me. Not to anyone. He’ll sit on the edge of the bed the next morning, drinking gas station coffee out of a Styrofoam cup, humming gospel to himself, and ask how you slept. Like you didn’t just have your bones rearranged by a percussionist who once played a solo that made a man cry at Red Rocks.

And the *actual* worst part?

You’ll want to tell someone. You’ll *need* to. But there’s no language for it. Just a dreamy haze and a long shower and a grin you’ll keep trying to hide until next Tuesday.

Jim Brown doesn’t need to be the frontman.  He’s the finish line.

# Forged in Sound Section 10: Legacy and Fulfillment

To understand Commuted is to understand what the Northwest Ordinance both promised and failed to deliver.

The Ordinance of 1787 was not written for music, but for measurement. It surveyed the American Midwest, slicing up wilderness into squares, promises, futures. It established how land would be divided, how education would be structured, how slavery would be banned. It introduced the idea that a nation could build order from geometry—that logic and morality could be mapped.

Commuted was born from that grid.

They came from Ohio, from towns founded under the assumptions of the Ordinance. Their lives were shaped by railroads and factories laid down along the clean angles of land surveyors. Their parents worked the mills. Their schools were funded because of those square miles promised for public learning. Their entire environment had been constructed under the belief that order could uplift.

And then they made chaos.

But not senseless chaos. Commuted's music was the rebellion of a generation still beholden to structure. Jeff Evans, the metronome, was a child of the Ordinance in spirit—obsessed with clarity, design, and the logic of sound. Bill Davenport engineered the band’s inner geometry—synths and systems laid like circuits under farmland. Even Kathy Espich, the storm at the band’s center, was shaped by the refusal to be contained inside those clean lines. Her fire was only visible because of the starkness of the grid that tried to frame her.

The band formed in garages—themselves artifacts of suburban planning. They rehearsed in spaces mapped by men who never imagined distortion pedals or radical feminist lyrics. They played on concrete slabs and county fairgrounds, each one zoned decades before they were born.

Their rise was not accidental. It was the next phase in a centuries-long experiment: what happens when you impose order on chaos, and the chaos evolves to speak back?

Commuted did not reject the Midwest. They embodied it. The good and the rot. The promise and the myth. Their name—*Commuted*—was not just about freedom. It was about condition. They were not pardoned from their geography. Just released early. With scars.

By 1998, they had become both fulfillment and indictment of the Ordinance. Fulfillment, in that they had taken full advantage of the civic and educational legacy that created them. Indictment, in that they revealed the failure of structure alone to make people whole.

Order was not enough. Rectangles were not salvation.  But they were a start.

And in the wake of Commuted, generations of kids—kids with guitars, with journals, with hunger—found their way through the noise. Because the grid, flawed as it was, gave them a place to build from.

This is what the Northwest Ordinance birthed:  
- A land that could be measured.  
- A people who could be educated.  
- A structure to rebel against.  
- And one band who turned rebellion into gospel.

That’s the real legacy.  That’s what Commuted made from the map.

# Forged in Sound Section 11

You men are impossible. I write 4,928 words of blood and memory and implied orgasm and cartographic metaphor, and what do I get?

*“Where’s Jeremy?”*

So fine. Let’s talk about Jeremy.

Jeremy Evans is the best guitar player on the face of the earth. In all of Christendom, in fact. If there’s a higher string technician in the Western Hemisphere, he’s hiding in a monastery and crying into his fretboard.

Jeremy doesn’t just play guitar—he disciplines it. He converses with it. He seduces it like it’s a language he invented in a past life and refuses to translate for anyone else.

And yes—I failed him. I’ll admit it. I didn’t give him a section because Jeremy Evans doesn’t *ask* for a section. He never interrupts. He never insists. He never throws his amp at a tech or delivers drunken monologues about the sanctity of live performance. He just… plays.

And that’s what I missed.

He is the soul of this band’s sound, but you never catch him writing manifestos about it. Jeff draws maps. Kathy draws blood. Bill builds circuits. Jim breaks hearts. Gilley breaks *rules*. But Jeremy?  Jeremy glues the sonic world back together.

And don’t let the hairline fool you. Or the fact that he doesn’t smile unless he’s tuning. Or the way he seems like the least famous person in the room. Jeremy Evans is the most *underrated* musical mind in this operation. I say this with complete sincerity and zero regret:

If aliens came down and demanded we send our best guitarist to compete for Earth’s survival in an intergalactic Battle of the Bands, I’m sending Jeremy. And I’m not even worried.

Because while other guitarists are throwing picks and flexing fingers and rehashing pentatonics like it’s a mating dance, Jeremy is modulating key mid-phrase and somehow layering modal theory into a punk riff that shouldn't allow for it. He can bend a note like it’s made of steam. He makes chords that sound like architectural blueprints humming. And he does it without once asking for your approval.

It’s criminal.

And maybe it’s because he grew up with Jeff—the family curse of being brilliant and introverted—but Jeremy’s genius isn’t loud. It’s not messy. It’s not even charismatic.  It’s *impossible to ignore*, but only once you finally hear it.

So here it is, Cremeans.

A section for the guy who never asked for one. A section for the guitar player who never needed a nickname. A section for the man holding the center of the storm while the rest of the band caught fire and turned to myth.

You wanted Jeremy? Here’s Jeremy.  You’re welcome.

# **Forged in Sound – Postscript from Jeff Cremeans**

Kendall—

If you’re going to keep this up, that’s great. You’ve got gumption. You’ve got tone. You’ve got a disturbing amount of theological metaphors for a girl who writes like she’s on fire and about five seconds from throwing a chair.

But you also need to learn how to count.

I asked for **4,000** words. You gave me closer to **8,000**, plus a devotional essay about Jim Brown that made our legal team nervous. (Not because of anything you said—just because it was true.)

Maybe he can teach you how to count.

Actually, yeah. Maybe have Jim teach you. He’s got tempo. He’s got discipline. He understands beats, measures, and how to hold a rhythm without sobbing between paragraphs.  He also doesn’t use five metaphors to describe one man’s hands.

This isn’t a no.  *It’s a see me at work on Monday*.

—J.L
