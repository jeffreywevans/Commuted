**📰 People Magazine, June 19, 1995 Edition**

**"Why Commuted’s Controversial Album Art Might Be the Most Honest Image of 1995"**

**Byline:**

Marcy Ellison, Senior Culture Reporter, *People*

**Opening Paragraph:**

"\*When Kathy Espich, frontwoman of rising alternative rock band Commuted, appeared in a leaked promotional image for the group’s upcoming album *That Look of Release,* few could have anticipated the reaction.

The photo — softly lit, tastefully candid, evocative rather than explicit — ignited a national conversation faster than any music video or award show performance this year.

It is not graphic.  
It is not salacious.  
It is not vulgar.

And yet, to some, it feels more dangerous than any scandal Hollywood has managed to cook up so far.\*"

**The Image That Won’t Stay Quiet**

"\*In the image, Espich stares directly into the lens.  
She is bare — but unafraid, unashamed, and (most dangerously) *unguarded.*  
There is no strategic shadow play, no desperate attempt to titillate or conceal.

What exists instead is a raw, golden intimacy:  
a woman allowing herself to be seen,  
without permission,  
without apology.\*"

**Industry Panic vs. Artistic Integrity**

"\*Executives at Island Records are reportedly split — some praising the 'authentic, disruptive spirit' of the campaign, while others fear major retail chains may refuse to stock the album unless alternative covers are distributed.

('We are committed to Kathy’s vision,' a label spokesperson said carefully last week, 'but also mindful of… the broader marketplace.')\*"

**Cultural Experts Weigh In**

"\*Dr. Sandra Kepler, professor of Cultural Studies at NYU, argues that the uproar is less about the nudity itself and more about what the image denies to its critics:

'This isn’t objectification,' Kepler notes. 'It’s self-possession. Kathy Espich’s direct gaze and unfiltered posture deny the viewer their accustomed power position. She is neither beckoning nor pleading. She simply is — and that is far more unsettling to American culture than anything explicit could ever be.'\*"

**A Generation’s Conflicted Response**

"\*Among younger fans, the photo has already become a banner of sorts — photocopied, scanned, and even tattooed onto forearms and shoulders.

('It’s not sexy,' said Allison Vega, 17, from New Jersey. 'It’s real. She looks like she knows something the rest of us are scared to find out.')\*"

**What Comes Next**

"\*As *That Look of Release* moves closer to its late summer launch, all eyes remain on Commuted —  
not because of what they are hiding,  
but because of what they are refusing to hide.

In a year of manufactured outrage and carefully controlled pop star meltdowns, Kathy Espich has offered something radically different:

**"a nakedness of soul, captured in amber, soft as a whisper — and just as impossible to contain.**"

**Closing Pull Quote:**

"\*Some albums break the charts.  
Some break the rules.

If the early signs are right,  
**Commuted may break America’s ability to lie to itself.**

And in 1995 —  
that’s the most dangerous release of all.\*"
