## **EXT. NORMANDY COUNTRYSIDE – LATE AFTERNOON – JUNE 6, 1998**

Hedgerows like green ribcages. A field of poppies flashing scandal-red toward the sea. The wind moves the wheat in long, collective shivers. The sky is a silk scarf, blue and a little drunk on summer.

The farmhouse is stone and stubborn; a temporary cathedral dressed in bunting. Inside the courtyard: tables in white cloth, glassware catching light like small bells. A quartet warms up under an apple tree. Someone is arranging oysters on beds of shaved ice that look like powdered moonlight.

---

## **TITLE CARD (INVISIBLE, FELT): JEFF + NETTIE.**

I stand with a program curled in my fist and pretend the ocean is a metronome. Jim’s beside me doing what he always does on the days I am made of nerves and knives—he practices a sunshine so casual you want to slap it off his face or marry it. Light denim jacket. Collar open. That infuriatingly easy stance like every room already loves him.

**JIM** (soft, warm)
We’re in heaven, Kendall.

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
I am in glass.

He knows where my eyes won’t go. Over there: Gilley, long frame in a suit that fights him, shoulders already loose with champagne. Bill, squared-off in black, scanning sound like a border guard. Jeremy—the younger—watching Jeff with the tethered pride of a little brother who built the rocket and now has to let it fly.

The absence hums as loud as the string quartet’s D. Someone should have invited the danger and didn’t. Kathy is not here. Her name rustles through the hedges, swirls into every pause like a draft that won’t stop. We steer our eyes around the empty place where she would have stood, hands stuffed in her jacket pockets, chin up like a dare. We pretend the breeze is all that is cutting our eyes.

---

## **INT. SMALL ROMANESQUE CHAPEL – MOMENTS LATER**

Stone cool as older hands. Bees weaving a lazy math in the flowers. Nettie walks the aisle in a simple dress that knows her shape and keeps its mouth shut. Jeff is every paradox he has ever been—still and volcanic, a dam, a prayer. The way he looks at her is what you call music when your throat won’t make sound.

Vows. A murmur of Latin like a velvet saw. I swallow hard because I love them and because I’m bad at not narrating, even in my own skull. The priest speaks of covenant; my ribs repeat percussion. The ring slides; the room lifts; the air changes species.

**APPLAUSE.** We mean it.

I glance at Jim. He’s grinning like summer itself put on boots and walked into church. I want to tell him my stomach is a rehearsal space where the floorboards keep giving way. I want to tell him the way I am here and also backstage inside a future scene, tasting salt and lighting and the private oath that’s not fit for the sacristy. But he squeezes my fingers like a drum count-in. One-two-three-four. We breathe.

---

## **EXT. COURTYARD RECEPTION – GOLDEN HOUR**

Cider in sweating glasses. Butter in the sunlight, soft as forgiveness. Laughter ricochets around the stone like coins in a fountain.

Speeches. Gilley goes for charm and lands on real. Bill keeps it spare and true, which is how he wires his machines and his heart. Jeremy tells a story that is all angles and tenderness and ends with everyone clapping harder than they need to. I take notes in my head, then in my little black book, pretending I’m doing my job because I am and because I need the cover.

You can hear when a party begins to believe in itself. Chairs skew. Jackets migrate to chair backs. A baby learns new vowels. A man from Nettie’s side tells me the light in Normandy tastes different from the light in Ohio, and I nod like that’s a normal sentence on a normal day. The quartet turns into a sextet after the caterer’s nephew pulls a fiddle out of nowhere. The grass becomes a forgiving dance floor.

Absence makes itself a guest. It keeps sitting in our laps. Two women near the cider barrel whisper “Was it necessary?” in perfect English and I look anywhere else. When I’m not looking up Kathy’s ghost in every corner, I’m watching Jim—his way of being haloed by ease. He laughs like a percussion roll, low and round. He floats. He’s so calm I want to crawl out of my skin just to show him the map of nerves tattooed on its underside.

**JIM**
You’re buzzing.

**KENDALL**
You’re not.

**JIM**
Somebody’s gotta hold baseline.

He pulls me into the shade of the apple tree where the sound board hums and cables nest like benevolent snakes. There’s a slab of stone there that looks like it used to be an altar and now is a table for extra napkins. We lean against it and watch the tide of cousins and bandmates and strangers smooth itself out.

**JIM**
Want to disappear?

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
Every minute.

## **EXT. CATERING TENT / SERVICE FLAP – DUSK**

Behind the white canvas is the country of **Backstage**, where the platos are plain and the air smells like nutmeg and dish steam. The cooks are speaking French that I understand with my wrists, not my ears. A woman with saint’s hands pipes cream into something so delicate it might faint.

The head caterer—a man with wrists like oaks—sees Jim, sees his smile, and gives us both a nod that says: yes, fine, hide here; this is a church too. Jim steals two strawberries and a laugh. He’s so buoyant I feel stupid for being human.

Beyond the canvas: another room—half corridor, half storage—spine of the farmhouse. Coats on hooks, stacked crates of cider bottles that smell like autumn’s throat. A single bare bulb makes everyone honest.

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
This is what we mean by backstage. Not velvet. Not mirrors. A spine of a building; a pulse where the night goes to change shirts. No one claps for it. Therefore it is holy.

I tug him into the dim. My lungs are a brass section, badly tuned.

**JIM** (softly)
Kendall.

The way he says my name is a drum brush on a snare: feathered, rhythmic, inevitability in drag. I want to believe in the version of myself who does not shake. I want to commit a small blasphemy to make the world level again.

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
Why I like it here: because the overture is different, and the audience is absence. Because this is where nerves become ritual. Because I can turn the mess of feeling into a sacrament with a single choice of mouth and spine.

I step into him. Kiss. It starts with breath and the apple on his tongue. His hands find my back and move like rehearsal—no panic, all time. The bulb hums above us like a chorus of crickets.

A coat hem brushes my calf. A crate creaks. The farm’s old bones listen.

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
There are twelve ways to say devotion and none of them belong in the program. So I pick the thirteenth. The one that empties my head and fills it with a low, stunned light. The one that takes the chaos of a wedding—joy and rage and absence wearing silk—and makes it gentler by letting it pass through me.

I kneel, not for him, not exactly. For *us*, which is bigger, which is a drumline that runs from Chicago load-in to a Norman corridor. He is warm. He is ugly-beautiful with need. He is still so relaxed it makes me laugh against him.

**JIM** (barely audible, a break in a wave)
Hey.

He tastes like summer noon and all the doors that weren’t supposed to open. He puts his hand in my hair and doesn’t direct a single thing. I do that. I decide the tempo. I decide when the room stops.

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
Why do I like swallowing backstage? Because there’s nowhere to set the glass down. Because the night is an animal and I keep it from bolting by letting it become a thing my body knows how to receive. Because I want to walk back into the sunlight of the speeches with the secret inside me, humming like a second pulse. Because it is private and it is not—because there’s always a clatter, always a footstep near enough to make me better and braver.

The bulb flickers once, twice. Somewhere a pan falls like a cymbal hit and someone swears in French that could bless a battlefield. I do not flinch.

When his breath catches I hold, steady as a stagehand. When the band in the courtyard starts something raucous, I close my eyes and swallow all the noise, the part of him that was tremor a second ago and is quiet now. The sacrament completes. The ritual ends when I decide it does: a thumb across my lip, a laugh I can’t help, a look up that says *we’re fine*.

**JIM** (quiet, astonished and not at all)
Christ.

He stoops and gathers me up with the patience of a man who knows there’s still cake to be cut. He kisses the top of my head like a benediction. He buttons my hair back into place with his fingers. He is so absurdly calm I want to cry with relief and fury.

## **INT. CATERING KITCHEN – CONTINUOUS**

We walk past the saints with piping bags. No one looks; we are just another pair of forks passing through the sink. The head caterer gives me a glass of water like he was always going to. I drink it like absolution.

**JIM** (leaning in, conspiratorial)
You good?

**KENDALL** (labeling it, because that’s what I do)
A little holy. A little hungry.

**JIM**
We’ll fix both.

He means cake. He always means cake. I smile like a criminal who stole the crown jewels and got away.

## **EXT. COURTYARD – TWILIGHT INTO NIGHT**

The air has gone from gold to bruised-blue. Fairy lights string their own constellations above us. Nettie’s uncle dances like a sailboat in heavy weather; Nettie’s aunt dances like a surgeon.

Jeff laughs and the sound shocks people. He doesn’t do it often enough for the room to have built a home around it. Jeremy watches that laugh like a guard dog allowed to lie down. Gilley steals the microphone and tells a story that ends with the word “tractor” and everyone howling. Bill has drifted to the musicians and is showing the fiddler something that bends the air warmer around them.

At the edge of the dance floor, absence stands. I swear I see Kathy in the angle two women make when they lean in to protect a cigarette from the wind. I see her in the thick cut of the moonlight. I see her in Jeff’s blink, the one he does when something hurts and he’s agreed not to discuss it. The story of why she wasn’t invited is a minefield with perfectly arranged flowers planted on top.

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
A wedding is a treaty. It’s also a purge. Today had to be clean. That’s the official sentence. But I can feel where the edit was made. I can feel the paragraph that got cut bleeding through the white space.

Jim’s hand finds my hip. Baseline, again. We dance a little the way people dance in places where their shoes weren’t designed to touch grass. He whispers against my temple how proud he is of Jeff, how right Nettie looks in the deep part of herself, how he’s thinking of buying a particular snare and naming it after the river we can smell on the night.

**JIM**
You okay?

**KENDALL**
I did a thing in the back room I can’t put in the guestbook.

**JIM** (smiles into my hair)
It’s in *my* guestbook.

He’s teasing, soft as laundry and wicked like a lit match. The calm of him makes the storm of me feel like weather, not disease.

## **EXT. EDGE OF THE FIELD – LATER**

Cicadas replace violins. The kids crashed on blankets look like discarded angels. The cake leans in a way that suggests it had a good time.

Jeff and Nettie walk the perimeter like two people learning how to occupy a larger outline. When they come to us, I hug him long enough that he pats my back in warning that I might drown the both of us. Nettie’s eyes are bright—not with tears, but with an accuracy that can see a hundred yards and still track your pulse. She squeezes my hands. We don’t mention the empty chair that followed them around the party like a polite dog.

**NETTIE**
Promise you’ll write the part where we got the vows wrong and it still worked.

**KENDALL**
Promise.

**JEFF** (looking dead at Jim)
You good?

**JIM**
Never better, brother.

They walk on. A breeze steals the sweat off my neck and returns it as gooseflesh.

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
There’s a thing that happens when you take a private devotion into your body and then try to rejoin the tribe. You feel split. You feel newly minted and also counterfeit. You want to tell someone and you want to keep your mouth stitched shut forever. You want to smash a glass for the noise of it. You want to be a glass.

Jim pulls me down to the grass. We lie on our backs and stare at the kind of night you could unspool and knit into a sweater. He links our fingers. His thumb moves in a rhythm that could power a city if you sank wires into my wrist.

**JIM**
We’ll go home after the breakfast thing. Crash for fourteen hours. You can yell at me for being a labrador.

**KENDALL**
You don’t get yelled at for being sunlight.

**JIM**
Everyone gets yelled at for something.

He’s right. My whole life is the list of things I yell at. My body is the list of things I bless by mouth.

## **INT. FARMHOUSE HALL / RESTROOM MIRROR – NEAR MIDNIGHT**

I check my face for the scandal of me and find only a woman who knows, now, a little more about how to work with thunder. Lipstick: slightly smudged, in the way that says yes, but tastefully. Hair: a history of fingers. Eyes: the kind of bright that makes people ask if you’re feverish or in love.

On the stall door someone carved initials twenty years ago and the knife work still looks fresh. I touch the groove and think about how the day was full of choices like blades—cut this, keep this, hide this, hallow this. I think about how I love them all even when I’m angry with half of them. I think about the memory settling warm and heavy in my gut, a small lit coal I chose to carry.

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
Why I enjoy it, the way I do, where I do: because I’m an archivist of the real, and sometimes the real needs a body to live in. Because backstage is where truth gets dressed, and sometimes I want to taste truth before it puts the dress on. Because I am a witness, and because witnesses are always a little hungry.

---
## **EXT. COURTYARD – LAST SONG**

Everyone sways to something borrowed from a jukebox that lived through 1979. Arms around shoulders. Two drunk men inventing a harmony so bad it becomes faith. Nettie’s head on Jeff’s shoulder, his hand on her hair, their gravity settled.

The night releases us one by one like a teacher ringing a bell. The quartet packs up. The caterers snap lids and vanish into the mystery of vans. Fireflies rehearse their Morse code.

Jim kisses me goodnight when I’m already awake. He is unbothered in the way that doesn’t insult your storm but gives it a porch to rain from. We walk toward the guesthouse, and I feel the step inside me that was not there when the day began.

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
We archive the power struggles and the public wars. We title them. We footnote them. But the liturgy of the small, the private—this is the ink that doesn’t fade. If someone ever asks why, I will say: because communion is not always bread. Because the body remembers what the mind wants to edit. Because I am my own chapel, and tonight I let the music in through the oldest door I have.

We pass the apple tree. The wind turns a page. In the dark, you can hear the sea erasing the world.

## **EXT. NORMANDY COUNTRYSIDE – AFTERNOON – JUNE 6, 1998**

The wheat bows like parishioners in the wind. A sky the color of linen stretched taut, stitched with gulls. On the bluff above Omaha Beach, a rented stone farmhouse has been sanctified into a cathedral of union. Jeff Evans and Nettie marry in the country where men once stormed sand with rifles; today they storm it with champagne.

**TITLE CARD:** Normandy, France. Jeff + Nettie.

The invitations had gone out like scripture, except to one name. Kathy Espich’s absence was not oversight—it was incision. Nettie cut her out the way a surgeon spares the heart from a tumor. The logic was simple: Kathy’s very presence was an accelerant. And lately? Kathy was a matchbox.

---
## **INT. CHAPEL – STONE AND LIGHT – 4 P.M.**

A Romanesque jewel box, all arches and beeswax candles. Nettie walks the aisle in silk that glimmers like mercy. Jeff is granite, trembling only in the eyes. The vows are the kind that make even atheists sit straighter.

Kendall (me, the archivist) sits with Jim. His hand is calm weight on mine, baseline steady. I keep scanning the pews, looking for a dirty plaid shirt that isn’t there. Kathy would have leaned in the doorway like a storm that refused to wait for the priest to finish. She would have howled when they kissed. Nettie knew this. Nettie said no.

---
## **EXT. FARMHOUSE COURTYARD – GOLDEN HOUR**

A reception as cinematic as a Ric Ocasek chorus: oysters sweating on ice, cider in sweating glasses, children already sticky with meringue. Gilley towers in a suit that mocks him. Bill fiddles with the PA like he’s tuning God. Jeremy (the younger) circles Jeff like a moon orbiting its planet.

Jim is untroubled, radiant even. He jokes with Nettie’s dad, eats strawberries like sacraments. Meanwhile I’m restless, mouth dry, eyes slipping to the periphery. I keep expecting Kathy to walk in, cigarette dangling, announcing herself the way Dylan announced electricity at Newport—one strike of distortion and everything changed.

## **INTERCUT – ELSEWHERE – THAT SAME HOUR**

**EXT. PARIS BAR – LATE AFTERNOON**

Kathy Espich is in a basement bar two hundred kilometers away. Dyke hair freshly shorn into a jagged bob, her plaid traded for leather. She kisses a woman named Camille against the bathroom door so hard the hinges complain. Camille moans, Kathy laughs, the mirror rattles. She is electric now, openly sapphic, her body a new tuning fork.

If she had come to Normandy, she would have dragged this hunger into the reception hall, would have made Jeff’s wedding feel like an after-party for a riot. Nettie wasn’t wrong.

**BACK TO NORMANDY – EVENING**

The band toasts. Gilley makes a crude joke that earns laughter like broken glass. Bill raises his glass and says something concise and devastating. Jeremy tells a story of brotherhood that makes Jeff look down, embarrassed at the love.

Absent Kathy is louder than present Kathy. People whisper in English and French: *Was she banned? Did she refuse?* The women at the cider barrel hiss, “Like Clapton going electric,” as if queerness is an instrument that ruins harmony.

Jim notices my nerves. He leans down:

**JIM** (soft, teasing)
We’re fine. You’re buzzing.

**KENDALL**
Because she’s not here.

**JIM**
That’s the point.

He smiles like the world’s not about to split down its middle.

## **EXT. SERVICE CORRIDOR – DUSK**

I drag Jim behind the catering tent, into the artery of the farmhouse. Crates of cider bottles. Coats on hooks. A single bare bulb humming overhead. Backstage, as sacred as any stage.

This is where my storm finds ritual. I kneel not just for him but for the chaos itself, swallowing it like communion. His calm makes my frenzy holy. His hand in my hair says: the world can break and we’ll still keep time.

Why do I love swallowing backstage? Because it turns absence into presence. Because the ghost of Kathy, the risk of explosion, the exile humming around us—it all pours into me, hot and undeniable, and I carry it back out onto the dance floor, secret burning in my chest like a coal.

---
## **EXT. COURTYARD – TWILIGHT**

Fairy lights string constellations above the tables. Jeff laughs, rare and volcanic. Nettie’s eyes are bright with triumph. The crowd sways to borrowed songs.

But every shadow is Kathy. She’s the cigarette cupped against the wind. She’s the uninvited chord humming under the vows. She’s the electricity no one dares to plug in tonight.

Nettie wins—her day remains intact, polished, orderly. But Commuted’s family is not a family without disruption, and everyone knows it. The marriage is sealed in joy, yes, but also in silence—the silence of the band’s most dangerous member kept two hundred kilometers away, kissing women and lighting fuses.

---
## **EXT. FIELD EDGE – MIDNIGHT**

Cicadas grind. The cake leans like it, too, has had too much champagne. Fireflies sketch Morse code for secrets. Jeff and Nettie walk arm in arm, new gravity between them.

I lie in the grass with Jim, his calm hand tapping rhythms on my hip. I can’t stop thinking of Kathy’s hunger, her new electricity, the way she’s rewriting herself in real time. It’s not just lust—it’s voltage. Like plugging the whole band into a new socket.

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
A wedding is order. Kathy is chaos. Today order won. But chaos is patient, and chaos is hungry.

## **INT. ROMANESQUE CHAPEL – NORMANDY – 4:02 P.M.**

Stone keeps secrets. Beeswax candles make the air taste like old honey. A small country church built for humility takes on the color of certainty: NETTIE in silk that understands restraint; JEFF at the altar, the fixed star the whole band once slung chaos around.

**PRIEST** (soft Latin, softer French)

## **EXT. BASEMENT BAR – RUE OBERKAMPF – PARIS – 4:02 P.M.**

Brick sweats. A neon sign spits its blue out onto the sidewalk like a dare. **KATHY ESPICH** stubs out a cigarette with the sole of a boot that’s done duty in snow and clubs and police stations. Her hair is jagged, her mouth a weapon, her body tuned to a new frequency. The doorman knows her by posture.

**DOORMAN (FR.)**
Bonsoir, mademoiselle.

She doesn’t answer. She descends.

Inside, the jukebox is a priest screaming Siouxsie. **CAMILLE** (black dress, shoulder-blade tattoo like a comma) presses a glass into Kathy’s hand. The glass is cold; the hand is not. Paris breathes her in like she’s what the city has been waiting to exhale.

**KATHY (V.O.)**
He wants the key back. “Late. Leave my key and go.”
Eight years of metal in my pocket, pressed to my thigh like a truth. Now he wants me house-broken, door-left-locked—grown-up. I should throw it in the Seine. I should swallow it.

She drinks instead. The room tilts, then levels. Camille smiles as if smiling were a verb with a body count.

## **EXT. FARMHOUSE COURTYARD – NORMANDY – GOLDEN HOUR**

**GILLEY** (grinning, to Jeff)
Welcome to the grown-up table, mister “don’t touch my signal chain.”

**KENDALL (V.O.)**
That’s the quiet coup. Gilley stepping into Jeff’s gravity, not fighting it. Nettie called the vote before anyone stood up to speak.

Don’t narrate. Dance.

## **INT. PARIS BAR – WOMEN’S TOILET – SAME**

The stall door is a makeshift confessional with a broken latch and lipstick hieroglyphs. Camille pushes Kathy against cool tile and kisses like an open flame. Kathy laughs into her mouth, pushes back, takes control the way she always does when every other door gets barred.

**KATHY** (against Camille’s lip)
Say my name.

Camille does. The syllables enter the room like the first note of a riot. Kathy hikes Camille’s thigh up, drags the fabric, finds slick heat. No euphemism, no poetry that lies—tongue, pressure, the gasp that turns into a curse. When Camille kneels, the laughter turns into breath that can’t choose a shape.

**KATHY (V.O.)**
I don’t care who’s aligning with who when you’re there. I don’t even hear the politics when you get your mouth right. I care when I’m alone. So I don’t get alone.

Camille licks like devotion, not duty. The tile is cold, the stall is heaven by accident. Kathy’s fingers in her hair say: harder. Kathy’s heel on the toilet paper dispenser says: now. Kathy’s throat opens and the sound that comes out is part wolf, part feedback, part prayer.

She comes loudly enough that the bartender out front raises an eyebrow and decides to turn the music up a notch. Kathy grins like a felony.

**KATHY**
At least when I’m being licked, I don’t fucking ***care***.

Her voice breaks on “care,” which is how you know the sentence is not true.

## **EXT. COURTYARD – NORMANDY – SPEECHES**

STANDING MIC under an apple tree.

## **INT. ISLAND RECORDS – CONFERENCE ROOM, MIDTOWN MANHATTAN – LATE JUNE 1998 – AFTERNOON**

Glass box thirty floors up. The view is a gray aquarium of heat; out there the city boils like a soup no one asked for. Inside, the AC wheezes like an old smoker and loses. The table is lacquered mahogany, polished to a false god’s mirror. Someone staged bottled water like crystal soldiers. A bowl of hard candies sweats. Everyone in the room would rather be on a dock somewhere watching the Fourth of July load into the sky — everyone except the two people who came to draw blood.

**CREMEANS,** black flannel, day-old eyes, patience of a long-haul trucker pastor, sets a leather folio down as if it contains scripture and a grenade. **BILL,** hair neat, jaw wired tight, Ohio shoulders stuffed into a jacket he hates on principle, sits at his right. Bill’s hands are quiet; his knees are a metronome the table can feel.

Across from them, **SARAH MALCOLM** — Island A&R, late-20s, pin-straight hair, pen poised like a conductor’s baton. Sharp posture. Beautiful mind. You can smell courage on her like citrus. Next to her, two **ISLAND SUITS** with beach-weekend eyes: **HUTTON** (legal), thin lips like paper cuts; **MATHERS** (finance), tie already surrendered a half-inch to the heat. Behind them, a glass wall of Manhattan summer, daring them all to be honest.

A receptionist brings in coffee no one drinks. She leaves. The door clicks. The room seals. The countdown starts.

**HUTTON** (to the table, perfectly bored)
Gentlemen, Ms. Malcolm — thanks for coming in. I’m going to be efficient because— well, because one of us has kids in a minivan to the Poconos in three hours.

A brittle laugh. No one joins.

**HUTTON (CONT’D)**
Island has reviewed *Leave My Key and Go* in its current configuration and is… not accepting delivery. There are concerns: radio format fit, thematic cohesion, title sensitivity, single strategy. We’ll need the band to return to studio. Fresh ears. Fresh guidance.

**CREMEANS** (pleasant)
Define “not accepting.” Our agreement is very clear about acceptance standards: technical completion, delivery of masters, print-ready packaging, artwork, lyrics, ISRCs. We’ve delivered. You mean you don’t prefer it.

**MATHERS** (pushing a paper like it’s a shield)
We’re talking commercial viability, Jeff. — Sorry. Mr. Cremeans. The marketplace is—

**CREMEANS**
—volatile. Like your org chart. We read the trades.

Sarah doesn’t look at Bill, which is how Bill knows she agrees.

**SARAH** (careful; honest)
There’s a lot of movement above me. Above all of us. New parent, rumored sale, budgets that do whatever they want. I’m not going to pretend any of that isn’t true. But that’s…context. The notes still stand. The record, as-is, will be difficult to work.

**BILL** (quietly, a sharpened edge)
Because the title says the quiet part loud?

Silence. The AC coughs.

Bill stares at the table because if he looks anyone in the eyes he’ll scorch them. He is thinking of a brass key riding a mail slot in Paris and the sound it made — metal on metal — theft disguised as logistics. He is thinking of outvoting and outliving and the ugly math of being a band.

**CREMEANS**
Our deal is lopsided. You made it lopsided, in *our* favor, in a bidding war! You did because you *believed* you were buying rocket fuel. Now that it burns hot, you want a refund for the heat?

**HUTTON** (tired smile)
We want a product we can stand behind.

**BILL**
We gave you a record you can’t stand in front of. Those are not the same.

Sarah’s pen ticks against her notebook. Tick. Tick. A drummer in the next world.

**SARAH** (aimed at Bill, but for the room)
Let’s separate two truths. One: the album is a monster. It’s brilliant and difficult and it doesn’t care about any playlist you’re going to put it in. Two: difficult records die all the time for banal reasons. If I walk upstairs with *Key* as-is, I may lose. Not because you’re wrong — because I don’t have a company right now, I have a set of forwarding addresses.

**CREMEANS**
So it’s not the record. It’s the politics.

She doesn’t answer because she already did.

**MATHERS** (pragmatic, begging the calendar)
We don’t have time to litigate philosophy. Solution: we pick a new producer partner, neutral to both sides, and you go back in for six weeks. Tighten. Clarify. Re-sequence. Bring us a single we can walk into Z100 and not get laughed out of the lobby.

Bill’s molars grind. Out on Sixth Avenue a bus sighs like a big animal giving up.

**BILL** (voice lower now)
There is no Z100 version of “return my key.” There’s only true or not.

**HUTTON**
Artistic integrity is admirable. It also doesn’t pay marketing.

**CREMEANS**
Yes it does. Just not on your schedule.

He opens the folio. Paper glows like knives.

**CREMEANS (CONT’D)**
Here’s our counter: fine. You choose a producer. You put your thumbprint on the process. We go back in. We cut what you think needs cutting, we isolate what scares you, we put three mixers in a room and let them fight to the death. We give you your shot at “viability.” And then? Acceptance is automatic. You picked the partner. If you reject the record after your man touches it, you’re rejecting your own judgment, not ours. The acceptance clause triggers if Island refuses to accept the deliverable for reasons of taste. Only material defects delay delivery. We’ll stipulate dates. We’ll stipulate fees. We’ll stipulate you own the embarrassment if the thing you asked for is the thing you can’t stomach.

Hutton’s smile cracks. Mathers looks at the calendar like it just bit him. Sarah, God bless that brain, looks at Bill for the first time, and there it is — a spark: she sees the trap and she likes it because truth should be a dare.

**SARAH**
And you’d agree to their name on it? Co-producer credit. Label partner. Someone we pick, not someone you wheel in on a velvet cart.

Bill swallows his soul and nods once. He can feel Kathy’s ghost licking the back of his teeth for agreeing to let someone else touch the sound. Four votes had already written this before the plane took off for LaGuardia. Jeff and Gilley hatched it — the worst kind of genius, where you surrender ground in order to control the entire war.

**BILL**
We’ll take your pick. You carry it.

It tastes like swallowing a car key. He does it anyway. Better this than exile. Better compromise than the cold outside of the room, which is where Kathy lives now — at least today.

**HUTTON**
You’re asking us to marry a failure in advance.

**CREMEANS**
You’re asking us to pretend your boardroom knows the difference between a chorus and a coven. Pick. A. Priest.

Mathers stares at Sarah. Sarah does the thing only the brave do in rooms like this: she tells the truth in an angle.

**SARAH** (professional; coded)
Commuted is right about the clause. And about the context. We have been—

(tiny pause; euphemism as bloodsport)
—reorganized three times in two years. There’s appetite upstairs for predictability. If we control the process, I can sell it. If they control it, this becomes a referendum on taste. And taste is a religion.

Hutton sighs like paperwork.

**HUTTON**
Names?

**CREMEANS**
We’ll entertain three from you. You’ll entertain three from us. You choose one of yours. We choose one of ours. Those two meet the band. The band chooses between the two.

**HUTTON**
Then you’re choosing.

**CREMEANS**
Choosing between your choices. That’s called consent in the real world.

Sarah’s mouth almost smiles. Almost.

**MATHERS**
Six weeks. Studio lockout. Weekly touch-bases. Two radio edits before week four. A sequencing pass delivered week five.

**BILL**
If we do radio edits, they will not lie. They will compress. That’s different.

**HUTTON**
We’ll see the lyrics before you track.

Bill laughs once; it sounds like a door bouncing on its chain.

**BILL**
You can see the lyrics. You don’t get to neuter them.

**CREMEANS** (never raising his voice)
Contractual: if Island’s chosen producer signs off on technical completion and delivery, you accept. We’ll include an objective engineer to certify. If you stall, you pay. If you reject, we’re free. Catalog reverts clause remains in force if Island defaults.

Mathers flips pages, calculating the price of courage. Hutton pretends to be offended by the smell of leverage.

**HUTTON**
That’s a weaponized acceptance clause.

**CREMEANS**
It’s the one you wrote when we were new and shiny and you thought you were immortal.

The air presses down. Thirty floors of June sit on everyone’s heads. Out in the city a siren wails like a diva. In here, silence feels like an x-ray — everyone’s bones lit up with what they won’t say.

**SARAH** (soft; chooses a side at cost)
I’ll shepherd. You’ll get process access. If you don’t pull a fire alarm every hour, I can keep the oxygen flowing. But I can’t promise what the building will look like in two months.

Bill hears the subtext: *I believe in your record. I might not have a badge to wear when it comes out.* He feels a bolt of ugly tenderness and files it under **People I will not hurt unless I have to**.

**CREMEANS**
Then we’re agreed on the skeleton. We’ll draft. Producers by Friday. We start after the holiday. Enjoy your long weekend.

He stands. Hutton stays seated a beat too long because power is a chair he believes in. Mathers has already mentally left to a grill he hasn’t cleaned since Memorial Day. Sarah stands when Bill does. They shake hands last, which is what you do when you want to say more with skin than you’re allowed to with language.

**SARAH** (quiet, just for him)
Don’t let them flatten the voltage.

**BILL** (hoarse)
They can’t reach the socket.

A flicker between them: complicity. Truth. A future where one of them loses a job and the other loses a little of himself and the record, either way, lives.

Bill closes his eyes and counts measures until wheels up become a promise: six weeks to build a trap and call it music. Six weeks to turn a label’s demand into an oath. Six weeks to prove an album about keys and doors can kick down the front gate of a dying company and leave the lights on behind it.

He breathes. He is a man who hates compromise and did it anyway. He is still in the room.
