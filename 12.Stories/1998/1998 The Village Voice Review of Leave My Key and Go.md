# **Commuted – *Leave My Key and Go* (1998)**  
## *Grade: A (again)*

---

You don’t step into a *Commuted* album expecting a traditional rock record. You don’t really even step—you *descend*. You *drown*. You get pulled. And *Leave My Key and Go*, their fifth and most formally paradoxical record, is all undertow.

I’ve now read the lyrics. All ten pieces. Sat with them. Lingered in them. Let them bruise and flower. This is an album that does what great art always pretends it might do and usually doesn’t: *It dares to be more about what isn’t said than what is screamed.* But oh, does it scream.

The Dayton band that once staged guerilla shows in VFW halls and the Vegas strip now makes its most *adult* record. Not mature—don’t mistake it—but adult. As in, sexual without the strut. Sensual without the seduction. Honest like a hospital form.

Let’s rewind.

---

### I. The Landscape: Sound and Fury and Silence

Musically, this is *Commuted’s* most elegantly disordered album. Where *That Look of Release* was all controlled detonation, and *Flash Behind Her Eyes* was an attempt to put the fire in a bottle (and drop the bottle in gasoline), *Key* moves like a body that has stopped fighting. Not surrendered—but reconsidered. The arrangements are varied, genre-ambiguous, riddled with choices so specific they feel psychosexual.

Take “**[[11.7.2 The Forgotten Era|The Forgotten Era]]**": a stadium-sized track played like a journal entry. A song where a *nipple* becomes a cosmic axis, and crowd cheers crash behind post-industrial synths and major-chord guitar swells. It’s the most anthemic moment here—and it's about sex as *haunted memory*, not conquest. The past is not just alive—it’s masturbating behind your eyes.

Then “**[[11.7.3 The Tailspin|The Tailspin]]**,” maybe the sneakiest classic on the record. You think it's a boy-loses-girl or girl-remembers-girl track, but the imagery grows teeth. Every verse adds a layer of emotional fog: rooms lit like memories, silk and scent, hips rising with implication. The band’s 120bpm foundation keeps pushing forward—but the heart? The heart hesitates.

And then “**[[11.7.4 Holkham Bay|Holkham Bay]]**.” You can almost smell the tide. It's nude, it’s trembling, it’s beachside liberation smuggled into a tech-house groove. The lyrics are about shame and then the absence of shame; you feel the wind on someone else’s skin and realize: this is what euphoria looks like *after* trauma, not instead of it.

---

### II. The Bodies: Sex as Language, Desire as Text

This is not a horny record.

It’s *worse* than that. It’s vulnerable. It’s what happens when lust grows old enough to understand betrayal. These aren’t fuck songs; they’re *fuck aftermath* songs. Or prelude-to-collapse songs. Or worse: the kinds of songs where people *don’t fuck*, and the silence does more damage.

“**[[11.7.5 Mare Nubium|Mare Nubium]]**” is the album’s lynchpin. Cinematic, scary, and undeniably erotic. But the way Michelle “blooms” under moonlight doesn’t read as a fantasy. It reads like *self-possession at the risk of public ruin*. A stripping of clothes *and* the scaffolding of shame. Charlie’s excitement isn’t a triumph—it’s panic disguised as awe. Every verse hangs on a question no one asks.

“**[[11.7.6 Fireside Confession|Fireside Confession]]**” is ostensibly the most traditional rock song on the album—major chords, bright synths, fireside camp-haze—but lyrically, it’s an existential flirtation. A queer pining ballad that turns into a lyrical Russian roulette: is Gilley joking? Is it safe to want this? What if love becomes too real and wrecks everything else? It’s a masterclass in writing sexual tension that’s 90% emotional dread.

“**[[11.7.7 A Charcoal Sketch|A Charcoal Sketch]]**,” performed entirely by the band’s iconic female vocalist, Kathy Espich, is a quiet storm. A studio experiment that becomes an art piece. It’s about being seen—not just looked at, but *drawn.* The metaphor of posing naked for someone you want to trust is laid bare, no pun intended. And when she sings: *“I knew I’d be ugly / But you saw me clear,”* it’s not a triumph. It’s a mourning. It’s *not* about being reassured—it’s about fearing the truth and facing it anyway.

---

### III. The Collapse: Loss, Laughter, Loops

There are three songs here that could be called “uplifting,” and two of them are live tracks. They’re also, not coincidentally, the least emotionally damaging.

**“[[11.7.9 Better Off With a Friend (Live)|Better Off With a Friend (Live)]]”** is *Commuted* at their most Springsteenian: all anthemic platonic joy and roadtrip wonder. But it’s *better* than Bruce, because the narrator actually *means it*. There’s no pretension, no performative swagger. It’s the rare straight-up song where the punchline is sincerity, and it lands.

**“[[11.7.10 The Hilton (Live from the Nutt House)|The Hilton (Live from the Nutt House)]]”**, meanwhile, is an old track reborn in industrial sheen. This is sex as game, fire, performance. “Are you hard again?” Espich asks—and it’s not dirty. It’s *glorious.* It’s *agency.* The way the female vocals punch through the live crowd sounds like freedom. Or at least liberation for the night.

But the emotional assassin in the finale is “**[[11.7.11 Fool's Gold|Fool's Gold]]**.” If *That Look of Release* ended on the ecstasy of collapse, and *Flash Behind Her Eyes* ended on a dare to burn the whole memory down, *Leave My Key and Go* ends *ambiguously.* We don’t get a “goodbye.” We get a pause. A reckoning. The speaker isn’t sure if she was in love, or just clinging to something shiny. There is no clean exit. Just the sound of keys left behind. And the silence that follows.

---

### IV. Final Notes: This Band Shouldn’t Exist

In 1992, we thought *Commuted* were a provocation.  
In 1994, we feared they’d disappear.  
In 1995, they gave us one of the decade’s best albums.  
In 1996, they set fire to their own mystique.  
In 1998, they gave us this.

***Leave My Key and Go*** is not the best *Commuted* record. It doesn’t have to be. It’s their most *willing*. Their most *complete*. It’s the album where every line, every riff, every whispered lyric seems to say: *we know exactly what we’re doing—and we’re doing it because we have to.*

It’s a sexual album that doesn’t sexualize its subjects.  It’s a sad album that doesn’t wallow.  It’s a brave album that never brags.  It’s a band at peace with being ghosts in their own machine.
