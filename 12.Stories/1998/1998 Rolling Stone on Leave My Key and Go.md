**COMMUTED: *Leave Your Key and Go* (Dayton Underground) A-**

Five albums in, these Ohio provocateurs have learned to weaponize their contradictions. Where *Flash Behind Her Eyes* felt like watching virtuosos wrestle with their own ambitions, *Leave Your Key and Go* finds Commuted comfortable in their role as America's most sexually frank rock band—which is to say, the most honest one.

Kathy Espich remains our most fearless chronicler of desire's messier territories. "*The Forgotten Era*" opens like vintage Zeppelin before morphing into something closer to Tori Amos fronting Jane's Addiction, all apocalyptic imagery wrapped around what might be the year's most unapologetically carnal chorus. When she sings about "*two fuckers free from fate's cruel cage*," you believe every syllable—not because it's shocking, but because it rings true to how people actually think and feel when clothes come off and inhibitions follow.

The album's secret weapon is its emotional intelligence. "*Better Off With a Friend*" could be dismissed as sentiment if it weren't so goddamn wise about the difference between loneliness and solitude, between settling and choosing. Meanwhile, "*Poe's Big Bang*" raids the literary canon for metaphors about sexual confusion that would make the Baltimore master himself blush, complete with a "Nevermore" reference that actually earns its keep.

Musically, they've found the sweet spot between their early punk-pop combustion and the more experimental terrain they've been mapping since *That Look of Release*. The rhythm section has developed into one of rock's most supple, capable of shifting from tech-house pulse ("*A Charcoal Sketch*") to driving anthemics ("*The Tailspin*") without missing a beat. Those "powerful guitars, driving drums" tag lines in their press materials undersell how sophisticated this unit has become.

What separates Commuted from the pack isn't just their willingness to sing about nipples and nudist beaches—though "*Holkham Bay*" might be the first great song about public nakedness since the Kinks' "Sunny Afternoon." It's their understanding that sexuality and vulnerability are the same conversation. "*A Charcoal Sketch*" captures the terror and exhilaration of being truly seen better than anything since Joni's *Blue*.

If there's a flaw, it's that Espich occasionally lets her literary ambitions outrun her melodic instincts—"*Mare Nubium*" works overtime to justify its classical allusions. But when Gilley hits his target, as on the devastating "Fireside Confession" or the triumphant closer "*The Hilton*," Commuted's operating on a level that makes most of their contemporaries sound like they're still playing dress-up.
