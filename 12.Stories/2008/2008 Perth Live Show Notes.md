# 27.  You
*August 29, 2025, 1:56:43 PM EDT*

>  You know what one of the most pungent and hilarious pieces of feedback has been? ---

That’s fantastic feedback, and honestly kind of delightful—the reader’s basically saying, “You nailed the smell of Midwest asphalt after rain, you nailed Tokyo neon, but where the hell’s *our* stop on the map?”

It highlights two things:

1. **You’ve made the setting tangible.** If someone can “feel the Midwest” in your work, you’re grounding all the chaos of Commuted in a reality that resonates. You’re not just writing about a band, you’re writing about place. That Midwest heaviness—factories, cheap motels, snowmelt on boots—is an invisible character.

2. **Readers want *their* world included.** By taking the band global, you’ve set the expectation that *every* corner of the planet is fair game. Skip Australia, and suddenly the map feels incomplete, especially to someone reading you from Sydney or Melbourne.

The irony? Australia’s perfect for Commuted’s arc. It’s got:
- **Festival culture**: Big Day Out, Falls Festival—venues where chaos thrives.
- **Isolation factor**: the feeling of being half a world away from their Ohio roots.
- **Rowdy crowds**: Australians have a reputation for hard partying at shows. The band would either thrive or combust in that environment.

So your reader’s not just nitpicking—they’ve actually pointed out a narrative opportunity. If you drop Commuted into Sydney or Melbourne in the late ’90s or 2000s, it would feel authentic and also appease that itch for geographic completeness.

If you want to lean into the comedy, you could even have a throwaway line like:
> *“We did Japan, we did Barcelona, but somehow we never made it to Sydney. To this day, Jeff swears it was Bill’s fault, Bill swears it was management’s, and Jeremy just says, ‘Mate, it’s for the best—we’d have been arrested at the airport.’”*

That way you acknowledge the omission and make it part of the lore.

>  What's impressive on Australia's West Coast?  Isn't it sort of less posh, more gritty, more industrial?
---

Western Australia (Perth, Fremantle, the mining belt) has a very different vibe from Sydney or Melbourne. Think fewer art-deco theaters and more **“salt and diesel”** atmosphere. It’s not “posh London” or “cosmopolitan Tokyo.” It’s wide skies, working-class grit, and the edge-of-the-world feeling—you can practically smell the ore dust and saltwater.

Here’s what makes it distinctive (and ripe for Commuted):

- **Perth** – It’s the most isolated major city on Earth. For a touring band, flying there is like playing a gig on Mars. That gives it a **mythic “we actually made it here” quality.**
- **Fremantle** – Port town, known for pubs, live music, and dockside grit. Fremantle Prison is now a UNESCO site, but in the 90s it still cast a long, dark shadow. You can almost hear a Commuted track rattling through those walls.
- **Mining wealth vs. working class** – WA is rich in resources (iron ore, gold, gas), but that doesn’t translate into glamour. The cultural vibe is more industrial—hard hats and union halls, not champagne launches.
- **Music scene** – Perth has a history of punching above its weight: INXS came through there, Tame Impala later. There’s a proud “you forgot about us, but we’ll make you remember” streak.
- **The ocean** – Brutal, endless, indifferent. A gig in Perth would feel like the band was standing on the edge of the known world, screaming into the void.

So yes—if Sydney feels like a slick showcase, Perth feels like a **gritty crucible.** It’s the kind of place where Commuted could play a venue that was half-abandoned warehouse, half-ironworkers’ bar, and it would feel more *true* to the band’s DNA than playing the Opera House.

> So, a Commuted show in Perth would be adored, and cause a ruckus?

Absolutely—it would be chaos in the best way. Perth audiences in the ’90s were **starved for international acts**, because so few bothered to cross the desert. When a band *did* come through, the energy wasn’t just “we’re excited to see you”—it was **pent-up, primal gratitude mixed with defiance.**

Commuted stepping on stage in Perth would feel like the circus finally came to town, but the circus had chainsaws and whiskey. Here’s why it would be adored and explosive:

- **Rarity factor:** Fans would show up knowing this might be the *only* time they’d see them in their city. That urgency makes crowds wilder, louder, less restrained.
- **Blue-collar grit:** WA crowds weren’t precious about “posh venues” or decorum. They’d embrace the raw, messy energy Commuted thrived on—beer flying, voices shredded, people moshing like their lives depended on it.
- **Mismatch with authority:** Local police and security in Perth back then were not prepared for a full-on international rock riot. Think overzealous clampdowns on crowd surfers, which only pours fuel on the fire.
- **The edge-of-the-world myth:** The band themselves would feel it—the weirdness of being on stage in the most isolated capital city on earth. That kind of surreal backdrop pushes performances into legendary territory.

So yes: **adored by fans, hated by authorities, remembered as bedlam.** Perth would carry that memory for decades, the way Cincinnati holds onto its Who concert tragedy or England romanticizes Nirvana’s Reading set.

I can easily picture locals telling each other years later: *“Were you at the Commuted show at the Fremantle docks? Mate, you missed the night the roof almost came down.”*

> How isolated is Perth from the East Coast traditional venues?

Perth isn’t just “a bit out of the way”—it’s **geographically brutal** compared to the east coast circuit.

- **Distance**: Sydney to Perth is about **3,300 km (2,050 miles)**. That’s the equivalent of New York City to Denver… with nothing much in between.
- **Flight time**: ~5 hours direct. Same as flying LA to New York.
- **Drive time**: If you’re insane enough to do it by road, it’s **3½ to 4 days** across the Nullarbor Plain—a barren, treeless desert. Gas stations double as motels, and “towns” might be just a roadhouse with two people and a dog.
- **Rail**: There’s a transcontinental train, the Indian Pacific, but it’s 70+ hours and more of a sightseeing cruise than a viable band tour option.

For international acts in the ’90s, that meant:
- Most bands would tour Sydney, Melbourne, maybe Brisbane… and stop.
- Adding Perth meant *an entire separate logistical operation*: flights, gear transport, crew per diems, hotels. For just one or two gigs.
- Promoters often balked—unless they knew the Perth crowd would make it financially worthwhile.

So Perth got this reputation as the **“forgotten city”** on the touring map. And when bands *did* make the effort, it was a huge deal—the audiences went feral because they knew they’d been skipped so often.

That’s why a Commuted show in Perth in ’98 wouldn’t just be another stop—it’d be a **folk legend**. The sheer distance, the rarity, the sense of “the circus actually came this far.” It would carry mythic weight, both for the band and for the fans.

> I'm thinking, the band would have no issue flying from Sydney to Perth.  It's smaller than the USA!  However, getting the gear from A to Z?  WHOLE different story.

Exactly—that’s the crux. The **people** can get on a plane, no problem. But **moving the circus**—the amps, drum kits, lighting rigs, cabling, tech gear—that’s where Perth becomes a monster.

Here’s why:

- **Distance + Cost**
  Shipping a full touring rig across the continent is like moving it from Boston to Denver. You either fly it (insanely expensive, especially in the ’90s) or truck it (three to four days across desert nothingness, drivers swapping shifts). Either way, the bill climbs fast.

- **No piggybacking**
  On the East Coast, you can move gear city to city in a night: Sydney → Melbourne → Brisbane. Perth? It’s its *own tour*. You can’t just “swing by” on the way somewhere else.

- **Gear fragility**
  High-end amps, vintage synths, and drum kits don’t love bouncing across 3,000 km of desert in a semi. Especially when the Nullarbor road has sections that are dead straight for 145 km at a time—the driver zones out, one pothole, and boom, your Marshall stack’s rattling like a maraca.

- **Local rentals = dicey**
  Sure, you *could* rent gear locally in Perth, but in the late ’90s, WA’s backline rental scene wasn’t nearly as robust as Sydney/Melbourne. The band would get stuck with mismatched, underpowered, or just plain *wrong* equipment—sacrilege to guys like Bill or Jeff who are neurotic about their sound.

So you’ve nailed it:
- **Band on a flight = easy.**
- **Gear on a flight/truck = nightmare.**

That tension actually makes for great story fuel: you can have the band arrive fresh(ish), only to find their gear is delayed, battered, or replaced with dodgy local substitutes. Chaos ensues—and yet, somehow, they still bring the house down.

And the thing is—because the band is stripped down, forced to rely on grit instead of spectacle—the show’s *better*. Raw, furious, unforgettable. The kind of gig that Perth locals will talk about in hushed tones:

The warehouse is thick with sweat before the band even takes the stage. Word of the gig spread like contraband: whispers in pubs, text chains, a late-night pirate radio DJ dropping hints. - **The vibe**: Rusted corrugated iron, salt in the air, shipping containers stacked like Lego. Fremantle had old woolstores and sheds that were often used for raves and underground gigs.
- **Why it works**: One rental truck, a skeleton backline, and you’ve got a stage. The echo off tin roofs makes it loud, raw, and imperfect—perfect for *You Cannot Refine It*.
- **Crowd size**: 1,500–3,000 packed in, shoulder to shoulder, with hundreds more outside straining to hear.
- **Law enforcement**: Cremeans’ “permits” basically mean “we’ll look the other way if nothing burns down.”
#### Fremantle Prison (decommissioned in ’91, heritage site by then)
- **The vibe**: Playing inside a 19th-century convict-built prison. Haunting, defiant, visually insane.
- **Why it works**: Symbolic. A band obsessed with authenticity plays inside literal history. It feels illicit but monumental.
- **Crowd size**: Limited, maybe 800 inside the yards, but the legend outgrows the attendance.
- **Law enforcement**: Needs Cremeans’ most creative paperwork. Technically a “private event,” but once the amps start rattling the limestone walls, the cops can’t stop it.
#### Esplanade Park, Fremantle
- **The vibe**: Open-air green space by the ocean. It looks almost normal… until you fill it with thousands of fans on a day’s notice.
- **Why it works**: Easier crowd flow, and it *feels* guerrilla because it wasn’t advertised. Fans hear, rush down, and suddenly the band’s surrounded by 10,000 screaming Australians under palm trees.
- **Crowd size**: Could explode if word spreads—this is where “one truck show” turns into a full-blown local riot.

Now that drops a hammer. With that context, a Perth guerrilla show in 2008 doesn’t just become a **rowdy gig at the edge of the world**—it becomes a deliberate counterweight to the *Great Lakes Trilogy* sitting at the heart of *You Cannot Refine It*.

So imagine the contrast:

- **In the studio**: They’re channeling Lake Superior, Whitefish Point, lighthouses blinking over wreckage, the ghost of the *Edmund Fitzgerald*. It’s maritime horror refracted through Midwestern eyes.

The symbolism is *thick*:
- Midwest inland seas → myth of loss and drowning.
- Indian Ocean coast → myth of survival and rebellion.
- Shipwrecks in both places → mirror images, but in Fremantle the band refuses to drown; they erupt instead.

That makes the Perth set not just a novelty. It’s **the band confronting another ocean on the opposite side of the world.** Fans who knew the Great Lakes trilogy would feel the tectonic resonance: these guys brought their ghosts halfway across the planet and threw them at the sea in Western Australia.

It’s the perfect “what’s new under the sun?” moment. They’ve sung the water’s wrath in the Midwest. Now they answer it with a raucous, illicit, open-air declaration on another shore entirely.

- **Dedicated band forums / fan sites** (every big act had one, often with obsessive moderators and endless “bootleg trades” threads).
- **Independent web forums** on platforms like ProBoards or InvisionFree. Think “alt-rockfans.net” with a pixelated banner and an overactive signatures culture.
- **Blog comments** (Pitchfork, NME, Stereogum, BrooklynVegan). Fans fought wars in the comment sections.
- **MySpace bulletins** (fading fast by 2008, but still alive for music).
- **Email chains and webrings** (old-school but still active for bootleg trading).

So for a shock show like Commuted’s in Perth, the fan chatter would ignite on:
1. **Local Aussie rock forums** (triple j message boards, FasterLouder, Perth bands forums).
2. **Global Commuted fan boards** — wherever diehards traded setlists and hissy mp3s.
3. **Blogosphere comments** — a post on Stereogum like “Commuted played a secret show in Perth?!?” would spawn a hundred comments in 24 hours.
