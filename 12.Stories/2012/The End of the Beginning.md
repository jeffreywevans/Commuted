# **INT. – BOSTON REHABILITATION FACILITY – NEURO UNIT – ROOM 14 – EARLY AFTERNOON – MARCH 5, 2012**

---

The air smells like sterilized sorrow. Beeping monitors pulse like a mechanical lullaby. And at the center of it all—

KATHY ESPICH.

Unmoving.  Unconscious.  Naked beneath the thin hospital sheet, a maze of IVs, EEG wires, and oxygen tubing looping across her body like a cruel remix of her own chords. Her chest rises and falls in rhythmic surrender—not by will, but by machine.

INTENSIVE CARE ORDERLY: LEON MONTGOMERY, 310 LBS.
Former Division III defensive tackle. Shoulders like granite.  Eyes like glass after 20 hours without sleep.  He hasn’t moved from his chair in three hours. He watches Kathy like she might wake up swinging. Or never at all.

**LEON** (quietly, to himself):
You were talking fine yesterday.

He adjusts her blanket—careful, gentle. His hands, made to break bones, now recalibrated to preserve a fading pulse.

**DOCTOR** (O.S.):
Neuro. We're doing another CT.

[A flurry enters—white coats, hushed tones, tension. The room floods with movement.]

**NURSE**:
Her vitals are stable. Pupils non-responsive.  Still no signs of voluntary function. We're calling in Dr. Chen.

**LEON** (muttering, not looking away):
Brain's gone dark, huh?

**NURSE** (soft):
We think… it ruptured.  Aneurysm. Deep.  Right side.  Like lightning.

**LEON**:
That woman walked in here twelve hours ago. Checked herself in. Laughed in the lobby. Said she’d be trouble.

**DOCTOR**:
Now she’s incontinent in bed.

[Silence. Just the machines.]

**LEON** (to Kathy, low):
Come on now.  You gonna let a rehab bed be your last stage?

Outside the room, the hallway is alive with buzzing phones. Reporters, lawyers, frantic label reps, and shadowy PR teams are converging.  Inside this room, Kathy is gone. Not legally. Not yet. But the only thing louder than the machines… is the certainty that she’ll never speak again.
