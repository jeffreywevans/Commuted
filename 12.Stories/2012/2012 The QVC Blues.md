
**GILLEY** (staring straight ahead, voice slow, low, no trace of performance):
You ever watch this shit and realize the only thing in the world that keeps spinning is a five-dollar bracelet on a plastic plate?

**GILLEY (V.O)**
I used to buy these for my mother. Not the real gold. God, no. The kind that leaves a green stripe on your wrist by Tuesday. She loved them anyway. Said it made her feel like she could walk into Kroger and not have to apologize for how she looked.

(Tilts his head, eyes not leaving the TV. The host is selling earrings now—rubies, probably glass. He doesn’t blink.)

**GILLEY**
You know what the worst part is? That lady on the screen—she means every word. Or she did, once. Maybe that’s the trick. Maybe you do it enough, you say “limited quantity” and “exclusive offer” until it’s not a lie, it’s just weather. It’s how you breathe.

(Turns the remote in his hand, thumb brushing the worn-down numbers. Still doesn’t look at Sarah.)

**GILLEY (V.O)**
There’s something to be said for people who can want things. I mean really want. I used to want everything, all at once. You ever want so bad you can’t sleep? That’s what it was like, every night for years. Like if I stopped wanting—stopped running—it would catch up. It always does, eventually.

**GILLEY**
Now… 
(he shrugs, as if surprised by the fact of it)  
Now, I can’t remember the last thing I actually wanted. Not like hunger, not like need. Not even you, Sarah. And I know what that means to you. I know what it means to me, too. 

(A faint, humorless laugh.)  
Hell, I wish I could still want you. Or music. Or the band. Or even a goddamn Raiders playoff game. But it’s all just… gold plating over cheap metal. Spin it, sell it, move on.

(TV show changes; the host leans forward, promising this necklace is a “one-time event.” Gilley watches, unmoved.)

**GILLEY (V.O)**
The last time I saw Kathy, she told me—

(his mouth twists)—she told me to “give ‘em hell,” like it was that easy. Like I was still the guy who’d run through walls for a good time, or a bad idea. She was dying, and she still sounded more alive than I ever did sober.

(Turns, finally, half-glancing at Sarah, but not really seeing her.)

**GILLEY**
I know you want to help. I know you want to make it better, or at least make it different. But there’s no sex in me. No jokes. No running. I’m all out. You ever feel that, Sarah? Like the world’s already spent you, and you’re just the thing left spinning on the plate?

(Silence. On TV, the price drops. He watches.)

**GILLEY**
If you want to stay, you can. If you want to go, you should. Doesn’t matter. They’ll put something else up next, call it a “special.” Everything’s always a special, until it’s not. Then it’s just leftover inventory.

(Puts the remote down. Sits, motionless. Lets the bracelet spin, lets Sarah stand wherever she wants.)

End of commercial.

---

**Sarah, in that moment, understands:  She could strip, scream, slap him, or kiss his hand, and it wouldn’t matter.  He’s not refusing her. He’s refusing the world.  
There is nothing left to want.**

That’s the monologue. No melodrama, just truth laid flat, and nothing left to do but watch the cheap gold spin.
