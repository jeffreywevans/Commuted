**📰 Springfield News-Sun – June 21, 1995 Edition**

**"Commuted Sparks National Uproar, But Hometown Roots Remain Unshaken"**

---

**Byline:**

Claire Whittaker, Staff Writer, *Springfield News-Sun*

---

**Opening Paragraph:**

"\*This week, as news outlets across the country debate the artistic merits — and supposed indecency — of Kathy Espich’s now-infamous album promotional photo, residents here in Bethel Township and greater Clark County find themselves asking a different question:

How do you reconcile a national controversy with the kids you watched grow up at Donnelsville Elementary, Tecumseh Middle, and Tecumseh High School?\*"

**The Band Next Door**

"\*Commuted — Kathy Espich (lead vocals), Jeff Evans (bass), Jeremy Evans (guitar), and Jim Brown (drums) — formed in 1989 while students at Tecumseh High.

Originally a garage cover band playing behind the Evans family’s barn, they quickly gained a loyal local following at church halls, VFW events, and eventually the now-shuttered Maxie’s Music Café.\*"

"*'They were just good kids,' said Bob Thorne, who taught Kathy freshman English. 'Creative, a little wild, but good-hearted.'*"

**Small Town, Big Shock**

"\*The now-viral photo — depicting Espich nude in soft, artistic lighting, gazing directly into the camera — has drawn furious reactions from national groups like the American Family Coalition and the Parents' Rights Alliance.

Yet here at home, opinions are divided.\*"

"\*'I don’t see anything wrong with it,' said Marsha Conley, who sold concert tickets for the band’s early shows out of her kitchen. 'It’s tasteful. It’s not nasty.

It’s just… honest. And maybe that’s what makes people so nervous.'\*"

**The Price of Leaving Home**

"\*Some residents worry the national attention will forever change how Bethel Township is perceived.

'It’s one thing to play your guitars loud,' said Bill Stephenson, a local father of three. 'It’s another to put it all out there like that, especially when people know where you come from.'\*"

"*Others see the controversy as evidence that Commuted hasn't forgotten their roots — they've simply outgrown their fear.*"

"*'They’re just telling their truth, and it’s not always pretty,' said Becky Alton, who once worked the lights for a Commuted show at the county fairgrounds. 'That’s what real artists do. And frankly, we ought to be proud.'*"

**Where It Goes From Here**

"*While representatives for Island Records confirm that That Look of Release remains on schedule for its late summer launch,*  
*no advance copies have yet reached Springfield — officially, anyway.*"

"*Still, the spirit of Bethel Township runs deep in every member of Commuted,*  
*and locals here suspect that no amount of national hand-wringing will ever change that.*"

**Closing Pull Quote:**

"\*Maybe the rest of the country sees scandal.

We see kids from our hometown making the world listen.

And honestly?

About time.\*"

**📝 Springfield News-Sun – Letters to the Editor (June 24, 1995\)**

**Regarding: "Commuted Sparks National Uproar, But Hometown Roots Remain Unshaken"**

---

**Letter Title:**

**"Not Everything Needs to Be 'Art'"**

**Author:**

Randy Elliot, Yellow Springs, Ohio

"**To the Editor,**

I find it deeply disturbing that so many people — especially those who claim to value decency, family values, and natural living — are rushing to defend what is, at best, a tasteless publicity stunt.

I am speaking, of course, about the so-called 'artistic' nude image of Kathy Espich promoting her new album *That Look of Release*.

As a longtime resident of Yellow Springs and a committed vegan who believes in nonviolence and ethical presentation of the body,  
I must object to this glorification of personal excess.

Real freedom does not come from displaying yourself like a commodity.  
It comes from rejecting the capitalist-driven need for attention and validation.

When I first heard about Commuted, I had hoped for a band that might embrace a more holistic, naturalistic philosophy — something truly revolutionary.  
Instead, we get another cog in the machinery of vulgarity and overconsumption, hiding behind a tattered flag of 'honesty.'

There is nothing revolutionary about flashing the camera.  
True revolution is quiet, deliberate, and sustainable — like composting, or a properly managed organic garden.

Ms. Espich and her bandmates may think they are challenging norms.  
In truth, they are merely trading one set of chains for another,  
and calling it liberation.

**Randy Elliot**  
Yellow Springs, OH"

---

**Editorial Note (small, italicized at the bottom):**

"*Editor’s note: Mr. Elliot's comparison of sustainable agriculture to emotional nudity is his own and does not reflect the editorial stance of the News-Sun.*"
