# ***Release Me***

**Subtitle:** *An Essay by Kathy Espich on Sex, Power, Culture, and the Lie of Modesty*

---

**I. Cold Open – “Yeah, I Did It”**

Yeah, I did it. You’ve probably seen it by now. Maybe on newsstands if they didn’t shrink-wrap it out of shame or maybe folded inside a buddy’s backpack like contraband. Maybe your girlfriend caught you staring too long. Or maybe you’re reading this right now, pretending you’re here for the article. Fine. I’m glad you’re here. Let’s talk.

Yes, I’m naked. Yes, it’s deliberate. Yes, I shaved. I know what year it is—1995—and I know what this looks like. But before you slot me into whatever prefab category helps you sleep at night—sellout, slut, irony-chaser, bad feminist—know this: I am *very good* at what I do, and I am done asking for permission to show it.

This is not a stunt. This is not rebellion for rebellion’s sake. This is not the desperate cry of a frontwoman whose band sold 17 million records and then had the audacity to *change*. This is *release*. Of shame, of compromise, of cultural obedience. It’s *that look* *of release*. We named an album after it. Now I’m living it.

And if a photograph of a grown woman—an artist, a songwriter, a daughter of the Rust Belt—owning her body without apology sends you into crisis? Then I suggest you take that discomfort, put it on a turntable, drop the needle, and let it spin. Because I didn’t do this to shock you.

I did this because it’s *mine*. And I’m done waiting for your permission to be seen.

**II. The Nineties Are a Lie, and You Know It**

We were all supposed to wear flannel and pretend we didn’t care. That was the deal. Grunge told you that angst was currency, that muttering into the mic was real, that being visibly female onstage was dangerous unless you played the part of a sad-eyed ghost with a broken Barbie guitar. God help you if you tried to be *loud* and *smart* at the same time. God help you if you wanted to be *hot* and *angry* in the same breath.

But I didn’t come out of Dayton just to mutter. I came out screaming. I came out playing chords on a guitar I bought secondhand and hammering lyrics into notebooks in the back of a bus while every man in the scene told me to “tone it down” or “smile more” or “stay behind the keys.” So, I did what every woman before me did: I got louder. Then I switched instruments. You want to know why I quit playing guitar? Because every guy in every crowd thought I was there to *prove* something. So, I stopped proving. I *became*.

You think Alanis is mad? You think Tori’s weird? Please. I’ve been swallowing rage like cough syrup since Reagan. I’ve been told I was *too much* in the same breath I was told I wasn’t enough. So, here’s your update: I’m everything. All at once.

We pretend the Clinton era is some golden age because a saxophone-playing president wagged his finger on late night and said he didn’t inhale. Meanwhile, half of us are gasping for breath, choking on glass ceilings and wondering how many years of “almost” it takes before someone lets a woman headline Lollapalooza without her tits or trauma being the headline.

So, I put both on the cover. You’re welcome.

**III. The Body, the Brand, the Burnout**

I used to think my body was a guitar—something to strap on, string tight, thrash around with until it made noise people couldn’t ignore. Turns out, it’s more like a billboard. Everyone has a take. Everyone has a plan. Everyone thinks they get to vote on whether or not I’m “doing it right.” You know how many people had an opinion on this pictorial before I even stepped in front of the camera? Seventeen. You know how many of them were in the band? Two. Guess how many of them I listened to? *Zero*.

Because this body doesn’t belong to the label, or the critics, or some A\&R guy trying to use the words “mature direction” to mean “put a shirt on.” This body got me through 500 shows. It got me through food poisoning in Chicago, an ovarian cyst rupture in Detroit, and a breakup on stage in San Francisco that somehow turned into a standing ovation. This body is mine, and it’s tired of being polite about it.

You want branding? Here’s my branding: woman, Midwestern, frontwoman, barefoot in the snow, lipstick on the mic, bruised knees from kneeling to no one. I am not your manic pixie dream girl. I’m the end of the dream. I’m what happens when you stop pretending your body is neutral and start treating it like the *amplifier* it is.

And burnout? Yeah, I’ve hit it. I’ve screamed until I puked and then finished the encore. I’ve been mistaken for my own groupie. I’ve been on magazine covers with headlines that asked *Is She Too Much?* and I’ve written songs that made people cry during sex and then lie about it later. I *know* burnout.

And you know what comes after burnout?

Fire.

And fire *spreads*.

**IV. Naked on My Terms**

There’s this myth that when a woman undresses for a camera, she’s surrendering. That she’s bartering power for attention. That she’s giving something away. Well, I’m here to tell you: I didn’t give you a damn thing. I *took* it. I took control of every angle, every frame, every shadow and glint of light in this shoot. I picked the locations. I picked the outfits—when there were any. I picked the frame that shows you my hipbone and not my face, because that’s where your eyes go anyway.

Playboy didn’t find me. I found them. After the chaos of *That Look of Release*, after the world had a panic attack over three-square inches of black and orange obscurity, I decided to double down. You wanna clutch pearls over an album cover? Let me hand you a gallery. Let me show you what it means to own the image instead of running from it. Let me make you uncomfortable enough to *question* why you’re uncomfortable.

This isn’t about sex. It’s about my agency. It’s about taking the same camera lens that’s been weaponized against every woman in the public eye—"Is she too fat? Too old? Too bold? Too quiet?" and turning it back into a mirror. A *true* one. This is me, naked. Not because I was told to be. Because I *chose* to be. Not for your approval. Not for your lust. For *myself*.

You don’t have to want me. You don’t even have to like me. But you’re gonna see me. You’re gonna see all of me. And that includes the scars, the stories, and the stare that says, “Yeah, I fucking dare you.”

Because if I don’t do it, they’ll put someone else’s body on the cover and *lie* to you. I won’t lie. This is what a woman looks like when she’s done asking permission.

**V. The Ride, the Reckoning, the Revolution**

I didn’t get into this to be anyone’s role model. I got in to scream. To make noise. To rage on a stage under a thousand megawatts and feel the crowd’s pulse sync with mine. But somewhere between the second gold record and the fifth emergency room visit, I started to realize that the stage isn't the only place I’m being watched.

You want to know what it’s like to be in the eye of a cultural storm? Picture this: you’re 25, your song’s on the radio every nine minutes, your body’s been dissected by men who can’t spell your last name, and your opinions are quoted in magazines like you’re running for office. And then they ask you, “Don’t you think you’ve gone too far?”

No. I don’t. Not even close.

Because every time I’ve “gone too far,” I’ve discovered that the line they drew for me was made of chalk, and all I had to do was step on it for it to disappear. Every time I pushed back, it wasn’t a rebellion. It was survival.

The ride? It’s not over. But there’s been a reckoning. I’m not the girl they saw in the liner notes of our first album anymore. I’m not even the woman they saw in the nude cover shoot for *That Look of Release*. I’ve burned down too many false narratives, cried behind too many locked greenroom doors, fucked too many wrong people, and loved too few right ones.

And the revolution? It’s not loud. It’s *lived*.

It’s in the women backstage who don’t apologize for wanting the spotlight. It’s in the girls who see this pictorial and realize they get to pick their own armor, whether it’s silk, leather, denim, or nothing at all. It’s in the idea that a woman can scream *and* whisper, strip *and* strategize, climax *and* command.

I’m not asking for your permission. I’m offering an invitation.

Join me.

Or get out of the fucking way.

**VI. Letter to the Girl I Was**

Hey you—

Yeah, the one hiding under the flannel, chewing your cuticles in the back of a tour bus, wondering if he’s gonna call, wondering if your voice matters, wondering if it’s already too late to become who you were meant to be.

Let me save you a couple decades of self-inflicted bullshit: he’s not gonna call. And even if he does, he’s not worth the static that lingers after. Your voice? It’s not just music. It’s *a shield*. It's *a* *blade*. And it’s gonna cut through a hell of a lot more than silence.

You don’t owe anyone pretty. You don’t owe anyone polite. You don’t even owe them coherent. You owe them honesty. You owe *yourself* unfiltered fury, fire, tenderness, and grit. You owe yourself a seat at the table—hell, build your own goddamn table and sing the screws into place.

And when they tell you to tone it down, smile more, stop screaming, wear black, stay small, *don’t pose nude*—know that you will do the opposite. And not because you’re defiant, but because you are *awake*. You are finally living in your skin, not dragging it behind you.

That magazine cover? That centerfold? It’s not a fall from grace. It’s you, climbing your own mountain barefoot, middle fingers up, tits out, daring the world to blink first.

So when you feel the panic set in, like you’ve gone too far, like you’re about to tip over the edge—breathe.

Because I’m already here, years ahead of you. And the view?

It’s fucking magnificent.

**VII. On Being Hairless in 1995**

You want to talk about rebellion? Try showing up hairless in a culture that still worships the au naturel bush as a symbol of “authentic” femininity. Try letting a razor speak louder than a manifesto. Try being twenty-five, naked in front of a lens, and making a decision not for titillation but for *clarity*—for control.

When we shot this spread, the editorial team asked if I had a preference. I said, “Yeah. I prefer to look like I belong to *me*.” And then I walked in with nothing between my thighs but confidence and intent.

It wasn’t pornographic. It wasn’t a fashion trend. It was a choice. A deliberate one. Aesthetic, personal, provocative. Not because it revealed more—hell, I was already baring skin—but because it revealed *intent*. I wasn’t there to please a reader’s fantasy of what womanhood should look like. I was there to plant a flag.

This wasn’t about shame. It wasn’t about male preference. It was about streamlining—about stripping down to something elemental and confronting the idea that a woman has to signal “naturalness” through someone else’s nostalgia. I wasn’t interested in being your memory of sex. I wanted to be the *now* of it.

And yeah, I knew it would raise eyebrows. Knew the whispers would come about being “too modern,” “too Hollywood,” “too polished,” “too weird.” I was told it might date the shoot. As if that’s a *bad* thing. You know what else dates the shoot? The face. The attitude. The refusal to smile just because they want you to.

And if it makes you uncomfortable? Good.

Hair grows back. But moments like this—they cut through the static.

**VIII. Self-Sabotage, Self-Possession**

It’s funny how often they get confused—sabotage and possession. When a woman steps out of line, she’s accused of burning bridges. When she sets boundaries, she’s called cold. When she claims space, she’s punished for the audacity of not asking first. For years, I thought I was breaking things. Turns out I was building something—*me*.

I used to think the sex, the volume, the spectacle, the fights, the high heels, the backstage backtalk—all of it—was evidence I was out of control. What I missed was that I was taking *control*. Over and over again, in places where women weren’t supposed to have any.

Yeah, I’ve been reckless. Yeah, I’ve scorched some earth. But it wasn’t because I hated myself. It was because I hated what I’d been told I had to be. Sweet. Soft. Second. The support act to someone else’s main event.

No, thank you.

This shoot? It’s not a spiral. It’s a stake in the ground. A woman not afraid of her body, her age, her power, her rage. I spent years dimming my light so I wouldn’t seem like too much. And for what? A pat on the head from people who didn’t give a damn about me when I was shrinking?

Self-sabotage is what they call it when a woman stops waiting to be told she’s allowed. Self-possession is what it *really* is.

And it looks fucking good in black and white.

**IX. To the Men in the Room**

Let’s talk directly, shall we?

You’ve run this industry since celluloid first flickered. Since reel-to-reel. Since radio static was sexy. You’re the green lighters, the gatekeepers, the gods of the edit room. You choose the covers, the headlines, the framing, the budgets. And I’ve played your game. I’ve smiled on cue. I’ve taken meetings I didn’t want, listened to praise that felt like a trap. I’ve watched you pretend you respect the women you fuck, or worse—pretend you *don’t*.

Here’s the deal you never say out loud but expect us all to follow: **You can be powerful if you never admit you liked sex.**  You can front a band, headline a tour, light up arenas—so long as the pleasure isn’t *yours*. So long as we think you were *used*, not *hungry*.  The second you want it? Really *want* it? You’re a whore.

That’s your line, not mine.

You want the real scandal? It’s not that I took off my clothes. It’s that I *wanted to do.* That I came to this photoshoot not as a reluctant princess, but as a motherfucking general. Because I *do*.

So, if that breaks your model of what a woman in power looks like—good. Let it break. 
This is me—hairless, fearless, photographed, and fully in charge.

If that makes you squirm, maybe it’s not me that needs to change.

**X. The Decadence and Truth of Rock and Roll**

People always ask if I’m ashamed. Of the nudity. Of the pills. Of the parties. Of the things we did in dressing rooms with half-empty bottles and no curtains. They want to know if we regret it—if, under the glitter and the grit, we wished we’d played it safer. Straighter. Sweeter.

No.

I’m not ashamed of the sex. I’m not ashamed of the drugs. I’m not ashamed of this body, or the choices it’s made. I’m ashamed of the *silence* that came before. Rock and roll isn’t polite. It’s not meant to be. It’s a scream from the basement. It’s blood on the snare drum. It’s the truth, turned up to eleven, because whispering it didn’t fucking work.

We didn’t do this to be liked. We did it because it *hurt* to be real and no one else was screaming. Excess isn’t a sin. It’s a ritual. It’s the dirty baptism of people too hungry to wait for salvation. We weren’t trying to build temples. We were setting fires just to see the color of the flames. And if that’s too much for you? You were never our audience.

Because the people who needed us heard it. They heard *me*.

**The Future Is Mine**

I’m not interested in the slow fade. The graceful exit. The soft-lit retrospective with strings playing behind me. That’s not how I came in, and that sure as hell isn’t how I’m going out.

I was naked before you knew my name. I screamed before anyone handed me a mic. And if you think I peaked already—then you haven’t been paying attention.

I don’t want a pedestal. I want a *platform.*  I don’t want a legacy. I want a *loudspeaker.* You’ll find me on the cover of *Vogue*, backstage at *Playboy*, front row at the revolution.  
You’ll hear me in midnight basslines and Sunday morning think pieces.  You’ll see me—undiluted, unbothered, undressed if I feel like it.

The future isn’t something I walk into. The future is something I *kick the fucking door off its hinges to reach.* And when do I get there? I won’t be alone.

**I’ll find the answers between my legs.**

With love and defiance,
