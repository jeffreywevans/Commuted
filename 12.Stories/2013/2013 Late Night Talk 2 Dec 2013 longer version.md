#### **Late Night Talk**
**Date**: December 2, 2013
**Location**: Bill’s Studio – a dimly lit, cluttered space with scattered drumsticks, coffee-stained notes, and a faint hum of equipment in sleep mode.

---

Jeff slouched on the worn leather couch, his guitar resting against his knee, strings deadened by his thumb. His eyes were bloodshot, the remnants of a bottle of Jack Daniels glinting on the table. Bill sat cross-legged on a stool near the drum kit, his shoulders hunched. Sweat clung to his T-shirt, though the room was cold. A metronome clicked idly on the console, the only rhythm breaking the silence.

"You know," Jeff muttered, his voice scraping the quiet, "this is all just a big cosmic joke." He leaned back, eyes fixed on the water-stained ceiling. "You pour everything into something, and the universe just flips you off."

Bill glanced over, his face drawn. His hands toyed with a drumstick, flipping it idly like it was an extension of himself. "You’re angry. I get it," he said, voice steady. "But you’re also drunk. And you’re still here, so the universe hasn’t won yet, has it?"

Jeff’s laugh was bitter. "Winning? What does that even mean anymore? Kathy’s gone. Everything’s just… gray. The music feels like static. You’re killing yourself in here, man, trying to make everything perfect, but for what?"

Bill set the drumstick down, his movement deliberate. "For us, Jeff. For her. Because if we don’t, what’s left?" His eyes were heavy but unwavering. "I’m not saying it doesn’t suck. It does. But you think she’d want us to quit? Turn our backs on what we’ve built?"

Jeff sat up, shaking his head. "It’s not quitting. It’s just… I don’t know how to do this without her. She was the fire, Bill. Now it’s just ashes."

Bill rubbed his temples, then looked up, his voice soft but firm. "We’ve been through hell before. And we always pulled through because we had each other. I know you’re angry—at God, at fate, at yourself. But we don’t get to stop now. Not when there’s still something left to say."

Jeff stared at him, the anger in his expression cracking just enough to show the raw hurt underneath. "You sound like Cremeans," he said, trying for a smirk but failing.

Bill smiled faintly. "Yeah, well, maybe he’s rubbed off on me. The guy’s annoyingly right most of the time." He picked up the drumstick again, flipping it once before catching it. "But seriously, Jeff. If you’re feeling this lost, then you’ve got to channel it. Pick up that guitar and let it bleed. That’s the only way any of us are going to survive this."

Jeff let the words settle, then reached for his guitar. The strings buzzed softly under his fingers as he tuned it by ear. "Fine," he said, his tone softer. "But only if you promise to stop sleeping here. You look like death warmed over."

Bill chuckled, shaking his head. "Deal. But only after we finish this track."

The metronome clicked in the background as Jeff started to play, a mournful, bluesy riff that filled the studio. Bill tapped out a beat on his thigh, the rhythm building between them, raw and unpolished but alive.

#### **Late Night Interruption**
**Date**: December 2, 2013
**Location**: Bill’s Studio

The mournful riff Jeff played was winding down into silence when a brisk knock echoed through the space. Both men froze, exchanging a glance. Visitors were rare at this hour—especially ones that knocked.

The door creaked open, and Cremeans stepped inside, a familiar Mountain Dew in hand. His flannel shirt hung loose, the sleeves rolled up to reveal tired but determined eyes. "Hope I’m not interrupting," he said, closing the door behind him. "Figured I’d find you two here."

Jeff smirked, setting his guitar aside. "You’re always interrupting, Cremeans. It’s your thing."

Cremeans ignored the jab, his gaze locking onto Bill. "I know it’s late, but we need to talk. About Jim."

Bill groaned, running a hand through his sweat-damp hair. "If he’s sent you to play middleman, save it. I’ve had enough of him breathing down my neck."

Jeff leaned forward, curiosity piqued. "What’s he done now?"

Cremeans sighed, planting himself against the wall. "He’s riding Bill about the mixes. Wants them faster, cleaner. Keeps throwing out terms like ‘market-ready’ like he’s a damn producer. I told him to back off, but you know how Jim is—he thinks deadlines are the gospel."

Bill’s stool scraped loudly as he stood, his exhaustion flaring into anger. "I’ve been in here day and night, and he thinks he can just waltz in and tell me how to do my job? I don’t care what he thinks is ‘market-ready.’ The album’s done when it’s done."

Cremeans held up a hand, calm but firm. "And I told him that. But you know as well as I do, Bill, he’s not going to listen unless we force him to."

Jeff stood too, crossing his arms. "So what’s the plan, Mr. Fix-It? You going to hold his hand until he sees reason?"

Cremeans smiled faintly, but his tone stayed serious. "I’m here to back you up, not Jim. The guy’s a pain in the ass, but he’s still footing the bill. We can’t just ignore him. Bill, I need you to focus on finishing the mixes your way—no shortcuts, no compromises. And Jeff…" He turned, his eyes narrowing slightly. "You’re going to help me keep Jim out of the damn studio."

Jeff blinked, caught off guard. "Me? Since when am I the diplomat?"

"You’re not," Cremeans said dryly. "But you’ve got a way of intimidating people when you want to. Use it. Keep him distracted, keep him happy—whatever it takes to buy Bill some breathing room."

Bill paced the studio, his hands flexing. "I don’t need a babysitter. I need Jim to shut up and trust me. This isn’t my first album, and it sure as hell won’t be his last."

"And that’s exactly what I’ll remind him," Cremeans said. His voice softened, and he stepped closer. "But I can’t do this without you, Bill. If you burn out, we’re screwed. So take the night, cool off, and hit it fresh tomorrow. I’ll handle Jim."

For a long moment, the room was quiet, save for the hum of the equipment. Finally, Bill exhaled, his posture sagging. "Fine. But if he comes in here one more time, I’m going to lose it."

Cremeans patted him on the shoulder. "That’s what I’m here for."

Jeff watched the exchange, shaking his head with a small grin. "You really do have a savior complex, don’t you?"

Cremeans grabbed his Mountain Dew, smirking. "Nah. I just hate seeing you guys fall apart." He turned toward the door, pausing. "Get some sleep, both of you. Tomorrow’s another fight."

As the door closed behind him, Jeff sat back down, picking up his guitar again. Bill returned to his stool, staring at the mixing board like it was both a challenge and a curse.

"Think he can actually keep Jim out?" Jeff asked, plucking a few quiet notes.

Bill snorted. "If anyone can, it’s Cremeans." He reached for his headphones, muttering, "Now let’s hope he doesn’t get himself killed trying."

#### **Old Wounds, New Scars**
**Date**: December 2, 2013
**Location**: Bill’s Studio

The studio was quiet again, save for the soft buzz of the equipment and the occasional clink of Jeff’s guitar strings. Bill had his headphones on, leaning into the console, meticulously isolating tracks. The rhythmic tap of his foot matched the faint beat from the speakers.

Jeff, sprawled across the couch, let his fingers wander aimlessly over the fretboard, the notes blending into a mournful, unresolved melody. The tension between them had eased, replaced by the kind of silence that comes only after years of shared battles.

Then Bill’s phone buzzed on the console, its vibration cutting through the stillness. He glanced at the screen, his expression immediately darkening.

"It’s Gilley," he muttered, pulling off the headphones.

Jeff sat up, his grip tightening on the neck of his guitar. "What the hell does he want at this hour?"

Bill hesitated before answering, his voice gruff. "Guess we’re about to find out." He swiped the screen and held the phone to his ear. "Gilley? It’s late. What’s up?"

Jeff watched as Bill’s expression shifted from irritation to something colder, harder. He could only hear one side of the conversation, but the tone was enough to make his jaw clench.

"What do you mean, ‘we’re losing her’?" Bill said, his voice rising slightly. He stood abruptly, pacing the room. "You think I don’t know that? You think I haven’t been killing myself trying to hold this together?"

Jeff set his guitar down, standing. "What’s he saying?"

Bill waved him off, his eyes narrowing as Gilley’s voice rattled on. "No, you don’t get to waltz in six months after the fact and act like you’re the only one who cared about her!" His voice cracked on the last word, and he stopped pacing, his free hand pressed to his forehead.

Jeff took the phone from Bill without asking. "Gilley, what the hell is your problem?" he snapped, his voice sharp enough to cut through whatever Gilley was saying. "Bill’s been here every damn night trying to make this album perfect. Where have you been?"

Whatever Gilley said next made Jeff’s face twist with anger. He gripped the phone tighter, pacing now himself. "Don’t you dare talk about Kathy like that. She—" He broke off, the words sticking in his throat. His free hand balled into a fist.

Bill watched, arms crossed, his jaw tight. Jeff’s face was turning red, his knuckles white as he held the phone. Finally, he barked, "If you’ve got something to say, Gilley, say it to my face." Without waiting for a response, he ended the call and threw the phone onto the couch.

The silence that followed was heavy, charged.

"What did he say?" Bill asked finally, his voice low.

Jeff didn’t answer right away. He ran a hand through his hair, his shoulders heaving with barely contained fury. "He said we’re wasting our time. That Kathy wouldn’t have wanted this."

Bill’s lips pressed into a thin line. "He doesn’t know what she would’ve wanted."

"Yeah, well, he sure as hell thinks he does," Jeff snapped. He leaned against the wall, his head falling back. "Bastard’s probably drunk. Always is when he calls."

Bill sat back down at the console, his movements deliberate. "Let him stew. We’ve got work to do."

Jeff nodded, though his anger hadn’t subsided. He picked up his guitar again, the strings groaning under his aggressive strumming. "You think he’s right?"

Bill didn’t look up. "About what?"

Jeff hesitated, his voice quieter now. "That this album’s a waste. That we’re just… dragging her ghost around."

For a long moment, Bill said nothing. Then he glanced up, his expression unreadable. "This album isn’t for Gilley. It’s not even for her. It’s for us. And if we don’t finish it, we’ll regret it for the rest of our lives."

Jeff nodded slowly, his grip on the guitar loosening. The anger ebbed, leaving behind something more fragile.

"Yeah," he said finally, his voice almost a whisper. "You’re right."

### **Unexpected Harmony**
**Location**: Bill’s Studio
**Date**: December 4, 2013

The faint hum of a private jet echoed over Cincinnati's winter skyline as Avril stepped onto the tarmac, a bundle of excitement and hesitation bundled against the chill. She hadn’t seen Jeff or Bill since the memorial. Her duffel bag slung over her shoulder, she arrived at Bill's studio a little after 9 p.m., her boots clicking against the pavement outside.

Inside, Jeff was slouched on the couch, idly strumming his guitar. Bill was meticulously setting up a mic stand, a deep focus etched into his brow.

"Guess who I found some time for," Bill announced, his tone deliberately nonchalant. He stepped aside as Avril appeared in the doorway, a confident smirk playing on her lips.

Jeff froze mid-strum. "Avril?" His voice carried equal parts surprise and confusion.

"Nice to see you too, Jeff," she said with a wink, hanging her coat on the rack by the door. Her eyes scanned the room before settling on him.

Bill grinned, oblivious to the tension simmering beneath the surface. "Thought we could use some of Avril’s harmonies on this track. Adds a little punch, right?" He gestured for her to step inside the booth.

Jeff stood, his guitar dangling loosely in his grip. "You, uh, didn’t think to mention this earlier?"

"Didn’t think I needed to," Bill replied. He adjusted a knob on the mixing console, utterly missing the brief exchange of looks between Avril and Jeff.

Their eyes locked for just a moment too long—an unspoken question hanging between them, unanswered.

---

**Studio Booth**

Avril slid on the headphones, her lips brushing the mic as she hummed softly. Bill queued up the instrumental track. Jeff stood behind the glass, arms folded, trying and failing to look disinterested.

"Okay," Bill’s voice crackled over the intercom. "From the second chorus. Feel free to layer it up a bit."

The playback started, and Avril’s voice cut through the melody like smoke through moonlight.

*Kathy, the weight is heavy now…*

Her harmony wrapped around the lead vocal with an intimacy that made Jeff’s throat tighten. He looked away, trying to focus on the notes, but his fingers drummed against his arm, betraying his restlessness.

By the second take, Jeff had abandoned pretense. He grabbed a pair of headphones and adjusted the levels on the board. "Pull the reverb back on her high end," he said to Bill.

Bill glanced at him but obliged. "You want to engineer this now?"

"Just trust me."

Avril caught Jeff’s voice over the intercom. Her lips curved slightly, and she doubled down on the emotional weight of the bridge.

*"Just let it end, a soft plea escapes…"*

When she finished, there was a loaded pause. Jeff leaned into the mic. "Again. Just the bridge this time. More sustain on the word ‘plea.’"

"Whatever you say, boss," Avril replied, a teasing edge in her tone.

The hours stretched into the early morning. Each pass felt like peeling away a new layer of the song’s soul. Avril's harmonies grew richer, more haunting, and Jeff found himself increasingly captivated.

---

**Control Room**

By 3 a.m., Bill was hunched over the console, eyes drooping. Jeff stood beside him, energized, his hand resting lightly on the back of Avril’s chair as she leaned forward to listen to the playback.

"That’s it," Jeff murmured, more to himself than anyone else. "That’s the sound."

Bill yawned, stretching. "You two wanna keep going, knock yourselves out. I’m calling it for tonight."

As Bill trudged off, Avril turned to Jeff. "You’re really pushing this one."

Jeff shrugged, his expression softening. "It’s an important track."

"For her," Avril said quietly.

Jeff didn’t answer. His gaze lingered on her, a flicker of something unspoken passing between them.

### **Crossed Wires**
**Location**: Bill’s Studio Parking Lot
**Time**: 3:17 a.m.

The studio’s fluorescent lights buzzed faintly as Avril and Jeff stepped outside into the cold December air. A thin fog hung low over the parking lot, muting the world beyond the stark yellow glow of the overhead lamps.

Jeff jammed his hands into his jacket pockets, his breath visible in the frosty air. "So," he started, pausing as if he wasn’t sure what to say next.

Avril leaned against her car, arms crossed, a coy smile tugging at her lips. "You’re not bad at this whole music thing, you know. I might even be impressed."

Jeff chuckled, shaking his head. "Yeah, well, don’t spread that around. Ruins my whole ‘apathetic artist’ vibe."

Avril tilted her head, studying him. "You’ve changed since the last time we worked together."

"How so?"

"You’re… softer." She paused, letting the word hang in the air. "But not in a bad way. More… real."

Jeff looked away, scuffing his boot against the asphalt. "Yeah, well, life has a way of doing that to you."

A silence settled between them, thick with unspoken truths. Avril finally broke it. "Hilton's five minutes from here. I’ve got a bottle of decent Scotch in my room. We could… unwind."

Jeff’s eyes snapped back to hers, the suggestion catching him off guard. He opened his mouth to respond, but the sound of the studio door creaking open interrupted him.

---

**From the Shadows**

Bill stepped into the lot, pausing when he saw them. He stayed in the shadows, unseen but close enough to hear. His brow furrowed as he caught the tail end of their conversation.

"Avril…" Jeff’s voice was low, cautious. "I don’t think that’s a great idea."

"Why not?" she countered, her tone light but her eyes sharp. "We’re both adults. It’s not like this is the first time we’ve been alone together."

Jeff sighed, running a hand through his hair. "It’s not about that. It’s just… complicated."

"Everything’s complicated, Jeff." Avril pushed off the car and stepped closer to him. "But tonight doesn’t have to be. Just come with me. No expectations, no strings."

Bill leaned back against the wall, his arms crossed tightly over his chest. His expression was unreadable, but his jaw clenched as he watched the exchange.

---

**Moment of Decision**

Jeff hesitated, his gaze dropping to the ground. "I appreciate the offer. Really. But I can’t."

Avril’s face fell, just slightly. She recovered quickly, flashing him a teasing smile. "Your loss."

Jeff chuckled, the tension easing between them. "Yeah, probably."

She reached out, lightly punching his arm. "See you tomorrow, rock star." With that, she slipped into her car, the engine purring to life as she drove off into the night.

As the sound of her car faded, Jeff turned and saw Bill stepping out of the shadows.

"How long have you been standing there?" Jeff asked, his tone equal parts curious and defensive.

"Long enough." Bill’s voice was calm, but his eyes held a flicker of something deeper.

Jeff sighed. "It wasn’t what it sounded like."

Bill raised an eyebrow. "Wasn’t it?"

Jeff didn’t answer. The two men stood in silence, the weight of the unspoken thick between them.

### **The Floodgates Open**
**Location**: Bill’s Studio Parking Lot
**Time**: 3:25 a.m.

Jeff and Bill stood beneath the weak glow of the overhead lamps, the night air brittle with cold and tension. Bill took a step closer, his boots scraping against the asphalt.

"You’re going to fuck this up again," Bill said flatly, his voice carrying the weight of years of frustration.

Jeff blinked, stunned for a moment. "What the hell are you talking about?"

Bill jabbed a finger toward the spot where Avril’s car had been moments earlier. "That. Her. You don’t think I saw what was going on? Jesus, Jeff, it’s like you’re addicted to making bad decisions."

Jeff’s jaw tightened. "Don’t start with me, Bill."

"Why not?" Bill shot back, his voice rising. "You’ve got that look again. The one you had with Kathy. And we both know how that ended."

Jeff’s face darkened. "Don’t you dare bring her into this."

"Oh, I’ll bring her into it," Bill snapped. "Because I’m the one who has to clean up the mess every damn time. You crash and burn, and somehow, it always falls on me to keep things together."

Jeff took a step closer, his posture tense. "You think you’re so perfect, Bill? Sitting up in your little studio, acting like you’re the only one who gives a shit? Newsflash: you’re not."

Bill laughed bitterly. "Perfect? Far from it. But at least I’m not out here sabotaging everything we’ve built because I can’t keep my head straight."

Jeff’s temper flared. "You don’t know a damn thing about what I’ve been through. What *we’ve* been through."

Bill’s face hardened. "I know enough. I know you’ve got a pattern, Jeff. And I know it’s only a matter of time before you drag us all down with you."

Jeff’s voice dropped, cold and venomous. "If you hate me so much, why the fuck are you still here?"

"Because someone has to be," Bill snarled. "Someone has to hold this band together while you’re busy tearing it apart."

The words hung in the air, sharp and cutting. For a moment, neither man spoke, their breaths visible in the frosty night.

### **Silence Broken by the Road**
**Location**: I-75 Northbound, 3:45 a.m.

The roar of the highway was a dull, endless hum as Jeff gripped the steering wheel. His knuckles were pale, the tension from the confrontation with Bill still clawing at him. He glanced at the clock on the dash: 3:45 a.m. Too late for anything, too early for everything.

The road stretched ahead in a blur of orange sodium lights, the occasional semi-truck rumbling past him in the right lane. Jeff didn’t turn on the radio. The silence, though suffocating, felt deserved.

He replayed the argument in his head. The bitterness in Bill’s voice, the finality of his words. *You’re going to fuck this up again.* It wasn’t just anger—there was something deeper, heavier. Disappointment.

Jeff swallowed hard, his throat dry.

The weight of Bill’s words gnawed at him. He wanted to scream, to curse, to let it all out, but instead, he pressed the gas pedal harder. The speedometer edged past 75, then 80. The needle climbed like it could outrun his thoughts.

---

#### **Exit Signs and Memories**

Around mile marker 22, Jeff spotted a sign for an all-night diner: *Hazel’s Home Cookin’—Exit 25.* He thought about stopping, about drowning himself in stale coffee and greasy eggs, but his hands didn’t move to the blinker.

Instead, his mind drifted to Avril. The way she looked at him tonight—soft, unsure, yet curious. There was something unspoken between them, a weight heavier than words. He wondered what might’ve happened if Bill hadn’t interrupted.

Then Kathy’s face flashed through his mind, pale and still in that hospital bed. Her presence lingered, a ghost haunting his decisions.

“You’re running on fumes,” he muttered to himself, his voice hoarse and cracked.

---

#### **A Restless Stop**

At Exit 32, Jeff finally pulled off. A gas station loomed in the darkness, its fluorescent lights buzzing faintly. He parked by the pumps but didn’t get out right away. Instead, he sat there, the engine ticking as it cooled.

He pulled his phone from his jacket pocket and stared at it. There was a message from Cremeans—*Call me when you can.* Nothing from Bill. Nothing from Avril.

His finger hovered over Avril’s name. Just a tap, and he could…what? Say something? Apologize? Explain what?

Instead, he threw the phone onto the passenger seat, where it landed with a dull thud.

### **Lost and Found**
**Location**: I-75 Northbound, 3:57 a.m.

Jeff squinted at the green highway sign as it whipped past him. **Exit 35A**. His exit had been **32**, and he’d blown right by it.

“Goddammit,” he muttered, slamming his hand against the steering wheel. His breath hitched as he gripped it tighter, his palms slick with sweat. Was he really so angry—so tangled up in his own head—that he’d missed the giant, lit-up clue to *go home*?

The realization hit him like a sucker punch: it wasn’t just anger. It was grief. A deep, gnawing ache that had been eating at him ever since Kathy landed in that sterile hellscape of a hospital bed.

He pulled onto the shoulder, flipped his hazards on, and sat for a moment, the low hum of semis flying past his car filling the void. The blinking hazard lights reflected against the slick pavement like tiny, mocking signals of his own indecision.

“Turn the fuck around,” he muttered to himself.

With a sharp twist of the wheel, Jeff swung the car into a U-turn across the empty median, the tires spitting up gravel as he straightened out, now heading back southbound.

---

#### **Rationalizing the Irreversible**

Jeff’s mind churned as the car picked up speed. He told himself it wasn’t about Avril. Not really. It wasn’t about her soft voice or the way her gaze lingered longer than it should. No, he needed someone—*anyone*—to listen. Bill didn’t get it. Cremeans didn’t get it. Hell, Kathy, in all her brilliance, wouldn’t have gotten it. Not this.

Avril might.

Or maybe she wouldn’t, but at least he’d feel something. Something *different*.

---

#### **Drowning the Noise**

Jeff reached for his phone, punching at the screen until his playlist opened. He didn’t care what came on as long as it was loud enough to drown out his thoughts.

The first song blared through the car’s speakers, an industrial scream of distorted guitars and guttural vocals. Nine Inch Nails—"Wish." Perfect.

*"This is the first day of my last days…"*

He cranked the volume to the max, the bass reverberating through the car. His fingers tapped against the steering wheel, matching the rhythm like a nervous tic. The music filled every corner of the cabin, shaking loose whatever emotions he couldn’t put into words.

---

#### **The Hilton**
**Location**: Downtown Cincinnati, 4:25 a.m.

Jeff rolled up to the Hilton, his pulse pounding in his ears. The lobby lights gleamed through the glass doors, sterile and far too bright for this hour.

He turned off the car, the sudden silence almost deafening. The clock on the dash glowed at him—4:25 a.m. He rubbed his face, the stubble on his jaw scratching against his palms.

He sat there for a moment, staring at the hotel entrance, Avril’s room somewhere beyond. Rationalizing.

*It’s not about sex.  It’s about feeling human again. It’s about making someone hear you.*

Jeff snorted bitterly, shaking his head. "Who the hell am I kidding?"

### **The Hilton: Room 517**
**Location**: Downtown Cincinnati, 4:35 a.m.

Jeff stood in front of the hotel room door, his heart pounding in time with the echo of his boots on the hallway carpet. His knuckles hovered inches away from the polished wood.

*Just knock,* he told himself. *She invited you here. You’re not breaking in.*

But the stakes felt enormous, the silence of the hallway deafening. Finally, with a sharp breath, he rapped on the door. Three quick knocks.

The sound of soft footsteps approached from the other side, a faint shuffle of movement. Then the lock clicked, and the door creaked open.

There she was. Avril.

And she was naked.

Not half-dressed. Not wrapped in a towel. Fully and unapologetically bare.

Jeff blinked, struggling to maintain eye contact as his gaze instinctively darted anywhere but down. The soft golden light of the room backlit her, highlighting every detail of her toned, tattooed frame.

She didn’t flinch, didn’t rush to cover herself. Instead, she smirked, holding out a half-full bottle of whiskey.

“You’re predictable,” she said, her voice a low murmur, smoky and tinged with amusement.

Jeff took the bottle, his fingers brushing against hers. It felt heavier than it should have, like it carried more weight than just alcohol. He stared at it for a moment, then back at her.

“And you’re not?” he countered, his voice hoarse.

Her smirk widened as she stepped aside, motioning for him to come in without saying another word. The door clicked shut behind him.

---

### **Room 517: The Spiral**
The whiskey burned as Jeff took a swig straight from the bottle, the amber liquid searing his throat and chasing away the residual numbness of the drive. He sank into the edge of the room’s plush armchair, letting his head fall back as Avril moved around him, as casual in her nudity as a cat prowling its territory.

“Nice decor,” he said, his voice brittle and cracking under the weight of sarcasm. “What is this, mid-century existential crisis?”

She didn’t laugh. Didn’t quip back. Instead, she perched on the bed across from him, pulling a pillow over her lap—not for modesty, but to toy with absentmindedly. “You don’t actually want to talk about the room, do you?”

Jeff let the silence stretch, twisting the bottle in his hands, letting the whiskey catch the dim light. “No,” he admitted finally. “But I don’t know what the hell I do want.”

Avril leaned forward, elbows on her knees, her gaze piercing. “You drove here, Jeff. You knew what you wanted when you knocked on the door. You’re the one who’s always got the plan.”

“Not this time.” His laugh was bitter, jagged. He leaned forward, cradling the bottle like it was the only thing holding him together. “This time, I’m just… lost.”

Avril tilted her head, her voice softening. “Lost in what?”

Jeff hesitated, his knuckles whitening as his grip tightened on the bottle. “Everything,” he said finally. “Bill. Kathy. The band. Me. It’s all…” He gestured vaguely, his hand slicing through the air. “It’s all broken.”

For a moment, Avril said nothing. She watched him with those sharp eyes of hers, the silence heavy but not unkind. Then she rose and padded to the mini-bar. Grabbing a second glass, she poured herself a generous amount of whiskey before sitting back down.

“To broken things,” she said, raising the glass in a mock toast.

Jeff huffed a breath that might’ve been a laugh. He clinked the neck of the bottle against her glass. “To broken things.”

They drank, the silence stretching again, this time more comfortable. Finally, Avril spoke, her tone quieter. “You want to talk about Kathy?”

“No.” The answer came too quickly, too forcefully. He stared at the bottle. “Yes. God, I don’t know. What’s there to say? She’s dying, Avril. She’s already gone, but she’s… still here. And I don’t know how to deal with that.”

Avril nodded, her eyes never leaving his. “So you wrote her a song.”

Jeff’s head snapped up. “It’s not—”

“Don’t lie to me,” she cut in, her voice gentle but firm. “I’ve heard enough of your lyrics to know when you’re bleeding into them. That song, Jeff? It’s her. It’s you begging her to let go so you don’t have to.”

His chest tightened, anger flaring for a moment before it crumbled into something raw. “You think I don’t know that?” he whispered. “You think I don’t hear it every damn time I play it back?”

Avril set her glass down and crossed the space between them, sitting on the arm of his chair. Her hand rested on his shoulder, grounding him. “Then say it. Say what you’re afraid of.”
