**INT. ERVIN J. NUTTER CENTER FLOOR — LATE AFTERNOON**

House lights like milk. Air-conditioning that smells faintly of penny dust and popcorn grease. The stage is a black altar half-built, trusses yawning up into shadow where the gods of lighting will someday sit and approve or smite. Five hours to doors, which is five lifetimes if you’re the kind of man who counts seconds like rosary beads. Bill does.

He’s alone in the lip of the stage, knees spread for balance, back straight in that military way he gets when he’s pretending not to care. On a rolling stand: the synthesizer—already spotless, a chrome-toothed smile under the dimmers. He treats it like a relic that survived fire. Microfiber in one hand, breath caught in his chest, he eases the cloth across the keys as if each is a soft tooth in a dangerous mouth. He polishes the already polished. He is not cleaning the synth; he is exorcising the future.

He tells himself it’s dust he’s after. It isn’t. He’s after control.

Behind him the empty bowl of seats listens, hollow and gossipy, the arena gossiping about all the other boys before him who made a mess of their chances. He hears that gossip and tightens. He makes circles with the cloth—small, precise, priestly circles. He keeps his wrists loose, the way he keeps his wrists when he’s programming LFOs, everything about modulation and nothing about doubt. A flight case thumps somewhere in the bowels of the catwalks. He doesn’t look up. He won’t give chaos the courtesy.

People call [[4.1 BILL DAVENPORT character bible|Bill]] a producer type, like that’s a personality flaw you can shave down. He leans into it. He’s young and already overboard with devotion, breath-holding, waiting for feedback squeals that only exist in nightmares, imagining cold solder joints and the silent failure of a power rail at bar one of song three when the cameras are rolling and the audience is reciting their love like a verdict. He wipes the pitch wheel, then the mod wheel, thumbs the rubber edge like it can confess. His reflection arrives in the glossy screen—boyish jaw, eyes too serious, a curl of hair that refuses to obey product. He examines himself the way he examines waveforms: for clipping.

You can smell the citrus of the cleaning fluid, low and sexy and inappropriate for a tool so clinical. He likes the contradiction. He likes that his neatness is a rebellion in a culture that worships “good enough.” Bill’s version of lust is this: a surface without streaks, a patch bank without orphans, a routing matrix that could take a thrown bottle and still sing. He whispers—not a prayer, not even words—just that hummed lattice he builds under every mix, the skeleton of his thinking. Genius without humility, failure without apology: he’s trying on both in front of the synth like jackets.

Stagehands pass and clock him, the way you clock a kid who’s already dressed for the job he hasn’t earned. Somebody coughs out a joke about “virginal keys.” Bill doesn’t rise. He believes in rising only when the faders do. He moves to the back panel, counts the jacks, traces each cable with two fingers as if reading a Braille romance: mains left, mains right, DI to the snake, spare line coiled like a sleeping snake that might dream itself awake if you breathe too hard. He wipes even the knurled metal around the inputs. Clean metal conducts better—that’s not true, but he needs it to be tonight. He needs to believe that ritual changes physics.

He imagines the soundcheck to come—the sibilants that will try to bite his ears, the monitors like horses who hate the bit, the drummer tapping an impatient Morse of “hurry up” even though there is no hurry, only precision achieved in public. He polishes the spacebar on the sequencer keyboard, ridiculous because who polishes a spacebar, but the gesture steadies him. He will not be the reason a song breaks its ankle. He will be the rail the song holds when the tempo staggers.

From the nosebleeds, the arena lets go a cold sigh that tumbles over him like river water. He inhales it and puts his mouth close to the chassis to speak in a voice he wouldn’t use for any person. “You and me,” he says, and the synth hears, because everything hears in a building like this if you love it hard enough. “No surprises.”

He flips the power on just to watch the boot sequence, then off again—superstition disguised as rehearsal. He wipes the display glass in long strokes. He thinks about flux and tape heads even though this isn’t tape; he thinks about crosstalk between people and parts, how a band is a bundle of dirty signals that you either gate or embrace. He picks lint from a fader with tweezers (yes, he carries tweezers; yes, he will die for the bit), then cleans the tweezers with the corner of the cloth. There’s a small cuticle tear on his thumb from a flight case hinge; he worries it the way he worries resonances, then stops himself. Blood on the synth would be a story. He wants a different story.

In his head he arranges the evening like patch cords: opener’s kick drum is 2 dB too fat; carve at 80. Lead vocal needs a halo but only in the choruses. The club last night taught him the room is honest when empty and a liar when full. He lists his sins and solutions: I push the lows when I’m nervous; tonight, keep spine tall and hands slow. I trust preset reverbs too much; tonight, author the damn space.

Someone asks from the dark if he’s seen the set lists. “Yeah,” he says, without turning. The cloth makes another slow pass, sanctifying the octave. “I’ve seen the shape of it.” Which is true in the way thunder is true about lightning. He has not only seen the set; he has already mixed it in his mind three times, once for triumph, once for disaster, and once for the ordinary cruelty of a show that is merely good. He refuses the third outcome like a bad cable.

The arena watches him fall in love with control again. The arena has seen worse romances.

There’s a tenderness to how he seats the dust cover when he’s finished, how he runs his knuckles along the edge to make sure it seals like a promise. He tucks the microfiber into his back pocket, then—because he can’t help it—he lifts the cover again, just a corner, to look. Still clean. Still his. He laughs at himself, a private, mean little church giggle, and finally straightens up, eyes adjusting to the catwalk glare, body unfolding like a stand locked in place too long.

When he steps off the riser, he checks the tape marks on the deck, verifies the exact geometry of where the stand will live once the drum world expands to its arrogant borders. He nudges the front legs half an inch, then back, finding true north the way sailors used to—by feel and stubbornness. He squats to tape the casters, then tapes the tape because he’s Bill and it is not enough for the world to behave; the world must behave twice.

Five hours to doors. Four hours to soundcheck. Three to panic, two to acceptance, one to grace. He measures all of it against the immaculate surface of a machine that cannot love him back and decides love doesn’t have to reciprocate to be holy. He presses the cover flat, palms it once more, and in the ghosted reflection of the glossy vinyl he catches his own face and the silhouette of the whole empty coliseum behind him. He looks very young and also like someone who will work until the work admits defeat.

“Right,” he says to nobody who will understand and to everyone who will feel it later. “Let’s make it safe enough to be dangerous.” Then he turns toward the snake pit and the coils and the quiet civil war of a thousand small choices that decide whether a night becomes legend or merely loud. His shirt sticks a little to his spine. The cloth warms in his pocket. The synth waits, perfectly clean, the calm eye in the storm he’s been building since breakfast.
