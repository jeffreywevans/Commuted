# **“Duct Tape, Thunder, and a Goddamn 1856 Cowbell”**  
**By Jim Brown**  
**As told to *Modern Drummer***

If you’re reading this, it probably means you’ve heard me hit something.

Maybe it was in Madison Square Garden, when we closed with “Come Inside Me.”  Maybe it was a bootleg tape from ‘98, back when Gilley used to shotgun PBRs and scream like his ex-girlfriend was chasing him with a machete.  Or maybe it was just last week, when Avril cut the lights during “*Come on Man, Act Your Age*” and I let the snare crack like a loaded revolver.

Wherever it was—you heard it. You felt it. And it all started with a birthday, a cow-tipping dare, and a cowbell forged before Lincoln was elected.

No joke.

See, people always ask me how I got my start in drums. And most of the time, I lie. I say, “marching band,” or “started in the church,” or “played trashcans as a kid,” because it’s easier than the truth:

I built my first kit like a sparrow builds a nest.

Pawn shop bass drum from 1978\.  Snare was made from three brands, none of them cooperating.  Cymbals cracked like windshield glass.  Hi-hats were so mismatched they argued.  And the cowbell? Oh, that beautiful bastard…

Let me tell you about the cowbell.

---

**The Night That Changed Everything (And Nothing)**

It’s 1990\. I’m broke. Like, flip-burgers-at-the-ice cream shop-and-sleep-under-a-Metallica-poster broke.

I’ve got no kit. No money. No plan. Just a band full of teenage rage and a rehearsal garage that smells like mildew and melted amp cords. One night, Gilley and Jeremy Evans decide we need to "blow off steam."

Their idea of steam? Cow-tipping.

Now, before PETA mails me a glitter bomb, we didn’t actually *tip* anything. What we did do was stumble into a barn outside Springfield that had been abandoned since before the state had decent plumbing.

And hanging from a rusted nail on the far wall — I swear to God, lit by moonlight like something out of a Spielberg movie — was a goddamn cowbell.

It was heavy. Forged, not welded. Cold like an engine block.  Stamped underneath? “Dayton Ironworks. Est. 1856.”

Gilley looked at me and said, “Happy Birthday, asshole.”  Jeremy just shrugged and said, “If it fits in the backpack, it’s meant to be.”

That bell has been on every kit I’ve ever played.  Every arena, every album, every goddamn encore.  That cowbell has heard Kathy’s voice.  That cowbell rang the night Gilley died.  That cowbell played the first track Avril ever sang with us.  And it’s still bolted to my rack, 35 years later.

---

**My First Kit Was a Frankenstein, and It Had a Soul**

That birthday cowbell was just the beginning.

The bass drum? A 22" Tama with a peeling wrap and a resonance like somebody getting punched in the diaphragm.  

The snare? Frankenstein’d from a Pearl shell, Ludwig throw-off, duct tape, and warped dignity.  

The hi-hats? One top, one bottom, probably from different decades, but they sizzled like bacon grease if you stomped ‘em just right.  

Cymbals? One used to be a crash until I dropped it at Gilley’s. I made it my ride. Out of spite.

Tuning? I used a screwdriver. Sometimes a quarter taped to a pencil. Never owned a drum key that wasn’t half-stripped.

But when Jeff dropped into a bass groove, and Kathy lit up that distortion pedal, and Gilley started howling like he was pulling ghosts out of the drywall?

That junkyard kit became a resurrection engine.

It didn’t just make noise.  It preached.

---

**If You're Just Starting Out**

Don’t chase endorsements.  Don’t chase perfection. Chase feel.  Chase grit.  Chase that one sound that makes the hairs on your arm stand up.  The best kit I ever played cost me less than $60 and my last good pair of gloves.  The most expensive kit I own cost more than my first house.  But guess which one I still think about?  Here’s a hint: it came with an 1856 cowbell and a lifetime warranty in spirit only.

**INTERVIEWER EXCERPT — MODERN DRUMMER, MAY 2025**

**On Avril Lavigne, the New Lead Singer of Commuted**

**INTERVIEWER:**  
Jim, you’ve got fans geeking out over your rack toms and triggers — but let’s be real: every headline lately has been about Avril. New frontwoman. New blood. New energy. What’s it like sharing the stage with her?

**JIM:**  
Look — there are singers who fill a room.  Avril *redraws the room.*

First rehearsal, she didn’t even sing yet. Just stood there, head tilted, arms crossed like she’d seen the ghost of Kathy in the rafters and wasn’t impressed. And then she opened her mouth and it was like —  *“Oh. Oh, we’re safe. We’re weird again. Good.”*

She’s not replacing anybody.  You can’t replace Kathy. Kathy was a meteor.  But Avril is a storm system. She shifts pressure. She stirs the air. She doesn’t copy. She *commands.*

And yeah — she’s gonna be the media focus. Of course she is. She’s sharp, strange, and sings like she’s testifying in three languages at once.

But here’s the thing:  She listens to the *drums.*  That’s all I ever ask.  You give me a singer who sings inside the pocket — not on top of it, not around it, inside it — and I’ll follow her through hell with a floor tom.

**SIDEBAR**

**“Phil Collins Is Zeus, and I’ll Die on This Hill”**  
**By Jim Brown**

You want to know who I still think about when I’m halfway through a three-hour set, sweat in my eyes, ears bleeding, and the crowd’s demanding blood?

Phil. Fucking. Collins.

And I’m not being ironic.

People forget — or maybe they just never knew — that Phil Collins wasn’t just some soft-rock dude with a mullet and an emotionally complex forehead. Before the ballads, before the divorce albums, before the Disney soundtracks…

He was a goddamn machine.

Listen to Genesis live from ‘77 to ‘83. You’ll hear time bend. You’ll hear drum fills that feel like weather patterns. And here’s the real kicker — he sang lead while playing drums like a warlock. Try it. Try doing *anything* more complex than “Billie Jean” on a kit and singing anything other than your Social Security number.

I get asked all the time, “Who influenced your pocket?”  That’s easy. Phil.  Those gated drums? That was an accident. A beautiful, magical studio accident. And instead of fixing it, he said, *let’s build an entire fucking decade on it.

“In the Air Tonight” isn’t a drum solo.  It’s a summoning.  It’s the sound of thunder getting mic’d.

He made restraint powerful. Groove holy. He taught me that you don’t need to be fast — you need to be *right.* Every hit should feel like it matters. Every silence should feel earned.

People wanna put Bonham, Peart, and Moon on the mountain — and they should. Those are titans.

But Phil Collins is Olympus.  Because he didn’t just hit drums.  He made the air *wait* for him.

Yeah, okay. I get it.  Every time I name-drop Phil Collins in an interview, someone smirks. Maybe they think I’m joking. Maybe they still haven’t forgiven “Sussudio.” Maybe they’re allergic to gated reverb.

Let me clarify something: I am absolutely serious.  And yes — it is an obsession.  But it’s *useful*.  I didn’t just admire Phil. I *absorbed* him.

There’s a fill from “Mama” that I worked into a live version of “Inspector #12” in 2016. Nobody noticed — except one sound guy in Brussels who came backstage after the set and whispered, *“Did you Collins that drop?”* And I almost cried. That’s how deep it runs.

The gated reverb snare from “In the Air Tonight”? I chased that sound like it owed me money for 15 years. Finally found a workaround in a Slovenian plug-in, then tracked it to tape just to make it crack like it did in ’81.

But the real thing?  The real gift Phil gave me?

The pause.  

The hanging silence.  

The “I could hit this drum now… but I won’t.” That restraint shaped my entire style.  Kathy once told me my playing makes her “feel like she’s watching someone build a cathedral one hammer strike at a time.” I said thanks. Then I whispered to myself, *That’s Phil.*

He made patience sound like power. He made drama out of delay. And the man did it all while looking like a tax accountant from Essex. That’s magic.

So yes — laugh at me if you must.  I’ve got two Grammys, one Hall of Fame jacket, and a five-minute gated snare solo on track six of our last album to back me up.  Call it obsession.  I call it respect with a backbeat.

**DRUMMER’S CORNER – TECH BREAKDOWN**

**“Phil Collins Is My Blueprint: A Technical Breakdown from Jim Brown”**  
**Modern Drummer, May 2025**

---

**DRUM MACHINES: NOT CHEATING — CHORAL DRUMMING**

“People always ask how we got that ‘two drummers but one brain’ sound on tracks like *Spoken Ransom* or *Antimony Parade*. My answer’s simple: I *am* both.”

Jim’s approach to drum machines is not about convenience — it’s about creating depth.

In the studio, and increasingly live, Jim blends live acoustic drums with *precision-triggered patterns* run from vintage and modern units simultaneously. The result is a three-dimensional percussive field: warm, organic snare hits layered over algorithmically perfect ghost notes; analog toms thundering alongside tuned samples mapped to melodic intervals.

“I don’t loop just for rhythm. I loop for tone. For space. For the percussive echo of the melody itself.”

His current live rig includes:

* Roland SPD-SX Pro – runs primary sample layers (many custom-mapped from early Commuted sessions)
* Elektron Analog Rytm MKII – sequenced per track, often sidechained against the acoustic kick for “breathing room”
* Custom midi-split chain feeding a second pad rig behind him — *“Because sometimes one human body isn’t enough.”*

---

**WHY THIS ISN’T JUST A GIMMICK**

Jim makes it clear: this isn’t just backing tracks. Every electronic hit is triggered, layered, or played in tandem with live input. The effect? It sounds like *two fully in-sync drummers*, one analog, one digital — with unified emotional timing.

Fans describe the live experience as “overwhelming in the best way.” Engineers describe it as “a fucking miracle.”

“I don’t use drum machines to replace me. I use them to *complete* me. I’m not fighting the machine — I’m partnering with it.”

---

**PHIL COLLINS, REDUX — IF PHIL HAD A SEQUENCER ARM**

Jim says Phil Collins laid the philosophical groundwork for this approach. *“Phil’s use of studio layering, gated effects, and Simmons pads — that was proto-hybrid drumming before we even had a name for it. I just evolved it.”*

Where Phil used gated ambiance to create impact, Jim uses parallel motion — stereo-separated hits that mimic dialogue. It’s rhythm as narrative device.

“The way I see it? One snare tells the story. Two snares — one live, one sampled — tell the *argument* behind the story.”

**GATED REVERB: WHAT JIM STOLE AND WHY**

“Phil’s snare in *‘In the Air Tonight’* isn’t a snare. It’s a thunderclap wearing a tuxedo.”

* Phil's Original Signal Chain (approx):  
  Snare → Overhead mics → Room mic (mono) → SSL listen mic compressor → gate → reverb
* Jim's Modern Hybrid:  
  Snare top/bottom → Ribbon mic (room) → API preamp → digital gated verb tail → parallel distortion  
  *“The difference is Phil had magic by accident. I had to chase it through plugins, heartache, and Slovenian software engineers.”*

Jim’s go-to setting on his SPD-SX replicates the 1985 Genesis “Mama” snare with layered analog samples and transient shaping. He uses it live in the bridge of “Serpent Halo” and the intro of “Silhouettes and Cities.”

---

**VOCALS \+ DRUMS: THE COMBO THAT SHOULDN’T WORK, BUT DOES**

Jim has never sung lead vocals — but he designs his fills with vocal phrasing in mind, directly inspired by Phil’s dual role. His fills in “Radiator Blues” are timed to leave space for Avril’s breathwork — a trick lifted from watching Phil track “No Reply at All.”

“Drummers who don’t think like singers are doomed to fight the frontman. I learned from the guy who *was* both.”

---

   * ***Jim:*** *“That’s my left-hand bible. Every time I need to clean up my flams, I just play that live fill for 40 minutes.”*

---

**PHIL'S IMPACT ON JIM'S PHILOSOPHY**

“Collins taught me that groove is architecture. You’re not decorating the song. You’re *supporting* it. You build the tension, and you know *exactly* when to let it drop.”

Jim openly credits Phil’s *Balance of Bombast and Silence* as one of his greatest lessons. Where Bonham was thunder, Moon was chaos, and Peart was intellect, Collins was geometry. Everything deliberate. Everything anchored.

**FINAL WORD: FROM JIM TO PHIL**

“I’ve met Phi a few times. Never shared a stage. But every night, before I walk out and sit behind that kit, I tap my left stick once on my right knee and think, *don’t overplay it.* That’s the gospel of Phil. And I play it loud.”

**THE CONCEPT: REPETITION AS HYPNOSIS**

The heart of “Come Inside Me” is a paradox: a beat so simple it borders on laughable — yet so unrelenting that it becomes mesmerizing. A straight 4/4 loop. Kick, repeat. No swing. No groove. Just bump pump… bump pump… bump pump… bump pump.

“The drum machine part is a joke,” Jim says. “No seriously — it’s so stupidly simple I almost turned it off halfway through tracking. But that’s the trap. That’s the hypnotism. That’s the tension. And then I come in and knock their brain out of alignment.”

The machine is the anchor — mechanical, merciless, and devoid of flourish. It’s the cage.  
Jim’s kit is the intruder. The protestor. The animal scratching at the bars.

**THE HYBRID SETUP**

Jim ran both hardware drum machines and live mic’d acoustic kit simultaneously, tracking in real time over a pre-sequenced loop.

**Drum Machine Backbone:**

* Roland TR-707 for foundational kick and rim patterns
* Elektron Analog Rytm for mid-range texture, occasional reverse clap burst
* Pattern: 4-bar loop, 120 BPM, 4/4 time, with 16-bar hi-hat stutter swell
* Sidechain compression triggered by vocal pulses in verse 2 and verse 4

**Live Kit Configuration:**

* DW Collector’s Series 22"x18" kick (vintage maple, Evans EQ3 batter head)
* Ludwig Black Beauty 14"x6.5" snare (dry towel fold, taped)
* 13" rack tom (Remo Ambassador, tuned high for melodic overtone)
* 16" floor tom (tuned low for heavy decay and “sighing” counterweight)
* Istanbul Agop 22" ride, crashable edge (panned hard left in mix)
* 14" Paiste Signature Dark Crisp hi-hats (tight-locked with pedal articulation layered)
* 1856 Dayton Ironworks cowbell (used sparingly — 4 strikes total)
* Roland SPD-SX Pro – mapped to modulated handclap bursts and bitcrushed rim accents

**THE STRUCTURE**

The track is built as a rapid spiral: each loop builds pleasure. Then, every few measures, Jim drops in a live fill or percussive “shiver” that bends the pulse without breaking it. The result is claustrophobic tension, made intimate by selective human touch.

**Mixing Engineer Quote – Tara Kim (2011):**

“At first I thought the machine was broken — it sounded so static. Then I soloed Jim’s kit under it and realized he was *having a conversation with a wall.* And somehow, the wall blinked.”

**TRACK TIMESTAMPS & EVENTS**

* **0:00 – 2:00**  
  Machine only. Single relentless loop: kick \+ rim \+ tight closed hats. Slight fills. No modulation. The silence between hits is louder than the hits themselves.

*“It’s the sonic version of a mattress thumping. That’s what we wanted.”* **— Jim**

* **2:15**  
  First live intervention: ghosted hi-hat sweep from left overhead mic, just barely audible.

*“I wanted it to feel like a breath you’re not sure you heard.”*

* **3:28**  
  Rim accents begin to layer in 3/16 displacement. Jim plays them manually to create a "glitch feel" that isn't quantized.

* **4:42**  
  **Cowbell strike #1.**  No lead-in. No reverb. Raw mic. Placed dead center.

* **6:00 – 6:15**  
  Dual tom fill cascade over 16 bars. First full fill across the kit, culminating in a ride bell decay with side stick shuffle underneath. This “break” isn’t structural — it’s emotional.

* **7:01 – 7:09**  
  Crossfire fill between rack tom and floor tom. Snares dropped out. Loop continues uninterrupted. Leads into false chorus with Kathy’s distorted vocal breath loop.

* **8:30 – 9:10**  
  Jim drops out completely. Machine loop takes over again, now stripped down. This moment is where the live/human contrast becomes most apparent — and most emotionally effective.

* **10:16**  
  Final fill: descending triplets on snare and toms, ending in dead stop. 1.5 seconds of full silence.

*“That stop? That’s me leaving the room.”*  
The machine loop returns alone for the outro — cold, unchanging.

“The machine doesn’t move. That’s the point. That’s the trap. That’s the f\*\*\*ing *lie*. The audience starts to feel like it’ll never end — and that’s when I come in, not to free them, but to *remind* them they’re still inside. That’s what I’m playing.”

The song’s title and sensual thematic structure are entirely deliberate. Jim refers to it as *“percussion as intimacy disorder.”*

**TECHNICAL SIDEBAR**

**Live vs. Machine: Jim Brown’s Rhythmic Split Personality**

***Modern Drummer*****, May 2025**

> [!quote]
> *“Look, I’m a live guy. I always will be. You can’t automate human instinct. You can’t write a plugin for a gut feeling. But sometimes? A machine is exactly what the song needs. Not because it sounds better — but because it doesn’t feel anything. That’s the whole point.”*  
> **—Jim Brown**

**PHILOSOPHY: LIVE IS THE LANGUAGE — MACHINE IS THE TOOL**

Jim Brown has long been known for his hybrid setups: layered kits, live triggers, custom loops, and drum machines blended with acoustic mics to create textured chaos that somehow holds a pocket.

But his approach isn’t about novelty. It’s about emotional intent.

*“If a song is about longing, betrayal, confusion — I’m playing that live. Always. Because I want my mistakes in the take. I want the hesitation. The breath. But if a song is about obsession? Repetition? Getting stuck in a loop you can’t escape? That’s when the machine gets turned on.”*

**LIVE PLAYING — WHY IT’S HIS DEFAULT**

* Micro-expression: Jim’s live playing includes ghost notes, off-center stick attacks, and subtle foot pressure shifts that no machine can convincingly fake.
* Feel-first fills: Jim never writes fills in advance — he tracks them in the moment based on how the melody hits.
* Drum tuning as expression: He retunes toms between takes. “If the lyric sounds lower, the drum should too.”

> [!quote]
> *“I can feel when a take is too clean. It starts to sound like a solution instead of a story.”*


**WHEN THE MACHINE EARNS ITS SPOT**

* Loop pressure: For songs like *“Come Inside Me”*, Jim uses a TR-707 or Rytm MKII to establish rhythmic claustrophobia — a rigid, merciless loop that creates tension.
* Metronomic cruelty: Sometimes the machine’s indifference is the point. “It doesn’t care what you’re feeling. That’s the emotional texture.”
* Cinematic layering: In sections where he wants a drum to echo like memory — something unnatural, unsettling — he’ll blend a live hit with two or three triggered layers behind it: pitch-shifted, detuned, and mangled.

**THE RULE: NEVER LET THE MACHINE BE THE STORY**

> [!quote]
> *“It’s a supporting actor. It’s the scaffolding, not the skyline. If you walk away from a track talking about the drum machine instead of what the drums made you feel — I failed.”*

**JIM BROWN — INTERVIEW EXCERPT, *Modern Drummer*, June 2025**  
*On the Infamous Meadowlands Blowout, 2000*

“That show? Jesus Christ. That wasn’t a concert. That was a controlled demolition with a rhythm section.

I still don’t understand how we got away with it. I mean—we opened a stadium show with *The Sopranos* theme. Unannounced. Zero warning. Then Gilley comes out dressed like Tony Soprano’s bastard son and seduces 80,000 people into thinking we were about to do a Sinatra tribute—and instead, we dropped 'Not Taken Kindly' without a light cue or an intro. Kathy screamed *'licking up his shaft…'* at 130 decibels, and the mayor of New York, Rudy Giuliani was in the fourth row.

And that was just the first five minutes.

What happened next was pure Commuted. We tore through a warpath of songs—half of them out of order, none of them introduced, and all of them delivered like we had 15 minutes before the cops shut us down. Kathy set her mic stand on fire. Jeff got two nosebleeds. Gilley stripped down to a gold chain and a leather trench coat by the encore.

By the time we got to ‘Come Inside Me,’ the second deck was *literally swaying.* The fire marshal showed up mid-set and *stayed to watch*.

I’ve played Red Rocks. I’ve headlined Lollapalooza. But that night in Jersey?  That was our riot in the cathedral.  That was the night we didn’t give them a setlist.  We gave them a hostage situation with rhythm and sex.  And I would do it again tomorrow if I could still feel my left knee.”

**JIM BROWN — INTERVIEW EXCERPT, *Modern Drummer*, June 2025**  
***On Covering “Born to Run” at the Meadowlands***

“Okay, first of all, let’s just be clear—I didn’t think we were gonna do it. I thought it was one of Gilley’s classic *‘what if we…’* rehearsal jokes. You know? Like, *'What if we open with “Superstition” but played backwards through a Leslie cab?'* That kind of thing.

But then soundcheck comes and Gilley’s humming the chords. Jeff starts sliding into that bassline like he’s been waiting twenty years for someone to ask. Kathy’s warming up vocals in full Jersey gospel. And I’m looking around like—*we’re actually doing this? Here?*

At the goddamn Meadowlands?

That’s *Bruce’s* church. You don’t walk into the Boss’s sanctuary with a lighter and gasoline. You walk in with ***fear***. Or not at all.  So yeah—I was nervous. Not because I couldn’t play it. But because I *couldn’t screw it up.* That’s a sacred track. Every fill in that song was built to feel like freedom punching through the windshield of a stolen Chevy.

But let me tell you—when we hit that breakdown, when Kathy hit *‘tramps like us…’* and 80,000 people screamed it back?

That wasn’t a cover.  That was a resurrection.

And the best part? I locked in the beat, closed my eyes, and for thirty seconds, I was seventeen again, playing in a garage in Bethel Township, dreaming I could matter.

Bruce, wherever he was that night—I hope he felt it. Because we did it for the right reasons.

And no, I don’t remember if we got the bridge right. I just remember the crowd didn’t care.”

The first time, Meadowlands 2000, it was *‘Born to Run.’* Totally Gilley’s idea. I thought it was nuts. Like walking into God’s house and lighting a cigarette. But it worked. And the crowd? They didn’t cheer. They *roared.*

The second time, days later at MSG, we did *‘Atlantic City.’* Slowed it down. Made it eerie. Kathy’s voice on that one? Unholy. And the whole arena went quiet. Not reverent. Just… **stunned.** Like we’d hijacked something sacred and handed it back different.
