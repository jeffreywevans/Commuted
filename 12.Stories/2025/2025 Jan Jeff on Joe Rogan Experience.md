Just after New Years 2025, the JRE lands [[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff Evans]], the bass player from Commuted. Joe is happy to welcome Jeff to the podcast.

(Intro music fades)

Joe: Welcome back to the JRE, folks! It’s just after New Year’s, 2025, and we’re kicking off the year with a real treat. We’ve had two members of the legendary rock band Commuted on the show already – Bill Davenport, their keyboardist and sound engineer, and Jim Brown, the powerhouse drummer. And today, we complete the trifecta. We’ve got the bassist, the creative force behind so much of their music, the man himself… Jeff Evans! Jeff, welcome to the Joe Rogan Experience!

Jeff: (A slightly gravelly voice) Thanks for having me, Joe. It’s… interesting to finally be here.

Joe: Man, it’s an honor. Commuted… you guys are legends. You’ve been a part of the soundtrack to so many people’s lives. It’s great to finally have you on the show.

Jeff: (A dry chuckle) Legends… that’s one word for it.

Joe: Well, it’s true! You’ve sold millions of records, you’ve had countless hit songs, you’ve influenced generations of musicians. You’ve even had a movie made about the band!

Jeff: (A slight pause) Yeah… the movie.

Joe: We talked about it with Jim. He had some… interesting things to say.

Jeff: (A low chuckle) I can imagine.

Joe: We’ve covered a lot of ground with Bill and Jim. We’ve talked about the band’s history, the music, the… complexities of the relationships within the band. We even talked about the burning down of the Taco Bell in Bethel Township.

Jeff: (A genuine laugh this time) Oh, yeah. The great Taco Bell tragedy. That’s a classic.

Joe: It’s an amazing story! Jim told it perfectly. He’s a great storyteller.

Jeff: He is. He’s always been good at… framing things.

Joe: So, Jeff, I’m really excited to finally get your perspective on everything. We’ve heard Bill and Jim’s sides of the story, now it’s your turn.

Jeff: (A slight hesitation) I’m not sure I have a… “side” of the story. It’s all… just the story.

Joe: Well, you were at the center of a lot of it. You wrote a book about it, even. Tour Bus Confessions.

Jeff: (A sigh) Yeah… that book.

Joe: It’s… a pretty revealing book.

Jeff: (A dry chuckle) That was the intention.

Joe: We talked about that with Jim too, about how much of that was… embellished for dramatic effect. He said it was actually toned down from what really happened.

Jeff: (A long pause) Yeah… that’s… probably true.

Joe: Wow. That’s… hard to believe.

Jeff: (A quiet voice) Some things are… hard to believe. Even when you lived through them.

(The atmosphere in the studio becomes more serious, the weight of the band’s history settling in as the conversation begins.)

Joe: So, Jeff, going back to the early days of the band, what was it like trying to find your place in the music scene? Who were your peers? Who did you feel a connection with?

Jeff: (A long pause, as if he’s searching through old memories) That was… one of the hardest things. Finding… peers. There wasn’t really anyone.

Joe: Really?

Jeff: Not really. There was only one band that made any sense, back then, in the early 90s. And that was… Nirvana.

Joe: Nirvana. Yeah. They were huge.

Jeff: They were… everything. They were the voice of a generation. Or so they said.

Joe: What do you mean?

Jeff: I never… bought into the whole… “voice of a generation” thing. I didn’t like the… nihilism. I didn’t appreciate Kurt Cobain singing about how he didn’t have a gun… and then… blowing his head off with said gun.

(Joe is visibly startled by the sudden shift in tone. Jeff’s voice has taken on a sharp, almost bitter edge.)

Joe: Wow. That’s… a pretty strong reaction.

Jeff: It was… a betrayal.

Joe: A betrayal?

Jeff: Yeah. They had… something special. That Unplugged performance…

Joe: Oh, man, that Unplugged… that’s iconic.

Jeff: It was… peak modern rock. It was… beautiful, it was raw, it was… honest. And then… he threw it all away.

Joe: You think they… threw it away?

Jeff: Absolutely. They had the potential to… change everything. To… inspire a generation. And they… blew it.

Joe: (Pauses, taken aback by Jeff’s intensity) That’s… a pretty harsh assessment.

Jeff: It’s the truth.

(Joe is a little startled. He’s heard that talking to Jeff can be like pulling teeth, that he can be quiet and reserved. But within seconds of this conversation, Jeff has already detonated a couple of emotional atom bombs.)

Joe: (Trying to steer the conversation back to a more neutral territory) So, you felt a connection to Nirvana’s music, but you disagreed with their… message?

Jeff: (Sighs) It wasn’t just the message. It was the… waste. The squandering of talent. The… self-destruction. It disgusted me.

(The atmosphere in the studio has become noticeably heavier. Joe realizes that this conversation is going to be anything but predictable.)

Joe: So, you’re saying that Nirvana was… musically influential, but culturally… a horrible example to be avoided?

Jeff: (Nods firmly) Utterly true.

Joe: That’s a pretty strong statement.

Jeff: It’s the truth. No offense to Dave Grohl, of course. He’s a… survivor. And, in some ways, we’re trying to… emulate him right now, you know? That process of… reinvention.

Jeff: Exactly. But… back to the point. When we started, we always felt… peerless.

Joe: Peerless?

Jeff: Yeah. But not in a good way. In a… negative way. We felt like there was no one… like us. No one who understood what we were trying to do.

Joe: So, you felt isolated?

Jeff: Yeah. Isolated. And… misunderstood. We were trying to create something… different. Something that was… more complex, more… nuanced. And no one seemed to… get it.

Joe: So, you didn’t feel like you were part of any particular scene?

Jeff: No. We were… outsiders. We were… on our own.
