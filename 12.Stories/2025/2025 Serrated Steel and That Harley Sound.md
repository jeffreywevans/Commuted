# **ROLLING STONE.COM**

*May 4, 2025*  
**“They Killed the Hall”: Commuted’s 16-Minute Magnum Opus Turns Induction into Apocalypse**

By *Rhett Collier, Senior Music Writer*

CLEVELAND — The induction crowd had already survived lifetimes of noise. They’d watched legends immolate their careers onstage, sat through sets that smelled like blood and kerosene, seen rock’s wildest years fade into something safe enough for VIP seating charts. If any room could claim immunity from shock, it was this one.

But immunity failed the second Avril Lavigne kicked the Harley into the aisle.

She roared into Public Auditorium like she was storming a barricade, black leather clinging to her frame, eyes promising trouble. She didn’t walk to the mic — she took it. And in place of a sanitized induction speech, she delivered a strafing run: Kathy Espich’s ghost name-checked like a war casualty, industry executives called out by nickname and sin, a filthy quip about Jeremy Evans’ tuning that landed like a slap. It was grinning contempt, and the room couldn’t look away.

Then [[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff]] and Bill struck the first chord. It was low, wet, and seismic — the kind of tone you feel in your pelvis before you hear it in your ears. Jeremy and Avril stepped forward and began passing solos back and forth, not so much playing as *pressing* into each other’s space. His runs came fast, serrated, slicing into the air. Hers bent and rolled, strings groaning into moans before snapping back, hips moving with the pull. They traded thirteen, fourteen times, each pass more explicit in its intent.

And then, without warning, the Harley returned — not onstage, but in the speakers. Bill had fed its voice through an arsenal of distortion until the idle was a growl, the shifts were guttural snaps, and the high revs became a banshee wail. The audience didn’t hear an engine. They heard a summoning.

That’s when the lyrics hit.

>*Static charged the air, unseen, / Hollow echoes where dreams had been…*

Avril spat them through clenched teeth, her body taut, her right hand stabbing the strings for punctuation.

>*The figure loomed—a wraith of night; / As bindings employed tied their blight.*

Jeremy’s guitar curled around her voice like a snake, each bend answering her syllables.

The story took the crowd somewhere they didn’t want to go — a purgatory motel, moonlight cutting the bed in half, the Devil close enough to smell his sulphury breath. Verses painted rope burns and shivers; prechoruses twisted the knife:

>*Each thrust an ache that twists and ties, / The glint of eyes that thirst for pain…*

The chorus wasn’t release. It was a sentence:

>*Death’s hymn resounded in her chest… / A twisted dance beneath the moon.*

By minute eight, she’d fought him off, the Harley’s pulse turning to open road. The song carried her east, the guitars shifting into something vast and wind-scoured. She hit the Jersey badlands under a pale sky, dunes and asphalt blurring, salt stinging her lips.

The crowd was rapt.

Verse five crashed in with sea-spray venom:
>*Down deep into that brackish sea— / No mirrors left for her to see— / Fucked under by a tide so cruel…*

The badlands gave way to boardwalk shadows; the salt in the air was no longer menace but promise. The final minute was Cape May at sunset — boots off, toes curling into sand, cold lemonade sweating in her grip while behind her the band burned every last note into harmony so sweet it ached.

And then — silence. The kind that makes you realize you’ve been holding your breath for sixteen minutes. Two label presidents stood and left without a word. Springsteen, who’d lived every mile of the roads she’d just sung, muttered, “Well, fuck,” and sat down hard.

When the applause finally broke, it wasn’t joy. It was survival.

Backstage, the words were already out: “They killed the Hall.” And after *Preoccupied and Engrossed*, with Jersey salt still in the room and the Devil’s shadow still clinging to the walls, no one could argue.
