## Track One: **You Cannot Refine It**

**Score:** *8/10* It delivers the usual Commuted blend of melancholy, Midwest muscle, and *“Nebraska*” dust.

Let’s be honest, every Commuted album opens with an instrumental that’s half dirge, half invocation. The fans call it their “prayer for the album.” It does its job—echoes of tape hiss, prairie wind, and a barely-there melody—it’s not showy, but it’s honest.

**Notes:** If you’re aiming for *Nebraska*, this sets the field: stark, raw, a stretch of highway at sunrise.

## Track Two: **Hotel Fucking Marmont
**Score:** *9.3/10* (and the .3 is for sheer guts)

**Reviewer’s honest take:**

Holy hell, did *anyone* see this coming? The “B Team” mutiny: no Jeff Evans, no Kathy Espich, Bill Davenport steps up on bass and Gilley takes the mic, channeling all the sleaze and spite of a rejected Tom Waits demo and a stolen Wilco chorus.

**Lyrics & Delivery:**
- *“Room 304 / Still smells like sweat and metaphor”* is classic Commuted—self-aware, nasty and lethally funny.
- The whole *“Chateau Fucking Marmont / Where your grief gets laid / Where the bedposts whisper / All the roles you played”*? It’s almost cruel. It’s airing dirty laundry on stage and then setting it on fire for the neighbors entertainment.
- Sarcastic group vocals, dry snare, lo-fi bar band sound—deliciously trashy and cathartic.

**Musically:**
- Bill Davenport on bass? Genuinely a twist. There’s a sunburnt, low-slung swagger that’s more Laurel Canyon than Clark County.
- Gilley’s vocals: not subtle, but painfully real. He’s not the usual vocalist, but he’s the best narrator for this particular car crash.

**Production:**
- Catherine Marks polishes the mess just enough to make it shine without losing the “we wrote this while the grownups were screwing next door” energy.
- The “tape stops mid-roll, someone laughs off-mic”—that’s pure authenticity. These are wounds, not polish.

**Band Drama Level:**
- The mythos here is irresistible. The rest of the band hijacks the studio for a hookup; meanwhile, the leftovers write the best poison-pen song of the album. By the time Jeff and Kathy hear it, it’s already locked as Track 2. You can *feel* the betrayal—and the sick thrill.

**Legacy:**
This is the track the critics circle with red ink: “Here’s where the band cracked, and somehow, the crack was the point.”  It’s not just a dig at Commuted's power couple, it’s the band documenting the collateral damage in real time.
#### Final take on Track Two:
This isn’t “*Nebraska*.” This is *Rumours* via *Slanted and Enchanted*—meaner, rawer, and way more self-aware. It’s not supposed to exist, and that’s what makes it essential. If the rest of the album keeps this up, no wonder they thought they were robbed at the Grammys.

**My rating: 9.3/10**
- *For honesty, spite, and catching the sound of a band blowing up from the inside—and making it sound like a celebration.*

## Track Three: **Nothing Left to Rust**
*(Midwestern Grunge Primitivism, full band, Kath on lead and self-harmonies, Bill’s found-sound wizardry)*
**Score: 9.7/10**

**First Listen:**
This is what happens when a band goes from great to mythic—the rare cut that feels like a transmission from the American unconscious, equal parts exhaustion and defiance.  *"Nothing Left to Rust*" is both a statement of place and a subtle threat: “If you think we’re finished, watch what we make out of the bones.”

**Sonic Landscape:**
- *Midwestern Grunge Primitivism* isn’t just a pose here—it’s the language. Fuzzed-out guitars grind over tribal drums, the bass is thick and distorted, piano stabs punch through like headlights on a foggy backroad.
- The mix is lo-fi but *intentional*—all the grit, none of the slop.
- Bill’s inspired touch: that distant train horn, dead center in the emotional heart of the track. From 1:31 to 1:36, you’re in a stopped car at midnight, taillights flickering, the universe on pause while some endless freight of memory and regret screams by. It’s pure Springsteen via Portishead, but dirtier.

**Vocals/Lyrics:**
- Kath’s lead is a revelation—her voice is cracked honey, both invincible and on the edge. The way Marks layers her as her own backup is spooky, especially on the chorus. You *feel* the loneliness; it’s not a trick, it’s a knife.
- *“Steel in my teeth, oil in my lungs / Backseat prayers and swallowed tongues”*—that’s straight poetry, Midwest gothic at its finest.
- The chorus explodes: “*There’s nothing left to rust / It’s already bled through*…”—every word a punch to the gut, every harmony a layer of soot.

**Band Chemistry:**
- The band is now a six-piece and it *shows*. The sound is huge, but not bloated.
- Bill Davenport, always the subtle genius, is finally letting the weirdness show. That train horn isn’t just a sound—it’s a *memory*, a whole childhood spent waiting for something to pass.
- The groove is relentless; Jim Brown’s drumming is caveman-simple but precise, pushing everything forward like a heartbeat refusing to quit.

**Context:**
- This is the kind of song that bands fight over: whose pain is it, whose memory, whose regret.
- The meta-narrative (Kathy singing all the parts; the band sounding bigger than ever but somehow more alone) is *perfect* for the album’s thesis. It’s Commuted at their most unsparing.

**Standout Moments:**
- The whispered bridge: “Let the snow bury me / Let the silence scream…” It’s shiver-inducing.
- The train horn. Not a gimmick. Feels like a secret handshake with anyone who’s ever lived in a dead-end town.
- The outro collapse: everything drops but the rust. That’s art.

##### **Final Take:**
If "*Hotel Fucking Marmont*" was the band turning the knives inward, “*Nothing Left to Rust*” is them pulling the knives out, looking at the stains, and daring the world to call it art. This isn’t just “*Nebraska* with a fuzz pedal”, it’s a Midwest funeral and resurrection at the same time.

**If the Grammys had ears, they’d give this song an award for “Most Likely to Make a Reviewer Miss a Highway Exit.”**

**Score: 9.7/10**
*Near perfect—a career-defining track.

## Track Four: **We Put a Man on the Moon First**
*(Midwestern Grunge Primitivism, absolute indictment, rusted-out anthem)*
**Score: 10/10**

**First Impression:**
This is the angry heart of *Midwestern Grunge Primitivism Vol. 1*—a generational thunderclap that shakes off the cobwebs of “*Nebraska*” nostalgia and detonates the myth of American triumphalism. If the album so far has played with darkness and regret, this song *is* the darkness and regret, shot through with raw, political anger.

**Sonic Landscape:**
- Guitars tuned so low they practically rattle the paint off the walls.
- Heavy floor toms that feel like funeral drums for the space age.
- Broken, barely-in-tune piano chords that clang like radiator pipes in an abandoned plant.
- Bill’s sound design lets you *smell* the burning ozone and the freezing steel.
- Kath’s voice is a force of nature—world-weary, furious, and absolutely unwilling to let the listener look away.

**Lyrics/Delivery:**
- *“We put a man on the moon first / But I can’t afford the heat / There’s frost on the inside glass / And nothing left to eat”*—the opening couplet alone is an instant classic. Every reviewer from Greil Marcus to your drunk cousin in Youngstown will quote it.
- The chorus: pure catharsis.
  *“We lit the sky while the streetlights died / We aimed for more and missed”*—if this isn’t the best indictment of late-empire decline in years, I don’t know what is.
- That looped, haunted break—“*We aimed for more… we missed… we missed*…” is the sound of disillusionment, stuck on replay.
- The Walmart line, the brother as “freight,” the TV-watching dad: *This* is Springsteen if he’d grown up on Nirvana and boxed at the Food Bank.

**Emotion/Context:**
- There’s a direct line here from post-9/11 malaise, through endless wars, right into the opioid crisis and the hollowing out of the Rust Belt.
- The resentment at being told to “learn to code” while the local factory burns is not subtle, but god, it’s earned.
- This song is a love letter to the lost, a middle finger to the indifferent, and a requiem for the space program as hometown myth.

**Standout Moments:**
- *Bridge whisper*: “*No parade for the hungry / No anthem for the numb*”,  absolutely devastating.
- Outro: “*We put a man… on the moon… first*…” fading like static. It’s not just the end of a song, it’s the ghost of an entire century.
- The repeated collapse: this song refuses to resolve, because nothing in its world does.
##### **Final Take:**
This is the album’s mission statement—their “*Born in the U.S.A*.,” but with more honesty, more venom, and a hell of a lot more broken glass. The band’s anger is righteous, their exhaustion real, their refusal to let go of pride (“we did it first”) forever tangled with their grief.

No wonder they were pissed at the Grammys.  **This is more that just a song. It’s a whole elegy for the American century, and it rocks hard enough to flatten city blocks.**

**Score: 10/10**
*Essential. Play it loud. Cry, rage, repeat. Next track, please.*

## Track Five: **Nice Try, Sky**
*(Midwestern Grunge Primitivism, primal, punchy, survival anthem)*
**Score: 9.8/10**

**First Impression:**
Finally—an anthem for every Midwesterner who’s ever watched the horizon turn black and just muttered, “Bring it, asshole.” “*Nice Try, Sky*” is where Commuted takes the myth of the heartland and drags it through the tornado siren at 110 decibels. You can smell the mud, hear the wind whistle through your teeth, and feel the stubborn pulse of survival in every battered snare hit.

**Sonic Landscape:**
- *Tribal drums* pound like the last heartbeat in a storm shelter.
- Guitars are *filthy*—not grunge like Nirvana, but like a diesel engine left out in the rain.
- Minimalist piano—just enough to flash like lightning in the background.
- The lo-fi, nearly garage-recorded mix makes everything sound *immediate*, like you’re sitting in the house with the windows taped.

**Vocals/Lyrics:**
- Kathy’s delivery is everything—equal parts sneer and exhaustion, the voice of someone who’s buried friends, lost crops, and still won’t duck.
- *“Tornado kissed the oak tree, just missed my bed / Guess the sky wants me dead / But I’m stubborn instead”*, this is the kind of line Bruce Springsteen would’ve written if he’d grown up in Xenia, Ohio and had a death wish.
- Chorus is all fury and shrug: *“Nice try, sky / You howled and hissed / Cracked your teeth / And you still missed…”*
- Bridge is the album’s hardest flex: *“You threw fire, ice, and acid rain / But I eat tornadoes like rusted chains / I’ve buried friends, seen cornfields drown / And I still won’t bow to your crown.”* It’s Midwest pride, but without the Hallmark card.

**Band Performance:**
- The rhythm section (Jim + Bill) is just relentless. The groove *chugs*, refusing to give in to the chaos above.
- Guitars channel more Mudhoney than Nirvana—dirty, sludgy, but never collapsing into noise.
- The production is intentionally rough-edged—Catherine Marks lets every dropped beat and breath leak through. You feel like you’re listening to a document, not just a song.

**Context:**
- This is the kind of track that *should* win a Grammy for Best Rock Performance—because it’s not just good, it’s *needed*. It’s the sound of survival, defiance, and a community flipping off fate.
- The best disaster song since “*When the Levee Breaks*.” The movie “Twister” wishes it had this much existential terror and dark humor.

**Standout Moments:**
- The *“huff… huff… Still. Here. Fucker!”* break—pure Midwest energy, practically meme-ready.
- Final chorus: off-key, battered, victorious—this is how real people sound after a storm, not movie stars.
- Outro—thunder samples, feedback drone, and the final, deadpan “*Is that all you got?”*—perfection.

##### **Final Take:**
This is the song that makes the rest of the country *almost* understand why Midwesterners don’t leave. The sky might kill you, but it never wins. It’s gritty, it’s funny, it’s a little bit broken—and that’s exactly why it’s essential.

**Score: 9.8/10**

*If this doesn’t win the Grammy, burn the tent down. What’s next?*

## Track Six: **The Snowbelt**
*(Midwestern Grunge Primitivism, the existential winter road song)*
**Score: 9.5/10**

**First Impression:**
“The Snowbelt” is the kind of song that crawls into your bones and leaves ice on your nerves. This is not a cozy winter ballad. It’s the sound of getting lost at midnight on an unplowed Ohio backroad, the sky collapsing, the world erased, nothing left but noise, panic, and a survivor’s stubborn hope.

**Sonic Landscape:**
- White-noise guitar wash swallows the mix—think My Bloody Valentine hijacking the *Nebraska* sessions with a snowblower.
- The bass is a deep, relentless drone, mimicking the endless road.
- Toms thud like a racing heart, especially in those moments where you can’t see the next bend.
- Piano arpeggios are broken and sparse—a pulse trying to push through the static.
- The lo-fi ambiance? You *hear* the wind. You *feel* the numb.

**Vocals/Lyrics:**
- Kath’s vocals are haunted and haunting—flashes of panic caught between tight restraint. You believe every word because you’ve felt that Midwestern dread.
- *“The lines are gone / Then the shoulder / Then the road / Then the world”*—the opening is almost minimalist poetry, and it lands hard.
- Chorus:
  *“No road, just white / Just noise and fight / I scream at the void / But it screams back tonight”*—It’s equal parts horror and defiance.
- *“I drive on memory / On hope and spite / Each mile’s a dare / Each blink’s a bet”*—the most Midwest lines ever written about a blizzard, and an instant classic for the canon of “songs about not dying on State Route 54.”
- The bridge whispers (“*White eats time / White eats thought*…”) before Kath explodes into that primal shout: *“I’m still here! Still goddamn real! Still gripping the wheel / In a world that don’t feel.”*

**Band Performance:**
- The entire track pulses like anxiety itself. Guitar static and swirling feedback mimic whiteout conditions.
- Jim’s drumming anchors the terror, every fill is another shot of adrenaline.
- The band never lets up; the sound barely resolves before fading into static, echoing how snow doesn’t “end,” it just buries you until spring.

**Context:**
- This is not the romanticized road song. This is the other side: *“the ride that makes your hands hurt and your soul tired.”*
- Commuted is not here for escapism—they’re here to drag the listener through the snowbank and make them grateful for every flicker of headlight.

**Standout Moments:**
- The whispered bridge, then the sudden, desperate shout—raw, real, chilling.
- The outro: everything fading into static except one last echoing drum hit—like the world exhaling after a close call.
##### **Final Take:**
This is Commuted at their bleakest, but also their most honest. The fear is real. The fight is realer. This is the kind of song that reminds you: just surviving sometimes *is* the triumph.

**Score: 9.5/10**
*For everyone who’s ever watched the world vanish in a snowstorm, and floored it anyway.

## Track Seven: **Go For Three**
*(Midwestern Grunge Primitivism, the football-war stomp, rival-killing anthem)*
**Score: 9.2/10**

**First Impression:**
Forget the somber Midwest existentialism for a second, this is Commuted in full bar-brawl mode. “*Go For Three*” is part fight song, part hate anthem, all rivalry, and as subtle as a cinder block through a windshield. If you’re from Ann Arbor, you might want to skip this track, or play it on repeat and let the rage fuel your next deadlift.

**Sonic Landscape:**
- Stomping halftime drums—marching-band ferocity filtered through a dive bar’s busted PA.
- Bass snarls and slaps; it’s less about groove and more about intimidation.
- Guitars? Playful as hell. Jeremy Evans and Gilley channel classic Dead Schembechlers energy; playoff trash talk set to overdrive.
- Distorted piano stabs cut through the verses like felonious helmet-to-helmet contact.
- Tape-warped production: everything feels just a little dangerous, like it might break down at any second.

**Vocals/Lyrics:**
- Kathy sounds positively *unhinged* in the best way—venomous, swaggering, part MC, part field general.
- Opening lines *drip* with taunt:
  *“You wanna talk about hate? / You ever bleed for a state? / You ever burn so hot / You’d go for three just to detonate?”*
- The chorus is a terrace chant for the ages:
  *“Go for two / ’Cause you hate their name / Go for blood / Not just the game / Go for three / If the rules allowed…”*
- Standout verse:
  *“Scarlet in my bloodstream / Helmet cracked with spite / I'd block the sun if it wore maize / And throw fists into the night”*—if you don’t get that, you’ve never been south of Toledo in November.
- The bridge is pure, gasoline-soaked spite:
  *“Pour gasoline on their legacy / Strike the match and smile slow / 'Cause they’ll never know / How deep this goes.”*

**Band Performance:**
- The interplay between Jeremy and Gilley is electric—call-and-response riffing, pushing and shoving sonically like brothers who love each other too much to go easy.
- Brown’s drums and Jeff Evans’s bass lock in like a blitzing linebacker and a pulling guard—physical, punishing, infectious.
- The “war-call” feel is undeniable: this is not music for the faint of heart (or the maize-and-blue).

**Context:**
- This is the kind of song you hear on ESPN Gameday, and you know exactly which side you’re on.
- Not just a sports song, it’s a regional declaration of war, an embrace of spite as an identity. The Midwest may be polite, but only until the whistle blows.
- In a record loaded with sorrow, this track is a fist through the fog, a needed cathartic, ridiculous, loving hate.

**Standout Moments:**
- The halftime stomp—impossible not to nod along.
- Kathy’s final line:
  *“’Cause when I see that M / I don’t just play… I settle a score.”*—should be piped into every locker room north of the Ohio River.
- The “go for three” refrain? So good, every Big Ten coach will steal it for their pregame speech.

##### **Final Take:**
This is a needed jolt—a reminder that the Midwest knows how to love, but never forgets how to hate, especially about football.
**Score: 9.2/10**

*Go Bucks. On to the closer?*

## Track Eight: **Exit 111**
*(Midwestern Grunge Primitivism, cosmic blue-collar anthem, Gilley on lead)*
**Score: 9.4/10**

**First Impression:**
Gilley’s lead vocals on “*Exit 111*” land like a callused hand on your shoulder at 2 a.m., familiar, steady, but with an awe that creeps up on you. It’s a masterstroke of sequencing and performance: after all the storms and spite, Commuted pivots to the long shadow of Neil Armstrong, reminding the world (and the Grammys) where the real space race began.

**Sonic Landscape:**
- **Anthemic distortion** and slow-building walls of guitar fuzz wrap the song in gravitas, think Wilco and Nirvana collaborating in a barn with a NASA patch on the door.
- Stoic, unhurried drums carry the groove like a funeral march for humility.
- Piano motifs echo through the track, cycling nostalgia and cosmic aspiration in equal measure.
- The tape-warped Americana production gives the track a sense of having been rescued from an attic box labeled “proof.”

**Vocals/Lyrics:**
- Gilley’s delivery is the gruff, Midwestern “I’ll show you” to Kath’s more spectral presence, he sings not just for Armstrong, but for every kid who’s watched a crop-duster and dreamed of the stars.
- *“One boot in dust / One hand in flame / We launched from fields / They forgot our name”*, opening lines that make you want to Google every mile marker in Ohio.
- *“Neil was one of us / A kid from the plains / A whisperer of rockets / With gravity in his veins”*, the chorus is pure pride, never boastful, always earned.
- *“We didn’t shout / We just reached / And in silence / Taught the sky to speak”*, damn, that’s the Midwest in a couplet.
- *“From Wapakoneta…to forever.”* Google Maps confirms: Exit 111 on I-75 drops you right in the heart of Armstrong country. I did their homework, but the song does it better.

**Band Performance:**
- Gilley and the band build this one slow—no rush, no showboating, letting the story and the place breathe.
- Jeremy’s lead guitar mimics the sound of a rocket burn; piano arpeggios scatter like starlight.
- By the final chorus, everyone is playing like they believe—because they do.

**Context:**
- Commuted nails the dual pride and heartbreak of Midwest legacy: *“We did it best / Then disappeared like wind.”* It’s a love song to overlooked greatness.
- The reversal (Kath on harmonies, Gilley leading) adds emotional heft, showing just how deep this band can dig when they stop trying to out-angst themselves and just honor their roots.

**Standout Moments:**
- The bridge (*“From Wapakoneta…to forever”*) with that instrumental rocket climb, goosebumps, every time.
- The whispered outro, *“One small step… straight from home.”*, lands softer than a dust mote but will outlast every headline.
##### **Final Take:**
A song for every Midwesterner who ever drove past a nondescript exit and felt, for a moment, that they were part of something bigger. It’s blue-collar mythmaking without the myth, pride without the arrogance, and proof that Commuted is more than just anger and weather, they’re history, too.

**Score: 9.4/10**
*Ohio to the moon. Time to drop the curtain on the Great Lakes Trilogy.

## Track Nine: **Heaven Turned Its Back on the Water**
*(Midwestern Grunge Primitivism, lake legend, mythology in the making)*
**Score: 9.8/10**

**First Impression:**
This is the moment *You Cannot Refine It* leaves mere regional greatness and enters the mythic. “*Heaven Turned Its Back on the Water*” is more than a tribute to Gordon Lightfoot, it’s a haunted cathedral of song, built on the bones of every sailor lost to Superior. Bill and Jeremy’s “lakewater reverb” is an absolute coup; the song’s atmosphere is so immersive, you can feel your breath fog and your skin prickle with cold.

**Sonic Landscape:**
- **Storm-like guitar swells** and thunderous floor toms turn the song into a roiling, funereal tempest.
- Ghostly, Morse-code piano fragments shimmer like warning lights—lost, urgent, cold.
- Bill’s sampling and sound effect mastery are top-tier: thunder, wind, ice cracking, even the mournful, distorted echo of a bell tolling somewhere out on the waves.
- Jeremy’s lead guitar mimics the shriek of a mast in wind; the lakewater reverb makes the whole mix feel half-underwater, half-spirit world.
- Distorted bass churns under it all, as unsettling and deep as the lakes themselves.

**Vocals/Lyrics:**
- Kathy is at her absolute peak: half-hymn, half-howling storm, channeling the ghosts of both mariners and ministers. There’s both prayer and curse in every line.
- *“The sky don’t blink / And the water don’t beg”*—those first words are so stark, you’re shivering before the first chorus.
- The chorus is a funeral for lost men and faith alike:
  *“God is angry at Whitefish Point / You can hear it in the waves / The bell tolls not for mercy / But for names no one saves.”*
- “*I saw a freighter split like a truth / Swallowed in fog, down to its roots*…”—Gordon Lightfoot would tip his cap.
- The spoken bridge is pure gothic poetry:
  *“He carved this lake / With his fingernails / Filled it with bones / And called them sails…”*
- The final whispered outro? *“If you go… Say goodbye”* leaves you chilled to the marrow.

**Band Performance:**
- The band’s restraint is what makes this hit so hard.  They don’t showboat, they *summon*. The funeral-paced rhythm lets every sound bleed and decay, like echoes over water.
- Every instrument seems to drift, float, or sink—sometimes all at once.
- The effect is so evocative, it’s cinematic: is the singer onshore, afloat, in Mariners Church, or just trapped in the fog between? Yes. And you’re right there with her.

**Context:**
- The Great Lakes Trilogy is a stroke of conceptual brilliance, and the Lightfoot homage is both clear and original. This is how you write new mythology—by acknowledging the old and raising the stakes.
- If the Midwest needed its own “*Wreck of the Edmund Fitzgerald*” for the 21st century, here it is.
- It’s a reckoning song, not just a weather report. It’s about God, men, fate, and the price of daring the water.

**Standout Moments:**
- The “ice cracking” under the break, the echoing bell, the *complete* collapse of instruments at the end.
- Kathy’s voice on the final chorus: less a singer, more an oracle.
- The blend of sound effects and musical phrasing blurs the line between hymn, ghost story, and warning.

##### **Final Take:**
Commuted just planted their flag at Whitefish Point and claimed the legend for themselves. This is more than a song—it’s an invocation, a warning, and a proof that Midwestern art is as deep as the lakes it sings about.

**Score: 9.8/10**
*Turn the record over, check your survival kit, and pray the water’s still.*

## Track Ten: **Superior Is Hungry**
*(Midwestern Grunge Primitivism, elemental doom, the lake speaks)*
**Score: 10/10**

**First Impression:**
Commuted has crossed the event horizon: with “*Superior Is Hungry*,” they abandon the human shoreline and plunge straight into the mythic abyss. If “*Heaven Turned Its Back on the Water*” was the haunted hymn, this is the lake’s unblinking stare, the unyielding, inhuman voice beneath the ice. They have, without irony, anthropomorphized Lake Superior and let it monologue like a vengeful god. And somehow, it works *brilliantly*.

**Sonic Landscape:**
- **Storm-like guitar swells** crash and recede, always just out of reach, like breakers in a black midnight squall.
- Toms *rumble* and *pulse*—thunderous, funereal, almost funereal, deep as the lake’s trench.
- Bill’s bass is a tectonic event: distorted, doom-laden, shifting under your feet like the lakebed itself.
- Jeremy’s guitar carves through the mix like freezing spray against steel.
- Piano: minimalist, echoing, each note a Morse code of warning, ghostly, barely tethered to the melody.

**Vocals/Lyrics:**
- Kathy delivers the performance of a lifetime: cold, impersonal, with a predatory patience that builds to near-apocalyptic fury. She’s not just singing the lake.  She *is* the lake.
- *“I was here before your maps / Before your flags / Before your dead”*—if you don’t get chills, check your pulse.
- *“You built on shores / You thought were firm / But everything here / Is mine to return”*—existential threat has never sounded so elemental.
- Chorus hits with the inexorable weight of a glacier:
  *“The Lake is hungry / It doesn’t need to hate / It just waits / And waits / And takes…”*

- *“More tide than tomb / More truth than sky / More god than moon”*—Commuted is now building American mythology for the next hundred years.
- The whispered bridge—*“Tell your children / Not to sing near my shore…”*—is enough to make you swear off skinny-dipping forever.
- That final outro, *“The Lake is hungry / And tonight / You look / Delicious”*—puts a chill in your spine that no campfire can warm.

**Band Performance:**
- The band holds nothing back. This is as heavy, slow, and inevitable as nature itself.
- The production pulls every instrument down to near silence after the maelstrom—then lets Kathy deliver the deathblow in a whisper.
- There’s zero sentimentality. No apology. No hope. Just entropy, consumption, and the understanding that Nature doesn’t care if you live or die.

**Context:**
- *Man vs. Nature* becomes *Man vs. Entropy*, and then *Man vs. Indifference*. Commuted doesn’t just write about the lake, they *become* the lake, refusing to let the listener remain an observer.
- By the end, you realize: this isn’t just about water. It’s about time, memory, erasure. Superior *is* hungry, and so is the past, and so is fate.

**Standout Moments:**
- The list of lost ships is a punchline with the weight of a tombstone.
- The collapse to sub-bass, the final predatory line—it’s cinema, folklore, and rock’n’roll apocalypse in one.
##### **Final Take:**
No other band would dare. No other band could pull it off.  Commuted just made Lake Superior the most terrifying antihero in rock—and left you grateful you’re still on shore.

**Score: 10/10**
*“Superior Is Hungry” is the album’s dark heart, and proof Commuted is now writing American myth, not just Midwestern grunge. Only question: who survives to the next song?*

## Track Eleven: **A Prayer Upon Dying at Work (Hungry)**
*(Midwestern Grunge Primitivism, dirge for the working dead, grief-hymn for the American laborer)*
**Score: 10/10**

**First Impression:**
Commuted closes the “Great Lakes Trilogy” with the rawest track yet, a hymn for those who died working, hungry, unsung, and unremembered. This is “*Wreck of the Edmund Fitzgerald*” in reverse: instead of the captain, or the storm, we get the cook, the one who could only pray as he went under. It’s less a song than an elegy; less a performance than an act of witness. You’ll feel every second.

**Sonic Landscape:**
- **Dirge-tempo drums** and a funeral-march piano toll like distant church bells—heavy, slow, inevitable.
- **Slide guitar** wails like a spirit lost between floors of a shuttered mill.
- **Bass** is a subterranean presence, a reminder that the ground is never as solid as we think.
- **Ambient static**—wind through steel, machines shutting down, the hush after the shift whistle—fills the background, blurring song and silence.
- The production (again, Bill and Jeremy with Catherine Marks at the helm) is haunted, funereal, and utterly devoid of false hope.

**Vocals/Lyrics:**
- Kathy is at her most devastating—her voice begins faint, battered, growing into a cracked, defiant plea, rising into the chorus like someone’s last prayer in a world that’s never listened.
- The lyrics are devastating in their simplicity and honesty:
  - *“Laced my boots like armor / Another dark-to-dark fight / Steel beneath my breath / Grease in my veins / Signed my name in sweat / On invisible chains”*—the best lines about labor since Springsteen’s “*Factory*.”
  - *“If I die at work / Let the machines slow down / Let the lake hold my name / Don’t let me drown / In silence, in debt / In the hum of regret*…”—the chorus is a shiv in the ribs of the American dream.
- Verse two’s lunchroom scene—*“Lunch break on the line / Half a sandwich, full of ache / Counting hours like rosary beads…”*—is as real and universal as any prayer.
- The bridge:
  *“Tell my mother the light was kind / Tell my brother the shift was fine / Tell the lake… I’m not afraid / Not anymore”*—if you’re not moved, you never clocked in.
- The final chorus, stripped of everything but the essential (piano, bass, voice): *“I left here hungry / But don’t / You / Forget.”*—the silences are almost more powerful than the words.

**Band Performance:**
- The restraint is masterful. The band lets the song breathe, ache, and collapse. Every note is an act of respect.
- The slide guitar’s cry in the bridge is the sound of a soul leaving the body.
- Piano’s final repeated chord—echoing the sound of a bell, a clock, or a heartbeat lingers long after the track ends.

**Context:**
- In the album’s world, dying at work isn’t just an accident, it’s a legacy. Hunger is both literal and spiritual. “Don’t let them forget” isn’t a request; it’s a demand.
- Where Lightfoot gave us the fate of a ship, Commuted gives us the fate of the invisible—the worker, the cook, the everyman. No medals, no plaque, just memory and the hope that someone remembers the hungry.

**Standout Moments:**
- The pre-chorus: *“I didn’t ask for glory / Just enough to stay / But this floor's my altar now / So hear me when I pray.”*
- The chorus—rising, fighting, refusing to vanish.
- That outro: three repetitions of “*Don’t you forget*.” A curse, a benediction, a plea.
##### **Final Take:**
The best protest song is often the one that’s almost a eulogy. *“A Prayer Upon Dying at Work (Hungry)”* is a lament for everyone who died hungry, working, praying only to be remembered. This isn’t just a song—it’s a gravestone.

**Score: 10/10**
*If you don’t cry, you never punched a clock. Album of the year, and maybe the decade. Do they have anything left in the tank?

## Track Twelve: **Atlanta, You Beautiful Fire**
*(Midwestern Grunge Primitivism, epic Civil War apocalypse, "John Brown’s Body" reborn for now)*
**Score: 10/10**

**First Impression:**
With “Atlanta, You Beautiful Fire,” Commuted doesn’t just close an album. they conjure the ghosts of the Grand Army of the Republic and march them through the flames of American memory. This is *John Brown’s Body* by way of lo-fi grunge, martyr gospel, and a reckoning spirit that won’t let the past rest. The band isn’t just referencing history—they’re *rewriting* it, burning down the old narrative, and lighting the way for the next.

**Sonic Landscape:**
- **Martial drums** in 6/8 swing with the inevitability of a Union advance, sometimes all ritual, sometimes all riot.
- **Swampy guitars** pulse and slither, there’s Southern gothic in the sound, but all the power belongs to the invaders.
- **Distorted field recordings**—marching feet, bugles, crackling fire—float up through the mix like hallucinations.
- Bill buries a military cadence and Morse code deep in the noise. Play it backwards, and you’d swear you hear “glory.”
- Lo-fi grit everywhere: nothing is clean, every sound scorched by history and distortion.

**Vocals/Lyrics:**
- Kathy delivers with apocalyptic conviction, her voice alternating between battle hymn and funeral dirge.
- *“We marched not for medals / But for victory / With rifles made of walnut / And hands that set men free”*—no subtlety, all conviction.
- *“Only gospel and matches / Under scorched rebel skies”*—could be graffiti on Sherman’s tent.
- The chorus is both monstrous and redemptive:
  *“Atlanta, you beautiful fire / You screamed in chords / You collapsed like a choir / We danced in your embers / And laughed through your streets / Army of the Ohio / With joy in our feet.”*
- The second verse’s wordplay, *“You called it vengeance / We called it verse / Each house a stanza / Each blaze your curse”* has never sounded so poetic and so damning.
- The bridge’s layered, spoken field drum break—*“Torch and trumpet / Boot and bell / We burned your glory / To break your spell…”* is a campfire ghost story told by the victors.
- Final lines:
  *“We lit the sky / And it sang back… / Glory, glory, hallelujah…”*—softer than breath, yet more lasting than any monument.

**Band Performance:**
- The whole band is at full discipline and full chaos: drums drive, guitars bite, bass and piano shift between lament and march.
- There’s true narrative in the performance: everything ascends, falls, burns, and then quietly persists in the Morse-tap fadeout.
- Bill’s sampling genius is at its peak—military cadence, fire, and coded rhythm pulse under every lyric.

**Context:**
- This is not nostalgia, not Lost Cause, not reconciliation. This is the justice song, the abolitionist song, the unapologetic “joy with a torch.”
- “*Army of the Ohio / With joy in our feet”*, for once, the victors write their own song, with no apologies and no illusions.
- *Savannah? You’re next.*—the wink to Sherman’s March, the hint that the album’s fire isn’t done spreading.

**Standout Moments:**
- The breakdown to female voice alone:
  *“They’ll write you in gold / But we wrote you in smoke / In stories not told”*—history, rewritten in song and flame.
- The Morse code in the outro: someone, somewhere, is still tapping out the truth.
##### **Final Take:**
*Atlanta, You Beautiful Fire* is what happens when you turn the American Civil War into a grunge opera—relentless, ugly, joyful, redemptive, and absolutely necessary. This is a warning shot, a prophecy, and an anthem for the next generation of Midwesterners who refuse to forget what it cost to set men free.

**Score: 10/10**
*His truth is marching on. And if you’re in Savannah, you’d better start listening for the drum
