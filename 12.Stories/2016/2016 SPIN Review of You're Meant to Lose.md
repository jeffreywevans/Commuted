# **Album Review: *You're Meant to Lose* by Commuted (2016)**

This is a visceral, uncompromising dive into the darker corners of the human psyche—a grunge revival that doesn't just wear the genre's aesthetic but embodies its fundamental disillusionment. Commuted has crafted something raw and authentic here, an album that refuses comfort or easy resolution.

## **Overall Themes & Sound**

The production throughout embraces the grunge/post-grunge sonic palette: thick, distorted basslines, reverb-drenched guitars, and dynamic quiet-loud-quiet structures that recall the genre's '90s heyday. But this isn't mere nostalgia—there's a contemporary edge here, particularly in tracks that incorporate industrial and EDM elements, creating a sound that feels both timeless and urgent.

The album explores self-destruction, addiction, disconnection, and the quiet violence of modern existence. These aren't characters triumphing over adversity—they're people caught in cycles they recognize but can't escape.

## **Standout Tracks**

**"[[11.15.2 Coma Kiss|Coma Kiss]]"** opens the lyrical portion with hypnotic drowsiness that erupts into sardonic choruses. The celebration of pharmaceutical oblivion ("Peace in a bottle, truth in a dose") is darkly comic yet genuinely unsettling—less a cautionary tale than an honest portrait of escape as luxury.

**"[[11.15.3 Paper Doll|Paper Doll]]"** delivers one of the album's most scathing critiques, dissecting emotional fraud with surgical precision. The dynamic shifts mirror the tension between appearance and emptiness, building to that devastating final admission: "you don't have a heart."

**"[[11.15.4 Ghost Signal|Ghost Signal]]"** captures digital-age alienation perfectly, with layered guitar feedback and synth interference creating a sonic landscape of disconnection. The image of searching your own name just to confirm you exist is haunting and painfully contemporary.

**"[[11.15.9 Asbestos|Asbestos]]"** (presumably the album's title track concept, given its thematic weight) starts from pure nihilism but pivots dramatically. That bridge—"Maybe it's love, maybe it's touch / Maybe that's the only crutch"—offers the album's most vulnerable moment, suggesting that even in meaninglessness, human connection might matter.

## **The Darker Explorations**

Several tracks examine sexual self-destruction with unflinching honesty. **"[[11.15.5 Dumb Call (2 AM)|Dumb Call (2 AM)]]"** and **"[[11.15.6 This Door|This Door]]"** both chronicle compulsive returns to destructive situations, mixing dark comedy with genuine pain. The repetition in "This Door"'s outro ("I walked out worse than I walked in" eight times) hammers home the futility without flinching.

**"[[11.15.7 Steel and Smoke|Steel and Smoke]]"** and **"[[11.15.8 Detuned Television|Detuned Television]]"** add soulful, yearning vocals to examine the gap between who we want to be and who we actually are. There's something particularly gutting about "I told myself I'd walk away… / Yeah, I lied again today."

**"[[11.15.11 Wolves at the Gate|Wolves at the Gate]]"** brings aggressive defiance, industrial-punk energy confronting temptation and manipulation. Unlike some earlier tracks that wallow in failure, this one fights back—even if victory isn't guaranteed.

**"[[11.15.10 Fading in Neon|Fading in Neon]]"** provides melancholic counterpoint, a haunting meditation on aging and obsolescence with reverb-soaked guitars creating an atmosphere of beautiful decay.

## **Final Thoughts**

*You're Meant to Lose* doesn't offer redemption arcs or inspirational messages. Instead, it presents unflinching portraits of people struggling with addiction, disconnection, self-sabotage, and the weight of existence. The album's strength lies in its refusal to moralize—these aren't PSAs but honest emotional documentation.

The musicianship is consistently strong, with particular praise due for the dynamic range and the way the arrangements serve the emotional content. This feels like an album made by musicians who understand that grunge was never just about distortion pedals—it was about emotional authenticity, even (especially) when that authenticity is ugly.

Not an easy listen, but a rewarding one for those willing to sit with discomfort. **8.5/1
