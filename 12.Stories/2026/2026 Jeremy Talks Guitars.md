## **EXT. METLIFE STADIUM – LOAD-IN RAMP – LATE AFTERNOON – SEPTEMBER 2026**

Sunny, dry, the kind of New Jersey day that pretends it never invented humidity. Forklifts hum, gaff tape blooms across concrete, and a small battalion of guitar cases sits sunning like black alligators. Jeremy Evans is already here—bald head under a cap, three-day stubble, wiry runner’s frame in a faded Dayton Dragons tee—doing what he always does: touching every piece of wood he’ll trust with a crowd’s nervous system. He runs now; he looks like it. He moves like a guy who can jog five miles to the gig, change strings at the curb, and still make soundcheck with a joke in his pocket.

I flip open my notebook and he gives me that sideways smile that says, yes, he’s going to answer the question, but only after he answers the better one you didn’t think to ask.

**KENDALL**  
You travel with thirty-two. I’m only going to make you be sentimental about ten.

**JEREMY**  
Ten’s fine. Ten’s honest. Thirty-two is superstition. And backups for backups for the song that decides to bite us on any given night.

A tech ghosts past with a dolly. Another—Bubba, all forearms and vigilance—parks himself at the wing and watches the hallway like Valhalla’s bouncer. Some people protect gear. Bubba protects *gravity*.

**JEREMY** (CONT’D)  
Alright, archivist. Ten guitars. No lying.

He taps the first case with his knuckle like he’s knocking on a church door.

—
#### 1. **THE BUTTERSCOTCH WITNESS**  
    1978 Fender Telecaster Deluxe (original wide-range humbuckers)
   
What it looks like: Butterscotch gone honey-gold, fretboard worn into a smile at cowboy-chord country. Big chrome Filter’Tron-looking rings around the CuNiFe pickups, buckle rash like a map of places he’s made audiences lose their breath. Reversed control plate so the volume’s at his pinky, tone pot removed and plugged with a nickel someone soldered in as a dare.

How he uses it: Stabs, sermons, and anything that needs to land *wrong* before it lands right. “After Hours” openers where he wants attack without shriek. He swears the Deluxe does a certain mid growl that makes Kathy sound taller and Jim sound meaner.

How he got it: Pawn shop off I-75 when they were still earning gas money in pizza slices. He tried to haggle; the guy looked at his hands and gave him the friends-and-orphans price.

Why it matters: “It’s the guitar that taught me control. You can’t bully a Tele. It just tells the truth louder.”

Customs: Treble-bleed cap on the volume so roll-offs don’t go muddy, compensated brass saddles, GraphTech nut, and a kill switch where the tone was—because chaos needs an off button, sometimes.

—
#### 2. **WEATHER VANE**  
    1962 Fender Jazzmaster (Olympic White → Nicotine Cream)
   
What it looks like: Olympic white that’s gone the color of old paper. Anodized gold guard that photographs like royalty. Mastery bridge, Lollar pickups, and the rhythm circuit disabled—he hates switches that invite accidents.

How he uses it: D-standard when the set needs sway. He goes to the neck pickup and plays *around* the vocal, like a good wingman who knows when to be pretty and when to shut up.

How he got it: An engineer in Boston bought it new, babied it for thirty years, and gave it to his nephew who hated the buzz. Jeremy traded him a reissue plus a lesson on how to fix ground loops with a $4 wire and a prayer.

Why it matters: “I learned patience on this thing. You don’t open a song with a chainsaw—you open it like a hand sliding up a thigh. The Jazzmaster’s foreplay.” (He grins. Somewhere, Kathy rolls her eyes in agreement.)

Customs: Foam under the pickups to tame microphonics, a subtle high-cut in his board so the glass lives at 8–10k and doesn’t icepick anyone’s left ear.

—
#### 3. **SUNDAY SCHOOL DROP-OUT**  
    2002 Gibson Les Paul Special DC (TV Yellow, P-90s)

What it looks like: TV Yellow that looks like the devil dressed for Easter. Twin P-90s that hum like they’re remembering a crime. Buckle rash? Full Jackson Pollock.

How he uses it: “*Come Inside Me*” outros and any song that needs grind without bloat. He rides the middle position, nudges the volume back, and lets the band breathe while he saws air into ribbons. The crowd doesn’t notice the restraint. The band does.

How he got it: Drunk-sober moment after a bad week—walked into a shop, laid hands on it, and knew. “That one fixed some things I couldn’t fix.”

Why it matters: “P-90s are the voice you use when you want to apologize and threaten someone at the same time.”

Customs: Wrapover bridge swapped for an intonated one, stainless frets, and the heaviest straplocks God sells.

—
#### **4. OHIO FOG**  
    Collings I-35 LC (Tobacco burst, semi-hollow)

What it looks like: Church furniture that learned to sin. The f-holes are modest; the top is not.

How he uses it: “*Why Her and Not Me?*” and anything that wants sustain without syrup. He says semi-hollow is where regret gets reverb.

How he got it: Studio loaner in Cincinnati, then an invoice he paid before the second chorus finished printing.

Why it matters: “It’s the one I play when I need to remember we know what we’re doing.”

Customs: PAF-ish pickups under-wound for clarity, tone pot wired 50s-style, and a limiter in the board that kisses 12k so he can bloom without spilling treble into the part of the spectrum that eats Jim’s snare.

—
#### **5. BLACKBIRD KNIFE**  
    Gretsch 6128 Duo Jet (Black, Dynasonic route)

What it looks like: Black tuxedo with a switchblade in the lapel. Chambered, light, fast.

How he uses it: “*The Left Hand*” riffs that need piano-hammer fundamentals. The attack is percussive; Jim smiles when he brings this one out because it makes the kick drum feel taller.

How he got it: Won it in a righteous argument: a label guy said Gretsches were only for rockabilly cosplay; Jeremy said “Cool, then you won’t mind me taking this one to prove you wrong.” He did. They never asked for it back.

Why it matters: “It’s the sound of saying no politely.”

Customs: TV Jones in the bridge, a Bigsby with a soft spring, and the tone switch deleted—again, no accidental sabotage.

—
#### **6. FLOODGATE**  
    Reverend Descent Baritone (tuned to B)

What it looks like: Battleship gray, long as a promise, with string gauges that make techs wince.

How he uses it: The laboratory guitar. In 2026 it became a rule: play where Jeff *feels* it, not where the treble lies to him. Jeremy will grab Floodgate whenever they’re mapping space for Jeff’s hearing: fewer notes, lower register, more air. It’s rhythm you can *lean* on.

How he got it: Bill shoved it into his hands at 3 a.m. and said, “Try this and shut up.” Jeremy played two notes and understood the assignment.

Why it matters: “Love is adaptation. Bands too.”

Customs: High-cut at 9.5k on the board, noiseless power, and a mute switch under his thumb so he can kill the line when the room starts to eat itself.

—
#### **7. LITANY**  
    1968 Rickenbacker 360/12 (Fireglo → Sunset)

What it looks like: Fireglo faded to late-summer peach. Twelve tuners like a crown of thorns. The kind of guitar that makes producers say the word *shimmer* even when they promised never to say *shimmer* again.

How he uses it: Sparingly, like salt. Intro textures, doubles an octave down, and always under a low-pass so the chime lives above the vocal but below the frequencies that vanish in Jeff’s left ear when the stereo field gets dense. “It’s pretty, not cruel.”

How he got it: Trade plus cash plus a handshake with a studio owner who didn’t realize it would sound like Commuted and not like college radio.

Why it matters: “Because sometimes you need light so the bruise shows.”

Customs: Half-round strings, nut cut for sanity, and the bridge pickup lowered until it stops auditioning for attention.

—
#### **8. MUNICIPAL**  
    Parts-caster Esquire (One pickup, all nerve)

What it looks like: Primer-gray body, single blackguard, one angry bridge pickup, no neck pickup, no tone, just volume and a red kill button. “Municipal” stenciled on the case in white like a city truck.

How he uses it: “*After Hours*” when they want to start a fight with silence. It barks, it doesn’t sing. He anchors himself by the snare and lets the audience realize they’re already inside the song before the lights agree.

How he got it: Built with a tech in a hotel ballroom between soundcheck and set. They were tired of a hum. They built an argument instead.

Why it matters: “Every band needs a hard yes.”

Customs: Evertune bridge (touring demands mercy), stainless frets, dead-flat fretboard up high for bends that don’t die.

—

#### **9. THE ZIMA (don’t flinch)**  
    1950s Harmony H-44 Stratotone (Cocoa-brown)

What it looks like: Brown paint like diner coffee. One DeArmond-style pickup that sounds like a broken radio in love with you.

How he uses it: Writing days, hotel rooms, and those middle-of-the-set detours where he wants “AM radio under a pillow” instead of hi-fi. It makes Bill grin. It made Gilley behave.

How he got it: Dayton, after a summer of hauling kegs for his dad’s beer warehouse gigs and working the flight line chalets at the Air Show for extra cash. He bought it because it was honest and ugly and would force him to be too. (Mr. Evans once bartered with Zima for tickets. Jeremy never recovered from the shame; thus the nickname. He earned the guitar; he lets the guitar roast him back.)

Why it matters: “Because music is funnier when the punchline’s on me.”

Customs: New pots, cloth wire, and a jack that doesn’t consider electrocution a lifestyle.

—

#### **10. NORTHWEST ORDINANCE**  
    Martin 00-18 (late 1940s, mahogany & wear)

What it looks like: The color of toast and morning apologies. Small-bodied, scarred, the pickguard eaten alive by years of arguments that ended in songs.

How he uses it: Pre-show sanity and post-show decompression. Whisper-writing in hotel rooms. He says he writes best when his right hand can hear the wood breathe and the left hand has nowhere to hide.

How he got it: A luthier in Cincinnati pulled it from the back for him after he played every new guitar on the wall and looked more and more miserable. “Try something that already knows what it is,” the guy said. He did.

Why it matters: “Jeff once told me the Northwest Ordinance explains Ohio and maybe us. This guitar is rule of law for my brain.” (Jeff would approve the metaphor, then hate that he approved it.)

Customs: Bone nut, light strings (he’ll go .011s on stage, .012s here), and a strap button installed against the gods of gravity.

—

We sit on two road cases. He absently wipes a tuner post with his thumb, then glances up at the bowl of the stadium, at the blue sky over the roofline. The ghosts in this building are his and not his—Giants Stadium down the road is where they learned pyrotechnics could be a sacrament and a municipal violation at the same time. He still talks about that weekend like it was a beloved crime.

**KENDALL**  
You endorse anybody?

JEREMY  
Not guitars. I’ll thank people by name; I won’t sell my hands. Strings? I’ve played the same set since Clinton had a job, but no one’s paying me to say that. If a Midwestern builder wants to do a quiet charity run, we’ll do it and not press-release it. But I’m not signing away a headstock. I pay for my tools so I can criticize them.

**KENDALL**  
Pedals?

**JEREMY**  
Pedals are snacks. Guitars are meals.

He gestures toward the stack. We both laugh. Then he gets exactly, almost comically, serious.

**JEREMY** (CONT’D)  
The real endorsement is arrangement. We learned a lot in the last couple years about ears going dark when the mix gets dense. That’s not *his* problem; that’s *our* job. So: fewer notes, lower tunings when we need to, and no pride about carving out the 13–14k where the snare disappears. If the songs are a city, the guitars are urban planning. No dead ends. No blind alleys where the drummer vanishes.

**KENDALL**  
Which one saves the show when everything breaks?

**JEREMY** (no hesitation)  
Municipal. If the lights die and the board catches fire, it still makes the shape of the song. A single bright line in a dark room.

**KENDALL**  
Which one breaks *you* if it goes missing?

He rubs his jaw, thinking. Then he shrugs with a grin that’s almost shy.

**JEREMY**  
The Martin. I pretend I’m a loud person for a living. That one lets me remember I’m not.

Bubba appears, offers a bottle of water like a verdict, and jerks his chin toward the stage: five-minute call. Jeremy stands, effortless, one hand already reaching for the Butterscotch Witness by muscle memory.

I follow him into the wing. The house is filling—the hum you feel in your ribs before you hear it in your ears. He pauses at the threshold, looks back at the line of cases like a dad counting heads on a field trip.

**KENDALL**  
Last thing: thirty-two on the truck, ten in your heart. What’s the rule they all share?

**JEREMY**  
They shut up when the song needs space. They talk when it needs teeth. And if I’m lucky, they forgive me when I play scared.

He grins, tucks the cable under the strap like he’s done since the garage days, and steps into the light. The stadium answers with a sound that feels like weather.
