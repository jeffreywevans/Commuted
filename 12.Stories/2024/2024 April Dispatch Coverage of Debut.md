**COLUMBUS DISPATCH**  
*April 14, 2024*  
# **Halftime at The Shoe: Commuted’s Comeback Stumbles Out of the Gate**

**By Alison C. Radcliffe, Pop Music Correspondent**

The annual Scarlet and Gray Spring Game is many things—preview of a team, rite of spring, excuse to tailgate in a still-chilly April sun. But this year’s halftime show was billed as something else entirely: the much-hyped return of Clark County’s prodigal sons, Commuted, with their new and controversial lead singer, Canadian pop-punk icon Avril Lavigne.

For weeks, Columbus has buzzed with rumors, memes, and nervous anticipation. Could Lavigne, best known for chart-toppers like “*Complicated*” and “*Sk8er Boi*”, fill the legendary (and much-mourned) shoes of the late Kathy Espich? Could a band synonymous with rustbelt grit and “Midwestern gospel” survive its boldest reinvention yet?

On Saturday, in front of an announced crowd of 102,478, the answer was…complicated.

After an on-field tribute to Espich, the stadium lights swept toward the temporary stage at midfield. Commuted’s core—[[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff Evans]], Jeremy Evans, Bill Davenport, and Jim Brown—took their places with the swagger of hometown heroes. Jeff Evans, shockingly as showman, led the crowd through a thundering “O-H/I-O” chant that shook the bleachers and probably a few dorm-room windows. For a moment, all seemed well.

Then Lavigne, dressed in black with a scarlet scarf, strode into the lights to a rumble of cheers and side-eyes.

And then—well.

The first notes of “*The Left Hand*” rang out, a song synonymous with Commuted’s rise in the 1990s. But where fans might have expected a torch-passing, they were greeted by something closer to a power outage. Lavigne’s vocals stumbled on the opening verse, pitchy and off-tempo, her movements stiff and visibly nervous. By the chorus, she appeared to lose her place, mouthing apologies into the mic as the band tried to hold the line behind her.

The second song, “*Speeding*,” fared little better—tempo lurching, harmonies absent, a sense of four veteran musicians gritting their teeth and willing the moment to pass.

By the end of the brief set, applause was—at best—politely Midwestern, tinged with unease. Social media lit up almost instantly: #NeverKathy trended alongside #Commuted3point0, with fans divided between those pleading for patience and others calling for an immediate “do-over.”

To their credit, Evans and company never broke character onstage. But offstage, word spread of slammed doors and an emotional Lavigne seen leaving the field alone, bundled tight against a wind as cold as the crowd’s reaction.

It’s too early to declare Commuted’s third act over before it begins. But Saturday’s halftime show was not the homecoming anyone wanted—not for the city, not for the band, and certainly not for a singer who looked for all the world like she wanted nothing more than to disappear into the concrete below Ohio Stadium.

Maybe, as one veteran fan put it online, “legends need more than one dress rehearsal.” Maybe Commuted will roar back with fire and fury on their own turf, in a club, a theater, an arena where the ghosts feel closer and the crowd is ready to believe. Or maybe, like so many spring games before it, this was just a preview of rough times ahead.

For now, all we know is what we saw:  A band looking for magic, a singer searching for her voice, and a city still waiting for the next true thing.
