# **“The Debacle at The Shoe”

**EXT. OHIO STADIUM – MID-AFTERNOON, LATE APRIL**

It’s late April, but the sky is winter-bright, the kind that makes every pore ache. The crowd is 102,000 strong—students in red, old men in plaid, middle-aged women in Commuted shirts and sunglasses, every last one itching to see if the stories were true. The place is a blood-red coliseum. Jeff is grinning—*god* is he grinning—his city, his people, every muscle loose, every inch of him riding the psychic jetstream of being a local who made it out and came back bigger.

The band strides out—Jeff, Bill, Jeremy, Jim—each one a survivor, jaws set, muscles tight. And then Avril, smaller than anyone expects, swallowed by the sound. A spotlight follows her like an accusation.

**BILL (backstage, thumping Jeff on the chest)**  
You ready, legend?

**JEFF (winking, eyeing the field through the tunnel)**  
I was born in that end zone. I’ll die at this mic.

Avril is behind them, chewing her thumbnail bloody, barely holding her shit together. Sweat stains her armpits before she even steps into the light.

**AVRIL (muttering, eyes wide)**  
It’s so loud. Jesus, they’re *everywhere*.

**JEFF (not looking at her, voice casual, almost dismissive)**  
Don’t trip over the cord, Canadian.

They march out.  Jeff leads.  Bill and Jeremy behind, Jim twirling a drumstick in his teeth.

**The PA BLARES:**  
Please welcome—your Commuted!

---

**ONSTAGE:**

Jeff shouts the call—“O-H!”—and the 100,000-strong horde answers, “I-O!” He drinks it in. The stadium *loves* him.

But when Avril hits her cue, it’s like all the air vanishes.

**EXT. OHIO STADIUM – HALFTIME – DAY**

*First chord—sharp, perfect. The band is locked. Avril takes her place at center stage, blinding lights in her face, looking out at a sea of judgment.

She lifts the mic.

**AVRIL (into the void):**  
"I… I am—"

A shudder.  Her brain misfires, just once.  But then—

**AVRIL (caught in a spiral):**  
"I am am am am am am am am am am—"

It’s like a fucking skipping record, a panic loop, her own mouth betraying her.  The band keeps playing, eyes wide, desperately hoping she’ll find the beat, the breath, the *anything*.

The crowd doesn’t know what to do—some giggle, some wince, a ripple of secondhand shame. Bill, behind the kit, mouths “Go!” Jeremy’s fingers are white on the frets. Jeff’s jaw is clenched so hard he tastes blood.

Avril’s eyes go glassy.  She tries again.

**AVRIL (smaller):**  
"I… am…"

Nothing.  Her voice squeaks, not a note in it.

A slow, horrifying realization moves across 102,000 faces:  She’s choking. On stage. In front of everyone.  A high school student near the fifty-yard line drops his phone. A woman in Row 24 says, “Oh, honey…” out loud, pity and disgust entwined.

Bill tries to bail her out, yelling backup— But the song’s already off the rails.

Avril, mortified, turns from the crowd, covers her mouth, and the band powers through—Jeff gritting out a verse, Jeremy and Jim playing as if the crowd could be willed into mercy.

The rest of the set is a blur—Avril never quite catches up, the crowd stuck between wanting blood and wanting to forget.

---

**INT. BACKSTAGE TUNNEL – SECONDS AFTER THE SET**

The concrete vibrates with the aftershocks of the disaster on stage. The crowd’s noise is still filtering through—some jeering, some stunned silence, mostly the kind of low murmur that means everybody’s texting about your fuck-up.

Bill rips off his headset, hurls it at the wall, and stalks up on Jeff—voice sharp as a blade.

**BILL**:  (voice just shy of a shout)  
You happy now?  That’s what you wanted?  You bring in a pop star, you get a pop star meltdown.

Jeff tries to hold Bill’s gaze, but he looks away, jaw tight.

**BILL**: (pointing down the tunnel)  
I *told* you—she’s not Kathy, she’s not Midwest, she’s not built for this.  You just had to be the genius.  You just had to have the headlines.

Avril is somewhere down the hall, hunched and shivering, but Bill doesn’t care if she hears. He wants the wound to show.

**BILL**  
That was our name, our home.  We don’t get a second chance, Jeff.  You just burned the whole fucking house down for a “maybe.”

Jeremy tries to defuse, but Bill puts a hand up—don’t even start.

Jeff’s voice, when it comes, is hoarse with regret.

**JEFF**  
She deserves a shot.

**BILL**  
She *got* her shot.  Now we’re the punchline.

He storms off, knuckles white, teeth clenched, the sound of drums still ringing in his ears—a rhythm of rage and disappointment.

---

**INT. BACKSTAGE – IMMEDIATELY AFTER**

The band stumbles off, sweat and shock mixing in the cinderblock underbelly of the stadium. Avril is white as chalk, lips quivering, arms wrapped around herself.

**BILL**  
(just says it)  
Jesus, kid. What the fuck happened?

**AVRIL**  
(voice barely there)  
I… I couldn’t—I thought I had it, I just—I kept thinking about Kathy and— I couldn’t breathe.

**JIM**  
Let’s just… let’s get her some air.

Nobody hugs. Nobody soothes. They’re too raw, too angry, too scared it’s all about to be over. Jeff stands back, breathing hard, looking at his hands like they belong to someone else.  He stares at Avril, then—almost in a trance—pulls out his phone and types “*Brian May panic*” into Google.

Avril collapses onto a road case, scrolling her own phone, watching the #IAM meme explode in real time. A new era, born in blood and public failure. And upstairs, in the glassed-in press box, every sportswriter in the state scribbles the phrase “train wreck” and “not ready for prime time.”  A thousand memes, a thousand takes. 

---

**BACKSTAGE:**

Avril heads straight to the nearest bathroom, locks the door.  Jeff kicks a trash can.

**BILL (muted, sympathetic)**  
Tough room, man.

**JEFF (bitter, voice tight as wire)**  
It’s not the room.

---

**INT. BATHROOM – AVRIL**

She’s hunched over the sink, sobbing. Mascara bleeding. The sound of 102,000 people not loving her still ringing in her ears. Phone buzzing: Her mom. Her label.  She doesn’t answer.

---


**INT. GREEN ROOM – POST-SHOW**

Catering untouched. Drum kit in the corner, still sweating with him. Avril’s on the couch, knees up, arms wrapped around herself. Jim walks in, not even looking at Jeff or Bill, grabs a bottle of water, takes a long, slow pull, and just stands there staring at Avril. You can feel the tension crackling.

He tosses the bottle onto the coffee table, and sits across from her, elbows on knees.

**JIM**: (voice low, calm, dangerous)  
What happened out there?

Avril doesn’t look up. She’s shaking.

**AVRIL**: (flat, exhausted)  
I blew it.

**JIM**  
No shit.  

(silence, then softer)  
You want to tell me why?

Avril shrugs, but Jim won’t accept that. He’s relentless—just drums and discipline.

**JIM**  
You freeze up, fine. Nerves, crowd, whatever.  But that—  

(points at her, sharp)  
—that was more than nerves.  What the fuck is going on?

Avril blinks, and for a second, she looks like she might cry. Jim doesn’t flinch. He hates this part, but he does it anyway, because that’s the job now—big brother, band dad, crisis negotiator.

**JIM**: (softening, just a little)  
You’re not the first one to tank a show.  You won’t be the last.  But if there’s something wrong, you tell me.  I don’t let my bandmates drown on my watch.

Avril starts to mumble something about the noise, the lights, the way the crowd *looked* at her. The Ohio wind in her throat, the memory of every X death threat and “she’s no Kathy” meme rattling in her head. The echoes of failure, like static.

Jim listens, and for once, he’s not judging—he’s clocking every word, every stammer. He’s pissed, but it’s the pissed of someone who refuses to let the whole thing go down without a fight.

**JIM**  
Look.  

(leans in, deadly quiet)  
You don’t have to be Kathy.  You don’t even have to be *good* tonight.  But you have to be honest with us, and you have to get your shit together by the next set.  If you need to scream, scream. If you need to puke, puke.  But if you freeze again, we’re dead.  Got it?

Avril just nods, eyes wet.

**JIM**  
Good.  Now drink some water and get your head right.  Because I’m not babysitting you through another trainwreck.

He stands, looks to Jeff and Bill—daring them to argue—and walks out.  The door swings shut, the silence heavier than before.  But at least now, she knows somebody in this band gives a damn if she lives through the night.

---

**INT. BUS – NIGHT**

Jeff sits with a beer, watching the replays on his phone—can’t stop. Can’t not look. He texts nobody.  The city outside is alive with post-game parties, but for Commuted? The whole bus is a coffin.

Avril stays silent.  Nobody brings up the afterparty.

---

The comeback fizzles.  The band’s chat is dry, full of one-word answers.

And the internet?  Already calling it:  
*“Commuted 3.0—DOA.”*

**ARCHIVIST’S NOTE:**  

I was in the press box. Not the main one—Commuted doesn’t get main anything anymore, not since Kathy died. I was in the auxiliary bunker, somewhere between the condiment station and a line of boosters, wedged next to a kid who was running TikTok for Ohio State and another dude in a “Free The Block O” hoodie who looked like he’d killed and eaten a Michigan fan for breakfast. My phone was set to vibrate but it was actually hot, like it might combust. That’s how many notifications were coming in.

The first ten tweets? I didn’t see them. My vision blurred, the crowd was making that hive-mind noise, and I was busy writing "Avril, don’t choke, please for the love of god, don’t choke" over and over on my notepad like a prayer.

But then X lit up. A tweet a second—maybe more. All the hashtags started trending before she’d even hit the second chorus. #CommutedLive #ScarletGrayMeltdown #AvrilAin’tKathy #TrainwreckAtTheShoe #NeverSurrender #AvrilOut #WhyGodWhy #OSUFansDeserveBetter

My phone’s screen looked like a machine-gun ticker-tape of disaster. First came the snark:

> “Commuted should’ve gone for three—for a new singer, a new band, a new idea.”  
> “Avril, blink twice if you need extraction.”  
> “Is it performance art or a hostage video?”

Then came the anger:

> “You NEVER replace Kathy. Not in Ohio. Not on our field.”  
> “If Woody Hayes were alive he’d sack the whole band.”

Then, the kill shot: LeBron James.  
*LEBRON JAMES.*  
His tweet landed like a grenade in a daycare:

> “Damn. That was rough. But respect for stepping up. Not everyone can take that stage. #OhioForever”

I swear my jaw hit the floor, bounced, and tried to tunnel into the earth.  Do you understand?  LeBron is *statehood*. LeBron is the Pope of the Buckeye Diaspora. LeBron’s tweets are worth more than local elections. When he weighs in, it’s like God interrupting church with a Yelp review.

I reloaded the app—because I had to know, I had to see how bad it was—and the replies were just carnage:  Memes, gifs, goat emojis, “Can we trade Avril for Anthony Precourt?” “This is why you don’t let Canadians front Ohio bands,” “Jim looks like he wants to commit violence.”

And the retweets—tens of thousands in ten minutes. It was a digital riot.

People think “meltdown” means a slow decline. No, honey. This was *atomic*. Ten seconds and the world had an opinion, a meme, a joke, and a reason to dunk on Commuted.  
My jaw? Dislocated. Spirit? On life support.  All I could do was sit there, thumb hovering, and think:  Well. This is what history looks like on fire.

And you know what?  Even in the middle of the worst blowout in the band’s life,  
it was beautiful.  Fandom alive, band bleeding, world watching.  That’s what I fucking live for.
