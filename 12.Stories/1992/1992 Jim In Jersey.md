**“Whose Lady?”**  
*A Short Story by Jim Brown (allegedly)*

---

I was nineteen, broke, and smelled like burnt hash browns and cymbal polish.

We’d played a club in Asbury Park the night before — some basement place with a warped stage and a ceiling that seemed aggressively allergic to bass frequencies. Kathy had cracked a floor tile. Gilley broke a mic stand. I dislocated something in my back trying to mic the kick drum with duct tape and divine intervention.

So naturally, I decided to spend my day off being a tourist.

I woke up on someone’s couch, found the least sticky pair of jeans I owned, and made my way to the ferry dock at Liberty State Park. I’d seen it in postcards — the big green chick with the torch. Statue of Liberty. Toga. Crown. Supposed to stand for something. Freedom. Big dreams. Indie record contracts.

I figured: hell, I’m already in **New Jersey**, and she’s right out there in the water — so she must be **ours**, right?

I was young.

I was wrong.

The ferry was this rust-splotched white-and-blue tub that smelled like barf and bleach. It vibrated like a headache with a propeller. I loved it immediately. I bought a coffee from a guy who looked like he’d just escaped a diner grease trap and took a seat by the railing.

The water was doing that shiny oil-slick shimmer thing, light bouncing off it like sequins on a prom dress. I watched gulls argue with each other midair, New York’s skyline punching out of the horizon like it wanted a fight. The boat listed. Children screamed for no reason. It was perfect.

And then I saw her.

The lady herself.

Big. Green. **Solid.** Standing tall like she’d been planted by God with a crane. Torch high. Face firm. Tablet in hand. I squinted.

“Wait,” I said, to no one. “Is she… in New York?”

A guy in a Red Bulls hoodie next to me said, “Huh?”

“The statue,” I said. “She’s in New Jersey, right?”

He looked at me like I’d farted in church.

“Dude, that’s New York.”

I blinked. “That can’t be right. We took off from Jersey.”

He shrugged. “Doesn’t matter. It’s federal land. But jurisdictionally? It’s New York.”

**Jurisdictionally.**  
Jesus Christ. You know it’s a bad day when ferry-goers start using legalese.

I stood up. Walked to the front. Tried to piece together the logic.

There she was: not fifty yards from the boat, sun glinting off the torch, foam crown like a punk rocker’s halo. We were getting closer. I was feeling patriotic. Furious. **Confused.**

We docked. People spilled off the boat, chattering and selfie-ing and adjusting their fanny packs. I moved slower. My feet hit Liberty Island with a solid thunk. I looked around for a sign that said “Welcome to New Jersey.”

Nothing.

Instead: National Park Service. And underneath that, in aggressive serif font:  
**U.S. Department of the Interior — New York District.**

I felt betrayed.

Now, listen. I don’t bleed state lines or anything. I’m not the kind of guy who tattoos *“Exit 117”* on his forearm. But still. **This was injustice.** We Jersey folks had the better view. We had the dock. The parking lot. The humble blue-collar origin story.

But she belonged to New York?

Because of some **“colonial water boundary line”** written in 1834?

*“The island’s landmass may be closer to New Jersey, but under the terms of a bi-state compact signed in 1834 and reaffirmed by the Supreme Court in 1998…”*

I read the sign like I was reading a ransom note.

What the hell was this? A custody battle? A bureaucratic theft?

I looked up at the statue. She stared straight ahead, unbothered. Proud. Defiant.

Typical New Yorker.

I walked the perimeter. People were laughing, snapping pictures, feeding pigeons with bad intentions. I sulked. Bought a foam crown in protest. Drank a second coffee. Finally sat on a bench facing New Jersey — the shoreline a smudge in the distance.

*We built our sound in Jersey garages.*  
*We got booed off Jersey stages.*  
*We played Jersey Shore bars with sticky floors and one working monitor.*  
*And now the most iconic symbol of freedom in the western hemisphere is technically… theirs?*

I pulled out a napkin. Started sketching setlist ideas. “Injustice Island (G minor).” “Torch Politics.” “Liberty: A Divorce.” The kind of song titles Gilley would roll his eyes at and Kathy would sneak into the bridge anyway.

Some kid next to me asked if I was famous. I said no. He asked what I did. I said drummer. He asked if that was like a roadie with sticks.

I considered hurling myself into the harbor.

Back on the ferry, I stood on the railing for the whole ride back. The boat rocked. The sky leaned pink. The statue faded behind us, torch still raised like she was judging me for being petty.

I didn’t care.

This was personal now.

As the boat hit the dock, the speakers crackled to life. An announcement about disembarking and trash disposal.

Then, for no reason at all, **Springsteen’s “Jungleland”** started playing through the tinny PA system.

I laughed so hard I nearly dropped my foam crown into the water.

I never told the band about that trip. Never told Gilley or Jeff or Kathy that I’d spent three hours getting emotionally worked up over **state lines and a statue**.

But to this day, every time we play a Jersey show and I see that skyline glinting across the bay, I think about that ferry. That legal footnote.

That big, bouncing harbor chick in a toga, pretending she’s not **absolutely from New Jersey**.
