# Commuted – *69 Reasons* (1992) – A Brash Debut That Lit the Bedroom on Fire

**Release:** April 1992 | **Label:** Island Records | **Sales:** 10+ million (Year One) | **Critical Reaction:** Polarized (from bafflement to adoration)

Commuted’s debut album *69 Reasons* arrived like a Molotov cocktail in the midst of rock’s 1991–92 revolution. At a time when Nirvana’s *Nevermind* and Pearl Jam’s *Ten* were redefining rock with angst and social commentary, Commuted crashed the party with something even more shocking: **raw, unapologetic sexuality** from a **female-led, Midwestern noise-rock band**. Produced in a blitz of 15 days by Ric Ocasek, *69 Reasons* was a **monster success** – over 10 million copies sold in its first year – yet it faced intense cultural controversy. Critics and listeners didn’t quite know what to make of it at first. *Rolling Stone* famously wrote that *“this album doesn’t play to an audience – it **accuses** one”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FBJ0uS51z3vp6wFlLAtQ4_D53ZvVRbB0). Some were outraged by the profanity and provocation, dismissing it as “pornographic” shock-rock[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF), while others hailed it as a revolutionary blast of honesty in a genre growing complacent. In retrospect, there’s no denying *69 Reasons* **changed the landscape**. As Nirvana and Soundgarden brought grunge to the mainstream and U2 and R.E.M. explored new sounds, Commuted grabbed rock by the throat – and a few other places – and made **desire** the arena of rebellion.

What follows is a deep, track-by-track exploration of *69 Reasons*. This album’s legend isn’t just in its commercial dominance, but in the way **Commuted turned lust into anthems** and **bedroom confessions into battlefield cries**. Each song will be examined for its lyrics, rhyme schemes, musical style, and the shockwaves it sent through 1992’s rock scene. Buckle up – in the words of producer Ric Ocasek’s warning scrawled on the band’s master tape: *“Have clean panties ready. These Dayton freaks will break you in half. Savage, profane, sincere.”*

## Track 1: **Untitled Opener** – *“A Garage Fire, Then an Alibi”*

Every great album needs an introduction, and Commuted chose to **open with an explosion**. The as-yet untitled opening track (often just called **“Track 1”** by fans) is a **cacophonous onslaught** that immediately separates the casual listeners from the brave. It begins with frontman **Gilley** screaming at the top of his lungs – *“something incoherent,”* as one Island Records exec described it[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FBJ0uS51z3vp6wFlLAtQ4_D53ZvVRbB0) – answered by **Kathy Espich**’s blood-curdling howl that *“sounds like a siren raised by wolves”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FBJ0uS51z3vp6wFlLAtQ4_D53ZvVRbB0). The band sounds absolutely feral here: **Jim Brown’s** drumming doesn’t just keep time; *“he harasses it”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FBJ0uS51z3vp6wFlLAtQ4_D53ZvVRbB0), pounding so hard that during recording they literally had to track the drums in a hallway because the snare hits kept knocking the studio lights loose[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FBJ0uS51z3vp6wFlLAtQ4_D53ZvVRbB0). **Jeremy**’s guitar and **Jeff**’s bass roar with feedback and gritty distortion, weaving in and out of sync in a way that at first feels like chaos – until you realize it’s *intentional* chaos.

Lyrically, the opener’s words are almost hard to discern beneath the noise, but Kathy and Gilley trade lines in a wild call-and-response. Notably, **their vocals don’t blend on purpose** – Kathy’s raw wail and Gilley’s throat-shredding shouts clash in stereo. *“The vocals don’t blend. That’s the point,”* notes Sarah Malcolm, an Island A&R rep who analyzed the record in-universe[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FBJ0uS51z3vp6wFlLAtQ4_D53ZvVRbB0). The **first chorus** hits like a brick through a window – Malcolm said it *“sounds like a garage fire”* – and by the second chorus the song somehow mutates, *“sounding like an alibi”* being frantically shouted[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FBJ0uS51z3vp6wFlLAtQ4_D53ZvVRbB0). In other words, the song itself feels like a crime of passion, then a breathless justification for that crime. **Rhyme scheme** is tough to pin down here amid the distortion; the lyrics employ a few blunt rhymes mainly for aggression. (One couplet that fans have parsed from the din reportedly rhymes *“fire”* with *“liar”*, fitting the destructive vibe, though the band has never published official lyrics.) What’s clear is the **tone**: defiant and abrasive.

Musically, think **grunge at its gnarliest** meets Midwestern hardcore punk. In late 1991 when this was recorded, bands like **Metallica** were polishing their sound for the masses (*The Black Album*), but Commuted went the other direction – **dirty, lo-fi edges, and live-wire energy**. It’s a statement of intent: if you can survive Track 1, you’re ready for the rest. As one critic wrote at the time, *“Commuted’s opener makes ‘Smells Like Teen Spirit’ sound like a lullaby.”* Indeed, where Nirvana’s biggest hit had a pop structure beneath the fuzz, Commuted’s first track offers no such shelter. It’s a barrage of sound that says *welcome to the freakshow*.

Despite (or because of) its harshness, the opener got people talking. College radio DJs bragged about playing it at top volume to blow out dorm speakers. Mainstream radio wouldn’t touch it, but the underground buzz was deafening. **In one daring move, Island Records’ strategy team actually used this track as a secret weapon**: internally, they said *“Play Track 3 loud. If no one asks about it in 48 hours, destroy this and forget we ever existed”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FBJ0uS51z3vp6wFlLAtQ4_D53ZvVRbB0) – implying that if the shock of this album didn’t provoke reaction, nothing would. Track 1 ensured that *69 Reasons* was *unignorable*. From its first seconds, Commuted lit the fuse.

## Track 2: **After Hours** – Illicit Passion Goes Platinum

[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)If the opener was all punk fury, **“After Hours”** flips the script with a seductive groove – yet it’s no less transgressive. Placed as the next track, *After Hours* immediately stands out for its **comparative polish and clarity**. It’s built on a sultry mid-tempo beat, reverb-drenched guitars, and **Kathy’s vocals front and center** without Gilley’s interference[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). In fact, Gilley doesn’t sing at all on this song – a deliberate choice that makes *After Hours* feel like we’ve cracked open Kathy’s diary. And what a diary it is: *After Hours* is an **unapologetic tale of infidelity and lust**, arguably the song that truly *“put Commuted on the map”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF) by becoming their first #1 single.

Right from Verse 1, Kathy paints a scene of secret desire with almost literary detail. *“Clock struck five, I punched out slow, / Folders stacked high, but my heart felt low”* she sings, grounding us in a mundane workday that’s about to turn into a reckless night[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Each line carries simple end-rhymes that give the narrative a storybook clarity – *“slow/low”* in the first couplet[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF) – lulling the listener before the moral transgression hits. By the chorus, the rhyme scheme tightens into a seductive chant: *“Oh, I knew it was wrong, but it felt so right”* is followed by *“A thief in the night, chasing sparks in the light”*, pairing **“right”** with **“light”** in a neat AABB scheme that makes the illicit affair sound almost romantic[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Then comes the kicker couplet, the line that had censors sputtering: *“I’d do it again, coz I’m very satisfied, / Caught in my web, we will **fuck** and hide.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Hearing the f-word delivered so calmly by a woman over an otherwise radio-friendly melody was jaw-dropping in 1992. That rhyme of **“satisfied”** with **“hide”** might seem simple, but it’s the blunt *“fuck”* that lands like a gunshot in the quiet night. Commuted wasn’t interested in euphemism – **they gave it to you raw**.

Critics at the time were split: some praised *After Hours* for its storytelling and boldness. *Spin* magazine marveled that “Kathy Espich sings about adultery the way Springsteen sang about escape – as something almost noble, definitely necessary.” Others, however, were scandalized. *Billboard* noted uneasily that *“radio hasn’t heard a female voice utter such things since… well, ever.”* Indeed, where male-fronted bands like the Red Hot Chili Peppers had songs about sexual escapades (e.g. “Suck My Kiss”), those came off as goofy or boastful. *After Hours* is different – it’s **sensual but unsentimental**, owning female desire without apology. The **chorus’s rhyme pairs** (“arms/charms” and “satisfied/hide”) are catchy, almost sweet – until you parse the meaning[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). This contrast between *sound* and *substance* is what made the song dangerous. It *sounds* like a love song; it *is* a lust song.

Musically, Commuted coined the phrase **“Grunge American Primitivism”** to describe their style, and here you can feel it: gritty guitars with a bluesy undertone, a steady rhythm that’s almost hypnotic[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT). The production is surprisingly clean – likely Ocasek’s pop instincts at work – which only makes the lyrical content pop out more sharply. The band holds back on their usual chaos, proving they can write a **hook** as well as anyone in the Seattle scene. In fact, “After Hours” was so melodically engaging that many radio stations initially gave it a shot, at least until complaints rolled in about that infamous lyric.

In terms of **rhyme and structure**, “After Hours” sticks to classic verse-chorus form. Verses use subtle rhymes (e.g. *“ghost/most”*, *“fire/higher”* in Verse 2[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)) to draw us into the narrative of sneaking around. The choruses, as noted, hit with direct rhymes and repeated phrases (*“felt so right”/“sparks in the light”*) that lodge in your head[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). This traditional structure made the song accessible – a Trojan horse for the subversive message. By the final bridge and outro, Kathy’s tone shifts just slightly: a touch of regret seeps in – *“Bittersweet regret lingers like smoke”* she admits in the bridge[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF) – but then she slams the door on second thoughts with the last line, *“You are god damned right, I’d do it again.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). It’s a mic drop of a ending, defiant and confident.

*After Hours* became a phenomenon. It gave *69 Reasons* its first platinum single and proved Commuted could conquer mainstream charts *on their own terms*. The song’s legacy is huge: Many have called it a precursor to the feminist frankness of later ‘90s artists. (Alanis Morissette’s “You Oughta Know” in 1995, for example, owes a debt to Kathy’s unapologetic tone – though Alanis’ rage was still more metaphorical than Commuted’s cut-to-the-bone lyrics.) In 1992, nothing sounded like *After Hours*. It was **seduction as revolution**, and it made America simultaneously swoon and squirm.

## Track 3: **Not Taken Kindly** – The “What the Hell Did She Just Say?” Moment

If *After Hours* lured listeners in, **“Not Taken Kindly”** blew the doors off. Slotted as track 3, this is the song that had everyone from teenagers to Tipper Gore clutching their pearls. *Not Taken Kindly* is an **expletive-laden, gender-dueling showdown** set to a grunge-punk assault. One reviewer in 1992 called it *“the filthiest back-and-forth since **Sex Pistols** met **Chrissie Hynde** in a bar fight,”* and that’s not far off. It’s also **brilliantly crafted debauchery** – a song that musicologist Sarah Malcolm identified as *“the first full-blown ‘what the hell did she just say?’ moment”* on the album[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT). Indeed, this track was **banned on many radio stations outright** and became legend partly because kids passed around dubbed tapes whispering, *“you won’t believe these lyrics.”*

And what lyrics they are. Verse 1 opens with Kathy practically purring a line that is decidedly NSFW: *“Licking up his shaft, oh, I’m right on track, / Need it in my mouth, no way I'm turning back.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Let’s pause here – that couplet not only sets the graphic sexual scene, it also **establishes a tight AA rhyme** (*“track/back”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)) that drives the point home. There’s a dark humor in how blunt it is. As the analysis in *Critical Consensus* notes, *“From line one, there’s zero hesitation… a blunt-force depiction of intimacy”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The **chorus** then turns the post-coital disappointment into a hook: *“Post-nut clarity, ‘you don’t like my head?’ / Flyboy so lordly, dumbass in my bed. / Moment of delight, after we finished, / Now it feels like my gift was diminished.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Here we see Commuted’s sly use of rhyme and meter. The first two lines rhyme **“head”** with **“bed”**, giving a sing-songy bite to Kathy’s vicious insult[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The next two lines rhyme **“finished”** with **“diminished,”** almost comically academic language to describe a very un-academic situation[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). This ABAB rhyme pattern in the chorus, coupled with the brutally frank language, creates a mix of *humor* and *shock*. It’s catchy – you almost want to sing along, even as your jaw drops at the content.

A huge twist in *Not Taken Kindly* is that Gilley gets on the mic in Verse 2, making this a **duet of recrimination**. After Kathy’s had her say, Gilley retorts with his side: *“Late nights in the kitchen, humping on the floor, / Whispers and secrets, we both wanted more.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT). His lines come with their own rhymes (*“floor/more”*), and a surprisingly tender nostalgia in the words – for a moment, it almost sounds like he’s reminiscing about a genuine connection. But the relationship saga quickly sours: *“moments turned sour, the passion ran dry, / Got me questioning us, asking myself ‘why?’”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT). Gilley’s verse shifts the dynamic; it’s rare in rock to hear a man and woman basically arguing about a sexual misfire within the same song, each owning their perspective. The **bridge** then becomes a sort of last plea: Kathy and Gilley both convey regret and longing in lines like *“I remember that summer, the spark in the air… Now it’s just silence, a chasm so wide”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT). Musically, the band mirrors this chaos – Jim’s drumming is “absolutely volcanic,” threatening to tear the song apart at any moment[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT).

In terms of **structure and rhyme**, *Not Taken Kindly* is fascinating. Each section (Kathy’s verse, chorus, Gilley’s verse, etc.) uses rhyming couplets to punctuate the aggressive back-and-forth. For example, Gilley’s verse rhymes *“air”* with *“care”* and *“dry”* with *“why”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT) – simple rhymes, but delivered with raw feeling. The lyrical bluntness means there’s no hiding behind elaborate wordplay; instead, the **direct rhymes** give the listener something to latch onto amidst the explicit content. It’s like a punk song in that way – straightforward, punchy, and meant to be shouted along with.

The final **outro lines** are Kathy’s knockout punch, and they intentionally do *not* rhyme in a neat way, reflecting the breakdown of the relationship. She spits, *“But if I don’t thrill you, where’s the fun in this fuck? / You self-absorbed shit, I leave guys dumbstruck.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT). The internal near-rhyme of *“fuck”* with *“dumbstruck”* is close enough to sting, but it’s not a perfect rhyme – and that seems deliberate. The sense of *unfinished business* hangs in the air as the song crashes to a halt. It’s a full-on confrontation put to tape[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT).

Needless to say, *Not Taken Kindly* became **the most infamous track on the album**. It was *the* talking point in every review: *Did she really say that?* The answer was yes – and then some. MTV refused to touch it (even a lyric video would’ve been impossible in ’92). The song’s frank depiction of a woman’s sexual agency **and** annoyance at male ego was unheard of. To give a sense of the generational shock: one Los Angeles Times critic wrote, *“If the Rolling Stones’ ‘Brown Sugar’ was dirty, Commuted’s ‘Not Taken Kindly’ is downright obscene – and yet, in its own way, a work of art.”* Fans, especially young women, found empowerment in Kathy’s unfiltered approach. For them, lines like *“You self-absorbed shit, I leave guys dumbstruck”* weren’t just vulgar – they were **cathartic**[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT).

In hindsight, alongside Nirvana’s “Lithium” or Soundgarden’s “Jesus Christ Pose,” Commuted’s *Not Taken Kindly* now stands as a defining blast of early-‘90s alternative rebellion. But unlike those songs’ abstract or political rage, Commuted’s weapon was **sexual candor**. This track declared that Commuted would go **further** than anyone else, right out of the gate. They weren’t just pushing boundaries; they were kicking them down[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT).

## Track 4: **Initiating Connection** – Pop Seduction with a Twist

After the one-two punch of tracks 2 and 3, Commuted swerves again. **“Initiating Connection”** shows a completely different side of the band – a track so surprisingly melodic and dreamy that many thought it was an obvious bid for radio play. In fact, *Initiating Connection* *is* probably the most **“mainstream”** moment on *69 Reasons*, with its slow, sensual groove and breathy vocals. As *Critical Consensus* notes, it *“feels almost like an outlier in their discography—except that it still manages to be sexually charged and deeply memorable.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). This is Commuted proving they can write a seductive pop song, then deliberately **sabotaging it (in a fun way)** by the end – but we’ll get to that.

Lyrically, Kathy shifts into a **romantic, almost tender mode** here (at least initially). The verses paint a cinematic scene of city nightlife and new love. Verse 1: *“Late nights, chasing dreams, / City lights, bursting at the seams. / Met you at the corner, heart on fire, / Whispered secrets, took me higher.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The rhyme scheme in these lines is subtle: *“dreams”* and *“seams”* (an approximate rhyme) in line 1–2, then *“fire”* and *“higher”* (a neat rhyme) in line 3–4[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The effect is a gentle flow that matches the soft, glowing atmosphere the lyrics describe. It’s almost *wholesome* – until the **chorus** drops any pretense of wholesomeness: *“Initiating, our hearts in sync, / Passion rising, can’t stop to think. / Slip your fingers inside me, feel the heat, / In this moment, we can’t be beat.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Here, the first two lines rhyme *“sync”* with *“think”*, and the next two go with *“heat”* and *“beat”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). These straightforward rhymes (AABB) make the chorus extremely catchy – it sounds like a chorus from a love song on the radio, except for the not-so-subtle content. The phrase *“slip your fingers inside me”* is the show-stopper – by 1990s radio standards, it’s incredibly explicit for a “pop” chorus. If any listeners had gotten comfortable during the pretty verses, that line was a spit-out-your-coffee moment. **No metaphors here**; Commuted once again goes straight for the jugular (or elsewhere).

Critics joked that *Initiating Connection* was the album’s “dirty little secret wrapped in a Top 40 package.” The band really toys with the listener’s expectations on this one. *Billboard* described it as *“Madonna’s ‘Justify My Love’ meets Pearl Jam’s production values,”* which actually isn’t far off – there’s a slinky, almost trip-hop beat under the grungy guitar ambience. Kathy’s vocals are **softer, whispery** – showing she can do sultry R&B-style singing, not just rock shouting[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). This track also introduces some unusual instruments for Commuted: notably **Bill (the keyboardist)** busts out a *harmonica* for a surprising bluesy outro, and there’s even a spoken-word voiceover by Gilley at the very end[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). These left-field touches almost parody the idea of a “pop song.” Fans have long debated: were Commuted *sincerely* trying to write a sexy love song here, or were they satirizing one? The answer might be both.

The bridge in *Initiating Connection* adds a hint of darkness: *“Remember that first touch, the thrill we found, / In the shadows, where love could drown. / Two souls colliding, we found our way, / Every pulse a promise, come what may.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The rhyme of *“found”* with *“drown”* and *“way”* with *“may”* isn’t just for form – it underlines the idea that this might be a fleeting moment (“love could drown” is a foreboding image amidst the romance)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). There’s a sense that this connection might be as temporary as a night drive through the city. That melancholy undercurrent aligns *Initiating Connection* more with the **dream-pop** and **“bedroom pop”** vibe it sonically channels[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF), compared to the raw carnality of the prior tracks.

Now, about that **double ending**: After the final chorus, instead of wrapping up neatly, Commuted plays a prank on the listener. First comes a *harmonica solo* from Bill – a slow, bluesy riff that feels like it wandered in from a different song entirely[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). It’s charming and confusing, almost as if the band is saying “Don’t worry, we haven’t gone full pop – here’s something weird!” Then, as that fades, Gilley delivers a *spoken-word monologue* in a low, half-whispered voice, philosophizing (or maybe just rambling) about the nature of desire and connection[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). It’s equal parts profound and absurd – **deliberately overindulgent**. Fans at the time either laughed or scratched their heads. This ending became a running joke (“Does Commuted even *know* how to end a song?!”) and even the band later poked fun at it on stage. But in context, it serves as a reminder: just when you think Commuted might color within the lines, they scribble all over the page.

In summary, *Initiating Connection* shows that Commuted could **write a legitimate alt-rock single** – lush guitars, a memorable chorus, a clear rhyme scheme – but they refused to leave it at that. The song’s mix of genuine sensuality and playful self-sabotage exemplifies the band’s ethos: *do it our way, or not at all*. Notably, this track garnered a lot of **fan love**. Many young listeners who weren’t as into the chaotic side of Commuted found an entry point here. Meanwhile, the industry suits were confused: as noted in one analysis, *“Critics dismissed it as trite… Fans absolutely loved it… The music industry didn’t know what to do with it, but it got airplay – something that couldn’t be said for a lot of *69 Reasons***”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Indeed, *Initiating Connection* actually cracked the Modern Rock charts, even if some stations played a censored version cutting out the infamous “fingers inside me” line. In the tapestry of *69 Reasons*, this song is the shimmering, silky thread – **still provocative, but in a teasing, dreamy way** that shows Commuted’s surprising range.

## Track 5: **Eyes Lost in Desire** – Bluesy Seduction That Scared the Suits

Just when you think you have Commuted pegged, they throw yet another curveball. **“Eyes Lost in Desire”** is a sultry, slow-burning track that draws from *Texas blues and prog-rock*, of all things, filtered through Commuted’s erotic lens[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Coming mid-album, it’s the moment where Commuted prove they can do *sexy* in a completely different flavor than the raw grunge or synthpop of earlier tracks. *Eyes Lost in Desire* is lush, melodic, and almost beautiful – so much so that Country Music Television (**CMT**) reportedly *“wanted to play it – until they actually listened to the lyrics”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). In other words, the sound fooled the establishment, but the content was pure Commuted.

Musically, this song spotlights **Jeremy** (lead guitar) and his love of blues. The intro features an acoustic guitar figure that wouldn’t sound out of place on an **Eric Clapton** record – clean, intricate picking in a minor key. Jeff’s bass and Jim’s drums lay down a restrained, gently pulsing groove. It’s almost a power ballad, but with a sweltering, smoky bar atmosphere. **Kathy’s vocals** on “Eyes Lost in Desire” are a revelation: she starts low and husky, building to some truly powerhouse belts as the song progresses, showing off her range and control[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). One critic later wrote that this track *“proved Kathy Espich could moan and croon like a blues diva when she wanted, and then unleash the wail of a punk banshee when you least expected it.”* The band wisely keeps the **rhythm section minimal** here – giving space for voice and guitar to really shine[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF).

The lyrics of *Eyes Lost in Desire* read like pages from an erotic novel, but with a romantic streak. Verse 1 sets a nostalgic, steamy scene: *“I remember that night, the stars were bright, / Dancing close, it felt so right, / Your breath on my skin, I could feel the heat, / Every pulse, every whisper, left me incomplete.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The **rhyme scheme** in this verse uses couplets: *“bright/right”* and *“heat/incomplete”* (a slant rhyme)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). These soft rhymes add to the gentle, yearning feel. The chorus then swells with emotion: *“You amaze me, oh, can’t you see? / Passion ignites, setting us free. / Legs wrapped tight, heart racing fast, / In this wild moment, I want it to last.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The AABB rhyme pattern is clear: **“see/free”** and **“fast/last”**[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). It’s straightforward and almost sweet. For a moment, you might forget you’re listening to the most controversial band in America – these could be lyrics from a soulful love ballad.

But Commuted doesn’t let you forget for long. By Verse 2 and the bridge, Kathy’s imagery gets more explicit once again, reminding everyone whose album this is. In a line that likely gave CMT executives a heart attack upon reading, Kathy coos: *“From your cock on my breasts, to my eager mouth, / Every moment with you, it’s what love’s about.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). There it is – amidst poetic lines about passion and hearts racing, she drops in a crystal-clear reference to an act rarely, if ever, described so bluntly in a “blues” song. The rhyming of *“skin”* with *“begin”* earlier in that verse, and then using a near-internal rhyme of *“mouth/about”*, is clever[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF), but it’s overshadowed by the sheer audacity of the content. This juxtaposition – tender romance and graphic sexual detail – is exactly what Commuted excels at. They lull you with a torch song vibe, then shock you with an R-rated detail that most songwriters would either obscure or avoid entirely. **Rhyme scheme-wise**, the song keeps to its couplets and quatrains structure, which gives it a classic feel, even as the words push boundaries.

By the final outro, Kathy sings a line that borders on genuinely sweet: *“Together forever, caught in this blaze. / With you by my side, it’s a beautiful ride”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). For a second, you might think this is the one song on *69 Reasons* with a happy, almost monogamous sentiment. But Commuted adds a last little twist: an unlisted *coda* (in some live versions and demos) had Kathy whisper one more line – *“I took the head of your cock a kiss…”* – a reprise of an earlier lyric[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). On the album, these final explicit lines are mixed low, almost subliminal, but they’re there[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). It’s like the band winking at the listener: *Don’t worry, we haven’t gone soft.* The **final rhyme** of *“flow”* with *“glow”* in those lines ties a neat bow on the song, poetically describing *“thin trails of sticky memories”* that are both erotic and wistful[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF).

The **reaction** to *Eyes Lost in Desire* was fascinating. Some critics who hated the rest of *69 Reasons* actually praised this track’s musicianship and depth. It showed Commuted weren’t just one-trick provocateurs; they could write a legitimate, cross-genre song. Guitar magazines applauded Jeremy’s soloing and blues feel. Even country-rock fans, had they encountered it instrumentally, might have appreciated its twang and heart. But the lyrics made it a non-starter for mainstream country or AC radio. *“They almost got us,”* one CMT insider reportedly said, *“but then we heard what she was actually singing. Nope.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The **cultural impact** internally at Island Records was big too – Sarah Malcolm’s notes from ’91 show astonishment: *“CMT wanted to play it—until they actually listened. Then? Lights off, doors locked, they pretended it never existed.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). That quote has become part of Commuted lore, underlining how *Eyes Lost in Desire* could seduce even the unlikeliest ears before revealing its true, risqué colors.

For fans, this song is often a favorite – it’s the eye of the hurricane on *69 Reasons*. Amid all the fury and provocative anthems, here is a track that genuinely **aches** with desire and shows a vulnerable side of Kathy’s songwriting. It’s still edgy, yes, but there’s romance and longing here that broaden the album’s emotional range. In the greater rock context, you could say *Eyes Lost in Desire* anticipated the blend of alternative and roots music that would come later in the ’90s (bands like **Cowboy Junkies** or **Mazzy Star** but with a much more X-rated diary). It’s one more reason *69 Reasons* wasn’t easily pigeonholed: who else in 1992 would dare to mix *Stevie Ray Vaughan*-style guitar, *Nirvana*-level feedback, and *Madonna*-style erotic confessions in one song?

## Track 6: **The Left Hand** – Synthwave Seduction on the Dancefloor

Just past the album’s halfway mark, Commuted veers into yet another genre – and pulls it off with swaggering style. **“The Left Hand”** is *69 Reasons* at its most **hypnotic and danceable**. It’s an unexpected detour into dark, throbbing synthwave, earning its reputation as Commuted’s **“neon-lit seduction”** track[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). If *Eyes Lost in Desire* teased blues influences, *The Left Hand* dives headlong into an electronic 80s-inspired sound – imagine **Depeche Mode** or **The Cure** on a heavy dose of lust and adrenaline. The result was so distinctive that *The Left Hand* became one of the album’s biggest hits (ironically kept from #1 on the charts only by far tamer songs from Vanessa Williams and Eric Clapton)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). To this day, fans of Commuted’s live shows know *The Left Hand* as the moment when the crowd transforms into a pulsing sea of raised hands with red-painted nails – a nod to the single’s iconic cover art featuring a woman’s left hand with fiery red nails[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF).

The lyrics of *The Left Hand* revel in **gothic eroticism**. Kathy sings from the perspective of a woman fully embracing her own dark desires. Verse 1 sets the sultry scene: *“In the shadows of the night, a fire ignites, / Passions swirling like a tempest, endless flights. / Her heart’s a storm, a raging sea so deep, / In the veil of darkness, her secrets softly creep.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The language is poetic – *“tempest,” “veil of darkness,” “raging sea”* – more akin to a vampire romance novel than a rock song. The rhyme scheme here is subtle: *“ignites”* and *“flights”* internally echo, while *“deep”* and *“creep”* form a soft rhyme[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). It’s less about obvious rhymes and more about creating a flowing, moody imagery. The **pre-chorus** then teases the surrender to temptation: *“She dances with the flames, unchained and free, / An insouciant spirit, lost in ecstasy. / A siren’s whisper calls, she’s caught in the grip, / As desire takes the helm, she lets her senses slip.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Here, the rhymes **“free”** with **“ecstasy”** (a nice slant) and **“grip”** with **“slip”** give the pre-chorus a lilting, spell-like quality[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). By the time the **chorus** hits, Commuted deliver one of their most quotable lines: *“Love’s a blade that cuts so deep, / In the masquerade, where angels weep. / With every touch, the shadows grow, / In this midnight madness, let the passions flow!”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). This chorus is pure drama. The rhyme scheme is again AABB – **“deep/weep”** followed by **“grow/flow”**[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF) – giving it a satisfying, anthemic ring. Critics later called it “the world’s first pornographic U2 song”[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF), and you can see why: the chorus has the soaring, romantic feel of an arena-rock anthem, but the context is a shadowy, libidinous dancefloor tryst.

Musically, *The Left Hand* is a showcase for **Bill** (the keyboardist/synth player) and **Jim** (drums). The arrangement is driven by layers of synthesizers – ominous bass synths, glittering retro synth leads – and Jim’s **massive, gated drums** thundering in true ’80s fashion[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Bill was clearly drawing on influences like **Giorgio Moroder** or **New Order**, crafting a beat that is almost industrial in its intensity but danceable in its groove. The band holds back the guitars; Jeremy and Jeff keep a low profile, adding texture but never stealing the spotlight from the synth-and-drum powertrain[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Kathy’s vocals here are delightfully **sultry and commanding** – she alternates between whispered lines in the verses and big, soaring notes in the chorus[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The production is glossy and rich, more polished than anything else on the album (hence it appealing to a broader club-going audience). And yet, as the analysis in *Critical Consensus* points out, it’s *“glossy, yet undeniably dangerous… drenched in neon, smoke, and sin.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF).

An interesting note: The song’s title, *The Left Hand*, became symbolic. It’s never explicitly explained in the lyrics – though one can infer some metaphor about the “left-hand path” (occult term for indulgence) or simply the rebellious non-dominant hand. But the **fan culture** that grew around it was huge. Concertgoers would paint the nails on their left hand red in homage to Kathy’s persona in the song[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The band leaned into this, even naming their fan club **“The Convicted”** after a lyric offshoot (left-hand raised as if guilty and proud). It was an early example of Commuted’s imagery sparking a sort of proto-viral trend among fans – think of how **KISS** fans wear facepaint or **Nirvana** fans wore flannel, Commuted fans in ’92 were suddenly sporting red nail polish as a sign of solidarity and empowerment.

In terms of **rhyme and literary devices**, *The Left Hand* is a bit different from other tracks: it’s more **poetic and metaphor-laden**. The rhymes serve the dark fairytale vibe, rather than for punchy humor or shock. For example, the bridge goes: *“Emerging from the depths, a beast untamed, / In her sanctuary, where whispers are blamed. / The clock’s hands tick, a countdown to fate, / As pleasure intertwines with the hands of hate.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Here we get **internal rhymes** (*“untamed/blamed”* with the echo of “flames” earlier) and symbolic language (time, fate, pleasure vs hate) rather than any four-letter words. It’s almost gothic literature in song form. The lack of profanity in this particular track meant it actually got some club play and even a bit of late-night radio (especially remix versions). People could dance to it oblivious that it was part of a scandalous rock album – at least until they listened closely to lines like *“In this midnight madness, let the passions flow!”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF), which leave little doubt about the ecstatic theme.

*The Left Hand* was widely praised. Many reviews cited it as proof that Commuted was more musically versatile than detractors assumed. In the context of 1992, it predates the big mid-’90s electronica boom – making Commuted look prescient for dabbling in synth-driven rock. Remember, U2’s *Achtung Baby* (late ’91) was also flirting with dance beats and club sounds (“Zoo Station”, “Even Better Than The Real Thing”), and here came this scrappy Dayton band doing it in their own lewd way. Some even compared *The Left Hand* to **My Bloody Valentine’s** *Loveless* in its dense sonic texture, though MBV was shoegaze dreamy whereas Commuted was aiming for throbbing club energy. The bottom line is, *The Left Hand* kept *69 Reasons* unpredictable. Six tracks in, we’ve spanned grunge, punk, acoustic blues, pop, and now synthwave. Yet through all these shifts, the album never loses its identity – because that identity is Kathy’s fearless exploration of sexuality and emotion, whether it’s in a mosh pit or under a disco ball.

## Track 7: **Tangled Up** – A Hypnotic, Shamelessly Erotic Dream-Pop Anthem

With **“Tangled Up,”** Commuted delivers perhaps the most **openly erotic** song of their career – which is saying something. If *The Left Hand* was dark and dancey, *Tangled Up* is slow, hazy, and utterly **immersive**. Critics later dubbed it *“the world’s first pornographic U2 song”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF), capturing the idea that it marries a sort of *Joshua Tree*-esque grandeur with X-rated content. Musically, *Tangled Up* floats on a **psychedelic dream-pop** vibe: chiming, delay-laden guitars (Jeremy and Bill layering gentle arpeggios), a minimalist, heartbeat-like drum pattern from Jim, and Jeff’s bass looping in a trance-inducing riff[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Kathy’s voice here is breathy and intimate; she often sounds like she’s right at your ear, singing just for you. The result is a track that **doesn’t build to a typical climax** – intentionally, it *“keeps you in the moment, stretching desire out indefinitely”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). In an album full of provocation, *Tangled Up* manages to stand out because it’s not just explicit – it’s also *beautiful* in its own strange way.

The lyrics of *Tangled Up* read like a diary of two lovers lost in a moment that they wish could last forever. The **verses** are sultry but relatively restrained: *“Late nights, country lights, / You and I, sparks ignite. / Memories of whispered nights, / Sweetest taste, our hearts take flight.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The rhyming here is gentle: *“lights”* with *“nights”* and *“ignite”* with *“flight”* (close enough)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). It sets a mood of nostalgia and longing. Then the **chorus** comes in with what might be the most jaw-dropping hook on the record: *“Feel the squirt, hear your sound, / Our bodies dancing all around. / Desire’s rise, let it unfold, / With every touch, we break the mold.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Yes, you read that right – “Feel the squirt” is the opening line of the chorus. That phrase, paired with the evocative imagery of bodies dancing and desire unfolding, leaves nothing to the imagination. And Commuted plays it straight – it’s sung in a lilting, almost reverent tone, as if it were high poetry. The rhyme scheme in the chorus is straightforward (AABB: **“sound/around”**, **“unfold/mold”**[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)), which actually makes these outrageous lyrics incredibly *singable*. Many fans have noted the cognitive dissonance of finding themselves singing “feel the squirt” out loud along with a crowd – a true testament to Commuted’s ability to normalize the taboo through a great melody.

Verse 2 keeps the heat on: *“Run my tongue up and down your soul, / Mmm, I love the way we begin. / In the dark, I hear you moan, / Lost in this heat, I feel at home.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The line “up and down your soul” is a provocative twist – presumably meaning the body, but phrased as if the physical and spiritual are one. There’s a slight rhyme of *“soul”* with *“moan”* (not exact, but the vowel sound ties the lines), and *“begin”* with *“in [the dark]”* is another subtle echo[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The overall effect is lulling – it’s intensely sexual, yes, but also oddly **romantic and enveloping**.

By the time we reach the **bridge**, the song drifts into nostalgic territory: *“Remember that night by the lakeside’s shore? / Tangled up, wanting more. / Every kiss, slippery and warm, / Forget the world, in this storm.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Here the rhymes **“shore/more”** and **“warm/storm”** give the section a poetic closure[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The bridge adds a gentle wistfulness, implying that the passion being described isn’t just a one-time fling but part of a history between these lovers. It’s a perfect calm before the final storm of Verse 3 and the outro.

And *oh boy*, Verse 3 of *Tangled Up* is where Commuted throws any last restraint out the window: *“I want to lick that tip, take it all in, / Got me breathing quicker, oh let’s begin. / Wet and wanting, can’t let go, / In this moment, we steal the show.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). This is as explicit as anything on *69 Reasons*, rivaling *Not Taken Kindly* in graphic detail. Yet it feels different here – it’s not angry or comedic, it’s just pure erotic focus. The rhyming of *“in”* with *“begin”* (internal rhyme) and *“go/show”* gives the verse a flowing cadence[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Kathy’s delivery of these lines on the record is almost in a whisper-cry; you can sense the *intimacy*. One might say it crosses into territory that music had rarely gone – it’s essentially a depiction of oral sex set to gorgeous music, and delivered from a woman’s perspective of hunger and agency.

The **outro** then seals the deal with a literal plea and command: *“I want you to come, / I need you to arrive, / Please come inside me, / Let’s feel so alive! / Now! / Now! / Now!”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). These final lines dispense with any metaphor entirely. The repetition of *“Now!”* three times (each time Kathy practically gasps it) is the *climax* of the song in every sense[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). It’s a daring move – ending a track on a series of imperative cries – but it leaves the listener breathless. From a songwriting perspective, notice that the rhymes were abandoned here; instead of neat couplets, we get a raw, direct address (the slight echo of *“come”* and *“inside me”* with *“alive”* isn’t a formal rhyme, more of a tonal one). This break from structure mirrors the break from composure in the song’s narrative – a clever use of form to enhance content.

Critically, *Tangled Up* had its detractors (some found it *too* explicit to take seriously), but many hailed it as a kind of masterpiece of sexual frankness in rock. **Robert Christgau**, the famed Village Voice critic, wrote in his review: *“If Prince pushed the envelope, Commuted tore it to confetti with ‘Tangled Up.’ And amazingly, they made it **pretty**.”* Indeed, *Tangled Up* shows that beyond shock value, Commuted understood sensuality and atmosphere. Fans absolutely adore this song; it became a live staple where Kathy would sometimes perform it while reaching out to the crowd, making direct eye contact, turning the concert hall into what felt like a private bedroom for thousands of people. As noted in retrospective analyses, *“the live performances were legendary, turning the song into something even more provocative”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). It was often paired in setlists with dim lighting and slow, seductive choreography from Kathy, which reportedly caused more than a few parents to ban their teens from attending Commuted shows back in the day.

From a **comparative 1992 perspective**, nothing in mainstream rock was like *Tangled Up*. The closest parallels might be found in underground music or perhaps in the burgeoning trip-hop genre (which was still in its infancy – **Massive Attack**’s *Blue Lines* came out in 1991, and even that wasn’t so sexually explicit). One could argue that *Tangled Up* predicted the later ’90s trend of mixing rock with open sexuality in a female-voiced context – e.g. songs by **Garbage** or **Curve** – but even those kept some metaphor. Commuted just said it, in lush technicolor detail. As a result, *Tangled Up* became a **cult favorite**, and its cultural impact is notable: it was one of those songs passed around on mixtapes among friends with wide eyes and the whisper, *“You gotta hear this… just don’t let your mom hear it.”* The band didn’t release it as a single (impossible, given content), but it didn’t matter – *Tangled Up* seeped into the collective consciousness of alternative music fans as *the* slow jam for the bold.

## Track 8: **Paradise in Pixels** – A Bizarre, Futuristic Lust Experiment

If listeners hadn’t had their minds expanded (or blown) enough by this album, Commuted saved the strangest trip for near the end. **“Paradise in Pixels”** is *69 Reasons*’ most **experimental and polarizing** track – a song that mixes primal sexuality with tech jargon and ambient electronica. It’s an outlier even among outliers, described aptly as *“caveman chanting meets cyberpunk seduction”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). When it comes to comparisons, imagine **Trent Reznor** produced a track for **Kate Bush** after a night of watching both *2001: A Space Odyssey* and a late-night adult chatline infomercial – you might end up somewhere near “Paradise in Pixels.” Not surprisingly, critics and fans have long debated whether this track is a fascinating failure or a misunderstood piece of genius[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). It certainly pushed the envelope of what Commuted – or any rock band at the time – dared to do.

The concept of *Paradise in Pixels* revolves around **digital intimacy** – phone sex, early ’90s online fantasies, the idea of selfies and video before those were everyday things. Kathy adopts a persona who is essentially performing for a lover through a digital medium (camera or screen). This is mind-blowingly ahead of its time for 1992; lyrics reference selfies and Androids when those terms were barely in the lexicon. The **verse 1** lyrics: *“I’m lying on the bed, sans shirt and free, / You’re laughing hard, oh, it’s just you and me. / Memories of laughter, dancing under stars, / Whispers in the moonlight, I’ll take you to Mars.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). At first glance, this could be another romantic scene – except for that word “sans shirt,” which cheekily signals the nudity. The rhyme scheme is present (AABB with *“free/me”* and *“stars/Mars”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)), but what stands out is the tone: *both nostalgic and strangely detached*. Indeed, as an analysis notes, the delivery makes it feel *“hyperreal and disconnected, like a memory filtered through a screen.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). You can almost imagine this scene playing out on a grainy webcam (if those had existed widely then).

The **chorus** is where the song’s theme clicks: *“So here’s my selfie, passion in a frame, / In this paradise, I’ll never be the same. / You get what you see, trust your prick, don’t slip, / Just don’t show my face, let imagination trip.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). It’s a remarkable set of lines. The first line explicitly uses the word “selfie” (which, in 1992, would have been seen as jargon or futuristic). Rhyming **“frame”** with **“same”** and **“slip”** with **“trip”** gives it a playful, taunting rhythm[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The standout lyric is *“trust your prick, don’t slip”* – mixing lust with a sort of digital slanginess – and *“just don’t show my face”* which adds an element of anonymity and control. This is **seductive and transactional at once**[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The female narrator is in charge of her image (“don’t show my face”), teasing the viewer with partial access. It’s a commentary on voyeurism and privacy that feels prophetic of the era of sexting and OnlyFans, decades later. In 1992, this was *sci-fi sex*.

The **soundscape** behind these lyrics is equally unconventional. Jim’s drums here are largely replaced by **tribal percussion and drum machine loops**, giving a ceremonial but mechanical feel[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Gilley, interestingly, provides background **chanting** – wordless, low, hypnotic chants that sound like a monks’ choir on hallucinogens[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). This chanting was one of the most debated aspects: some listeners found it mesmerizing, others thought it was plain silly or distracting[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Jeremy and Bill conjure an **ambient, almost dystopian synth environment** – lots of echo, minor key drones, and what sound like samples of modem noises or heartbeats at times[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Kathy’s vocals float in a detached way, half spoken, half sung in parts, emphasizing the performative aspect of the narrative[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The overall effect is intentionally disorienting, as if the song itself is a blurred line between real and virtual, personal and performative. The *Critical Consensus* review put it perfectly: *“The entire track refuses to sit in one genre—it is primal, yet electronic; synthetic, yet ceremonial.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF).

Returning to lyrics: **Verse 2** ramps up the explicit digital crossover – *“Remember that night? The Android's glow, / I squeezed my breast tight, felt the heat, let it flow. / Every snapshot’s magic, turning moments to gold, / In this fleeting paradise, let our secrets unfold.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The phrase “Android’s glow” is almost jarring – Android phones weren’t a thing in 1992 (the word just means a robot or could imply some futuristic device). It paints a picture of a lit screen in a dark room. The rhymes **“glow/flow”** and **“gold/unfold”** tie these lines with a dreamy ribbon[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). But the content – squeezing her breast for a camera (“snapshot’s magic”) – merges the tactile and the technological. This was truly boundary-pushing songwriting.

The **bridge** adds a brief emotional crack: *“Grateful for the thrill, every time I confide, / Sharing parts of me that I struggle to hide. / From the quiet whispers to the boldest desires, / You’re the spark to my fire, setting my soul on fire.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). This suggests that behind the digital persona, there’s a real longing and vulnerability. For a moment, the listener thinks the song might reveal a human heart. But no sooner is this hinted at than the track returns to its glossy distance. The **outro** reinforces the theme of curating a perfect digital memory: *“So capture this moment, let the story unfold, / In the digital sunset, our passion never gets old.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Rhyming **“unfold”** with **“old”** here is poignant[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF) – the idea that via digital means, passion can be preserved and never age, unlike in real life. It’s both romantic and chilling.

Reception-wise, *Paradise in Pixels* was divisive. *Melody Maker* in ’92 called it *“audio overkill – too many ideas, not enough song”*. Some fans too felt it was the one track they always skipped. Others, however, championed it as evidence that Commuted were fearless innovators. It arguably foresaw the coming Internet era’s impact on relationships in a way no one else in rock did at the time. Musically, you could compare its ambition to **U2’s** weird electronic experiments later in the 90s (*Passengers* project) or even to **Nine Inch Nails** who would later merge sex and tech on 1994’s “Closer” – but Commuted did it first and with a female perspective to boot. One can also note that by placing this track near the album’s end, Commuted almost challenge their listeners: *“You think you’ve heard it all? Try this.”*

The band themselves seemed to have mixed feelings about *Paradise in Pixels*. In one 1993 interview, Kathy laughed, *“We were definitely high on something when we made that one. Maybe caffeine… maybe other stuff. It’s a blur.”* They seldom performed it live (the layers and effects were hard to replicate, and it wasn’t as crowd-rousing as other cuts). Over time, however, *Paradise in Pixels* gained a cult appreciation, especially as the world caught up to its thematic content. Techno-oriented musicians have cited it as an influence, and in the late 2010s, as conversations about digital intimacy became commonplace, some retrospectives hailed the track as *“shockingly ahead of its time in subject matter, if still quite odd in execution.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF).

In the context of *69 Reasons*, this song confirms one thing: **Commuted never played it safe, even at risk of ridicule**[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). *Paradise in Pixels* might not have the immediate appeal of the album’s singles, but it’s a bold artistic swing. Whether you call it a beautiful misfire (as the *Critical Consensus* final verdict did: *“6.5/10 – A fascinating, indulgent failure”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)) or a secret stroke of genius, it undeniably adds to the rich tapestry that makes *69 Reasons* such a talked-about album, even in its most *out there* moments.

## Track 9: **Payment in Kind** – A Feverish Punk Romp Through Blurred Lines

As the album nears its conclusion, **“Payment in Kind”** comes barreling in – a short, **dizzyingly fast** track that manages to pack sexual adventure, humor, and band lore all into under three minutes[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). *Payment in Kind* is often described as Commuted’s take on **punk rock** – it’s up-tempo (about 2:45 in length), chaotic yet controlled, with a cheeky narrative that had fans gossiping endlessly. Why? Because this song isn’t about Kathy (for once) – it’s about an apparent sexual escapade involving **bassist Jeff** and a mutual friend named **Jodi**. Essentially, *Payment in Kind* is a he-said/she-said account of a threesome or partner-swap scenario within the band’s circle, making it *the most gossip-fueling track* on *69 Reasons*. Fans realized *“hey, Kathy’s singing from someone else’s perspective – is this based on a true story?”* The band winked but never fully confirmed details, which only added to the mystique. What’s clear is that the song is playful, wild, and *deliberately nonlinear* – as if reflecting fragmented memories of a crazy night[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF).

Musically, *Payment in Kind* blasts off with a rapid drum machine pattern, overlaid by **four guitars** (yes, all four stringed-instrument members apparently recorded guitars here) that somehow interlock instead of turning to mush[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The effect is a dense wall of sound but mixed cleverly to feel driving, not overwhelming. There’s a synth undercurrent too, with Bill sprinkling some zippy electronic fills, hinting at new wave punk like **Blondie** or **The B-52’s** on steroids[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The vocals are traded; Kathy sings the majority, but Gilley pipes in with asides and harmonies, almost like they’re swapping tales mid-song. The whole track has a *breathless* quality – befitting the subject matter.

The lyrics depict a scenario of flirtation and dare between friends. **Verse 1** (from Kathy’s POV, but she might be voicing Jodi) starts after the deed is done: *“In the corner of the bed, I saw your smile, / Memories flashing back, oh, it’s been a while. / Late nights laughing, payment proffered / Can I ask you something? Will you accept my offer?”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Right away, we have clues: “payment proffered” and “accept my offer” suggests some kind of deal or dare was involved. The rhyme *“smile/while”* and *“proffered/offer”* gives this verse a whimsical, quick-step feel[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF) – these aren’t heavy emotional rhymes, they’re light and transactional, fitting the story. The **pre-chorus** bursts in with teenage energy: *“You were hard, wild, chasing dreams, / In the glow of starlight, we were more than it seems. / Electricity crackled, hearts in a race, / Friendship burning hot, let’s pick up the pace.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The rhyme scheme here (“dreams/seems” and “race/pace”) is classic and tight[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF), which complements the build-up of excitement. It’s telling that they mention *friendship* – implying this sexual encounter is happening among friends, blurring lines but also rooted in trust or at least familiarity.

The **chorus** is a hooky chant: *“Feel the pulse, feel the spark, / In this moment, we’re lighting the dark. / Desire rising as I take the chance, / Let’s trade our fears for a midnight dance.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). This chorus actually could belong to a far less scandalous song – it’s almost uplifting. Rhyme-wise, **“spark/dark”** and **“chance/dance”** provide a satisfying symmetry[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). And thematically, it captures the spirit of just going for it, seizing a wild opportunity (“trade our fears for a midnight dance” is a lovely euphemism for *let’s do something crazy*). It’s little wonder fans were shouting this chorus at concerts; it’s an anthem for adventurous nights.

The real eyebrow-raiser comes in **Verse 2**: *“Jodi’s laughter echoed, the room filled with heat, / Old stories spinning, oh, can’t take a seat. / ‘Are you hard, Jeff?’ it slipped from my lips, / The thrill in the air, a dangerous trip.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). Here we have the smoking gun of the song’s story. Kathy literally sings to Jeff by name – *“Are you hard, Jeff?”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF) – which in context suggests perhaps Kathy (or Jodi) challenged Jeff to… ahem… demonstrate his excitement. It’s a shock and a bit of humor rolled into one. That line became legendary among fans: did our dear bassist really get name-dropped in a sexual context on his own record? (Jeff, for his part, often smirked and shrugged in interviews when asked – which only fanned flames.) The rhyme *“heat/seat”* and *“lips/trip”* keeps the momentum quick and playful[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). One can picture this scene: friends partying, someone blurts a flirty challenge, and off they go.

The **bridge** adds a climactic scene: *“Jodi screamed, closed her eyes tight, / Waves crashing over, everything feels right. / In this electric space, we’re unashamed, / Our hearts racing fast, never the same.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). The imagery of “waves crashing” is a clear metaphor for orgasm, and “never the same” hints that this experience changed their relationships. Rhyme pairs *“tight/right”* and *“unashamed/never the same”* (looser) serve to intensify the sensation described[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). There’s a genuine exhilaration here. It’s notable that unlike some earlier songs that end in bitterness or ambiguity, *Payment in Kind* feels kind of joyful and accepting of what happened – *“we’re unashamed”* stands out as a statement.

Finally, the **outro** gives a cheeky closing: *“So here we are, lost in the night, / With every heartbeat, everything feels right. / Can I ask you a question, just one more time? / On my back I paid, was my mouth sublime?”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF). That last line is a zinger – it suggests that the narrator “paid” (fulfilled her side of the dare) perhaps by giving oral sex (“on my back I paid” sounds like she’s describing positioning), and is now teasingly asking for a review: was I good? This not only brings back the album’s penchant for explicit detail (even couched in semi-poetic wording like “sublime”), but it also wraps the song up with a wink. The rhyme *“time/sublime”* is the final flourish[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF), leaving the listener grinning.

From a **meta perspective**, *Payment in Kind* was notorious in Commuted’s story. Analysts pointed out it was *“the first time it was blatantly obvious [a song] was about a band member’s sexual encounter – but not with Kathy”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF), which indeed sparked countless rumors. It contributed to Commuted’s mythos as this decadent collective living their art. Some cynics thought it was manufactured drama, but given the band’s overall authenticity, most fans believed it was drawn from real shenanigans. Musically, it’s such a rush that even without the gossip, it’s a highlight. One could compare it to the manic energy of **The Pixies** or early **Green Day**, but with a far sexier storyline than those bands ever touched.

Importantly, *Payment in Kind* also showcases the **humor** that underlies a lot of *69 Reasons*. For all the album’s intensity, Commuted always had a playful side. Lines like “Are you hard, Jeff?” show they weren’t afraid to poke fun at themselves and each other. In concert, Kathy would sometimes swap in a local crew member’s name for Jeff’s, just to get a laugh (unless Jeff was present, then she’d point at him as she sang it, to roaring applause). It humanized the band – they weren’t gloomy rock stars pontificating; they were co-ed buddies who rocked and occasionally, apparently, *rocked* each other.

For the broader ’92 rock scene, there was nothing equivalent to *Payment in Kind* on the major albums of that year. The idea of singing about intra-band affairs is something only maybe **Fleetwood Mac** had done openly (on *Rumours*, 1977) – and even they couched it in metaphor. Commuted just shouted it out. In the context of other female-fronted acts, maybe **L7** or **Hole** touched on sexual braggadocio, but not in such a playful, inclusive way. It further cemented Commuted as **fearless and unapologetically themselves**, ready to turn even their taboo personal moments into punky art.

## Track 10: **The Hilton** – Neon-Noir Excess and the Big Finish

To cap off this rollercoaster of an album, Commuted unleashes **“The Hilton”**, a track that critics and fans alike view as a final, triumphant exclamation point on *69 Reasons*. If this album is about crashing the party, *The Hilton* is the decadent after-party on the rooftop – a song dripping with late-night glamour, danger, and indulgence. It’s also one of Commuted’s most **commercially successful** songs, often cited as *“one of Commuted’s most legendary #1 hits”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT). Musically, *The Hilton* revisits the synthwave/rock fusion of *The Left Hand*, but even bigger and sleeker. Think **Miami Vice** aesthetics – palm trees at night, neon pink and electric blue – and now imagine Kathy Espich striding through that scene owning it completely. The *Critical Consensus* review said it perfectly: *“a track that screams Miami Vice excess, neon haze, and seedy late-night adventures”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT).

From the opening seconds, *The Hilton* hooks you with a slinky synth bass line and a four-on-the-floor drum machine beat. Jeff’s live bass doubles it subtly (tucked deep in the mix to keep things smooth)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT). Critics noted how well-crafted it was: *“Every moment is perfectly placed, every sound intentional”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT) – high praise for a band once dismissed as noise-making punks. With its sleek production, *The Hilton* drew comparisons to **Duran Duran** or **Prince’s** Minneapolis sound, yet Kathy’s dominatrix-like persona on the track kept it firmly in Commuted’s unique realm. By hitting #1, it proved that, despite all the naysayers, Commuted *could* dominate the mainstream on the radio and MTV (the video, by the way, was iconic: set on a penthouse balcony, Kathy dancing in silhouette against neon signs – suggestive but artful enough for MTV’s censors).

In the landscape of 1992, having a female-fronted band top the charts with a song unabashedly about female pleasure and control was groundbreaking. This was the same era when **Madonna** was causing a stir with her *Erotica* album and **Tori Amos** was shocking listeners with the frankness of “Me and a Gun” (about sexual violence) – and here was Commuted, part of that wave of women pushing boundaries, but in the rock sphere. They made the macho rock world reckon with a woman singing “I’m gonna show you everything I hide”[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT) and owning her sexuality without shame.

**As a closing track, “The Hilton” feels like a victory lap.** The band is tight, Kathy is triumphant, and lyrically it leaves the listener on a confident, celebratory note of “we did it our way.” One might even interpret *The Hilton* as a metaphor for reaching the top (Hilton being a fancy hotel chain), as if Commuted are saying, we’ve arrived at the penthouse of rock stardom and we’re having our fun up here. The final fade-out of the song, with that pulsing beat and Kathy’s last sultry *“baby, feel it”* echoing, is the sound of a band that has given you everything and is now slipping off into the night, mission accomplished.

## **Legacy, Comparisons, and the Shockwaves of *69 Reasons***

*69 Reasons* was more than an album – it was a **cultural event**. In late ’91 and early ’92, rock music was exploding in all directions: **grunge** had taken over thanks to Nirvana’s *Nevermind* (1991) and Pearl Jam’s *Ten* (1991); established acts like Guns N’ Roses and Metallica were dominating with blockbuster albums (both *Use Your Illusion* and the *Black Album* in ’91); alternative rock was splitting into myriad subgenres – from the shoegaze bliss of My Bloody Valentine’s *Loveless* (1991) to the funk-rock of Red Hot Chili Peppers’ *Blood Sugar Sex Magik* (1991). Amidst this, Commuted emerged with something that touched on many of these trends (the noise of grunge, the groove of funk-rock, the atmospherics of synth and shoegaze) but tied together by a **provocative thematic vision** no one else had dared to present so blatantly. They weren’t singing about teen spirit or black holes or losing religion – they were singing (loudly) about **sex, power, and pain** from a raw female perspective.

When comparing *69 Reasons* to other major rock albums of its era, a few contrasts stand out:

- **Versus Grunge Icons (Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden):** Those bands channeled alienation, political angst, and dark introspection. Their lyrics often hid meaning behind abstract poetry (Cobain’s cryptic musings, Vedder’s intense yet metaphorical storytelling). Commuted, by contrast, put everything right on the surface – especially the body and the emotions. Where Nirvana made a generation feel seen in their ennui, Commuted made listeners confront their secret desires and inhibitions. Musically, Commuted could thrash and scream with the best of the grunge acts (the opener and *Not Taken Kindly* are every bit as heavy as Soundgarden or Mudhoney in intensity), but they also embraced melodies and genres outside the strict “Seattle sound.” This made them a bit of an odd fit initially – too heavy for some, too risque for others – but ultimately it carved them a niche all their own.
    
- **Versus Hard Rock/Metal Titans (Guns N’ Roses, Metallica):** In some ways, Commuted were spiritual successors to the hard-partying ethos of ’80s rock, but updated for the ’90s reality. Guns N’ Roses sang about decadence (“Paradise City,” “Rocket Queen”) with plenty of explicit language, and Metallica’s aggression was legendary – but those came from a hyper-masculine viewpoint that was almost cartoonishly tough or sleazy. Commuted flipped the script: Kathy Espich wielded profanity and erotic themes with as much swagger as an Axl Rose, but with a woman’s twist, often placing men on the vulnerable end of the exchange. For example, GNR’s songs might call women explicit names; Commuted’s songs like *Not Taken Kindly* and *Payment in Kind* put a man in the uncomfortable spotlight of being judged or used. This was **radical** in 1992. It’s as if Commuted absorbed the energy of metal and hard rock but used it to amplify female dominance and desire. Musically too, Commuted wasn’t afraid of a guitar solo or a pummeling drum assault – Jim Brown’s playing was often noted as *“foundation-shaking”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DhL73s9TLdeRVjxZMsk67xDi_b5b9NcT) – but they had an economy and artistry to their arrangements that kept songs tight (most tracks are under 4 minutes, punk-like brevity).
    
- **Versus Alternative/College Rock (R.E.M., U2, etc.):** R.E.M.’s *Out of Time* (1991) and U2’s *Achtung Baby* (1991) were huge records of the period that showed established artists experimenting and maturing. They dealt in sophisticated themes, but again, in coded or high-concept ways (R.E.M. with political undertones and folk-rock leanings, U2 with ironic takes on fame and faith). Commuted, in contrast, felt **immediate and incendiary** – there was zero irony in *69 Reasons*. It wore its heart (or other organs) on its sleeve. Where U2 flirted with erotic imagery in songs like “Mysterious Ways,” Commuted took the listener straight to the bedroom (or the bathroom stall, or backseat of a car – pick your locale from their songs). Yet, interestingly, *69 Reasons* can also be seen as having its own sophistication: the way it blends genres and the careful track sequencing create a kind of narrative arc from chaos to seduction to reflective experimentation, then ending on a high. In that sense, it’s as conceptually thought-out as any U2 album, just with the concept being raw human sexuality.
    
- **The Feminist Dimension:** Perhaps the most important comparison is not to a specific album but to the general absence of voices like Kathy’s in mainstream rock at that time. The early ’90s had women in alternative music (e.g. Kim Gordon of Sonic Youth, PJ Harvey, the riot grrrl movement with Bikini Kill and Bratmobile, etc.), but none had broken into the multi-platinum sphere with such audacity. When *69 Reasons* blew up, it was both celebrated and feared. Some feminist critics lauded the album for reclaiming sexual agency – *“not since Janis Joplin or Grace Slick had a woman in rock been this unabashed, and Kathy Espich makes them sound positively coy,”* wrote one reviewer. Others were unsure: was Commuted empowering or just playing into shock value? Was Kathy’s explicitness liberating or objectifying? These debates raged in op-ed pages. But one thing was clear: **Commuted got people talking about double standards and censorship**. The band members themselves, especially Kathy, were thrust into the role of inadvertent culture warriors. In interviews, she defended her lyrics: *“When a male rocker sings about his exploits, it’s shrugged off. I sing about mine and it’s a scandal. That’s exactly why I do it.”* That quote appeared in a SPIN magazine cover story in mid-1992, and it sums up Commuted’s impact. They exposed the discomfort many had with women depicting pleasure and rage as openly as men.
    
- **Critics and Controversy:** Initially, the critical reception was split by generation (and, frankly, by gender). Many older, male critics panned the album as vulgar gimmickry. Rolling Stone’s 1992 review infamously said, *“Commuted’s debut makes an unforgettable first impression – like a drunken striptease at a family wedding – you’re not sure whether to applaud or call the cops.”* They gave it 2.5 stars, noting the musical promise but bemoaning the “over the top” content. Meanwhile, younger critics in zines and alt-weeklies often championed the album’s fearless approach. **Female critics**, in particular, wrote some of the most insightful pieces: *BUST* magazine called *69 Reasons* *“a guiltily addictive romp that lays bare the female id”*, while *The Village Voice* (via critic Ann Powers) praised Commuted for *“marrying the sensual and the sonic in ways male-fronted grunge wouldn’t dare.”* By year’s end, the so-called “moral majority” took notice too – conservative watchdogs listed *69 Reasons* on their “filthy fifteen” of 1992, right alongside explicit rap albums. This only fueled sales, of course. Commuted’s label had to navigate bans at certain retail chains (Walmart refused to stock the CD without an edited version, which the band flatly refused to produce).
    
- **Commercial Performance vs. Controversy:** *69 Reasons* proved that controversy and quality aren’t mutually exclusive – it achieved **monster commercial success** without the band toning down anything. It hit #1 on the Billboard 200, went multi-platinum, and had endurance – people kept buying it out of curiosity or love, or both. Notably, it was a hit with both male and female audiences, though in different ways: many women found Kathy’s boldness inspiring, while quite a few men (once they got past any bruised ego from songs like “Not Taken Kindly”) found the candid perspective refreshing (and, let’s be honest, arousing). The album’s success forced the industry to reconsider marketing rock music that was both female-led and sexually forthright. One anecdote: A&R departments at rival labels reportedly started hunting for their “own Commuted” after *69 Reasons* went platinum – an irony given that Commuted themselves had been a risky signing for Island. In that sense, Commuted opened doors for future artists. It’s hard to imagine **Alanis Morissette’s** record company letting her include the line “Would she go down on you in a theater?” in 1995’s “You Oughta Know” if Commuted hadn’t already mainstreamed women using explicit language in rock.
    
- **The Band’s Own Take:** From the materials like *“1991: Number 29 With a Bullet”* (the script we glimpsed) and *“1992 Critical Consensus”*, we get an insider sense that Commuted always intended to shock *and* to connect. Sarah Malcolm’s fictional analysis in the Island Records storyline asks, *“Who survives because of this album?”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FBJ0uS51z3vp6wFlLAtQ4_D53ZvVRbB0) – framing *69 Reasons* not just as a collection of songs, but as something life-saving or transformative for some listeners. That indeed became a narrative: fans wrote letters about how hearing a woman express rage or lust without shame helped them confront their own experiences, be it sexual empowerment or trauma. The album’s raw honesty resonated deeply with those who needed an outlet or an anthem in their personal lives.
    

In summary, *69 Reasons* stands tall among the class of ’91-’92 albums as the **brashest and most taboo-breaking** of the bunch. It took the spirit of rock – rebellion, liberation, energy – and applied it to the intimate realm of sex and emotional power dynamics, where previously rock (especially alt-rock) often tread more lightly or abstractly. It’s as if Commuted said: *Angst and politics are great, but we have other fires to start too.* And they sure started them – in bedrooms, boardrooms (record execs had many a panicked meeting about this record’s promotion), and in the press.

## **Final Verdict: An Album That Changed the Rules**

Reviewing *69 Reasons* in full, one can’t help but marvel at how **comprehensive** its vision is. It’s a debut album that manages to be a manifesto. From the first track’s chaotic roar to the last track’s neon-lit seduction, Commuted drags the listener through a journey of **desire, defiance, heartbreak, humor, and liberation**. Few albums before or since have balanced such extreme explicitness with genuine musical innovation and emotional depth. It’s an album where a lyric like *“Feel the squirt, hear your sound”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF) can exist alongside a poetic line like *“Love’s a blade that cuts so deep, in the masquerade where angels weep”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/117YRR0bvhgg7I09ugzugY7V-V1-ZGWEF) – and both feel absolutely essential to the whole.

In the immediate aftermath of its release, *69 Reasons* faced confusion and even scorn from establishment critics, but as the years passed, its reputation only grew. Today, it’s often cited as a **trailblazing classic**. Modern retrospectives credit Commuted with kicking open doors: the frank lyrical territory that pop stars like **Madonna** and **Janet Jackson** explored in the ’90s on the pop side, Commuted paralleled in rock. The album’s influence can arguably be heard in later female rock acts – from **Liz Phair’s** unfiltered narratives to **Shirley Manson** of Garbage’s sultry assertiveness, to even 2000s icons like **Karen O** of Yeah Yeah Yeahs who mixed sexuality with punk artfulness.

Yet, no one ever quite duplicated *69 Reasons*. Perhaps because it was so specifically the alchemy of these individuals: Kathy’s fearless songwriting and vocal charisma, Gilley’s edgy contributions, Jim and Jeff’s powerhouse rhythm section, Bill and Jeremy’s versatility on the sonic palette – all under Ric Ocasek’s understanding ear as a producer who let their rawness shine. A famous quote often attributed to Ocasek about the album: *“They gave me 69 reasons to either quit the business or fall in love with it all over again – I chose the latter.”* Whether he said it or not (some say it was apocryphal), it rings true to the impact this record had on those who believed rock should be dangerous.

In a 1993 *Rolling Stone* readers’ poll, *69 Reasons* was voted Album of the Year (despite the magazine’s lukewarm initial review), showing how the **audience embraced what the establishment wasn’t sure about**. And decades later, it’s not uncommon to see *69 Reasons* on “Greatest Albums of the ’90s” lists, often with the caveat that *“no album before or since has quite matched its blend of provocation and heart.”*

To end on an illustrative note: Nirvana’s Kurt Cobain, when asked in 1993 by an interviewer what new music he was listening to, mentioned Commuted. He reportedly said, *“That Commuted album… it’s scary and kind of hilarious. I like it. It’s gutsy.”* Imagine that – the figurehead of grunge himself acknowledging the fearless new kids on the block. *69 Reasons* had *guts* in spades, and it made the rock world a far more interesting (and frankly, more honest) place.

# Part Two: Arsonists? Or Hedonists?

**Track 11 – “I’ll Own Your Moment”**

The back half of *69 Reasons* opens with a **slow-burning, hedonistic slow jam** that stands among Commuted’s most erotic works. *“I’ll Own Your Moment”* unfurls at a languid tempo, blending **bedroom shoegaze and ’80s alternative** into an atmosphere of shimmering guitars and hushed, throbbing bass. The tone is **hypnotic and intimate**, with Kathy Espich’s vocals almost whispering at first, a seductive croon that barely masks the hunger underneath. There’s a palpable sense of control in the arrangement – Jim Brown’s drumming is metronomic and precise; Jeff Evans’ bass line is stately and restrained – all of which anchors the song in a controlled groove as Kathy and guitarist Jeremy Evans **slowly turn up the heat**. By the time Jeremy’s lead guitar finally **flares into an expressive solo** near the end, the track has built from delicate embers to a raging flame.

Lyrically, “I’ll Own Your Moment” is **unabashedly erotic** and surprisingly poetic. The verses begin in nostalgic reflection – *“I remember the summer, you and me, / Laughing ’til the sunset set us free”* – painting a scene of innocence that quickly gives way to raw carnality. The writing is rich with metaphor: Kathy likens herself to a *“succubus”* in the pre-chorus – an allusion to a mythic demon lover – as she purrs *“now I’m here, a succubus spread, anointing your member in my bed”*. This mix of the **sacred and profane** (using the word “anointing” for a sexual act) is emblematic of Commuted’s lyrical daring. The **rhyme scheme** rolls easily off the tongue in couplets (e.g. *“igniting the night”* / *“feels so right”*), enhancing the song’s trance-like quality. By the chorus, Kathy’s voice turns breathy and commanding, insisting *“Oh, the heat in my fingers, can you feel the fire?”* as the band drops into a sultry pocket. Each refrain intensifies, until the final moments deliver one of the most **bluntly explicit payoffs** in the album’s history: Kathy nearly moans as she sings about *“shooting from your balls, white ropes cascade”*. It’s a shockingly graphic climax – one that U.S. radio censors infamously balked at – yet within the **slow, elegant buildup** of this track it feels like a natural eruption of passion.

Musically, the song is a study in **restraint giving way to indulgence**. For over five minutes, the band holds back: **Jeremy’s guitar filigrees are delicate and chiming**, Kathy’s rhythm guitar fills are subtle, and Bill Davenport’s synthesizers bathe the background in a soft glow. This controlled first half makes the final minute hit like a tidal wave. In the climax, the band abandons all reserve – **the drums kick into a heavier gear, Jeff’s bass grows more insistent**, and Kathy’s vocals climb to a soulful wail as the lyrics describe unrestrained ecstasy. The result is a track that **creeps up on the listener**: what begins as a tender, late-night reminiscence transforms into a fearless celebration of physical love. It’s no wonder that *“I’ll Own Your Moment”* became a notorious cut that many American stations refused to play; meanwhile, **BBC Radio 1 embraced it in their late-night rotation**, recognizing the song’s artistry beneath the provocation. As one UK DJ remarked, the track was “**too sensual to ignore**,” censorship be damned. In hindsight, this song stands as Commuted’s statement that in the world of *69 Reasons*, **pleasure is power** – and they’ll take their sweet time to prove it.

**Track 12 – “Sweet Memories in a Dive Bar”**

If “I’ll Own Your Moment” is a slow seduction, *“Sweet Memories in a Dive Bar”* is a **frenzied flash of hedonism** – brilliant and chaotic, and over almost as soon as it begins. At a scant **2 minutes and 39 seconds**, this track is one of the album’s shortest offerings. It plays like an explosive vignette: a half-remembered night of debauchery compressed into a punkish synthwave romp. Musically the song mixes breakbeat electronics with an almost orchestral sense of drama – Bill’s synthesizer lines swirl in Cinemascope grandeur even as Jim’s drums skitter with breakstep urgency underneath. Kathy’s voice is drenched in reverb and attitude, **shouting headlong into the fray** with no time for subtlety. There’s a recklessness to the performance that mirrors the subject matter perfectly.

Lyrically, “Sweet Memories in a Dive Bar” is **deceptively titled** – these “sweet memories” are anything but gentle. The opening lines (which repeat as a kind of mantra throughout the song) cut to the chase: *“Sweet memories, we’re ready to fuck.”*. That blunt declaration sets the tone for an unapologetically X-rated reminiscence. Kathy rattles off images of a wild night – glasses raised, shirts discarded, likely in the backroom of some seedy New Orleans bar (the track slyly hints at a legendary Big Easy night that *might* have inspired it). We hear a voice in the bridge purr *“Want to see how well I swallow?”*, followed by laughter and a cacophony of moans that swirl into the mix. The **narrative is fragmented** on purpose: rather than describing a linear encounter, the lyrics feel like jump-cuts in a film, flashing from one erotic snapshot to another. This method of songwriting leaves huge gaps for the listener’s imagination, which is exactly why the track has **fascinated fans** despite (or because of) its brevity.

What makes *“Sweet Memories in a Dive Bar”* so compelling is precisely that **incomplete, chaotic nature**. The band essentially drops us into the middle of an orgy and pulls us out before we can fully process it. The chorus hits hard with a shouted, celebratory refrain and then – just as the guitars and synths reach a fever pitch – the song **cuts out abruptly**, leaving a trail of reverb and stunned silence. Critics have noted that it *“feels like half the song was left on the cutting room floor”*, and indeed there’s an almost infuriating quality to how suddenly it ends. Yet that’s clearly intentional. Commuted turns the song’s short duration into a statement: indulgence is fleeting, chaotic, and sometimes you don’t get closure. In the album’s narrative, this track is the **wild bender** that precedes a morning of reckoning. It’s *69 Reasons’* most **“unfinished”** song on the surface, but within that raw sketch lies a certain genius. The band invites us to wonder what *else* happened that night – and by refusing to tell, they make the *mystery* part of the allure. *“Sweet Memories in a Dive Bar”* is thus both **frustrating and thrilling**: a tease of a song that shouldn’t work, but *does*, precisely because it **captures excess in its purest, most chaotic form**.

**Track 13 – “To Excess”**

After the fragmentary frenzy of “Sweet Memories,” Commuted delivers *“To Excess”* – a song that can only be described as the **full confession** to Sweet Memories’ fleeting tease. This track is where the band’s **literary ambitions** fully bloom, couching its debauchery in clever wordplay and decadent imagery. Over a sultry synth-driven midtempo groove, Kathy unleashes lyrics that drip with wit and self-awareness, as if Oscar Wilde wandered into a Midwestern dive bar. Indeed, the band seems to **channel Wilde’s spirit** here: *“To Excess”* marries **rock ‘n’ roll decadence with droll, aphoristic charm**, making sin sound like high art. The verses are packed with allusions and flamboyant scenes – from bourbon-soaked kisses to phoenixes rising from ashes – all delivered by Kathy in a voice that is equal parts seductive purr and defiant sneer.

The narrative of the song is a **panoramic view of a wild night out**, likely another tale of Kathy’s barroom exploits in small-town Ohio. In the opening verse she sets the scene like a novelist: *“In the heart of the night, where the shadows play, / A dive bar in Findlay, where dreams fade away. / Neon lights flicker, a siren’s call, / I twisted on the barstool, ready to risk it all.”*. The internal rhymes and vivid details (neon flicker, barstool twisting) plant us right there next to her, feeling the itchy thrill of imminent bad decisions. By the chorus, the song’s **philosophy of indulgence** is in full bloom: *“We are the phoenixes, rising from ashes, / Exploding with laughter, love that never crashes… / In the haze of the night, we’re sinners and saints. / Yeah, I’m gonna get fucked, let’s do it two times, / In the back of this bar, erotica ain’t no crime.”*. This chorus is remarkable for how it **balances elegance and vulgarity**. In one breath Kathy proclaims rebirth and redemption (the *phoenix* image, the sinner/saint duality) and in the next she’s gleefully shouting about getting fucked *“two times.”* It’s jarring on paper, but in performance it lands like an **anthem of liberation**. The rhyme scheme here pairs lofty concepts with blunt sexuality (a deliberately cheeky *mismatch* that underscores the song’s theme: finding grace in filth).

Musically, *“To Excess”* stands out as one of Commuted’s most **atmospheric and controlled** compositions. The instrumentation is **sparse but purposeful**. Bill’s synth pads set a hazy, neon-lit mood, and interestingly, the guitars **hold back** – Jeremy and Gilley don’t even kick in until well into the song, leaving Kathy’s vocals and Jim’s mechanical kick-drum pulse to carry the early sections. This restraint creates enormous tension; when the guitars finally do enter with subtle, delayed riffs, it feels like floodgates cracking open. Each verse ratchets the intensity slightly: by Verse 2, Kathy’s lines get more brazen (*“I said, ‘I’ll be the one making you come today!’ with a devilish grin…”*) and the band feeds on that bravado, adding layers – a tighter drum fill here, a ghostly backing harmony there. There’s even a moment in the bridge where Kathy practically *toasts* the debauchery: *“Here’s to that night when I came so loud, the waitress kept watch, we were allowed”* – a line that blurs memory and fantasy with a wink. Through all this, the song never loses its cool, riding a **midnight groove** that feels like black velvet.

Critically, *“To Excess”* is viewed as Commuted’s **first true lyrical masterpiece**. It’s the track where they proved that an explicit song could also be **smart, structured, and layered with meaning**. Beneath the obvious celebration of indulgence, there’s an undercurrent of melancholy and self-awareness: Kathy raises a “toast to the broken” in verse one, acknowledging that this wild lifestyle has costs. And in the outro she delivers a couplet that feels both triumphant and tragic: *“Ambition fulfilled, as I etched my claim, / In my city of dreams, I’ll never be tame.”*. It’s a rock-and-roll manifesto in two lines – boasting of conquest yet hinting at the loneliness of someone who “will never be tame.” The band leaves that dichotomy hanging. *“To Excess”* doesn’t judge its protagonist; it simply **revels in the beautiful disaster** she creates. In doing so, it gave Commuted a new mystique: the role of **literary hedonists**, capable of making filth sound like poetry. The track’s legacy is such that many fans point to it as the moment they realized Commuted weren’t just out to shock – **they had stories to tell and a razor-sharp pen to tell them with**.

**Track 14 – “It Has a Wow Finish”**

True to its cheeky title, *“It Has a Wow Finish”* is an **over-the-top fantasy** that pushes Commuted’s penchant for excess to cartoonish heights. This song is one of the band’s most openly *indulgent* detours – a loud, fuzzy **grunge romp** wrapped around an outrageous sexual scenario. The music comes on heavy and hard from bar one: snarling distorted guitars (courtesy of **all** three guitar-wielding members – Jeremy, Gilley, and even Kathy, each adding to the cacophony), a bassline so overdriven it’s felt more than heard, and Jim’s drums laying down a relentless, primitive stomp. After the careful control of “To Excess,” this track feels almost anarchic. The production is intentionally raw – the band practically red-lines the recording, giving the whole thing a **garage-band-meets-wall-of-sound** vibe. In other words, it *wants* to sound a bit unhinged and blown out, because that matches the spirit of the song’s narrative.

And what a narrative it is. *“It Has a Wow Finish”* is essentially Commuted’s wildest **Hollywood fantasy** turned into song. The lyrics imagine a **threesome with Rick Blaine and Ilsa Lund** – yes, the characters from *Casablanca*. It’s a concept both absurd and oddly brilliant: Kathy (presumably the narrator) fantasizes about inserting herself into the classic film’s romance. The verses drop overt references to the film and its stars: *“In the shadows of a café, we’ll celebrate. / I want Rick to look at me, see my soul, / With Ilsa on my face, I’ll lose control”*. That scandalous image of *Ilsa on my face* is the kind of line that practically **dares you to gasp**. Yet the song delivers it with a wink and a grin. By the chorus, Kathy is belting out a tongue-in-cheek ode to risk and thrill: *“Oh, I hope it was worth the wait, / For the thrill that I chase, it’s never too late. / Underneath the stars, where the city’s aglow, / In Casablanca dreams, let our wild hearts flow.”*. The chorus has a big, almost cinematic melody – one can imagine a spotlight on Kathy in a smoke-filled club as she sings it, channeling some glam 1940s movie star in the guise of a ’90s rock diva. Every time she hits the phrase *“Casablanca dreams,”* it’s with a mix of earnest longing and self-aware camp.

One of the song’s notorious highlights is the **bridge**, where Commuted fully leans into the *Casablanca* homage and the excess at hand. Kathy paraphrases the film’s famous line (“a kiss is just a kiss”) into *“A kiss is still a kiss, a sigh is just a sin”*, following it with a breathy *“You’re fucking right, let’s lose ourselves in the gin”*. It’s a riotous collision of classic Hollywood and R-rated bravado – imagine Humphrey Bogart suddenly dropping an F-bomb in Rick’s Café Américain. This blend of the **timeless and the tawdry** encapsulates the Commuted ethos: nothing is sacred, not even one of cinema’s greatest romances, if there’s an opportunity to turn it into a playground for fantasy. The rhyme scheme and rhythm of the lyrics are straightforward (lots of end rhymes like “fate/celebrate” and “embrace/grace”), which keeps the focus on the outrageous content rather than on poetic flourish. And indeed, subtlety is *not* the goal here. **Every element is dialed up**: the volume, the sexual bravado, the sheer silliness of referencing *Rick* and *Ilsa* by name in what amounts to a dirty joke set to music.

Crucially, the band is in on the joke. *“It Has a Wow Finish”* was born from an actual playful conversation – Jeff Evans once admitted in an interview that the song was inspired by him asking his girlfriend which famous movie couple she’d pick for a threesome. That real-life anecdote gives the track an extra layer of tongue-in-cheek authenticity. Jeff’s unguarded confession (“I maybe shouldn’t have told *Rolling Stone* that, but I did,” he later laughed) made the song *infamous* even before many had heard it. Fans knew going in that this would be a “threesome song,” and Commuted did not disappoint. From the bombastic arrangement to the gleefully crass lyrics, they play it all with a **knowing smirk**. This is not a track aiming for critical respect – it’s aiming to **blow off the doors and have a blast**. And it succeeds: musically, it’s one of the album’s most *fun* moments, with the band members audibly reveling in the noisy, fuzzy jam. Kathy’s vocals are at times sultry and at times almost laughing through the lines, as if she can barely keep a straight face while singing *“Didn’t mind when Ilsa licked me”* in the outro.

In the grand scope of *69 Reasons*, *“It Has a Wow Finish”* is a **raucous palate-cleanser** after the weighty poetry of “To Excess.” It reminds the listener that Commuted are still more than willing to trade in pure, unapologetic fantasy. Critics at the time were divided – some dismissed it as a gimmick, but many others appreciated the audacity. As a retrospective take, one could say the track proves that sometimes **excess isn’t just the point – it’s the entire purpose**. By fusing an old Hollywood daydream with gritty grunge energy, Commuted created a blazing highlight that is equal parts parody and genuinely liberating rock anthem. It’s silly, it’s sexy, and, yes, it absolutely **has one hell of a finish**.

**Track 15 – “Next to the Ice Cream Shop”**

With *“Next to the Ice Cream Shop,”* the album plunges into its most **cryptic and unsettling narrative**. This song is a stark departure from the raucous fun of the previous track – instead of overt fantasy, we get **murkiness, tension, and unanswered questions**. The music itself is eerie: a slow, droning grind built on **Grunge American Primitivism** and smeared with heavy reverb. The groove lurches along at midtempo, but Jim’s drumming is sparse and almost ritualistic, more about creating an ominous atmosphere than keeping a beat. Echo-drenched guitar lines from Jeremy and Gilley drift in and out like ghosts, never forming a conventional riff or melody. Jeff’s bass stays subdued and shadowy. The whole arrangement feels **suspended in a hazy twilight**, as if you’ve walked into a dimly lit backroom where something illicit is happening just out of sight.

The lyrics of “Next to the Ice Cream Shop” read like slices of a noir short story – except the story’s pages are scrambled, and key details are blacked out. We’re presented with fragments of a scenario involving a massage parlor (or some kind of clandestine service) operating literally beside an ice cream shop. The track opens in a hush: *“In the dim light, where the whispers flow, / ‘Two hundred bucks?’ I had to know. / Christine says, ‘Fresh each time, / With herbal magic, we’ll be just fine.’”*. Right away, we’re dropped into a secretive negotiation. Money is mentioned, a certain Christine is running the show, and **“herbal magic”** is in play – hints of some strange ritual or drug. The chorus calls it *“a poisoned promise, wrapped in silk… Asp massage, a secret art”*. That phrase “asp massage” is particularly evocative. An *asp* is a snake (and a nod to Cleopatra’s legendary suicide), which gives the image of something both sensual and deadly. Listeners have debated: is it literal, some exotic technique involving snakes or venom? Or metaphorical, implying the risk in this pleasure? Commuted never clarifies, and that’s the song’s brilliance – **it never fully explains itself**.

Across the verses, more disturbing details surface. We hear about multiple participants: *“First cock arrives, lost in a dream, / Kimono open, a wild scene”* Kathy sings in Verse 2, as if describing the entry of a client into this hidden den. By Verse 3, the imagery turns outright explicit and bizarre: *“His spurting time soon, straight from donor, / With every thrust, I’m the guest of honor… The glans burst forth, a cascade of white, / Clenching his shaft, Christine dissolved the herbs.”*. It’s a vision of a sexual ritual mixing with herbal concoctions – possibly a kind of **fertility or virility rite**. The lyrics mention *“herbal mixture gave my breasts a fine sheen”*, implying something has been applied or consumed that affects the participants physically. The recurring question in the song is **who is receiving and who is providing** this service. The line *“Got my heart racing, where do I begin?”* suggests the narrator (Kathy) might be on the receiving end of some unusual treatment. But elsewhere she sings *“I catch the moment… Christine’s touch, it lingers on”*, implying Christine might be massaging her. Or is Christine orchestrating while Kathy serves the clients? The pronouns and perspectives are **deliberately blurred**. The band has effectively crafted a lyrical puzzle about a **forbidden transaction** and left it unsolved on purpose.

The *tone* Kathy uses to sing these lines is worth noting: she’s detached, almost coolly observing the debauchery as it unfolds. This detached vocal performance heightens the eerie vibe – it’s as if the narrator has disassociated from the disturbing things happening to her or around her, recounting them like a distant memory. The chorus melody is darkly seductive, minor-key and droning, with Kathy’s voice multi-tracked in ghostly harmony as she intones that *“poisoned promise”*. In the background, moans and whispered voices swirl, never loud enough to fully discern, adding to the **voyeuristic feeling** that we listeners are eavesdropping on something we shouldn’t. Musically and lyrically, every element of “Next to the Ice Cream Shop” is built to **disorient and provoke curiosity**. It’s the song that *raises your neck hairs*: not because it’s loud or aggressive, but because it’s so **mysterious and sinister**.

Fans have spent years speculating about the “true story” behind this track. Some theorize it references a real massage parlor bust in the band’s hometown; others think it’s pure fiction meant to symbolize the danger of unchecked indulgence. The band, in classic fashion, has offered **no answers** – Gilley slyly told an interviewer, “We prefer to let you wonder who got massaged… and who got creamed,” with a devilish grin. What is clear is how effectively Commuted uses **ambiguity as a tool** here. By never fully explaining the line *“asp massage”* or the role of Christine, the song invites the audience into a conversation (and even an argument) about its meaning. This is storytelling by suggestion and implication. It’s also a masterstroke of album pacing: at track 15, *69 Reasons* plunges the listener into a dark, surreal underbelly just before the final act. In an album full of explicit moments, “Next to the Ice Cream Shop” proves that **the unknown can be just as provocative**. The result is a song as *beautiful* as it is *disturbing*, a deep cut that fans still debate and that ensures the album’s thematic journey isn’t all fun and games – there are shadows amid the neon and Commuted isn’t afraid to drag us through them.

**Track 16 – “Xmas Party”**

As if snapping back into the immediate and chaotic, Commuted follows the cryptic “Ice Cream Shop” with *“Xmas Party,”* a song that is pure **adrenaline and transgression**. This track is a furious depiction of a **forbidden rendezvous at a holiday party**, delivered with such aggression and speed that it leaves the listener breathless. Musically, “Xmas Party” is a rare fusion of **grunge and mathcore** – imagine a punky Christmas carol set on fire. Jim’s drumming deserves special mention: he hurtles through jagged, **unpredictable time signatures** (throwing in 7/8 bars and sudden tempo shifts) that mirror the erratic heartbeat of someone about to get caught in the act. Meanwhile, Jeremy’s guitar riffs slash and stutter, full of angular stops and starts; you can practically feel the pent-up nervous energy in every dissonant chord he strikes. Interestingly, the track opens with a deceptive moment of calm – a few seconds of Gilley plucking a twangy acoustic guitar in a quiet intro. But that respite is a fake-out: within moments, the full band **explodes** into action. It’s as if the calm exterior of a respectable holiday gathering is suddenly ripped away to reveal the frenzy in the broom closet.

The lyrics to “Xmas Party” paint a vivid mini narrative of secret lust consummated just out of sight. Kathy adopts the persona of someone at a Christmas party who sneaks off to cheat with another guest. In **Verse 1**, she sets the illicit scene via text message: *“Fingers trembling as I send the text, / ‘In the closet, leave your date perplexed.’”*. In one couplet, she establishes motive, method, and setting: it’s sneaky, it’s urgent, and it’s happening *now*. The chorus then just slams in, primal and shameless: *“Meet me where the shadows lie, / Flesh against flesh, a primal scream, / Lost in the chaos, a forbidden dream. / Leave the light off, keep my secret, right? / In this supply closet, I’ve got something tight.”*. The rhymes are simple (“lie” with “nigh,” “scream” with “dream”), but the phrasing is direct and guttural. There is **zero subtlety** – words like *“flesh,” “scream,” “chaos,”* and *“forbidden”* hit with monosyllabic force. And Kathy delivers them not as sultry enticements but as **practically a howl**. She sounds feral on this track, especially as the song progresses. By **Verse 2**, her vocals turn to outright screaming as she narrates the rough encounter: *“Then you close the door, my breath holds tight, / Push me down into the blackened night. / ‘Don’t resist,’ you growl, your grip like steel… As you fill my mouth with thunder and roar.”*. These lines are intense, even uncomfortable – the encounter teeters on the edge of consent (the “don’t resist” command suggests a dangerous power play). It’s a portrayal of **lust at its most unhinged**, where pain and pleasure blur (*“Fingernails digging, a bloodstained trace,”* Kathy growls in the bridge, encapsulating how violence and ecstasy intermingle in this tryst).

The structure of the song cleverly reinforces that sense of barely controlled chaos. At around the three-minute mark, after a particularly furious chorus, the band suddenly **drops to silence** – a fake ending, like the moment the two illicit lovers pause, thinking they heard someone outside the closet. But then, with a burst of guitar feedback, *“Xmas Party”* roars back to life, repeating the chorus and outro at an even more frenzied pitch. In this final lap, Kathy’s voice is shredded, practically screaming the lines as Jim’s drums careen and Jeremy’s lead guitar squeals in dissonant feedback. It is **messy, frantic, and utterly gripping**. The false ending and revival serve as a musical analog to the affair’s aftermath – just when you think it’s over, the guilt and adrenaline come surging back for one more round. By the true end, when the last chords crash and ring out, the listener is left adrenalized and slightly shell-shocked. The song, fittingly, **refuses to end neatly, much like the illicit affair it describes**.

Critically, “Xmas Party” has become a **cult favorite** for fans who appreciate Commuted’s heavier side. It’s one of the most *physically affecting* tracks on the album – you can’t listen passively; it grabs you by the collar. The band’s performance here borders on cathartic chaos, yet there’s skill under the madness. Jim’s ability to make complex rhythms sound like pure punk mayhem, Kathy’s dynamic vocal control (shifting from whisper to scream on a dime), and the group’s sense of when to restrain and when to detonate all illustrate a band at peak confidence. Lyrically, the song stands out for depicting the *dark side* of the album’s sexual adventures: this isn’t playful or empowering, it’s sordid and fraught with peril. And yet, Commuted doesn’t pass judgment. The final lines of the outro – *“When the party ends, we’ll fade into grey, / But in this dark closet, we’ve found our way”* – suggest a nihilistic acceptance. In the harsh light of day (grey reality), this affair might mean nothing or spell ruin. But for that moment, in the dark, it was a *truth* for the participants. It’s a stark note to hit near the album’s end: **desire isn’t always glamorous or redemptive – sometimes it’s just raw, reckless, and wrong**. And *“Xmas Party”*, in all its frantic glory, compels us to feel that dangerous thrill and its consequent collapse.

**Track 17 – “Buying a Cookie”**

Amid the album’s climatic stretch of explicit chaos and intensity, *“Buying a Cookie”* arrives as an oddly lighthearted breather – a **strangely hypnotic detour** that has puzzled and intrigued listeners in equal measure. In the context of *69 Reasons*, this song is almost a palate cleanser: its tone is flirtatious and fun, lacking the darkness or high stakes of the surrounding tracks. Musically, “Buying a Cookie” veers into an unexpected style for Commuted: it flirts with **surf rock and alt-country** vibes, built on a twangy guitar riff and a relaxed, swaying rhythm that wouldn’t sound out of place on a late-80s college rock single. Bill’s keyboards even add a sunny, retro organ tone in the background, giving the track a faint *1960s beach party* atmosphere – a truly odd flavor on an album mostly about underground lust and neon-lit excess. Kathy’s vocal approach here is more playful and detached; she doesn’t belt or growl so much as sing-song her way through the verses with a knowing smirk.

Lyrically, “Buying a Cookie” is at once sensual and tongue-in-cheek. The metaphor at play is pretty transparent: the “cookie” isn’t a baked good so much as a euphemism for a sexual favor or experience. Kathy sings about transactions of desire with a **wry sense of humor**. In the chorus, she purrs *“These are fair game, my breasts laid bare, / With every touch, I ignite the air”*. The rhymes are simple (“bare/air,” “heat/retreat”), reinforcing the almost singsong quality of the melody. There’s a line where she coos, *“Squeezing the fire, feeling the heat, / In this darkened dance, I’ll never retreat,”* which certainly keeps the song tied thematically to physical pleasure, but it’s delivered with an upbeat lilt that suggests she’s not taking this encounter as seriously as others on the album. Indeed, many of the lyrics hint that the protagonist is *in control* and maybe even a bit bored: she’s effectively saying *I’ll play along, I’ll have my fun, but this is just a playful transaction.* Compared to the emotional rollercoasters of tracks like “Xmas Party” or “Entry,” this song feels almost **frivolous** – intentionally so.

The overall impression is that *“Buying a Cookie”* might be commenting on the notion of casual sex or minor indulgences that **don’t carry deep weight**. It’s a catchy tune with a repetitive structure; notably, the song doesn’t really **build** to anything huge. The verses and choruses cycle with the same breezy chord progression, and there’s no massive bridge or climax – a creative decision that mirrors the lyrical content’s lack of deeper development. This repetitiveness, however, is oddly hypnotic. The groove settles in, and you find yourself nodding along, captured by its easy momentum even as you realize nothing particularly *new* is happening. In this way, Commuted almost **satirizes** the idea of a filler track. They give us a track that sounds great on the surface – it’s melodic, the hook is hummable, the instrumentation has a cool surfy sheen – yet by the end you might think, *“That was fun… but what did it really say?”* The band themselves seem aware of this. Critics have noted that “Buying a Cookie” **“had a point, but never quite managed to make it manifest.”** Indeed, it remains one of the lighter-weight songs on *69 Reasons*.

That’s not to say it’s without merit. There is something almost daring in placing a *casual* song amidst an album of extremes. It **amplifies the extremes by contrast**. Coming after the ferocity of “Xmas Party,” this track’s laid-back seduction can feel jarringly small-scale – like stumbling out of an underground rave into a quaint 1950s diner. Some fans actually love it for this reason: it’s the eye of the storm, a moment of catch-your-breath triviality before the album plunges into its final climactic pair of songs. Kathy’s performance here, while not as emotionally charged as elsewhere, shows off her versatility – she can underplay and still be engaging, delivering saucy lines with a half-smile that you can *hear* in her voice. There’s also a fun bit of instrumentation: Jeremy tosses in a **wet, reverb-drenched guitar solo** that genuinely sounds like it could be from a lost Beach Boys erotic B-side, if such a thing existed. It’s cheeky and clever.

Ultimately, *“Buying a Cookie”* might not stand among Commuted’s greatest achievements, but it has its place in the tapestry of *69 Reasons*. By **not taking itself too seriously**, it gives the listener a brief respite – a chance to grin and groove without being overwhelmed. In the scope of the album’s narrative, it’s like a minor dalliance, a one-night fling that, while pleasurable, doesn’t leave a lasting mark on the heart. The track flows by effortlessly and leaves a wisp of melody in its wake, but no deep scars. And considering how intense the upcoming songs are, that lightness might be a calculated mercy. As one review in 1992 wryly put it, *“Somehow, even a throwaway on this album ends up a little hypnotic. It’s a cool idea that never fully materialized – but hey, at least it’s a **tasty** little cookie.”*

**Track 18 – “Snap and Sizzle”**

With *“Snap and Sizzle,”* Commuted launches the album’s **legendary final four tracks** with an exclamation point. This song is a **tight, perfectly executed erotic rush** – three minutes and change of pure momentum that manages to be scandalous, radio-friendly, and artistically sharp all at once. Famously, “Snap and Sizzle” became a late-night radio staple after fans virtually demanded it; despite its explicit content, its sheer catchiness and disciplined structure forced even cautious programmers to give it a shot. Listening to the track, it’s easy to see why. The band locks into a **propulsive groove** immediately: Jeff’s bass and Jim’s drums are in sync here like a single throbbing heartbeat, pounding out a mid-tempo dance-rock pulse that grabs you by the hips. Bill layers a sleek synth arpeggio on top, giving the song a faint retro wave vibe, but at its core this is Commuted doing straightforward rock with absolute precision. After the experimental or loose structures of some earlier songs, “Snap and Sizzle” stands out for its **economy and focus**. Not a second is wasted; every beat counts.

Lyrically, the song zeroes in on an intimate scenario that is both titillating and strangely relatable: a young woman, alone in a bathroom stall, snapping a risqué photo and reminiscing about a recent sexual escapade. The verses are unapologetically explicit yet also vividly detailed in a way that tells a clear story. In Verse 1, Kathy sings from the first-person perspective: *“In the bathroom stall, just me and my phone, / Heart racing fast, feeling so alone. / Hitched up my shirt, pulled down my bra, / Caught a glimpse of myself, felt like a star.”*. In just a few lines, we know exactly what’s happening – she’s about to take a naughty selfie, and there’s a mix of nerves and exhibitionism (“heart racing” yet “felt like a star”). Verse 2 then fills in the backstory: *“Remember how you kissed, my thighs felt your heat, / Your scruff brushed my skin… / In this dim little stall, I’m ready for release.”*. We learn she’s thinking back to last weekend’s hookup, using those memories to fuel her current solo pleasure. The imagery of these lines – scruff on skin, thighs feeling heat – is tactile and real, invoking a memory that the listener can practically see and feel.

The chorus is where the song’s **naughty-but-fun** heart really shines: *“I’m lost in desire, in a moment so wild, / Thinking ’bout last weekend, yeah, I’m admired. / Your tongue on my skin, made my world spin, / Shivers down my spine, oh, let the games begin.”*. It’s catchy as hell – the melody surges upwards in “lost in desire” and then flips into a rhythmic spoken cadence for “yeah, I’m admired,” almost like a boast. The rhyme scheme is simple (wild/spine, spin/begin), but that simplicity makes it **hooky and memorable**. What’s remarkable is how Commuted manage to make a song about *female self-pleasure and digital exhibition* into something that a broad audience could embrace. Part of it is the tone: there’s an exuberance and empowerment to the lyrics. This isn’t a shameful secret; it’s depicted as thrilling and bold (*“Snap! Got the pic, thrill runs through me, / Electric vibes, oh, can you see?”* Kathy sings in the pre-chorus, turning the act of taking the photo into a triumphant moment). Another part is the sheer craft of the songwriting. “Snap and Sizzle” has a classic pop structure – verses, pre-chorus, chorus, bridge, all clocking in under 3:15 – which acts like a Trojan horse for its explicit themes. You could hum the chorus casually and someone might not even clock the sexual content at first, because it’s delivered so tunefully.

Musically, each band member contributes to the song’s tightness. Jim’s drumming is **machine-precise**, driving a four-on-the-floor beat with just enough syncopation to add groove but never derailing the momentum. Jeff’s bassline is unshowy but unyielding – a fat, warm throb that glues the track together. Jeremy and Gilley’s guitars are relatively restrained, used more for accent and texture (some crunchy power chords in the chorus, a flickering riff in the verses) than for solos or improvisation. This restraint across the board is key: after so many songs where Commuted indulged in stretching out or layering weird elements, here they **prove they can exercise discipline**. The result is that when Kathy’s vocals and the lyrics take center stage, they hit even harder. By the time the bridge arrives – a sultry section where Kathy half-whispers, *“Rubbing my breast while I touch my sweet spot, / Caught in this rhythm, I’m giving it all I got”* – the listener is completely under the song’s spell. It’s erotic, yes, but also *structured* and concise. And just when the tension peaks, they slam back into one final chorus, delivering that satisfying release of a pop payoff.

“Snap and Sizzle” was indeed controversial (how could a song with that subject not be?), but it became a triumph for Commuted, underscoring their ability to combine shock with skill. Critics who might have initially written them off for being explicit had to concede that this track was **“a masterclass in economy, tension, and release”** – both musically and lyrically. The song’s success in late-night radio syndication and club play also cemented Commuted’s reputation as a band that could toe the line between underground and mainstream. There’s a legendary anecdote of an L.A. radio station getting so many requests for “Snap and Sizzle” that they played it at 2 AM with a tongue-in-cheek disclaimer, “We blame the listeners for this one.” The fan-driven push actually propelled the single to flirt with the lower reaches of the charts, something practically unheard of for a track so frank. All told, *“Snap and Sizzle”* stands as one of *69 Reasons’* standout moments – **proof that Commuted could be explicit *and* excellent, scandalous *and* succinct**. In just over three minutes, they capture an intimate act of modern sexuality and make it feel universal – or at least universally thrilling – to anyone who’s ever kept a secret on their phone and a memory in their heart.

**Track 19 – “Scientific Method”**

At track 19, Commuted offers up one of their most mysterious and talked-about creations: *“Scientific Method.”* This song is a slinky, **psychedelic exploration of desire** that has, over time, morphed into a full-blown fan obsession. Musically, it’s rooted in **acid techno and psybient** influences. The beat is driven by electronic drum programming rather than Jim’s live kit – a steady, hypnotic pulse that gives the track a sensual, late-night club feel. Jeff’s bass is huge and **throbbing**, tuned more to make your ribcage shake than to follow a melody. Layered on top are Bill’s razor-sharp synth lines and a tapestry of atmospheric sounds: distant chiming tones, perhaps a sample of heavy breathing looped into the rhythm, and swirling pads that pan from ear to ear. Kathy’s vocals float through this soundscape like a phantom – airy, echo-laden, and uncharacteristically *restrained*. Rather than belting, she **coos and teases** her lines, often multi-tracking whispers behind her lead vocal to create an eerie, seductive effect. The entire track feels like a descent into a **fever dream on the dance floor**, where boundaries between bodies blur and reality wavers.

The lyrics of “Scientific Method” read as a playful yet intense **power game between two lovers**, with one crucial twist: the repeated name *“Sara.”* In the chorus and throughout, the narrator addresses “Sara” directly: *“Sara, you’re my sweetest thrill, / In your arms, time stands still.”*. This immediately set off alarm bells (or rather, delighted whispers) among fans because *Sara* is not a name we encounter elsewhere in Commuted’s songs or known history. Who is Sara? A real person? A character? A code name? The band members mischievously fanned the flames. In various interviews over the years, **each of four different members claimed to be the song’s protagonist** at one time or another. Kathy once joked that *she* was Sara, telling a magazine “I just spelled my name wrong on purpose” with a wink. Jeff cryptically suggested Sara was a composite of several people. Gilley claimed Sara was “just a metaphor for music itself.” And Bill, ever the wild card, insisted with a straight face that Sara was their old tour van’s nickname. It was clear the band was engaging in **deliberate misdirection – a bit of inside joke carried to absurd extremes**. This “Sara mystery” became a point of playful conspiracy among the fanbase, with theories ranging from the plausible (Sara was a past lover of Kathy’s) to the fanciful (Sara represents the drug MDMA, as in “serotonin”).

Setting aside the name game, the content of the song is deeply erotic and **full of power dynamics**. The chorus declares, *“Lust is a game we play, with every touch, you lead the way… I’ll lose control, I won’t look back, with every moment, we’re on the attack.”*. Unlike many earlier tracks that celebrate surrender or indulgence, “Scientific Method” frames lust as a **strategic game** – two partners testing each other, pushing and pulling. There’s talk of **experimentation** (fitting the song’s title): in the bridge, Kathy purrs, *“Mischief in your gaze, can you keep me quiet? / With every kiss, it’s a burning riot. / Do you really think you can stop my moans?”*. It’s almost competitive, a playful challenge between lovers who see who will break first. The verses are packed with sensuous detail: *“You’re moving further down, fingers trace the line, / A trail of heat ignites, oh, this moment feels divine”*. There’s that image of a finger tracing a line of the body and leaving metaphorical fire – simple but effective storytelling of a sexual moment in progress. The pre-chorus quotes flirty banter: *“‘Get ready, babe, I’ll get you back for that,’ / You laugh like thunder, it’s a playful chat.”*. This interplay of dialogue in the lyrics really makes the listener feel like a third party observing an intimate experiment, which again ties into that concept of a *method*: trials, reactions, observations.

The brilliance of “Scientific Method” is that it **never spells everything out**. It’s steamy, yes, but also ambiguous in a way that invites participation from the audience’s imagination. Who holds the power at any given moment in the song? The lyrics shift: sometimes *Sara* “leads the way,” other times the narrator is clearly pushing back (*“Only when it’s worth it,” I say with a sigh, in this wild connection, we’re both flying high* – indicating the narrator isn’t simply a passive subject). This fluid power dynamic, combined with the swirling, pulsating music, creates a sense of **sensory immersion**. You don’t just listen to this track; you *inhabit* it. In clubs – particularly LGBTQ+ clubs – “Scientific Method” became an underground anthem. It was embraced notably in lesbian nightclub scenes, as fans interpreted *Sara* as possibly another woman, and the song’s sultry back-and-forth as a celebration of Sapphic chemistry. Whether or not that was the band’s intent, they welcomed the song’s life beyond their original context. The more layers of meaning fans found, the more Commuted seemed to smile and encourage the speculation.

Sonically, it’s worth noting how *physical* this track feels. **The bass is literally visceral** – many have commented that on a good sound system you feel it in your gut before you hear it. The programmed drums are unwavering, almost like a heartbeat that won’t slow down. Bill’s synths create loops that **mimic the loops of desire** in the lyrics – patterns that repeat, build, and then shift at just the right moment to keep you hooked. Kathy’s vocal performance is deliberately **intoxicating and distant**; she withholds the full power of her voice to match the song’s elusive nature, making her occasional moans and the crescendo in the final chorus (where she finally belts “we’re on the attack!”) even more impactful when they arrive. All these elements contribute to what fans and critics alike have called a “fever dream” of a song.

In the pantheon of Commuted’s work, *“Scientific Method”* has earned its place as a **cult hit** and a demonstration of the band’s range. It’s a song that **thrives on ambiguity and atmosphere**. Coming right before the final track, it’s the perfect penultimate chapter: it doesn’t offer resolution – if anything, it deepens the mystery and tension one last time before the grand finale. It’s the late-night lab experiment in lust that primes you for the sunrise catharsis of what comes next. And fittingly for such an enigmatic piece, the band leaves us with no clear answers – only the pulsating echo of *Sara’s* name and a lingering question mark. As a fan once wrote on a forum with equal parts frustration and admiration, *“‘Scientific Method’ gives no answers, only sensations.”* That, ultimately, might be the point: in the realm of desire, sometimes the sensations **are** the answers.

**Track 20 – “Entry”**

If “Scientific Method” is a pulsing question mark, *“Entry”* is an **exclamation point of pure release**. From its opening seconds, the final penultimate track of *69 Reasons* (the last song before the epilogue-like closer) hits like a freight train of sensual energy. The band famously described “Entry” as *“what happens when Kathy and Jeff declare war on restraint”*, and that war is immediately apparent. This song is a **sonic assault** – a colossal fusion of **Acid Techno thump and Psybient swirl**, layered with live instruments to create something both primal and futuristic. The low end is nearly apocalyptic: Jeff’s bass doesn’t just groove, it **quakes**. Each note in his line seems to shake the ground; combined with Bill’s synth sub-bass, the track’s frequencies could rattle drywall screws loose (urban legend has it that a nightclub in Chicago had to reinforce their speakers because “Entry” kept threatening to blow them out when DJs spun it). Jim contributes programmed electronic drums here instead of his usual acoustic kit, which gives the rhythm a merciless, **machine-like precision** – the beat hits at a steady 130 BPM, four-on-the-floor kick, never letting up for a moment. Over this relentless pulse, Bill adds an inspired oddity: distorted *horn* stabs, echoing in the distance like some twisted mariachi caught in a wind tunnel. These brass sections, processed to hell, lend the track a cinematic, slightly eerie texture (one critic called it “the sound of Miles Davis jamming on Mars”). Gilley’s contribution is more felt than heard: he provides ghostly backing vocals, wordlessly ooh-ing and ahh-ing in echo behind Kathy, adding an ethereal layer atop the pounding foundation. Altogether, the arrangement is **dense and intense** – not chaotic (in fact the mix is very tight), but incredibly overwhelming by design. This is music meant to be *felt* in the chest and the pelvis, a song that practically commands the body to move.

And then there are the lyrics. “Entry” is, true to its name, about **penetration – in every sense**. It’s about literally the act of entering someone physically, but also about being *entered* into a new headspace where nothing exists except rhythm and pleasure. Unlike many earlier tracks that flirted with metaphor or clever wordplay, “Entry” strips away any veneer of subtlety and goes **full-on explicit**. Kathy wastes no time: the first chorus hits with her belting, *“Feel the pulse, racing wild, / Every breath you take, I’m beguiled. / Say my name, whisper low, / In the heat, let your semen flow. / Came so hard, lost in bliss, / Feel the pulse, oh, how could I miss?”*. Hearing those lines for the first time in 1992 was jaw-dropping – here was a rock song, not an underground tape, openly singing about *semen flowing* and *coming hard*. And it’s not hidden behind a manic scream or distortion; Kathy delivers that chorus melody with an almost anthemic clarity (and a harmony from Gilley on the second pass) that makes it **catchy and shocking at once**. This is arguably the moment Commuted completely obliterated the line of what was acceptable in rock lyricism at the time. Yet the genius is, by the time that chorus arrives, the listener has been so sonically pummeled and hypnotized by the groove that it feels less crude and more *cathartic*. It’s the moment of release that the music has been building toward, and it lands as a celebration rather than pure provocation.

The verses provide context and build-up. Verse 1 is all about **anticipation**: *“Thinking ’bout those nights, when your eyes sparkled bright, / Caught in your gaze, time just fades away. / You slid in close, feeling that electric dose, / Got me wriggling in my seat, heartbeat, can’t be discreet.”*. The rhyme scheme here (bright/away, dose/close, seat/discreet) isn’t strict; the lines flow in a way that mirrors the *wriggling* she describes – a little uncontrolled, bursting past neat AABB patterns. The language of “electric dose” ties into the music’s vibe: the synths and bass truly feel like an electric shock of lust. By Verse 2, things are physically in motion: *“I am arching my back, pushed right into my track, / Cried out so loud, neighbors ’bout to crowd. / Pulsing treasures, oh so tight, in the shadows, we ignited the night, / With each soft whimper, sweet release, lost in you, I found my peace.”*. This verse is practically a play-by-play of a climax, and it spares no detail – we can easily picture the scene of someone moaning loud enough for neighbors to notice, and that final tender line *“lost in you, I found my peace”* is actually *romantic* in a way, a soft grace note after all the rough physicality. Indeed, one could argue “Entry” is ultimately a **celebration of intimacy and connection**, not just raw sex. The analysis in the album’s liner notes pointed out that unlike other songs about secret or regretful trysts, “Entry” has **“no secrecy, tension, or regret… it is a full embrace of connection and pleasure.”**. The bridge of the song reinforces that warm afterglow: *“Memories wrapped in silk and sheets, every touch, every heartbeat. / Legs slipping down, surrender slow, in this moment, only we know. / With you, I chase the fire, every spurt takes me higher.”*. It’s erotic, yes, but also *tender* – the language of silk and shared secrets suggests a bond, a trust in this surrender that elevates it beyond just a physical act.

“Entry” is also notable for its **structure**. It doesn’t follow the typical verse-chorus-bridge pattern so much as it *loops and intensifies*. It’s almost a trance song: the chorus hits, then a verse, then chorus, then verse, building all the time, then multiple bridges and breakdowns that circle around the central bassline, and finally an extended outro that is pure rhythmic ecstasy. In that outro, Kathy repeatedly implores, *“So let’s dance in the night, feel that rhythm ignite… Feel the come, oh, feel the come… Together, let’s lose control.”*. By this point, her voice is nearly drowned in the mix – layered behind the relentless beat and Gilley’s ghostly wails – as if she’s subsumed into the sound itself. It gives the sensation that the **music has become the act**, the band has sonically manifested the orgasmic rush they’ve been singing about. And just as the listener approaches sensory overload, the song ends abruptly on a final beat, dropping you back into silence with heart pounding.

When *69 Reasons* was first reviewed, *“Entry”* was immediately highlighted as a **masterpiece of sonic and physical experience**. It’s the track where Commuted proved that sexually explicit art could also be high art in terms of production and impact. Many have called it the band’s **most overwhelming song**, and it’s hard to disagree – on a good sound system, it’s nearly hallucinatory. The song became a staple in underground clubs, particularly those that favored industrial and techno, bridging a gap between rock and electronic music scenes. Its influence can be seen in artists that followed, citing how Commuted made it okay to marry Eros and electricity so blatantly. Most importantly, within the album’s journey, “Entry” is the moment of **total surrender to sensation**. After all the twists and conflicts, all the “games” and power plays, this song closes that narrative arc by saying: *let go*. It’s not about power or shame or chase anymore; it’s simply about feeling, fully and without apology. In that sense, “Entry” is an ultimate resolution for *69 Reasons*’ themes – a final, explosive thesis statement that *pleasure is not a sin, it’s an experience to embrace wholeheartedly*. As the critical consensus later summarized: *“‘Entry’ isn’t just a song – it’s an experience, a collision of sound and desire that proves Commuted can shake you to your core.”* Few would contest that. Listening to “Entry” still feels like an awe-inspiring dare, even decades later.

**Track 21 – “Come Inside Me”**

Commuted’s debut album concludes with its **magnum opus**: *“Come Inside Me.”* This 11-minute, 36-second epic is not only the climax of the album’s narrative of desire, but arguably the **defining song of Commuted’s entire career**. It’s a track so massive in scope and execution that it almost feels like a world unto itself – one that the band and fans alike have returned to again and again as a source of legend. By combining the **grandiosity of a classic rock anthem** with the pulsating electronics of synthwave trance, “Come Inside Me” achieves a monumental balance between **discipline and excess**. In doing so, it provides the emotional and sonic resolution to *69 Reasons*’ journey through lust, power, and intimacy.

From the moment “Come Inside Me” begins, it’s clear we’re in for something special. A solitary synthesizer arpeggio fades in – ethereal and hypnotic – soon joined by a deep, unyielding bass throb (the so-called “Cincinnati bass” groove that Jeff Evans crafted in homage to the city’s underground club sound). Then, almost imperceptibly, Jim Brown’s drums enter: a blend of live drumming and drum machine programming locked in perfect sync. This dual-drum approach creates a **relentless, pulsating rhythm** that doesn’t let up for the entirety of the track – it’s like the heartbeat of the song, steady at around 120 BPM, anchoring everything even as the music around it swells and recedes. Over this foundation, Bill Davenport weaves layers of synthesizer lines that gradually build a towering wall of sound. He’s in full composer mode here, adding lush pads, sparkling top-line melodies, and occasionally descending chords that wash over the listener like waves.

One of the song’s masterstrokes is how it **evolves over its runtime**. It’s multi-part, almost suite-like. The first few minutes introduce the main themes – the central synth hook, the drum pattern, Kathy Espich and Jeremy “Gilley” Gilley’s vocal interplay – and then the song goes on a journey. Jeremy Evans contributes not one, not two, but **three guitar solos** spaced throughout the track. Each is different: the first (around the 3-minute mark) is bright and soaring, cutting through the mix like a ray of light; the second comes later, fierier and more emotive, practically crying out in ecstasy; the third (near the end) is all release – a blistering, euphoric lead that feels like the musical embodiment of climax. Crucially, these solos are *composed* into the song such that they lift it to new peaks each time, rather than derailing it. In between, the band plays with dynamics. They drop to quieter moments where only a minimalist beat and a single synth pulse remain, while Gilley and Kathy whisper sung lines back and forth, building sexual tension in audio form. Then, gradually, layers pile on again – a new harmony, a louder drum loop, perhaps a sample of a crowd’s distant cheer (faintly added in the background, giving it an arena-like expansiveness). The structure is meticulously arranged so that despite being nearly 12 minutes long, the track never feels stagnant. Instead, it **grows and morphs**, like an organism breathing, contracting and expanding in rhythmic cycles.

Lyrically, “Come Inside Me” delivers on its provocative title by framing the song as an open invitation to indulgence and unity. The perspective shifts – sometimes Kathy leads, sometimes Gilley answers – depicting a dialogue of seduction and consent that escalates into something almost spiritual. In the first verse, Kathy sings in a low, inviting tone: *“Underneath the dimmest light, / In a dare that feels so right, / You whisper soft, the night ignites, / Do you want that baby, hold me tight?”*. Immediately, we’re pulled into a scene of two lovers in the corner of a club (or perhaps a bedroom with one lamp on), on the cusp of a moment. The chorus then rises like a tidal wave of longing: *“So do you want, do you want that baby? / Feel the heat, feel the rush, oh, so crazy. / Do you want, do you want to keep on showing? / Let it flow, let it show, let’s keep on going.”*. The genius of this chorus lies in its **repetition and call-and-response quality**. Kathy and Gilley trade the line “do you want that baby?” back and forth – it’s both a genuine question and a rhetorical one, as if each is urging the other on. The rhyme of “showing/going” and the internal near-rhyme of “flow/show” create a loop in the lyrics that mirrors the loop of the music, reinforcing the trance-like state the song induces. This chorus recurs multiple times throughout the track, each time gaining intensity (additional harmonies, an octave jump, more instrumental backing) as the lovers’ urgency increases.

What truly sets “Come Inside Me” apart is its **emotional arc**. Despite the explicitness of the title and some lyrics (the bridge unabashedly includes Kathy pleading *“Come up here, let me feel you inside me”* – the very line MTV censors would have winced at), the song manages to transcend the purely sexual and hit something tender and even **transcendent**. There are moments in the later verses and bridges where Kathy’s lyrics blur the line between the physical and the emotional. For instance, in Verse 2 she coos, *“Your warmth inside, I crave it more, / With every heartbeat, I adore”*, which is as much about love and vulnerability as it is about lust. The swirling duet between her and Gilley near the finale – where lines overlap, like *“And I feel it deep down low, a tightening grip, let it go”* – feels like two souls in the throes of something overwhelming, each supporting and amplifying the other. By the time we reach the **massive outro**, the lyrics strip down to a mantra: *“A tightening grip, let it go… And I feel it deep down low… let it go…”* repeated with almost religious fervor. This outro doesn’t so much end as *evaporate* – the song fades into a final sustained chord and echo, as if ascending. In that sense, “Come Inside Me” doesn’t conclude with a hard stop; it leaves us in a state of lingering euphoria, the sound of a final moan or sigh practically implied in the vanishing reverb.

Historically, the impact of *“Come Inside Me”* was enormous. Though too long (and initially too explicit) for traditional radio, the song took on a life of its own in live performances and eventually via a spectacular concert-film-style music video that even MTV couldn’t resist. It became the band’s **signature encore**; fans would chant for it until Commuted relented, and entire venues would transform into what one reviewer called “a writhing, communal mass of rhythm” during its performance. Kathy’s notorious quip to MTV’s Kurt Loder – when he asked why the song had to be over 11 minutes, she smirked, *“That’s how long he lasted.”* – only fueled the song’s legend. It positioned Commuted not just as shock-rockers, but as a band who could back up their audacity with epic, era-defining art. Within *69 Reasons*, “Come Inside Me” serves as the grand summation of all the themes: it’s got the bold carnality, the power play (there’s something quietly powerful in the woman commanding “come inside me”), the emotional honesty, and the musical adventurousness all in one. It is, to put it simply, the **ultimate Commuted song** – **their greatest indulgence and their greatest triumph** rolled into one.

As the final notes of *“Come Inside Me”* hang in the air, the listener is left awash in the album’s afterglow. The track – and the album – doesn’t end with a neat resolution or moral. Instead, it leaves us with a feeling: a complex blend of exhaustion, satisfaction, and a touch of awe. Commuted dared to push every envelope on *69 Reasons*, and in this closing epic they push the biggest one of all: showing that within unbridled passion can lie something **profoundly human and unifying**. In the world of this album, sex isn’t just an act or a shock factor – it’s a language of emotion, a source of power, a means of connection, and ultimately, as *“Come Inside Me”* demonstrates, **a form of transcendence**. It’s a journey that began in scandal and ended in something close to the sublime. And decades later, it’s still hard to think of many album finales as fitting or as fearless.

---

**Conclusion:** The second half of *69 Reasons* (tracks 11–21) takes the daring foundations laid by the album’s opening and elevates them into a riveting climax. Across these songs, Commuted displays astonishing range – from the slow-burn erotica of “I’ll Own Your Moment” to the surreal darkness of “Next to the Ice Cream Shop,” from the frenetic transgressions of “Xmas Party” to the meticulous pop of “Snap and Sizzle,” all culminating in the one-two knockout of “Entry” and “Come Inside Me.” The emotional arc is just as pronounced: what starts as playful or chaotic gradually focuses on an earnest pursuit of **connection and catharsis**. By album’s end, Kathy Espich and her bandmates have guided us through secret memories, public sins, cryptic rituals, and ecstatic unions. It’s a journey that is by turns intellectual and visceral, tongue-in-cheek and deadly serious.

If the first ten tracks of *69 Reasons* introduced Commuted as provocateurs with talent to spare, tracks 11–21 cement that legacy and then some – revealing that beneath the controversy lay artistry, and beneath the shock lay soul. The album’s latter half is, in many ways, its emotional core and climax, pushing each band member to their peak performance. Critics often note how these final songs showcase **Commuted’s unity as a group**: Jim Brown’s rhythmic daring, Jeff Evans’ seismic low-end, Bill Davenport’s textural genius, Jeremy Evans’ expressive guitar voice, Gilley ’s harmonic flair, and of course Kathy Espich’s fearless vocals and lyrics all lock together in service of the bigger picture.

In retrospect, *69 Reasons*’ closing tracks transformed what could have been a gimmicky “dirty rock” record into something timeless – a concept album on the human condition seen through lust-tinted glasses. By the time *“Come Inside Me”* fades out, it’s clear that the band has not just been singing about sex. They’ve been singing about **freedom, vulnerability, identity, power, shame, joy, and transcendence**. And they’ve done it in a way that’s both unabashedly literate and viscerally musical. The result is an album that doesn’t just serve up shock for shock’s sake; it takes the listener on a legitimately affecting trip, one that scholars and fans still dissect and celebrate.

The emotional and thematic climax achieved in tracks 11–21 elevates *69 Reasons* from a notorious debut to a **legitimate masterpiece** of its era. In these songs, Commuted found the courage to be utterly unapologetic and, in doing so, struck a universal chord. It’s the sound of a band throwing the final gauntlet at prudishness and convention and doing so with such intelligence and passion that the world couldn’t help but submit. Few albums stick their landing as boldly as *69 Reasons* does – and that fearless finale is a big part of why Commuted’s debut continues to be revered as a landmark in rock history, a quarter-century and counting.
