# **“October 1990”**  

The sun set over Cincinnati like it was being lowered by hand.

[[4.4 JIM BROWN character bible|Jim]] leaned on the rail in Riverfront Stadium, watching the Reds take infield like they were getting away with something. His dad stood next to him, sipping from a plastic cup of fizzy Pepsi, eyes narrowed like he was trying to memorize the shape of the outfield.

“Looks different than I remember,” his dad said.

“Everything does,” Jim said.

**October 16, 1990 Game 1 of the World Series**

The Reds were hosting the mighty Oakland A’s, and nobody, not even the guys wearing red in the stands, really believed it was happening.

But it was. And Jim was here. Eighteen. One class away from dropping out of Wright State. Living at home. Flipping burgers at Dairy Freeze. Playing drums in a band that didn’t exist yet on paper.

He was also thinking seriously about quitting college. Which is why they were here.  Jim’s dad had been a single parent since Jim was ten. A mechanic. A quiet man with shoulders like stone pillars and a voice that only got loud for Marvin Gaye records and Bengals games.

He didn’t talk much about feelings.

But when Jim had told him, *“I think I might drop out,”* he’d said: “Alright. Let’s talk about it at the ballpark.”

The stadium was half full an hour before first pitch. The whole place had this weird, dreamlike shimmer. The kind of energy you only get when **no one expected to be there**.

Jim looked around. Men everywhere. Guys in flannel, guys in uniforms, guys in red foam hats, guys in Starter jackets. Young, old, dads, sons, grandfathers. The concourse was packed with men who all looked like him — or might have two years ago. Same jawlines. Same high school haircuts. Same skinny necks in ROTC collars.

“I know half these guys,” Jim said.

His dad nodded. “Bet some of them know you too.”

Jim looked at his hands. They were still calloused from work. From sticks. From holding too many things too tight. They took their seats behind third base.

The anthem played. Jim stood, hat in hand. He scanned the players, the crowd, the box seats, the sky. He thought about **Saudi Arabia**.  Operation Desert Shield had started in August. The draft wasn’t happening, not yet,  but hundreds of thousands had already been deployed. A few of Jim’s classmates were on their way to Riyadh by the end of the month.

He could name names. Chris Blanton. Nick Frye. Kyle Wallace. Guys who’d barely passed Algebra, who got high in gym class, who once duct-taped Jim’s locker shut just to hear him swear. Now they were **going to war**.

And Jim was sitting in a baseball stadium thinking about majoring in nothing and maybe devoting his life to beating on things. He felt a little sick.

The game started.

And then, like a prayer getting answered too fast to trust, **Eric Davis hit a two-run bomb off Dave Stewart** that soared into the night like it was trying to leave Earth entirely.

Jim and his dad exploded.  **“OH MY GOD!”**  

His dad was on his feet, nearly spilling his drink on the guy in front of them.  Jim was screaming, hugging strangers, shaking his fists at the sky like he’d just been vindicated by divine baseball.

**“DID YOU SEE THAT?!”**  “He killed it! He **killed** that ball!”

It was the kind of hit that **didn’t just score runs** — it **reset belief**.  Eric Davis wasn’t supposed to homer off Stewart. The Reds weren’t supposed to be in the Series. Jim wasn’t supposed to feel this alive in a moment where everything felt like it was breaking apart.

But here they were.  And it was **only the first inning.**

---

Top of the fourth. Still 2–0.  The stadium buzzed. Everyone was either shouting or standing or reliving that home run in their minds.

Jim leaned in.  “I want to do it.”

His dad didn’t look at him. He watched the pitch.  “Do what?”

“Quit school. Play full-time.”

Another pitch. Foul ball.  Jim’s heart raced. He tried again.

“I’m not gonna be a star, probably. I’m not even sure we’re good. But I feel… **better** behind the kit than I do in a classroom. I don’t even like school. I don’t even like me when I’m in school.”

His dad nodded, still watching. A long silence. Then he said: “You know I had a scholarship, right?”

Jim blinked. “You did?”

“Football. Didn’t take it. Your mom got pregnant. I stayed here. Took a job. Never really regretted it.”

Jim didn’t know what to say.

“I guess what I’m saying is,” his dad said, “if you’re gonna throw something away… make sure it’s for the right reason. Not because it’s easier. But because it’s *yours.*”

Another Rijo pitch. Strikeout. Cheers.

His dad looked at him.  “You sure?”

Jim looked back. Clear-eyed. Unshaking.  “Absolutely,” he said. “Trust me.”

His dad reached into his coat, pulled out a flask, and poured a splash into Jim’s Coke.
“Then we drink to bad decisions.”

They clinked plastic cups.

The Reds won 7–0.  They swept the A’s in four. The nation couldn’t believe it. But Jim could. He’d seen stranger things that night.  He’d seen a man who never said much say exactly what he needed to. He’d seen the world split open, war on one side, noise on the other, and somehow choose **rhythm**. And that’s when Jim knew. He was going to be a drumme
