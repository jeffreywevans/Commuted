# **Jeremy “Gilley” Gilley: The Frontman, the Fog Machine, the Hormone Earthquake**

The name thing was functional at first. There were two Jeremys in the band. One had a severe buzzcut and spoke in chords. The other was 6’4”, had hair like a cartoon Viking, and was already trying to charm teachers out of giving him detentions. “Gilley” wasn’t a nickname, it was a survival mechanism. You couldn’t have two Jeremys in one van. The universe wouldn’t allow it.

And besides, the name *fit*. Gilley didn’t just act like a frontman—he *radiated* it. His guitar work was fine, if a little diffident, but when he stepped up to the mic, something clicked. He had the kind of animal magnetism that made bouncers nervous and booking agents giddy. If you gave him five minutes with a reporter, you got a feature story. If you gave him ten minutes with your girlfriend, you got a breakup.

Still, there are questions. Mostly whispered ones. Such as, is Gilley the horniest member of Commuted?

Jeff Evans, never one for public emotion, once muttered into his coffee during a radio interview:
> “It’s unbelievable. I’ve seen, with my own eyes, at least four different women come in and out of his hotel room in the same *afternoon*. One had a guitar. One had a flute. One had a goddamn PowerPoint. I don’t even know what was happening anymore.”

Kathy Espich, when pressed by a journalist, blinked once and said:
> “No comment.”  Then she smiled so faintly it made the room go cold.

Jeremy Evans—the *other* Jeremy—has only ever responded to the topic by saying,
> “I’m the real Jeremy,”  with the same tone one might use to contest a parking ticket.

But the best line belongs to Bill Davenport. When asked if Gilley had ever stayed over at his apartment, Bill stared for a long beat and replied:
> “Jeremy Gilley is a lifelong friend. And that’s why I will never, under any circumstances, allow him to use my bathroom.”

And the thing is—they’re all right. Gilley wasn’t just sexually active; he was *socially radioactive*. It wasn’t about conquest. It was about momentum. Gilley didn’t chase women—he just existed in a state of perpetual flirtation, like it was a natural byproduct of respiration.

But even this undersells him. Because while his libido was legendary, his emotional intelligence was… complicated.

He could hold court in a greenroom, disarm a label exec in under sixty seconds, and calm Kathy down mid-meltdown by complimenting her pedal configuration like it was a poem. And somehow, you never knew if it was genuine or strategic.

That was Gilley’s true power: ambiguity. He could be charming, loyal, absurd, infuriating—and it would all come wrapped in the same grin.

By 1998, he was still doing his thing. Touring. Flashing smiles. Leaving a trail of keycards and scented candles in his wake. But there were rumors that even he was getting tired.
Not of women. Of always needing to be *that guy*. 

So is he the horniest?  Probably. But only because he’s the only one who *let it become a job*. The others kept it private.
