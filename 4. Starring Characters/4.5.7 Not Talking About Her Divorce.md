# **Not Talking About Her Divorce**

---

## **Implosion and Divorce**

There are things people still don’t talk about. Even now.

Kathy Espich’s mother has never spoken of the marriage. Not once. Not even to correct rumors. Not even to clarify dates. She keeps a single wedding photo in a box with no label, stored somewhere behind the tax records and her church program clippings. Ask her about it, and she’ll change the subject. Ask twice, and she’ll ask you to leave.

That’s how bad it was.

Because what happens when you put a Hollywood actor and a budding rock star together and ask them to share a mortgage and a bed and a tax bracket? You get blood in the sink. You get PR statements drafted on hotel stationery. You get voicemail messages you wouldn’t play in front of a priest. You get *drama*—the kind people pay to watch onscreen but nobody survives off it.

Kathy thought it would work. Of course she did. She was twenty-five and trying to outrun herself. The band was splintering. Gilley was off chasing mirrors, [[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff]] had turned into a cipher, and even [[4.1 BILL DAVENPORT character bible|Bill]] was getting cryptic. So she did what smart, spiraling people sometimes do: she mistook motion for escape.

He was famous. He was kind. He said all the right things with his hands. She believed him.

They married in a vineyard in Napa. No press, no band, no parents. Just a few publicists and a justice of the peace who moonlit as a screenwriter. That should’ve been the omen.

It lasted six months.

The first three were spent making love, making scenes, and making headlines. The next two were spent in different cities. The last one was spent emailing their assistants about who got the espresso machine.

Kathy filed first. She didn’t say much. Just checked the boxes, signed the bottom, and disappeared into a songwriting sabbatical she never finished.

The band didn’t know what to do with her.  Jeff tried to keep rehearsals normal, which meant they were silent and brittle. [[4.4 JIM BROWN character bible|JIM]] baked her banana bread, which she left on the bus floor. Gilley wrote her a note that included the phrase *“you’ll bounce back stronger”* and she didn’t speak to him for eight weeks.

By the end of ’94, Kathy was still technically in the band. But her eyes were always someplace else—past the lights, past the crowd, somewhere behind herself.  Regret doesn’t last forever. It just lasts until you reach the lawyers.  But the silence?  The silence sticks around.
