### **Jeff Evans: The Programmed Metronome**

If the Northwest Ordinance had a favorite musician, it would be Jeff Evans.

He is, without exaggeration, the embodiment of that document’s logic—its sacred geometry of surveyed plots and moral stipulations. Sectioned. Structured. Sober. Land was to be rational. Education was to be guaranteed. Slavery was to be banned. Likewise, Jeff’s basslines were never improvised. His gaze never wandered. And his thoughts? Always calculated for maximum clarity, minimum exposure.

[[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff]] didn’t speak unless he needed to. Not because he was shy, exactly, but because language was imprecise. He preferred systems. Routines. He organized his notebooks chronologically *and* by mood. He refused to drink pop unless it was ice cold. Once, on tour, he re-tuned a borrowed bass because the previous player had set it up “according to feel.” Jeff did not believe in feel. He believed in reproducibility.

In high school, he was the only student who cited historical land ordinances during a debate on zoning laws. That wasn’t a phase. It was a worldview.

He joined [[A Garage Story]] not to make noise, but to make sense of it. His basslines didn’t drive the band—they stabilized it. While [[4.2.1 Jeremy Gilley (Gilley) Quick and Dirty]] riffed, [[4.5 KATHY ESPICH character bible|Kathy Espich]] howled, and Jim forgot what time signature they were in, Jeff was the one who snapped everything back into place. He was the silent hand on the grid, saying: *No. This goes here. That stays there. This is how we don’t fall apart.*

It would be romantic to say he was the band’s conscience. But no—Jeff was its scaffolding. Uncelebrated but essential. You could tear down everything else, and he’d still be there, blinking behind his wire-rimmed glasses, asking if the pedal board was alphabetized.

He was also, heartbreakingly, the one who *couldn’t adapt*. As the band swerved toward chaos—Kathy’s volatility, Gilley’s theater, [[4.6.1 Jeremy Evans Quick and Dirty]]’s perfectionism—Jeff remained painfully committed to the idea that order could still save them. That if you just played the right notes, in the right order, with the right tone, the world might briefly make sense again.

When Kathy threatened to go solo in ’96, Jeff didn’t protest. He didn’t beg. He just quietly rewrote all the setlists to remove her verses—just in case. He told himself it was preparation, not pessimism. And when she didn’t leave, he didn’t celebrate. He updated the setlists again, without comment. As if admitting relief would destabilize the next show.

By 1998, Jeff Evans was still the first one to tune up and the last one to speak. He wore the same three shirts on rotation. He calibrated tone curves like other men adjusted their belts. He had nothing left but precision, and it was still enough.

The Northwest Ordinance promised order, progress, and moral geometry. Jeff believed it.

But the problem with surveyed land is that it doesn’t bend when the river moves.
