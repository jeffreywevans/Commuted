# **THE WEIGHT OF THE DEAL**

---

## **INT. GOTHENBURG LANDVETTER AIRPORT – GENERAL AVIATION TERMINAL – LATE AFTERNOON – DECEMBER 17, 2023**

Scandinavian winter spits in their faces as Jeff and Jeremy Evans drag themselves down the steps of the NetJet, boots crunching frost. The sky is a sullen, purple bruise, the last of the sun circling the drain before 4 PM. The general aviation terminal? Too clean, too Swedish—glass, brushed steel, and zero soul, the kind of place you could stage a Bond villain's divorce hearing.

Jeff’s bones protest as he stretches, vertebrae popping like bubble wrap, every movement a slow-motion confession of how far he’s flown from Cincinnati. He rubs his eyes, blinking hard, fighting the narcotic hangover of sleep in first class. Next to him, Jeremy’s all voltage—snapping the iPad shut with a click as clean and final as a snare shot in a silent cathedral. He’s alert, predatory, scanning every suit and steward by the door, mind whirring at three deadlines per minute.

And there, in the eye of this well-dressed storm—**Tim Wright**. Not just present: *arranged*. The overcoat—tailored, sharp, dark enough to look accidental. Black turtleneck, polished boots that cost more than Jeremy’s first Telecaster. The VR glasses—narrow, surgical, backlit by a ghostly pulse. The effect is somewhere between “cyberpunk CEO” and “guy who knows how to launder money in three time zones.”

Jeff and Jeremy approach, footsteps echoing in the cathedral hush of moneyed travelers and bored flight crews. Tim, as always, is half-present: a flicker of internal code behind his gaze, eyes on a world they can’t see yet. Then—snap. He slides off the VR glasses with a flourish, smile blooming wide enough to hide most of the secrets he’s already stolen.

**TIM**  
Brothers! My brothers in arms! You made it!

He’s on Jeff first, in with the bear hug—muscles wired under tailored wool, cologne just expensive enough to read as apology.

**TIM**  
Jeff, you look like you just woke up from a cryogenic freeze. Good flight?

**JEFF** (still yawning, more animal than man)  
Slept the whole way. Best way to beat jet lag.

Tim turns, palm slapping Jeremy’s shoulder, eyes laser-bright, voice dropping an octave for the sibling rivalry.

**TIM**  
Jeremy, always working. You never stop, do you?

**JEREMY** (the half-smile that never touches his eyes)  
Deadlines, Tim. You know how it is.

Tim gives him a wink, sly as a blackjack dealer.

**TIM**  
Oh, I know more than you think. Especially about *certain* deadlines.

Jeremy’s jaw ticks, eyes flicker—he’s clocked the dig, storing it away. He flicks a glance at Jeff, who’s too busy fighting gravity to care.

Tim ignores the little current of suspicion—too professional, too caffeinated to let the moment linger.

**TIM** (voice turning up, volume set to “public address announcement”)  
Anyway! Enough chit-chat. I’m actually thrilled you both made it on such short notice. I know your calendars are a viper’s nest. Especially after that little Norwegian incident a few months ago.

He waves them toward the doors like a gameshow host, suit unruffled. They follow, moving past the sea of chrome and Scandinavian vowels into the night.

**TIM**  
We have a very important meeting. A very… *special* meeting. And trust me, you don’t want to be late.

The doors whisper open—Gothenburg’s city lights pulsing, winter air punching them in the lungs. Parked across three zones, the *Cybertruck*: matte black, proud, illegal as hell. Jeff’s face—equal parts kid at Christmas and man denied sex for weeks.

**JEFF**  
You’ve got to be kidding me.

Jeremy, arms folded, dry as January gin, watches Jeff drool.

**JEREMY**  
What is it? Another one of your midlife crisis vehicles?

**JEFF** (not looking away, hands on the metal like he might lick it)  
This isn’t a crisis, Jeremy. This is… *art*. The future. This is…

**TIM** (waving them in, pure salesman)  
Hop in, gentlemen. Let’s roll.

Jeff climbs in, touching the dash like it might make him younger. Jeremy sighs, follows, sliding into the back seat with a practiced resignation.

**JEFF**  
I’ve wanted one of these for ages. But my daughters? They think it’s an abomination.  
“Dad, you’ll look like a space dork.” That’s a direct quote.

**TIM**  
So? What’s stopping you? You’re *Jeff Evans*. You can do whatever you want.

**JEFF**  
Yeah, but I’m a dad. I have to care what they think.  Imagine dropping them at college in this thing.  They’d change their last name.

**TIM**  
But think about it! The utility! The *presence*! This thing could outlive the apocalypse.

Tim drops the Cybertruck into gear, gliding out into the night traffic like he’s on rails.

**JEFF**  
I know, right? Practically bulletproof.  I was even talking to Elon the other day about redesigning the windshield wipers—honestly, they’re the weak spot.

For half a second, Tim forgets who he’s dealing with.

**TIM**  
You talk to *Elon*? As in, Musk? *That* Elon?

**JEFF**  
Yeah. Well. He’s busy these days, but yeah, we chat.

Tim shakes his head, a smirk tugging at the edge.

**TIM**  
Right. Sure. You’re practically neighbors on Mars—or is it just the nearest redheaded pussy?

It’s a direct hit. Jeff and Jeremy collapse in laughter—old, cruel, necessary, the kind that empties your lungs and scrapes out the last of the jet lag. The whole car smells like expensive leather, resentment, and the shared joke that Jeff will never quite outlive.

**JEFF** (wiping his eyes, finally, the grin back)  
You know me too well, Tim. You know me too fucking well.

**TIM**  
So, Jeremy… how long’s it been now?

Jeremy cracks his knuckles, jaw set like a guy refusing the first drink at a wedding.

**JEREMY**  
January 4th, 2021. Best decision I ever made. Haven’t felt this good in more than a decade.

He pats his belly—flat now, no more flab hiding the anxiety. His hair’s gone the way of his worst habits: thinned out, but his beard’s thick, salt-and-pepper, makes him look like a mountain climber or someone finally okay with how he’s aging.

**JEREMY**  
I’m running five miles a day now. One hundred and thirty-nine days straight. No skips, no cheats.

He whips out his iPad, thumb flying. Shows them a fitness app—GPS trails, split times, even resting heart rate, as if he’s daring them to call him a liar.

**TIM**  
One hundred and thirty-nine days? Jesus, Jeremy.

**JEREMY**  
Might even run my third marathon next year.  
(half-grin, self-deprecating, but proud)

Tim raises his eyebrow, but it’s genuine—maybe even envy.

**TIM**  
Marathons now? That’s… fuck, man. That’s commitment.

**JEREMY**  
It’s…  
(long beat, honesty cutting through)  
It’s a substitute. I guess.

**TIM**  
For the booze, yeah?

**JEREMY**  (Nods, doesn’t look away)  
Yeah. Pretty much.

Tim’s face does this little shiver—he’s got his own history, scars under that turtleneck, the kind of man who only got out by going under the knife.  Former Navy corpsman, stomach cut and re-routed, still can’t run a mile without picturing his old hospital wristband.

**TIM**  
I wouldn’t run unless I was being chased by a reindeer.

Deadpan. Jeff snorts, Jeremy gives him side-eye: is he joking? Probably not. The truck hums on through the old city, none of them speaking for a minute.

Tim swings the Cybertruck into a parking lot, tires crunching ice. The sign’s clear: **EMERGENCY VEHICLES ONLY**. The lot is full—cops, paramedics, even a fire engine idling, blue lights strobing the dusk.  Swedish voices cut the air, too fast for translation.

**JEFF**  
What is this place—emergency services convention?

**JEREMY**  
I don’t know a single badge. And my Swedish is limited to IKEA furniture.

**TIM**  (grinning like a wolf)  
You don’t even know how to say “Do you want to fuck?” in Swedish, Jeff? Embarrassing.

**JEFF**  
Only Gilley would have bothered to learn that. And probably used it in the wrong context.

That hits, the banter drops. For a beat, it’s just the hum of idling engines and old ghosts.

Tim’s whole body language shifts—shoulders back, eyes on business. Jeff and Jeremy straighten, the temperature in the car dropping to “board meeting.”

**TIM**  
All right. Let’s get down to it.

He turns, voice clipped, head cocked so they know this isn’t about the Cybertruck anymore.

**TIM**  
I’ve talked to Bill. I’ve talked to Jim.  And both of you, one-on-one, these past weeks.  I think I’ve got the situation mapped better than your old tour manager’s liver.

**JEFF**  
That wouldn’t be hard.  
(smiles, then serious)  
But you always do, Tim. You’re a human search engine with a bad attitude.

**TIM**  (smiling, but not amused)  
Well, here’s my conclusion: you’re all hitting the wall on Gilley’s replacement.

**JEFF**  
That’s putting it mild. Every time we sit down, we end up in the same cul-de-sac. Nobody wants to be the asshole who says the wrong name.

**JEREMY**  
It’s been… rigorous. Honest. I’m actually proud—  
(voice cracking a bit, then recovering)  
It could have been a fucking bloodbath, but everyone’s acted like adults.  For once.

**TIM**  
That’s…  
(pauses, as if trying on the word)  
That’s growth. I’m impressed.

**JEFF**  
I just wish it was 2004. Back when our biggest problem was what kind of whiskey to keep on the rider.  Now? Water under the bridge. Mostly.

He lets out a long, tired sigh. The kind that comes from forgiving people who never apologized.

**JEFF**  
Lot of old—  
(he searches for the word)  
Acrimony has cleared up these last six weeks.  Maybe it’ll even stick.

Tim narrows his eyes, reading Jeff, then aiming the knife.

**TIM**  
What kind of changes are we talking about?  You finally giving up your… extracurriculars?

Jeremy cuts in, defensive, trying to protect and distance at the same time.

**JEREMY**  
He’s…  
(voice forced casual, not fooling anyone)  
He’s being more strategic.  Not chasing every woman who glances his way.  Especially not our friends.  
(eyes dart to Jeff—there’s history, and it stings)

**JEFF**  (shrugs, can’t fake it)  
I’m not a monk. But it’s not 2011 either.

Tim studies him, tone tightrope-walking between joke and concern.

**TIM**  
So zipper control’s… what, trending upward?

The words just *hang*, not funny, not quite friendly.

Tim clicks his key fob—some secret signal—and a door in the side of the building buzzes open. Jeremy’s still processing, Jeff’s gone quiet.

A middle-aged Swedish officer walks up, face stiff as a Protestant funeral. Nods to Tim—*he’s expected*.

**TIM** (to Jeremy, not missing a beat)  
Slide over, Slick. We’ve got company.

Then, sotto voce to Jeff, just loud enough for Jeremy to hear if he wants—

**TIM**  
Buddy of mine, same problem as you. Solved it.  Twice.  But that’s a story for tequila and a very forgiving senorita.

---

Jeremy, still caught off-balance by the whole secret-agent shuffle, scoots across the backseat. There’s a hydraulic *hiss*—the rear door of the Cybertruck lifts like a spaceship ramp. And sitting inside, framed in Swedish dusk and cold LED—**DAVE GROHL**.

He’s slouched in the back row, legs spread, classic rock icon posture, leather jacket already soft from thousands of green rooms and worse. Smile like he owns the room, but soft around the eyes, welcoming, a little tired. If Tarantino were shooting it: slow push-in, classic rock needle drop, everyone holding their breath.

**JEFF** (breaking into a real, wide, open-mouthed smile)  
Dave! Holy shit!

**JEREMY** (can’t help it, pure delight, almost laughing)  
Wow! Dave… what the fuck are you doing in Gothenburg?

**DAVE** (rises, hand out, grin morphing to a sly smirk)  
Jeff! Jeremy! Damn good to see you guys. It’s been what—three years? Last time was that MTV thing, right? The one where Kanye tried to…  
(he waggles his eyebrows, lets it hang; everyone knows the story)

**JEFF** (shakes hands, not letting go for a beat)  
Yeah. That’s right. Jesus, man, this is wild. Good to see you.

Jeremy leans in for the handshake, grip tight, real.

**JEREMY**  
Yeah, man. Too long.

**TIM** (hanging back, arms folded, cat-with-the-canary smile)  
Gentlemen, I trust you’re reacquainted.

**DAVE** (tilting his chin at Tim, voice mock-conspiratorial)  
This guy here—don’t ever play cards with him. He’s a fixer. Told me some old friends were coming to town and figured, fuck it, I’m in. Been too long.

**JEFF** (eyes darting between Tim and Dave)  
You brought *Dave Grohl* to Gothenburg, Tim? What, did you promise him an Ikea kitchen and a box of Cubans?

**DAVE** (shrugging, expression settling, serious but never stiff)  
Nah, man. I’m here for a reason. Local foundation, raising cash for families of the ones who died in that fire last month. Four gone, just like that.

(pauses, the humor gone; real grief in the air)

**DAVE**  
Firefighters, EMTs, you know. Actual heroes. People who risk everything, every shift, and sometimes don’t come home.  Figure if my ugly mug can help raise a little more, it’s the least I can do.  I got some history here, too. This city’s saved my ass before.

He glances out at the swirl of sirens and Swedish, then back at the Evans brothers.

**DAVE**  
It’s been an honor, honestly. You see how tight these people are. They deserve more than just a few checks and speeches.

It lands. Jeff, Jeremy, Tim—all three quiet, nodding. For a second, the rockstar act slips: it’s just three men who know exactly what it means to lose people for real.

Dave leans back, boots up, the plush seat creaking, old scars coming out in the stillness. The steady whirr of ambulance engines and cold wind outside is the only soundtrack.

**DAVE**  
So—Tim says you’re looking to fill Gilley’s shoes.

He lets that hang, like the punchline to a joke nobody’s ready to tell.

**DAVE**  
I get it. I do. You’re a band, you’re not dead, you want to keep making noise. But let me save you a year of heartbreak— You don’t *replace* Gilley. You can’t. You just fucking can’t.

His eyes are laser-sharp, suddenly, the mask off.

**DAVE**  
You don’t replace anyone. Especially not a legend.  Not when it’s someone like that.  What you do—you find somebody with their *own* fire. Their own freak thing. Doesn’t have to look like him, sound like him, fuck, doesn’t even have to like the same pizza.  You don’t clone the dead. You start something new.

He leans forward, elbows on knees, eyes on the floor, talking like he’s confessing.

**DAVE**  
After Kurt—  
(pauses, struggles, then keeps going)  
It was the apocalypse. Nirvana… that was everything. It wasn’t a band, it was oxygen.  
Then *gone*. Just *gone*.  
(snap of the fingers, the sound rings off steel and leather)

**DAVE**  
There’s no filling that hole. Not possible.  How do you replace a fucking lightning bolt?  
You don’t. You find somewhere to hide when the sky splits open.

He looks out the window, lights reflected in his eyes, lost in the swirl of memory.

**DAVE**  
I drifted. Didn’t want to make music. Didn’t even know if I *could* without feeling like a traitor.  
But eventually…  
(long breath)  
I realized if I didn’t start again, it’d be even worse.  You can’t live forever in the graveyard.  
So I started Foo Fighters.  It wasn’t Nirvana, never tried to be.  It was just what was left. Me, the music, and all the parts that didn’t die with Kurt.

He’s quiet now, then looks up—face tight, honest, open.

**DAVE**  
It was hell, starting over.  But it was better than being a statue.  And Kurt… I think he’d rather see me fucking up something new than doing a cover band of his old self.

He softens.

**DAVE**  
So that’s what you have to do. Don’t find another Gilley.  Find the *next* thing.  
Someone who brings something you never knew you needed.

He’s looking at both of them, reading their faces for bullshit, but Jeff’s just nodding, eyes somewhere far off. Jeff, the only man alive who still says out loud that Kurt Cobain was “overrated”—but now, he’s not saying a word.

**JEFF**  
I… I hear you, Dave.

**JEREMY** (quiet, no mask)  
Yeah. That…That makes a lot of sense.

The inside of the truck is close, thick with the weight of shit no one wants to talk about and the music that’s kept them all alive, if not well.  Dave glances at Jeff—knowing, maybe even forgiving, just for a moment.  He lets the silence be a benediction.

**DAVE**  
You know what, Jeff? I appreciate you keeping your goddamn mouth shut.  
(pauses, lets it register)  
Seriously. That’s… that’s actually pretty big of you.

Jeff’s not used to praise, especially from someone who’s been there and *lost* what he’s lost. His mouth twitches, a sheepish smile, eyes down.

**JEFF**  
Thanks, Dave.

For a moment the truck is all soft dashboard glow and shadows. Dave pulls in a long breath, exhaling like a man stalling at the edge of something that hurts.

**DAVE**  
Look… It’s not just about losing a bandmate.  It’s about losing a brother.  The guy you laughed with, got drunk with, got thrown out of hotels with. The guy who knew your darkest secrets and still went on stage next to you.

His voice cracks a little—no performance, just truth.

**DAVE**  
Losing Taylor… it was…  
(shakes his head, as if to clear it)  
It was like getting run down by a truck.  Again.  You think you know loss—you think it can’t possibly hurt more, but then… Bam.  It’s worse.

He turns to Jeff and Jeremy, eyes rimmed red, but steady—battle-tested, not broken.

**DAVE**  
It’s not just the music, you know?  It’s the… inside jokes.  The late-night bullshit.  The arguments at 3 a.m. about what key the bridge should be in.  It’s the look you give across the stage when something’s about to go completely off the rails, and you’re the only two who notice.  It’s…  
(shrugs, can’t quite find the word)  
It’s the years, the sweat, the *weirdness* nobody else ever saw.

He goes quiet, lets the hum of the truck and the distant sound of sirens fill the space.

**DAVE**  
Spend enough time with these people—they’re not “bandmates.”  They’re family.  
The family you pick, not the family you get stuck with.  And when you lose one—  
(pauses, rubs his forehead)  
It’s like losing a limb.  You just never feel right again.

He fights for composure. For a second he’s back in that dressing room in Bogotá, or Seattle, or whatever city left the biggest scar.

**DAVE**  
And then you have to figure out how to go on.  How to honor them—without making them a fucking waxwork.  Because you *can’t* recreate it.  You just can’t.

He fixes Jeff in place with that tour-battered, arena-lit stare.

**DAVE**  
You can’t replace the irreplaceable.  You can’t clone magic.  All you can do…  
(all the grit, none of the hope stripped out)  
Is try to find a *new* kind of magic.  A new pulse, a new kind of fucked-up alchemy.

He breathes, gathering the shards of what he’s been meaning to say.

**DAVE**  
That’s what I’m telling you:  Don’t find another Gilley.  Find someone who breaks it wide open again.  Who makes you scared and alive.  Who lets you *grow*.

He glances at each brother—old, blunt honesty, nothing disguised.

**DAVE**  
Because that’s what Gilley would’ve wanted.  Not a tribute act.  He’d want you moving forward, still making noise, still pissing people off, still *living*.

He softens—his voice almost a prayer.

**DAVE**  
And Taylor… He’d want the same for us.  No memorials. No mausoleums.  He’d want the amps on and the neighbors angry.

Dave glances out the window again, the Swedish night deep and indifferent.

**DAVE**  
That’s what it’s about.  The music.  The way it connects.  The wild shit you survive together.  That’s what lasts.

He looks back, steady now, decision made.

**DAVE**  
So don’t focus on finding a replacement.  Focus on how to keep the music alive.

There’s a pause— Tim leans in, eyes gleaming.

**TIM**  
He’s just getting started. You should see him on the second bottle.

Dave cracks a grin, something wicked flickering. He leans forward, dropping his voice, conspiratorial and gleeful.

**DAVE**  
As Gilley would say… “Now we shall touch penises together, and be brothers forever!”

The effect is nuclear.  Jeff and Jeremy explode—pure laughter, ugly, snotty, doubled over, tears streaming. For a second Gilley is there in the truck, cackling at his own posthumous punchline.

**JEFF** (gasping, can’t breathe)  
Oh my god… how did you even—

**JEREMY** (wheezing, dabbing eyes)  
That’s… that’s ancient.  Justified and ancient.

**DAVE** (wiping his own eyes, but still smirking)  
Word gets around. Especially when it’s *that* classic.

He sobers, gaze turning clinical again, all business, all surgeon.

**DAVE**  
Look—  The answer to your problem?  It’s right in front of you.  But you’re too close, you’re looking past it.

He points—between the brothers, then up toward the front of the truck, the self-driving dashboard blinking.  The unspoken answer dangling.

**DAVE**  
Your new frontman…  
(it lands heavy, no blink)  
…it should be Jim.

Jeff and Jeremy exchange a look. For once, no joke between them, just the sound of brakes failing in the dark.

**JEFF**  
Jim?

**JEREMY**  
*Jim Brown?*

**DAVE**  
The one and only.  Think about it.  Jim’s voice—deep, rich, a fucking baritone that could out-sorrow Mark Lanegan, God rest his throat.  And Bill—he’s a riot, but he never lets the mask slip for the crowd.  You need a voice the fans already know.  Especially since…

He glances sideways at Tim, then right back, eyes sharpening.

**DAVE**  
…especially since I’ve got this gnawing suspicion you’re about to add a new female vocalist, too.

Jeff and Jeremy both throw Tim a look that could kill and then bury. This wasn’t for outside ears—at least, not yet.

**DAVE** (waves it off, doesn’t give a shit)  
Don’t worry.  I’m not the press.  Now, listen:  Jim makes sense. He’s always made sense.  
He’s got the history, the loyalty, the scars in all the right places.  The only thing holding him back is the story you keep telling yourselves about needing another Gilley.

Dave leans in, voice gathering power, a preacher on the edge of his own pulpit.

**DAVE**  
Picture it:  Jim steady as gravity, the anchor, that voice holding down every room you walk into.  And you bring in something new—someone with different blood, different teeth, a woman who won’t let the boys’ club grow old.  It’s not erasure.  It’s resurrection.  
Not revolution, just what evolution looks like when it’s got a hangover.

He gets animated, hands flying, every word landing like a call to arms.

**DAVE**  
You’ve been hunting for a stranger to fit the Gilley-shaped hole, but that mold’s already fucking dust.  You *can’t* find it again.  What you have is a raw jewel in your pocket, but you keep reaching for treasure maps.  Polish what’s right in front of you.

He hammers it, the old performer pushing every syllable into the stage.

**DAVE**  
Jim’s not a Gilley replacement.  He’s *Jim Brown*.  That’s the trick.  If you want a legend, you have to stop making monuments and start making new myths.

He pauses, breathes, lets the pitch drop to truth.

**DAVE**  
This isn’t some Craigslist weirdo with a Soundcloud link.  This is Jim.  Family.  
Been through every tour, every backroom disaster, every fucking intervention.  
He knows what the music means.  He knows where the bodies are buried.  You don’t find that on Bandmix.

He leans back, the edge still there but now brushed with a little hope.

**DAVE**  
I know it’s not a small decision.  You don’t just swap frontmen like you swap tour vans.  
But I’m telling you:  If you stop looking for what you lost, and start looking for what you *have*, you might find out you’ve been holding the answer the whole goddamn time.

It settles.  Jeff and Jeremy stare at each other, like they’re realizing the punchline of a joke that’s been running twenty years.

Tim, never letting things go quiet too long, leans in, all teeth.

**TIM**  
See? I told you—he’s just getting started.

Dave, as if someone’s just spiked his water, sits up, the fire back.

**DAVE**  
Right!  So you wanna talk survival?  Let’s talk about *making it* in this fucking racket.

He leans in, voice dropping to a bark, hands out, preacher’s mode.

**DAVE**  
It’s not for the gentle.  It’s a meat grinder, a snakepit, a perpetual parade of backstabbers in borrowed leather.  You want to know how you survive?  Grit.  Stupid, ugly, obsessive grit.  Not just talent—fuck, talent’s everywhere.  You gotta want it enough to play every dive, every third-tier Euro festival, every gig where the only guy in the crowd is the bartender’s cousin.  You sleep in the van, you eat gas station sushi, you play for sound guys who would rather be dead.

He punctuates it with a snap.

**DAVE**  
And you get *smart*.  You figure out the contracts, the splits, the publishing.  You read every word your lawyer sends.  Because if you don’t, you’re gonna get fucked.  And not in the way you remember fondly.

He lets up, tone softening, turning the knife inward.

**DAVE**  
But above everything—you stay true.  You never forget the first moment you plugged in and thought,  *This is why I breathe*.  Because that’s what you’re left with when the tours dry up and the critics turn on you.

He’s got them, and he knows it.

**DAVE**  
That’s the only thing that keeps you upright when it all goes to shit.

Another pause—then the closer.

**DAVE**  
And that’s where Jim comes in.  He *is* your connective tissue, your anchor, your living memory.  Add new blood—watch it explode.

He settles back, finally satisfied.

**DAVE**  
So… what do you think?

---

Dave pivots in the seat, the heavy sermon melting into a sidelong smirk.

**DAVE**  
Anyway—enough heavy shit.  Rumor mill says you guys are finally getting dragged into the Hall of Fame this year.  
(leans in, faux-whispers)  
About fucking time. Congratulations. The fact they waited this long is a crime.

He fixes the brothers with a look, equal parts curiosity and old conspirator.

**DAVE**  
So. Who’s presenting you?  Don’t tell me you left it up to a committee.

Jeremy bats the question away like it’s a mosquito.
