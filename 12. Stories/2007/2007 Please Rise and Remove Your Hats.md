## **INT. GREAT AMERICAN BALL PARK – CINCINNATI, OHIO – 6:07 P.M. – JUNE 2007**

The sky’s dull and threatening—late afternoon humidity hanging over the diamond, thick as old sweat. Outfield grass electric green under stadium lights, the air stinking of bratwurst, nacho cheese, the ghost of fireworks.

The stands are maybe half-full—kids in cheap caps, old men with radios pressed to one ear, a couple making out in the nosebleeds as the PA system hisses.

Down by the dugout, two figures wait—**KATHY** in a wrinkled black skirt, faded Chucks, hair in a knotted bun; **JEREMY** in a thrift-store suit, tie yanked loose, Telecaster slung silent over one shoulder, fingers drumming on the case.

A skinny man in a polyester Reds polo hovers, clutching a clipboard like it’s a riot shield.

**REDS REP** (smiling too wide, too corporate)
We just think, for, you know, *consistency*—if you could go ahead and pre-record your part, Kathy, and Jeremy, we’d just have you do the… (searches for the phrase) …the *air guitar thing* on the field. It’s what works for TV.

**JEREMY** (deadpan, not even pretending to smile)
So you want me to stand out there and mime the National Anthem like I’m on "America’s Funniest Home Videos." Without a guitar. Or a mic. Just…flailing.

**KATHY** (staring at him, then back at Reds Rep)
You want the voice live, but the hands fake? Or you want both of us fake, because that’s easier to manage? Is this about insurance, or is this just—(shrugs, barely biting down the word)—idiocy?

**JEREMY** (leans in, low enough only Kathy hears)
We could smuggle in C4. Blow the fucking PA system up during “home of the brave.” That’s rock and roll, right?

**KATHY** (eyes narrowing, voice pitched sweet, dangerous)
I think *explosive rhetoric* is more our speed, babe. Don’t want to get banned from all Ohio, not just Cincinnati. Yet.

The Reds Rep is sweating, tapping his pen against the clipboard like a Geiger counter. Above them, the big screen loops old footage—flag, flag, fireworks, a faded grainy face singing from another century.

**KATHY** (turns, direct, calm as Sunday service)
We’ll play it live. Or you find someone else.

**JEREMY** (shoulders squared, grins sharp)
Or just queue up Whitney Houston and let me dance with a broom.

The Rep stares, mouth open, the little league coach energy leaking out like helium.

**REDS REP** (trying for a smile, failing)
It’s…for broadcast standards. We just—never mind. I’ll check with…upstairs.

He hurries off, clipboard flapping. A group of interns in red shirts and white shorts trail after him, whispering.

**KATHY** (exhales, glances at Jeremy)
Ever want to just…drop a match and see what happens?

**JEREMY** (eyes fixed on the empty field, one corner of his mouth curling)
Every fucking day.

Above them, thunder rumbles. A hot gust stirs the flags. The crowd swells, restless, already halfway through the anthem in their heads.

**KATHY** (soft, to herself, looking at her battered setlist)
Let’s make it a funeral march. For their dignity, not ours.

Jeremy tunes his silent guitar, fingers tight on invisible strings. Somewhere, a child shrieks. The sky flickers. The lights flare. Kathy hums, low, slow, a kind of threat and benediction all at once. The camera lingers: Their hands, tense. The field, waiting. The stadium, an unfed tiger.

A line of birds crosses the sky. No one breathes.

The scene seals itself—like a fuse lit, smoldering beneath the national anthem, waiting for someone, anyone, to strike the match.

## **INT. GREAT AMERICAN BALL PARK – FIELD LEVEL – 6:37 P.M. – TWILIGHT, JUNE 2007**

Wind flicks the bun loose at the nape of Kathy’s neck. Overhead, the sky is the color of bruised peaches, heavy clouds trying to swallow the last sunlight. The stadium is humming now—26,000 in their seats, cheap beer and pride fizzing through the crowd like static. Every eye on the diamond, every kid with a sticky flag, every father nudging his son to stand up straight.

Down by the third-base line, Jeremy stands beside a battered old Fender amp—just loud enough, just cheap enough to piss off anyone who thought he’d bring the Tele. He cradles a cherry Les Paul, thumb smoothing over the binding, eyes flicking up at the stands, then to Kathy.

Kathy closes her eyes for a breath. She’s sung for seventy, eighty thousand—crowds roaring at music festivals, at political rallies, in cities where no one speaks English but everybody sings. This? Singing is easy. Except for the Anthem.

She flexes her jaw, tongue darting over her teeth, the bitter copper of nerves at the back of her throat.

**JEREMY** (murmurs, leaning in, voice all cigarette smoke and dry salt)
You want a pitch? Or just dive in and let them chase you?

**KATHY** (opens her eyes, grinning, wild and wry)
What, you scared I’ll leave you behind?

**JEREMY** (shrugs, strikes a muted chord)
You hit the key, I’ll light the fuse.

The crowd hushes. The stadium PA crackles, announcing names, stats, sponsors, then fading into the low, expectant rumble of 26,000 Americans waiting for something sacred or at least loud.  Kathy’s name is spoken. Jeremy’s too, as if they’re local legends or dangerous imports. She steps up to the battered mic, sneakers planted, back straight, a glint of sweat at her hairline.  The Star Spangled Banner is a bastard to sing, a flag raised and torn and mended by every note. High and hard, with no forgiveness for the weak or the hungover.

**KATHY** (under her breath, a ritual, smiling crooked)
Here we go, motherfucker.

Jeremy strikes the opening chord, heavy, crisp, shivering up her spine. No backing track, no sweeteners, just the pure, raw stretch of her voice rising over the chord, warping stadium air.

*O say can you see—*

The anthem rolls out slow, hungry, bigger than the stands. Kathy pulls it taut, her voice hot and bright at the edges, eyes never leaving the upper decks. There’s grit under the vowels, velvet over the thunder, her lungs wide open, heart beating steady as a snare drum. She takes the hard notes on a tightrope, lets the soft ones flutter out over left field, drags the “land of the free” across her tongue like a promise and a dare.

Jeremy’s guitar swells and stings, each note looping behind her, more defiance than decoration. The Les Paul rings out through the speaker—no frills, just iron and sweat and muscle memory. The sound bounces off the backstop, rides the hush, climbs the stadium, eats the night air alive.

The anthem isn’t pretty. It’s not meant to be. Kathy makes it sacred, not safe—her voice cracks, just once, on “perilous fight,” and she lets it, because there’s something honest in the strain. Every broken note is a name, a history, a knuckled prayer thrown skyward.

The last line comes slow, the whole field holding its breath.

*Home of the brave—*

Her throat is raw now, mouth filled with copper, knees trembling. She grins—no one but Jeremy sees. He lifts the Les Paul in salute, fingers burning, the last chord buzzing, blurring into the evening. The crowd erupts, all noise and hunger, a wave of relief and memory. Kathy bows her head, hair in her face, sweat soaking her collar. Jeremy catches her gaze, nods once, the small crooked smile that says: we did it, and fuck them if they ever doubted.

Behind them, the PA blares, the players line up, the first pitch is minutes away.

But the Anthem lingers, sharp and wild as ozone. The camera catches her hands shaking. The lights flare white on sweat. Jeremy tucks the guitar cable loose, mouth open in a silent, ragged laugh.

Scene ends on their shadows stretching out across the infield, long and dark and defiant, sealed by applause.

## **INT. SERVICE CORRIDOR – UNDER GREAT AMERICAN BALL PARK – 6:45 P.M. – STICKY TWILIGHT, JUNE 2007**

Fluorescent lights buzz overhead, unkind and unblinking. Pipes sweat along the ceiling, paint peeling, dust collecting in ancient webs along the cinderblock. Somewhere down the hall, a vending machine rattles out a can of Sprite, the echo weirdly holy after the roar of the crowd.  Kathy leans against a utility door, dragging both hands up through her hair. It’s plastered to her temples, damp curls stuck to the side of her face. The halter top—black, glitter faded, band name in letters long since peeled—clings to her chest like another layer of skin. Her jeans are dark with sweat at the waistband, a smudge of eyeliner caught in the corner of her eye.

**JEREMY** (unlatching the battered guitar case, grinning like a thief)
I’ve seen you sing through food poisoning, thunderstorms, midwestern church choirs with pitch forks, but that—(*points at her, at her throat*)—that was like watching you wrestle a mountain lion in front of twenty thousand drunk people and a fireworks team.

**KATHY** (panting, chest heaving, every inch of her sticky)
Shut up. No, really—(*wheezing laugh, wiping her brow*)—I could do Bowie, I could do Queen, I could scream through a Rage cover, but that fucking *anthem*—who decided it needed to cover every note known to man, like, “Hey, sing this shit sober at 7 p.m. with hot air blasting in your face and see if your vocal cords survive.”

She rolls her neck, throat gleaming, collarbones beaded. She’s radiant in the ugly light, skin flushed, damp all the way down her ribs.

**KATHY** (cont’d, breathless, incredulous)
How the fuck did Whitney do that? Like—did she have bionic lungs? Were there, like, secret oxygen tanks backstage? I grew up thinking she was magic. Turns out, yeah—she actually was. That note, at the end—(*she mimes, voice cracking into a high, heroic wail*)—I thought my esophagus was gonna just fucking explode, like *pop*, guts on the dugout, national tragedy.

**JEREMY** (chuckling, slings his guitar over his shoulder, a little too proud)
You looked like you were gonna burst a blood vessel. But you didn’t. You nailed it. That’s the job, right?

**KATHY** (yanking at the fabric between her breasts, sweat glistening at her sternum)
It’s eighty-eight degrees! I shouldn’t be this disgusting. You’d think I just ran suicides with the bullpen. Jesus. I’m soaked. I could wring this thing out and flood the dugout.

She tugs at her top, the fabric making a wet, sticky sound—*schlk*—as it peels away from her skin, only to cling again the moment she lets go.

**KATHY** (half-laugh, half-growl)
And next time some asshole asks why I won’t do the anthem at their high school graduation, I’m just gonna point to this top and say, “You want to see the real Fourth of July? Get me a towel and some Gatorade.”

**JEREMY** (leans back against the wall, arms folded, voice softening)
You killed it. Nobody cared about the range—hell, half of them probably just heard noise and nostalgia. But you—(nods at her, reverent)—you made it real. Even if you almost passed out.

He fumbles for a water bottle, cracks the cap, hands it to her. She downs half in a single breathless swallow, water spilling down her chin, her throat working, voice shredded but satisfied.

**KATHY** (voice hoarse, but fierce)
Just once I’d like to see the anthem written for alto, or, like, a lazy baritone who doesn’t get out of bed before noon. No more high notes. No more showing off for ghosts.

She grins, feral and sweat-drenched, flipping him off and laughing, the sound echoing through the corridor, wild and alive. The camera lingers on the shine of sweat under the stadium lights, the slow, shaking exhale, the band shirt stuck to her back. The echo of her voice—raw, spent, immortal—still tangled in the pipes overhead.

## **INT. STADIUM SERVICE CORRIDOR – GREAT AMERICAN BALL PARK – 6:48 P.M. – AIR CONDITIONED HELL**

Kathy is still pressed against the cool cinderblock, Gatorade cap wedged between her molars, a rivulet of sweat tracing the dip of her spine. The stadium’s muted roar seeps through the concrete—foul balls, organ riffs, the announcer’s distant monotone. Jeremy slots his Les Paul into the battered case, hands shaking out the adrenaline.

**JEREMY** (sighs, tugging his tie loose)
Could’ve been worse. Blue Jays could’ve been in town and then you’d have to do “O Canada.”

**KATHY** (tilting her head, eyes slitted, squinting up at him through a wet fringe)
What the fuck are you talking about, bro? *No.* No, I am not singing two anthems.

**JEREMY** (deadpan, dragging a ragged towel across his jaw)
You realize, right, that when an American League team is in town—Riverfront, Great American, whatever they call this concrete oven—you gotta do both anthems? I’m serious. It’s, like, MLB protocol or some shit. You’d be up there sweating through “*O say can you see,*” and then right into “*O Canada*”—back to back, no water break, no mercy.

**KATHY** (loud, incredulous, a little desperate)
Is that in the fucking Constitution or what, bro? They hiding this shit in the Bill of Rights? **Article Five:** thou shalt fuck over the opening act and stretch their vocal cords until they snap? Son of a *bitch*.

She’s grinning now, despite herself, tugging at the sodden fabric of her top, eyes wide with mock outrage.

**KATHY** (cont’d, flipping him the bird with both hands)
What’s their fucking anthem anyway? “*O Canada, land of maple syrup and universal healthcare*”? Sing me the lyrics, Jeremy, let’s see how many times I can hit a high C before I bleed out on the infield.

**JEREMY** (sing-song, tuneless)
*O Canada, our home and native land… True patriot love, in all thy sons command…*

**KATHY** (overlapping, her voice hoarse, wheezing with laughter)
I swear to god, if you get me booked for that shit, I’ll defect on the spot. I’ll move to Montreal and do Leonard Cohen covers for the rest of my life.

**JEREMY** (shrugs, smirking)
Could be worse. At least it’s not “*God Save the Queen.*” You ever try to out-sing a royal funeral march on a Tuesday night?

**KATHY** (wiping tears from her cheeks, breathless, throat raw)
If they ever ask me to sing for the Royals, you’re carrying me out in a body bag, man.

Jeremy laughs, the sound echoing off the pipes, the rhythm of two people who know just how close they came to combusting in public.

The scene seals: the two of them sprawled against the wall, wrung out, ridiculous, laughing about anthems and constitutions while the game rages on overhead, the echo of their voices still ringing in the cinderblock belly of Cincinnati.

## **INT. STADIUM SERVICE CORRIDOR – GREAT AMERICAN BALL PARK – 6:51 P.M. – STILL SWEATING**

Jeremy’s laughter is barely winding down, but Kathy’s already upright, chest heaving, the Gatorade bottle rolling across the grimy floor with a hollow *clack clack clack*. She plants her hands on her hips, hair coming loose in humid tangles, eyes sharp with mischief.

**KATHY** (throws her head back, yells, her voice shredded and triumphant)
*God save the Queen!*
*She ain’t no human being!*
*There is no future—in England’s dreaming!*

Her boots slam the floor for percussion, voice ricocheting down the corridor—pure London snarl, Sex Pistols venom, every word spat with the conviction of someone who knows exactly what it means to have no future but tonight.

**JEREMY** (grinning, clapping time, mugging like an idiot)
Don’t be told what you want, don’t be told what you need!

Kathy’s laughter rips out of her, wild, unstoppable. She leans in, shoving Jeremy’s shoulder, eyes gleaming, sweat running down her temples. She gives the next line everything she’s got, voice cracking on the echo:

**KATHY** (singing, wicked and raw)
*No future! No future!*
*No future for you!*

Her voice bounces off the concrete, mixing with the muted roar of the crowd above, the pulse of the stadium, the distant, distant fireworks of some kid catching a foul ball.

For a second it’s not Cincinnati. It’s not even America. It’s every shitty, beautiful backstage tunnel they’ve ever wandered, every border town and parking lot, every moment before and after the show where the music is just for them.

**KATHY** (out of breath, giggling, voice softer now)
I’d do the anthem every night if I could sing *that* instead.

She leans against the wall, body vibrating, sweat shining on her collarbone, hair wild and mouth open in a real, reckless grin.  Jeremy bows, overdramatic, like the crowd above can see him.

**JEREMY** (deadpan)
You just turned the American national pastime into a punk show, kid.

**KATHY** (sighs, playful, breathy, voice honey and gasoline)
God save the Queen, God save my fucking vocal cords.

Their laughter rattles the pipes, a little act of rebellion sealed under the city—anthem or no anthem, it’s theirs.

## **INT. SERVICE CORRIDOR – BASE OF THE STADIUM – 6:54 P.M. – TUNNEL LEADING TO ELEVATORS**

Their laughter’s still hanging in the air, a private little riot echoing off old cement. Kathy hooks a finger through her belt loop, sweat cooling under the blast of a ceiling vent, voice dropping to a low, conspiratorial hush.

**JEREMY** (grinning, shoulders hunched like he’s bracing for a sprint)
Let’s catch an elevator, get up to the luxury box. Goddamn, this is—(*shakes his head, wipes sweat off his brow with the heel of his palm*)—I mean, I want some fucking beer and nachos. Like, immediately.

**KATHY** (raising one eyebrow, eyes glinting with fresh hunger)
Beer and nachos? Amateur hour, my dude. Do they sell margaritas in the big-money sky lounge?

**JEREMY** (mock insult, gesturing grandly toward the elevator sign)
It’s a luxury box. Free booze, free beer, free food. You want a margarita, you get a margarita. You want sixteen? Fuck it, go for sixteen.

**KATHY** (shoving him gently as they walk, mouth twisting into a crooked, greedy smile)
Why don’t we go to more of these? Why do we always end up in the weirdest, shittiest green rooms in the world, eating Cheetos out of a vending machine at 1 a.m.?

**JEREMY** (shrugs, presses the elevator button with the back of his knuckle, the little light blinking orange)
When was the last time you were in Cincinnati? August? Yeah, like—(*does some finger math, brow furrowed*)—eleven months ago? I don’t know. Time isn’t real. Time’s a tour manager who lies to you about every ETA.

**KATHY** (rolling her eyes, but softer now, staring at the scuffed toes of her sneakers)
Being a global star is fucking tiresome. Trying to remember what city you’re in, what room number, which goddamn hotel chain… where you’re gonna sleep at night.

The elevator dings, doors sliding open with a lurch. The two step inside—still sweat-slick, wild-haired, half-drunk on adrenaline.

**JEREMY** (punching the button for “CLUB LEVEL,” voice sly, lips barely hiding a smirk)
Aren’t you usually sleeping on top of Jeff? Or does he rotate with the luggage and the keyboard case?

**KATHY** (mock gasp, shoves him with her hip, laughter cracking through the tiny metal box)
You little shit. Maybe I *am* sleeping on top of Jeff. Maybe I just use you all for insulation in the winter, like a sexy band-shaped blanket.

**JEREMY** (leans back against the rail, eyes sparkling)
I’m honored, truly. I’d die for the cause. Especially if it involves free shrimp cocktail and open bar.

**KATHY** (exaggerated sigh, tossing her hair back, voice all drama)
God save the Queen. God save my dignity. Let’s get wasted and see if we can get banned from the luxury box before the seventh inning stretch.

The elevator hums upward, fluorescent lights flickering. Kathy sways in place, toes tapping a rhythm nobody else can hear, Jeremy fiddling with his lanyard, the city flickering in and out through smudged glass.

Above, the crowd erupts at a pop fly, but down here, in their little metal sanctuary, it’s just the two of them—rockstars for hire, conspirators on the edge of exhaustion and mischief. The doors shudder open, spilling them into the cool, air-conditioned sprawl of luxury: plush seats, white tablecloths, rows of gleaming bottles and sweating glasses, a spread of food that looks like it’s meant for royalty, not runaways.

Scene ends on Kathy slipping her arm through Jeremy’s, both of them grinning, unwashed and radiant as they stride into someone else’s palace, ready to eat, drink, and maybe—just maybe—raise a little more hell.
