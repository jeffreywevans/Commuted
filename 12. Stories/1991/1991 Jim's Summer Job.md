**FOUR ON THE FLOOR AND NO PEDAL**  
*Summer 1991 — Dayton, Ohio*

---

Jim Brown was supposed to be off that night.

He had sung his last “Sweet Adeline” in a red vest and striped bowtie six hours earlier at King’s Island, smiled through exactly one thousand forced harmonies, and was halfway through a cheeseburger behind the Tilt-A-Whirl when his payphone rang.

He let it ring twice. Then answered.

“Yeah?”

“You’re Jim Brown, right?” said a voice like static and whiskey. “Drummer?”

“Technically.”

“You sing too?”

“Only if there’s a harmony chart and a paycheck.”

“Good. You’re playing the county fair tonight.”

Jim blinked. “Am I?”

“Band’s called Mongrel Seed. Their drummer broke his wrist trying to fight a bee.”

“A bee?”

“Big one, apparently. You in?”

Jim looked at the remains of his cheeseburger. It stared back sadly.

“How much?”

“Thirty bucks and a drink ticket.”

Jim sighed. “Yeah, alright.”

The stage was three pallets and a tarp behind a livestock tent.

The “fair” was mostly a tractor pull, two lemonade stands, and one suspiciously limp bounce house.

Jim arrived with his snare, sticks, hi-hats, and The Cowbell — always The Cowbell — figuring he could make anything work with that much soul.

Then he saw the kit.

No throne. One cymbal. Two toms that looked like they’d been salvaged from a fireworks explosion.

And no bass pedal.

As in, **no pedal. At all.** No beater. No strap. Just a bass drum sitting there like a sad trash can.

Jim turned to the guy handing out corn dogs near the stage.

“Hey. Where’s the kick pedal?”

The guy shrugged. “Gone.”

Jim stared. “What do you mean, gone?”

“Stolen last week. Guy said it was a Tama Iron Cobra, looked expensive. Whole thing disappeared during the dog show.”

Jim turned to the band. Mongrel Seed looked back at him like it was *his* fault.

“You didn’t bring a pedal?” their bassist asked.

“You didn’t bring a **kit**.”

“Fair.”

They were set to play ten songs. Jim had to make a choice.

He took off his left shoe.

Taped a drumstick to his sock with gaffer tape.

Angled the bass drum forward on two bricks.

Then he sat down on an overturned five-gallon bucket and used his **bare foot** to jab the stick into the drumhead every quarter note.

It was stupid.

It was genius.

It **worked.**

By song four, he was locked in. The cowbell was singing. The snare cracked. His foot was going numb. And the crowd?

The crowd was **loving it.**

Teenagers dancing in corn husks. A man in overalls doing high kicks. A toddler asleep on a cooler. It was the worst gig Jim had ever played — and it was working.

After the set, the band handed him his thirty bucks in sweaty singles.

“You saved our ass, man.”

Jim wiped the sweat off his forehead and looked down at his taped sock.

“I think I just committed foot-based percussion fraud.”

“You ever think about doing this full time?”

Jim blinked. “Drumming?”

“No, man. The singing. You got a voice.”

Jim nodded slowly.

“Yeah,” he said. “But I still think your gig is gone to hell.”

Then he limped away, barefoot, holding a cowbell older than the Emancipation Proclamation, and whistling “Sweet Adeline” under his breath.
