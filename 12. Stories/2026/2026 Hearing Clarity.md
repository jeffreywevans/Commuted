# **HEARING CLARITY**

# **CHAPTER ONE**

---
## **INT. CREMEANS’ OFFICE – EARLY JANUARY 2026 – LATE AFTERNOON**

A clean, orderly space overlooking downtown Dayton. The walls are modest: framed contracts, a diploma from Wright State, a small print of Psalm 121\. A full case of Mountain Dew rests beside the filing cabinet.

**CREMEANS** sits at his desk, reviewing venue contracts. His posture is calm, hands folded. The only sound is the hum of the office heater and the ticking of an analog clock.

His computer chimes—an alert for an unscheduled appointment.

He taps the mouse. A professional-looking woman appears on the video preview. The name reads: Carla Dell'amato, Director – Applied Acoustics, SignalBridge Technologies.

He presses “Accept.”

**CARLA** (ON SCREEN): 
Mr. Cremeans. Thank you for taking a moment. I’ll keep this brief.

**CREMEANS**:  
Certainly. How can I help?

**CARLA** :  
Our firm has adapted a noise-filtering headset originally developed for field infantry communication. We’ve repurposed the core tech for stage environments. Automatic feedback suppression, crowd volume gating, and adaptive EQ monitoring. It’s tuned in real time, and frankly… the data looks good.

**CREMEANS**:  
And your interest in us?

**CARLA** :  
Your band works loud. Extremely loud. You also work clean—engineer-forward, low margin for error. We believe Commuted is the perfect proving ground. If your players accept the gear—and if Mr. Evans agrees to endorse it—our product will reach a new audience overnight.

**CREMEANS**:  
And you’re prepared to provide units for live rehearsal testing?

**CARLA** :  
Yes. Five full rigs. Custom-fitted. No cost. We’d simply like to monitor usage anonymously for telemetry and response curves.

**CREMEANS** (pauses, then nods):  
I’ll need written terms—IP retention, safety indemnity, performance boundaries. Nothing goes near Jeff without that in order.

**CARLA**  
Understood.

**CREMEANS**:  
He won’t wear it if it pings Bluetooth without notice. And if he hears even one artifact in his left ear during a set, he’ll toss it into the river.

**CARLA**  (smiling):  
Noted.

**CREMEANS**:  
If you’re serious, send over two prototypes by Friday. I’ll run them through Bill and Jim in rehearsal. If Jeff likes the idea, you’ll know it because he won’t say a word. He’ll just keep wearing it.

**CARLA**
That’s fair.

**CREMEANS** (with a faint smile):  
That’s trust. But we don’t give it away.

**CUT TO:**

### **INT. CREMEANS' OFFICE – LATER**

He sits alone again. Opens a Mountain Dew. Sets a small leather-bound Bible on his desk. Flips to Romans.

His voice is quiet.

**CREMEANS** (to himself):  
Do not conform to the pattern of this world, but be transformed…

He looks up. The headset still sits in front of him. Not touched yet.

**CREMEANS** (softly):  
We’ll see.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – OVER-THE-RHINE, CINCINNATI – LATE AFTERNOON**

The studio is dim but warm. Dust catches in the slanted golden light. Shelves overflow with cables, half-drunk energy drinks, and marked-up sheet music. A vintage Rhodes piano rests beside a modular synth wall that looks like a Cold War artifact.

[[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff Evans]], in his usual faded flannel and denim, stands near the amp stack, adjusting patch cables. Head down. Focused. His bass leans against a flight case like a sleeping dog.

The door clicks open. **CREMEANS** steps in, closing it gently behind him. He’s wearing a brown parka, scuffed boots, and a look that says he’s already been up for nine hours longer than anyone else.

He carries a small hard-shell case.

**CREMEANS**
Jeff.

**JEFF** (grunts without turning)  
What, we got a fire code violation already?

**CREMEANS**:  
No. Just a minute of your time. If it’s not too much to ask.

Jeff finally looks up. Sees the case. Sees the subtle, earnest way Cremeans holds it—careful, not pushy.

**JEFF**  
Let me guess. That’s not a Bible.

**CREMEANS** (smiles faintly):  
No. Though I did read from Romans this morning.

(beat)

This is a headset. Adaptive. Filters the worst of the crowd roar. Cancels feedback. Adjusts the mix by predictive taper.

Jeff blinks. Just once. Looks at the case. Doesn’t move.

**JEFF**
Sounds like an ad for toothpaste.

**CREMEANS**  
It’s DOD surplus. Repurposed R\&D. Meant to protect hearing in hostile environments. They want to test it on… well, hostile environments of a different kind.

**JEFF** (snorts)  
So we’re battlefield-adjacent now.

**CREMEANS** (quietly):  
You’ve said yourself your ears still haven't recovered from São Paulo. 2010\. Bill’s delay tail looped back mid-bridge.

Jeff doesn’t answer. He remembers.

Cremeans gently unclasps the case and sets it on a stool. Inside, the headset: matte black, slightly oversized, vaguely like something an Apple designer rejected for being “too tactical.”

**CREMEANS** (CONT’D):  
I know it looks like it belongs on a state trooper. But the tech underneath’s real. Bill and Jim are willing to run it this weekend.

Jeff tilts his head, sizing it up like a dog staring at a vacuum cleaner.

**JEFF**:  
You wearing one?

**CREMEANS**:  
No. Mine is simpler. Scripture and Mountain Dew.

**JEFF** (dryly):  
You should get that on a mug.

Cremeans doesn’t flinch. Doesn’t try to sell it. He simply sets the headset down on the amp cabinet and steps back.

**CREMEANS**:  
If you want to try it, try it. No pressure. No cameras. No endorsement.

(beat)

Just… don’t dismiss it because it’s new. That’s all I’ll ask.

Jeff studies it for a long beat. Then—

**JEFF**:  
If it screws with my tone, I’m burning it in the alley.

**CREMEANS** (nods, calm):  
Fair enough.

He turns to go, pausing just before the door.

**CREMEANS** (CONT’D):  
I’ve seen you play through pain, Jeff. More times than I can count. But just because you can doesn’t mean you should.

He lets that settle. Then he’s gone. Jeff stands still. Then slowly walks to the headset. Picks it up.  We don’t see what he decides.

**CUT TO BLACK.**

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – MAIN ROOM – EVENING**

Guitars lean against walls like tired fighters. The air smells faintly of solder, old gear foam, and reheated chili from a slow cooker Bill forgot to clean out. The room hums with anticipation—the usual pre-practice static.

**JIM** is seated behind his kit, stretching his wrists and rolling his shoulders. He’s in a faded RUSH 2112 shirt that’s older than some interns. **BILL** is hunched over the mixing board in the control room, checking his signal chain for the third time.

The door creaks open.  **JEFF** walks in. He’s wearing the headset. It’s bulky—clunky, even. Matte black. Slightly angular. The kind of thing a Cold War aviator or a revered Tesla engineer might wear while working on a ham radio.  The room falls silent. Jim looks up first.

**JIM** (grinning):  
Well, damn. Look at you. Straight outta 1983\.

Jeff raises an eyebrow. Doesn’t answer.

**JIM** (CONT’D):  
No, seriously. You look like a synth-pop producer who drinks coffee with both hands and rides a motorcycle he doesn’t own.

Jeff plugs in his bass and starts adjusting the levels. He ignores the bait. Bill enters the control room, holding a mug of tea and looking half-interested—until he sees the headset.  He stops cold.

**BILL**:  
What the hell is on your head?

**JEFF**:  
It’s a gift from the future. Or Cremeans. Hard to tell.

Bill circles him slowly, like a wildlife biologist studying an unusually nervous bird.

**BILL** (deadpan):  
You look like a dork.

**JEFF**:  
That’s rich coming from a man wearing cargo pants and a scarf.

**BILL**:  
It’s functional layering, thank you very much.

**JEFF**:  
So is this.

Jeff taps the headset gently. The lights on the side pulse with subtle, tasteful blue. It’s tracking levels.

**JIM**:  
You look like you’re about to remix *Stranger Things* for Dolby Atmos.

**JEFF**:  
You want your bassist to go deaf by summer? Or you want him to survive these sessions with both ears?

**BILL** (grumbling):  
I just want him to not look like a Bluetooth-enabled action figure.

**JEFF** (shrugs):  
Too late.

**JIM** (nods):  
Still. Retro as hell. I respect it.

He taps his sticks together.

**JIM** (CONT’D):  
Alright, Robo-Bassist. Let’s see if you still groove with that thing strapped to your skull.

**JEFF** (quietly):  
I always groove. You're the one who forgets how time signatures work after 90 minutes.

Bill chuckles behind his mug.  Jeff closes his eyes. Begins to play a low, warm run in D minor. The room vibrates. Clean. Sharp. No hiss. No shriek.  He smiles—barely.  And says nothing else.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – MOMENTS LATER**

The air is thick with amp buzz and expectation.

**JIM** taps out a slow rhythm on the hi-hat, keeping time while sipping an Alani. **BILL** finishes balancing levels at the console and leans back with a half-grin. The mood is loose, but electric.

The door bursts open.  **JEREMY EVANS** enters in his usual half-ironed shirt and hair that’s trying to quit. His Strat is slung low. He doesn’t say hello. He never does. He just plugs in.

**JEREMY** (to no one):  
Alright, let’s see if my hands still work.

No warning.

He rips.

A fast, arcing run across four positions, major to minor, a lightning pivot into modal territory. Not showboating—just reminding everyone who he is. Fingers blur, clean and razor-sharp, like a man who spent all morning loading beer kegs and all afternoon dreaming in fretboard language.

Jeff doesn’t flinch. He listens for four seconds—maybe five. Then?

He answers.

His fingers slid up the neck of his bass like they’ve been waiting for this. He drops into a groove beneath Jeremy’s riff: syncopated, sly, intricate. A sideways smile of a bassline. No fuss, no bravado—just locked-in brilliance.

Bill glances up from the control room. Raises one eyebrow.

**BILL** (murmurs):  
Well, I’ll be damned.

**JIM** (still playing):  
They’re gonna start dueling again, aren’t they?

Jeremy slides into a bluesy bend, then double-times the scale. A dare. Jeff responds instantly matching him note for note, but in counterpoint. Then adds harmonic overtones that shimmer through the studio like ghosts. It’s funky. It’s weird. It’s better than anyone expected, especially with that bulky headset on his head.

He doesn’t adjust. Just plays.

**BILL** (shaking his head):  
You know… he actually sounds better with that ridiculous thing on.

**JIM** (grinning):  
It’s like watching RoboCop jam with Stevie Ray Vaughan.

Jeremy finally stops, hands floating above the strings like he’s not quite done yet.

**JEREMY** (to Jeff):  
You been practicing behind my back?

**JEFF** (flatly):  
No. I practiced in front of your back. You just weren’t paying attention.

Jeremy cracks a rare smile.

**JEREMY**:  
Fair.

They both nod. Nothing more said. Nothing more needed. Jim counts them in with four quick taps.

**JIM**:  
Alright, children. Let’s go to work.

And just like that, the session starts—tight, dangerous, alive. Jeff never touches the headset. And nobody asks him to.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – NIGHT IN FULL SWING**

The room pulses with energy, warm lights, cranked monitors, and half-empty water bottles rolling across the floor. What started as a warm-up has quietly become a duel.

**JEREMY** is grinning now; eyes locked with **JEFF** across the room. **BILL**, from the control booth, doesn’t dare touch a fader. He just leans back, watching the chaos unfold with a growing smile. **JIM**? He's barely breathing, foot on the hi-hat pedal, waiting for the downbeat—if it ever comes.

**THE RULES OF THIS DUEL ARE SIMPLE:** No words. No genre sacred. Just feel.

### **ROUND ONE – JAZZ**

Jeremy opens with a smoky Lydian flourish—something late-Coltrane but bent through a Rust Belt filter. Jeff doesn’t blink. He drops in a Mingus-flavored counterline, playful but dense, like he’s daring Jeremy to make it messier.

They weave through dissonance and resolution like old sparring partners.

**JIM** (whispering to Bill):  
This is the part where normal bands break up.

**BILL** (smiling):  
Yeah, well, we’ve never been *normal*.

### **ROUND TWO – AFGHAN WHIGS**

Jeremy snaps the tone knob back and dirties it up. Now it’s all Greg Dulli swagger—half-chords and blue notes, all mood and danger.

Jeff slides into a slinky groove that slithers around it, hips-first. It’s dark, it’s raw, it’s Cincinnati in winter.

**JEFF** (quietly, to himself):  
You wanna brood? Let’s brood.

Bill gives an approving nod. He remembers that Hara Arena show in '92. So does Jeff. So does Jeremy.

### **ROUND THREE – DEF LEPPARD**

Jeremy laughs out loud and kicks in a delay pedal.

**JEREMY**:  
Let’s see what you do with ***THIS*****.

He goes full hair-metal, unapologetically launching into a screaming “*Photograph*”-esque riff. Jeff’s eyes widen—then he grins.

**JEFF**:  
You are an absolute idiot.

He responds with a thunderous bass line pulled straight from “*Rocket*”—syncopated, over-compressed, and so precise it almost feels fake. But it’s not. It’s surgical.

**JIM**:  
God bless the '80s.

Bill hits the record button—silently.

### **ROUND FOUR – THE BEATLES**

A hard pivot.  Jeremy cuts his volume and starts fingerpicking—“*Dear Prudence*” style. Slow. Delicate. Suddenly, the air changes.

Jeff, unphased, drops into a Paul McCartney groove. Think “*Something*.” Warm, elegant, emotional. He hits harmonics that make the room go still.

**JEFF**:  
You sure you want to end with a melody?

Jeremy pauses.

**JEREMY** (softly):  
Only if it’s mine.

They both smiled. The music softens. Fades. A perfect coda.

### **AFTERMATH – SILENCE**

They stop.  Nobody speaks. Jim finally exhales. Bill presses stop.

**BILL** (quietly):  
We keep that?

**JEFF** (shrugs):  
Don’t need to. It’s in our bones now.

Jeremy just nods once. They bump knuckles without looking at each other. Then they go back to setting levels like nothing happened. Because for Commuted?

That was just Tuesday.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – MOMENTS LATER**

The air still sizzles from the duel, everyone catching their breath. Empty cans roll underfoot. Jeremy is half-smiling, hand on the whammy bar, like a gunslinger waiting to see who blinks first.

**JIM** leans forward, twirls a drumstick, and grins.

**JIM**:  
Alright, alright. Enough foreplay. Let’s get weird. I say we try R.E.M.

**JEFF**:  (suspicious)  
Which one?

**JIM**:  
“*It’s the End of the World as We Know It*.”

**BILL** (off-mic):  
You mean the lyrical aneurysm song?

**JIM** (grinning):  
Exactly. It’s a panic attack in sixteenth notes. Let’s go!

Jeff stares at him.

**JEFF**:  
You’re serious?

**JIM**:  
Deadly. I want those high-frequency bursts. Fast hat. Tight kick. Machine-gun snare. Like a woodpecker on Red Bull.

**JEFF**:  
I don’t do woodpecker. That song is wired like a rabbit’s nervous system.

**JIM**:  
Exactly! That’s the point. Let’s see what happens when we don’t sound like us.

**JEREMY**:  
This is going to be a train wreck. Let’s do it.

### **TAKE ONE – CHAOS**

**JIM** launches in. Perfect tempo. Snare like a whipcrack. Hats slicing like scissors.

Jeff tries to jump in—but stumbles. His fingers hit the wrong pocket. He misses the stutter-step cadence. The groove never lands. He frowns. Tries again. Still off.

**BILL** (in the booth):  
You’re ahead of him. You’re pushing.

**JEFF** (mutters):  
He’s the one sprinting through a jungle gym.

**JIM** (grinning):  
You can’t hang?

**JEFF**:  
I can hang. I just can’t float on that. You sound like someone shaking a bag of forks.

**JEREMY** (mock concern):  
Is this how the world ends? Not with a bang, but a bassist imploding?

Jeff glares.

**JEFF**:  
You try riding a groove when the drummer’s doing *Typewriter: The Musical.*

**JIM** (innocent):  
The pattern is in sixteenth note bursts. Classic Berry, sped up. It’s not my fault you breathe in quarter notes.

Jeff walks over, pulls off the headset for a second, exasperated.

**JEFF** (to Cremeans, if he's nearby):  
Tell me again why I agreed to test this during this song?

**CREMEANS** (calmly, from the back):  
Because you don’t want to go deaf. Not because you want to suffer.

Beat.

**JEFF** (dry):  
Noted.

### **TAKE TWO – SLIGHTLY LESS CHAOS**

They try again. Still rough. Jeff hesitates. His lines aren’t wrong, they’re just earthbound. The song wants him airborne.

**JIM** (calling out mid-play):  
Loosen your right hand\! Let it bounce\! You’re trying to lock in. This one’s about barely hanging on\!

Jeff sighs, nods. Still not quite right.

**BILL** (to himself):  
Feels like watching a jazz pianist try to play punk.

**JIM**:  
We can drop it if you want.

**JEFF** (flat):  
No. We finish it.

**JIM** (smiles, then quieter):  
Alright. Then we’ll go again. And again. And again. Like it’s the end of the world… until it isn’t.

**FADE OUT.**

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – LATER THAT NIGHT**

The studio’s energy has drained. Jeremy’s leaning against the wall, idly scrolling his phone. Bill is labeling WAV files. Jim’s pacing the drum rug, still amped, still loose.  Jeff sits alone on a stool, his bass in his lap. The headset rests gently over his ears. He stares at the floor.  Jim hits the click. The hats tick out the opening rhythm of “*It’s the End of the World as We Know It*” again—tight, fast, cutting.

Jeff closes his eyes. Tries to lock in. Again, he misses it. He stops playing. No announcement. Just silence.

**JEFF**:  
Everyone shut up a second.

The room pauses. Bill turns from the board. Jeremy lifts his gaze. Jim stops moving.

**JEFF** (quietly):  
Play the hat line, solo. No snare. Just the top.

Jim obliges. Sixteenth-note bursts. Tight as ever. No variance. He’s nailing it. Jeff listens. 
Frowns. Then he reaches up…and takes off the headset.

**JIM**:  
Something wrong with the tech?

Jeff doesn't answer.

**JEFF** (to Jim):  
Play it again. Same pattern. Real quiet.

Jim does. This time—Jeff’s face changes. His brow furrows. His head tilts like a dog hearing something just out of reach. He taps the beat on his knee, then on his bass.  The timing is right now.

**JEFF** (flatly):  
There it is.

**BILL**:  
What?

**JEFF**:  
I couldn’t hear the hats. Not really. The headset filtered them out—compressed ‘em. Just enough to blur the edge.

**JIM** (confused):  
But they’re up around 14k. You should’ve felt them.

**JEFF** (quieter):  
No, Jim. I should’ve heard them.

Long beat.

**JEFF** (CONT’D):  
My left ear’s worse than I thought. I’ve been leaning on tactile response and muscle memory for two years. That’s why I need Bill’s subfloor feedback in live sets. That’s why I go to the amp instead of the monitors.

**BILL** (softly):  
Jesus.

**JIM** (quieter now):  
You’re losing top-end definition?

**JEFF**:  
Losing? I think it’s gone.

Nobody speaks.  Jeff holds the headset in both hands. Not angry. Not bitter. Just quiet.

**JEFF** (CONT’D):  
I wasn’t missing the beat. I was chasing a ghost I couldn’t hear.

**CREMEANS** (from doorway):  
That’s not failure. That’s the moment you understand the terrain.

Everyone turns. They didn’t hear him come in. He walks over. Calm. Gentle.

**CREMEANS** (CONT’D):  
We’ll find a workaround. You’ve got more awareness than most men twice your age. That’s what matters.

Jeff exhales.

**JEFF**:  
I need time. To retrain. To adapt.

**CREMEANS**:  
You’ll have it.

**JEFF** (nods):  
And I’ll keep the headset. But not for “*End of the World*.”

Jim chuckles, low and rueful.

**JIM**:  
Gotcha.

Jeff sets the headset back on the amp. Then he picks up his bass.

**JEFF**:  
But I'm not done tonight. Not yet.

**JIM** (grinning):  
Then let’s find a groove that doesn’t sound like a fax machine on fire.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – PAST MIDNIGHT**

Empty cans and scribbled notepads litter the control desk. A half-eaten pack of saltines sits on a kick drum. The fluorescent lights hum faintly overhead. It's quiet now—that good kind of quiet, the kind that only exists between people who’ve stopped pretending.  Jeff is back in his stool. Bass in his lap. No headset now—just ears. He’s listening hard.

**JIM** (to Jeff):  
Alright. Let’s go bottom-up.

**JEFF** (nods):  
Give me low tom. Kick. Right foot only.

**JIM**:  
Copy that.

**JIM** launches into a low, slow groove. Kick, tom, floor tom. Everything deep. Everything earthbound. No sharp attack. No high hats. Jeff closes his eyes.  He plays under it. Just tone. He doesn't even look at his fingers.  It’s working.

**BILL** (to himself):  
He’s locked in.

**JEREMY** (softly):  
It’s in his ribs, not his ears.

**JEFF** (without looking up):  
Exactly.

They try three more patterns. Still good.  Jeff’s posture changes. He leans into the neck. Starts to slide. Like he’s rediscovering the joy of resonance itself.

---

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – 1:44 A.M.**

**JIM** (grinning):  
Alright, let’s go sharp. Upper tom, tight snare, hats barely open. Jeremy—play something clean, mid-register.

Jeremy plays a standard funk vamp. It’s got movement, but he stays out of the way.  Jim introduces the pattern. Machine-gun snare bursts, a crisp, militaristic cadence.  **JEFF** falters.  Just a moment. But the groove turns to mud in his left ear.

He stops.

**JEFF** (quiet):  
That one went sideways.

**BILL**:  
What’d you lose?

**JEFF**:  
Snare vanished behind Jeremy’s top-end. Like it dropped through a trap door. I couldn’t tell if Jim was even hitting on the beat or between them.

**JIM**:  
It was dead center. 16ths at 135 BPM. No ghost notes.

Jeff nods. Calm. Clinical.

**JEFF**:  
Yeah. That’s the frequency trench. Somewhere north of 13k, maybe 14\. But if there’s another signal up there—Jeremy’s fingerpicks, synth shimmer, anything—I lose the snare entirely.

He sets the bass down for a moment. Rubs his temples.

**JEFF** (CONT’D):  
It’s not just tone. It’s placement. The more dense the stereo field, the more the beat crumbles.

**BILL** (gently):  
That’s spatial masking. You’re not crazy.

**JIM** (nodding slowly):  
Okay. So… we do it differently.

**JEFF**:  
Exactly.

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – 3:12 A.M.**

Now it’s a lab. Not just a jam.  Jeremy switches guitars—goes down to a baritone tuned to B. He backs way off, playing once every four bars. Jim sticks to toms, ride bell, kick. He does not touch the snare. Bill routes synth output through a sub stack only. The result?

Jeff plays like a fucking cathedral bell.  His tone is thunderous. He’s fluid. He’s free.  No buzz. No strain. No fear.

**CREMEANS** (from the side wall):  
The frequencies are telling you the truth.

**JEFF** (without looking up):  
Yeah. And some of them are lying to me.

**INT. BILL’S STUDIO – DAWN**

No one’s gone home.  Coffee has replaced soda. The air is stale and holy. The gear is scattered like relics after a riverboat explosion.  Jeff sits on the floor now, back against an amp, bass in his lap. The others are quiet. Just listening.

**JEFF** (softly):  
It’s not about quitting.

**JIM**:  
We never thought it was.

**JEFF**:  
It’s about adjusting. Compensating. Listening with more than ears.

**JEREMY** (quiet):  
We’ll play the way you can feel it. That’s the rule now.

**BILL** (from the control booth):  
Jeff… you’ve still got more tone than most players dream of.

**JEFF** (smiling, finally):  
Yeah. It’s the high stuff that’s slipping away.

**CREMEANS** (stepping forward):  
Then let them go. The sky doesn’t need you. The ground does.

**JIM** (grinning):  
That’s the most poetic thing I’ve ever heard him say.

**CREMEANS** (gently):  
I’ve been waiting years to use it.

Everyone chuckles. It feels like family. Jeff lifts the bass again.

**JEFF**:  
Let’s go low. One more set.

**FADE OUT.**

## **INT. BILL’S STUDIO – CONTROL ROOM – MORNING**

The light is too bright.

A sunbeam slices through a tear in the blackout curtain and lands squarely on **BILL**, who’s curled sideways on the leather couch under a fraying flannel blanket. One boot is still on. A crushed protein bar wrapper sticks to his forearm. His synth hoodie is bunched under his neck like a failed pillow.

He blinks awake. Squints. Groans. Then: rage.

**BILL** (muttering):  
No. No, no, no, no…

He bolts upright, matted hair, eyes bloodshot, breath sharp with stale bourbon and bad coffee.  He checks the wall clock. 10:02 AM.

**BILL**:  
I missed the window.

He kicks off the boot. Grabs his phone. No texts from Jeff. No missed calls. The studio is quiet, too quiet. Last night’s gear still sits like abandoned ruins—a bass cable coiled like a sleeping snake. A drying mug ring on the synth rack. Jeff’s headset still plugged in, hanging off a mic stand.

Bill stares at it. Hard.

**BILL** (V.O.):  
It’s not a monitoring problem. It’s not compression. It’s not latency. It’s interpretation.

He stands, slowly. Begins pacing.

**BILL** (V.O.):  
The headset works. It’s brilliant. Too brilliant.
