## **INT. BILL’S GARAGE – NIGHT – APRIL 28, 2022**

Tools on pegboard. Ashtray full. A Strat with two missing strings leans against a mini fridge. There’s an unopened amp box in the corner labeled *Jen’s Birthday*. It won’t be used for a while.

Bill lights a joint with the calm reverence of someone lighting a prayer candle.  Cremeans sits across from him in a lawn chair. Shoulders like busted scaffolding. Staring at the floor.

**BILL**  
You ever seen someone look like that and still be alive?

Cremeans doesn’t answer.

**BILL**  
She’s not dying. She’s starving. There’s a difference. One you fight. The other you watch.

He taps the joint into a mason jar lid. Hands it over.

**CREMEANS**  
She’s never smoked. I don’t know if she even *knows* how.

**BILL**  
She’ll figure it out. Or I’ll come over and teach her. We’ll listen to *Frampton Comes Alive*. I’ll call it a clinic.

Cremeans tries to laugh. Fails.

**CREMEANS**  
She’s not asking for anything. Not food. Not sleep. Not me.

**BILL**  
She doesn’t need to ask. You’re the one who needs to listen.

He opens a small silver tin. Inside: five joints, each rolled like little surgical instruments. Precise. Not sloppy. Sacred.

**BILL**  
One in the morning. One at night. Put on something familiar — *Mr. Rogers*, *The Cosby Show*, I don’t know. Not the *news*. Nothing where people yell.

Cremeans is blinking too fast. Trying not to cry.

**CREMEANS**  
This isn’t what I thought “in sickness” meant.

**BILL**  
It never is.

Beat.

**BILL**  
But I’ll tell you what happened with Charlotte. First night, she giggled. Couldn’t stop. About an ad for Swiffer mops. Second night, she ate a quarter of a watermelon. Third night? She made *me* mac and cheese.

Cremeans looks up.

**BILL**  
Fifth day, she got her period. First one in a year.  I cried in the CVS parking lot like a man who’d just seen God and she was wearing Always with wings.

Silence.

**CREMEANS**  
You’re serious.

**BILL**  
Dead-ass.  And I’d give up both testicles and my left eye for you to get five days like that with Jen.

Cremeans nods. Takes the tin. Holds it like it’s a chalice.

**BILL**  
This ain’t about getting high. This is about staying *here*. Staying human. Staying *wanting things*.

**CREMEANS**  
What do I owe you?

**BILL**  
Nothing. You already gave me two hit songs, a reason to live past 35, and the keys to your house in ‘98 when I had nowhere to go.

**CREMEANS**  
So… nothing?

**BILL**  
Bring her back. That’s the payment plan.

### **INT. KITCHEN – CREMEANS HOUSE – 2:12 A.M. – MAY 6, 2022**

The overhead light is off. The under-cabinet strip light glows faintly yellow.  **JEN** is sitting at the table. Another bowl of mac and cheese half-eaten in front of her. No makeup. No wig. The bones in her face make her look older and younger than she is.

Cremeans walks in barefoot, still awake. Not surprised to see her. She speaks before he can ask.

**JEN**  
I didn’t think it’d work. The weed, I mean. I thought it was just a thing people *say* helps. Like chicken soup or thoughts and prayers.

She pokes the noodles with her spoon.

**JEN**  
But then I took three puffs. Just three. Didn’t even know how to hold the smoke right — I coughed like a teenager behind a 7-Eleven.

(pauses)  
And then the weirdest thing happened.  I remembered bacon.

She chuckles, just once.

**JEN**  
Like, *viscerally*. Like it had parked itself in the corner of my mouth. Bacon on a Sunday. Maple. Thick-cut. I could hear the sizzle. I could taste salt. I almost cried.

Cremeans leans against the fridge, silent.

**JEN**  
You know how long it had been since I wanted anything? Not just food. *Anything*. I didn’t want to wear clothes. Didn’t want to hear music. Didn’t want to kiss you, or talk to the girls, or be seen by anyone.  And then I was high. And suddenly, I wanted *bacon*. And *air*. And *you*.

(beat)

Jesus says He’s the Bread of Life. But sometimes? You need actual bread. With butter. And maybe half a brownie after. That’s not sin. That’s staying alive.

**CREMEANS**  (carefully)  
You want me to cook the bacon?

**JEN**  
I want to *graduate with my daughter*.  I want to outlive the chemo.  I want to eat without wincing, and sleep without sobbing.  I want to want things again.  If bacon’s the start of that, then yes, honey. Cook the damn bacon.

He moves. Opens the fridge. She smiles faintly, watching him.

**JEN**  
You remember when I said I’d rather die than turn the kids’ schoolwork over to you?

**CREMEANS**  
Oh, vividly.

**JEN**  
I lied.  I just didn’t know how to *let go* without falling apart.

**CREMEANS**  
You didn’t fall apart.

**JEN**  
No.  I *melted*.  And now I’m cooling back into something solid.

He brings the skillet down. She watches the flick of the flame.

**JEN**  
Jesus saves, sure. But even Jesus let the bleeding woman touch His hem before He healed her.  She reached for Him.

(beat)

This? This weed? This is me reaching.  Not because I gave up my covenant.  Because I finally understood what it’s *for*.

She wipes her mouth with a napkin. Reaches out. Touches his wrist.

**JEN**  
Make it crispy, okay?

### **INT. CREMEANS FAMILY KITCHEN – NIGHT – 2:13 A.M.**

The light is low, barely enough to cook by.  The skillet is already hot. He’s already moving.  Cremeans doesn’t speak. He can’t. His throat has closed too tight around whatever prayer he was holding.

Jen sits in the corner, hunched in her robe, frail but glowing — **the color has returned to her cheeks.** Faint. But real. Like sunrise behind heavy clouds.

He lays the **first strip of bacon** in the pan.  It sizzles — loud, almost vulgar against the silence.  She watches the grease bubble.  Watches his hands. His wedding ring clinks the skillet edge as he flips it.

Her breath catches. Not from the smoke. From the smell.  **From hunger.** **Real, human, not-death hunger.** She tears up. Doesn't wipe it away.

Cremeans sets a plate gently in front of her — three slices, thick-cut, browned to the edge of carbon. Still, he says nothing. She picks up a piece with trembling fingers.  Bites. And **moans.** *Actually moans.* Then she smiles. Mouth full.  Her eyes say *thank you*.  

His say *please don’t die*.

### **INT. LIVING ROOM – LATER THAT NIGHT**

The stove light is off now. Jen’s asleep on the couch, breathing soft. There are crumbs on the blanket. The empty plate is near her hip. Cremeans is sitting alone, Bible open on his lap, but unread.  He stares at the cross on the wall.

Whispers, barely audible:

**CREMEANS**  
You healed with dirt once.  This isn’t dirt.  But… it worked.

A pause.

He closes the Bible. Gently.  Picks up the lighter from the counter and places it into the drawer.  Not hidden.  Just… placed.

Like something holy that came in an unmarked bottle.

### **INT. CREMEANS KITCHEN – MORNING – 7:12 A.M.**

The kitchen smells like **heaven and hell at the same time**.  Thick, **sinful pancakes** stack high on a platter. Bacon crackles in the oven, not the pan — he couldn’t bear to wake her again. But the scent still dances through the house like a hymn in the wrong key.

And underneath it all, faint but clinging: **Weed.** Not floral. Not spicy. Just **blunt and wrong-smelling** to anyone who’s never needed it.

**SAMANTHA (17)** enters first. Bleary-eyed.  She sniffs. Stops. Eyes narrow.

**SAMANTHA**  
Dad?

**CREMEANS**  
Pancakes. Bacon. Syrup’s warm.

**SAMANTHA**  
Why does it smell like…?  
(beat)  
Oh my God.

**CREMEANS**  
Sit.

She does. Slowly. Suspiciously.

**ABBY (15)** and **RUTH (12)** shuffle in behind. All three girls freeze. Jen isn’t there. The empty plate and a clean fork sit at the end of the table like a sacred relic. Cremeans doesn’t speak for a while. He flips a pancake instead. Adds more butter than usual. He sets the table with too much care.

Then:

**CREMEANS**  
Your mother is eating again.

No one breathes.

**CREMEANS (CONT’D)**  
I gave her something…Different. Not from a doctor. But…It worked.

**SAMANTHA**  
Is it drugs?

**CREMEANS**  
It’s… a plant. Rolled up. Lit.  
(beat)  
It made her hungry. First time in weeks.

The girls glance at each other. Abby already looks angry. Ruth’s chin is trembling.

**ABBY**  
You had Mom smoke weed?

**CREMEANS**  
Yes.

**RUTH**  
Isn’t that a sin?

He stops. The room still smells like contradiction.

**CREMEANS**  
So is letting her starve.  
(beat)  
So is pride. So is fear.  Sometimes we pick the lesser one. And then we pray we were right.

Silence again. Then: the faint creak of the hallway.

**JEN**, in her robe, walks in. She’s not radiant. She’s not strong.  But **she’s walking**. Her hair’s a mess. Her eyes are pink.  **But she smiles.**

**JEN**  
Are those pancakes?

Ruth bolts from her chair and hugs her so hard Jen winces. Samantha sits back down and stares at her plate like she’s trying to do math on the world. Abby’s eyes are still full of fury — but her fork is in her hand.

**CREMEANS**  
Girls.  You’re going to hear a lot about right and wrong in your lives. But this?  This is **necessary.**

The family eats in silence, except for syrup sounds and the tiny, broken sob Abby lets out when her mother finishes the first pancake.

### **EXT. CREMEANS BACKYARD – SAME MORNING, 8:02 A.M.**

Coffee steam ghosts into the chilly air. **CREMEANS** stands near the deck rail in a flannel shirt and socks. He’s not cold. He’s beyond it. **JIM BROWN**, in jeans and a battered bomber jacket, holds a mug with both hands like it’s a talisman. He doesn’t say much — he never has. But he showed up, and that matters. They’ve been quiet long enough that the sound of the wind feels rude.

Finally:

**JIM**  
Bill tell you where he gets it?

**CREMEANS**  
Didn’t ask.  Didn’t care.

**JIM**  
Noble shit.

(beat)

**JIM**  
Kathy would’ve approved. She’d have rolled it *for* her.

**CREMEANS**  (quiet, a breath like a swallowed prayer)  
I know.

Jim sips. Nods toward the house.

**JIM**  
She eat?

**CREMEANS**  
Asked for seconds.

Jim lets out a slow, half-laugh — not at the joke, but at the **absurd mercy** of it.

**JIM**  
Hell. That's something.

He pauses.

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
We all bitched when you left. But none of us were surprised.

**CREMEANS**  
Wasn’t just leaving the band.  It was leaving what we thought life was gonna be.

**JIM**  
Yeah. Well. Life doesn’t care about the setlist.

Beat.

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
You need anything — even if it’s just a night off from being the guy who’s holding it all together…You call.

Cremeans nods once.

**CREMEANS**  
I’m scared, man.

**JIM**  
Of course you are.  That means you’re still in the fight.

They stand together in the cold, the sun struggling to rise behind the trees. **Two men who’ve seen the inside of greenrooms and ICUs.** Jim finishes his coffee. Sets the mug on the railing. Pats Cremeans once, clumsily, on the back.

**JIM**  
Tell Jen she’s got a hell of a tribe behind her.

And walks off across the yard, **no goodbye needed.**

### **INT. KITCHEN – JIM AND KENDALL’S HOUSE – SPRINGBORO, OH – LATE NIGHT**

The house is quiet. Too quiet for a Saturday. Through the window: porch lights blink down a cul-de-sac that used to be soybeans.

**JIM** sits at the kitchen table with his elbows on the wood, fingers laced in front of his mouth. A beer sweats beside him. Untouched. **KENDALL** pads in barefoot, oversized *Commuted* band tee to the knees, hair in a knot. She doesn’t speak yet — just opens the fridge, pulls out a leftover biscuit, and slides it onto a plate.

**JIM**  
She looks like a ghost.

**KENDALL**  
Jen?

Jim nods. Doesn’t move.

**JIM**  
Not the soul. That’s still there.  Eyes still fightin’.  But the body? Goddamn.

(beat)
There’s not enough weight left on her to anchor her to the earth.

Kendall sets the plate down. Sits across from him. Doesn’t touch his hand — just lays hers down near it.

**KENDALL**  
Did she know you saw that?

**JIM**  
Yeah.

**KENDALL**  
Good.

Beat.

**KENDALL (CONT’D)**  
You can love someone, even in pieces.

**JIM**  
She’s not pieces.  She’s…like a house someone tried to burn down, but only the porch caught.  You walk through, and everything looks *fine*, but it smells like smoke and grief.

He finally picks up the beer. Sips once. Winces.

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
This place used to be corn and trees. Now it’s all granite countertops and mailboxes with fake brick.  Nothing stays sacred long.

**KENDALL**  
You do.

He looks at her. Something in his jaw softens.

**JIM**  
I hated it.  The way she smiled at me. Like she didn’t want me to lie and say she’d be okay.

**KENDALL**  
Maybe she just needed someone to witness it.  You’re good at standing still when the world burns.

**JIM**  
I hate being good at that.

Kendall slides the biscuit across to him. He doesn’t touch it.

**KENDALL**  
You’re allowed to.

She leans in, gently — kisses his temple like a benediction. Her lips linger.

**KENDALL (CONT’D)**  
You keep showing up, Jim.  That’s the only miracle anyone needs right now.

And for once, he lets that be true.

### **INT. BEDROOM – JIM AND KENDALL’S HOUSE – SPRINGBORO, OH – NIGHT, CONTINUOUS**

The bedroom is lit only by a soft lamp — the kind bought for ambiance and now used for tension. Jim stands near the dresser, unbuttoning his shirt without urgency. Kendall enters behind him, slow-footed and determined.

**KENDALL**  (soft, but clear)  
You want me to take care of you?

Jim doesn’t turn.

**JIM**  
I don’t think I can come back down tonight.

**KENDALL**  
I didn’t say anything about *you* coming.

He finally looks up, and she’s there — unbothered, unsentimental, but charged. A flicker of something bright behind her eyes.

**JIM**  (quiet)  
Not tonight. I’d just bring the storm in with me.

**KENDALL**  (sighs, sharp)  
So bring it. Let me drown some of it.

(beat)

You think I don’t know what grief sex is? You think I don’t know how to ride a mood like yours until you finally get your voice back?

Jim flinches — not from cruelty, but recognition.

**JIM**  
Not no. Just… not now.

She stares at him, jaw tight. Then nods, once — soldier-like.

**KENDALL**  
Fine.  But don’t act like I didn’t offer the most time-tested marriage counseling known to woman.

She grabs her phone from the nightstand and heads for the door. Pauses.

**KENDALL (CONT’D)**  
I’m going to call Jen tomorrow. See if she wants company.  I’ll bring rice cakes. The kind with fake chocolate, so it feels like rebellion.

She turns back, eyes narrowing.

**KENDALL (CONT’D)**  
Maybe I can get her to eat.  Or laugh.  Or lie about how she’s doing. I’m good with any of those.

**JIM**  
Tell her I said…  
(swallows the rest)  
Just tell her.

**KENDALL**  
I will.

She exits. Jim stands alone, in boxers and nothing else, looking like a man halfway between the man he was and the man he’ll have to become if Jen dies. The silence is thick with things unsaid.

### **EXT. ROADSIDE HILL – OUTSKIRTS OF SPRINGBORO – NIGHT**

Headlights blur into comet trails down I-75. The hum of semis and Honda Civics is constant, like an electric lullaby that no one sleeps through.

Kendall sits on the hood of her SUV, legs stretched long, a 22 oz Coors in one hand, the tab still hissing. Her face is all fury, no filter. She takes a long pull. The beer is cold. The night is colder. Her thighs stick slightly to the vinyl seat where her shorts have ridden up. She doesn’t care. She watches the traffic — north to Dayton, south to Cincinnati — as if it holds the answer to a question she hasn’t asked yet.

**KENDALL**  (to herself, low)  
Jesus, Jim. I offered you a blowjob, not a fucking therapy session.

She takes another sip, bitter now.

**KENDALL (CONT’D)**  
Or maybe I did.

A Honda Civic changes lanes without signaling. She flips it off automatically, just for something to do. Her jaw tightens. Her neck pulses. She wipes at her eyes before they do anything stupid.

**KENDALL (CONT’D)**  (quiet)  
I could’ve helped. I *wanted* to help.

She finishes the beer, crushes the can like it owes her money, and sets it gently on the hood beside her. The wind picks up. She shivers once. Somewhere on the highway below, someone is blasting a song she used to dance to barefoot in her kitchen.

**KENDALL (CONT’D)**  
I’m not mad at Jen. I’m mad at the world.  And maybe a little at myself.  For thinking I could compete with cancer for your attention.

The traffic keeps humming. Life goes on. Kendall just watches it all from her borrowed cliffside like a pissed-off sentinel in running shoes and smeared eyeliner.

### **INT. KENDALL & JIM’S KITCHEN – 11:08 P.M. – THAT NIGHT**

**KENDALL**  
You don’t want help? Fine. I’ll go help someone who actually wants to live.

### **INT. CREMEANS HOUSE – KITCHEN – APRIL 6, 2022 – 8:34 A.M.**

The door creaks open before Kendall can knock twice.

**CREMEANS**, unshaven and wearing pajama pants and a flannel shirt that has known better days, ushers her in with a nod and a grunt. His eyes are hollowed out but kind. A soldier still walking. Kendall steps into the house. The **smell hits first**—burnt weed, buttered popcorn, and some kind of bacon miracle. It’s dizzying and wrong and **weirdly holy**.

Then the **sound**: teen girl voices, low but present, murmuring about coefficients and parabolas. And then—**JEN.**

Sitting at the breakfast table in a **purple bathrobe**, **bare feet** tucked under her, **fuzzy wig** askew, **eyes glassy**, face sunken but somehow still radiant. She’s holding a bowl of popcorn like it’s a Eucharist. She’s already halfway through it. She beams.

**JEN**  
Kendall. You beautiful weirdo. You brought snacks, right?

Kendall stands in the doorway, mouth slightly open. The rice cakes are in her arms like a peace offering from a better-fed civilization. She tries to speak. Fails.  Jen munches. A kernel falls onto her robe. She doesn’t notice. Or doesn’t care.

Behind her, **Samantha**, **Abby**, and **Ruth** are hunched over workbooks at the table. They’re doing math like it’s the only thing holding gravity in place. One of them scratches her head with a pencil. Another blows bubblegum. They barely glance up. Kendall finds her voice.

Jesus, Jen.

**JEN**  
Not quite. Just me.

**KENDALL**  
You look like—

Jen does it for her.

**JEN**  
Like a raccoon got into a hospice center?

**CREMEANS**  
Jen.

**JEN**  
What? I’m dying, not delicate. Let her see it.

You’re eating.

Great. Crunchy sadness. Let’s get weird.

Don’t mind the girls. They’re on Chapter 12 of Algebra II and pretending I’m not dying.

**RUTH**  
We’re not pretending. We’re compartmentalizing.

**ABBY**  
Shut up, Ruth.

**JEN**  
That’s my girls. Future therapists of America.

Kendall swallows hard. Her eyes sting, but she won’t cry. Not now. Not here. Jen crunches. Stoned, skeletal, and **gloriously alive**, if only for this fleeting, popcorn-scented morning.

**JEN**  
Tell Jim thanks for the offer last night.

Kendall’s head snaps around.

**KENDALL**  
He told you?

**JEN**  
Nope. You just did.

**JEN**  
Now, pass me another one of those cardboard coasters. They’re delicious.

### **INT. CREMEANS KITCHEN – MOMENTS LATER**

Kendall is still seated, rice cake uneaten, hands clutched tight in her lap. Jen munches popcorn with surreal ease, legs drawn under her robe like a college kid pulling an all-nighter. And then Kendall sees it—really sees it.

**Jen’s chest**—flat. A towel lining the inside of her robe, barely hiding the truth: **32A breasts, gone.** The curve erased. Her collarbones are sharp enough to slice paper. Her skin hangs at her triceps like someone let the air out. And then—**Jen curses.**

**JEN**  
Shit. I got popcorn in my wig again.

She flicks at it. It sticks. She shrugs.

Kendall blinks. That word. *That word*.  Jen hasn’t said “shit” in her entire adult life. Not even when the bus broke down in Oklahoma, or when she gave birth in 90 minutes and no drugs. *Not even when her mother died.*

**KENDALL**  (quiet)  
You said “shit.”

**JEN**  
Sure did. You want a medal?

**KENDALL**  
You… never say “shit.”

**JEN**  
Yeah, well. Dying makes you reconsider your diction.

Kendall opens her mouth. Closes it. Opens it again. She’s watching the woman who used to quote Psalms during road trips now flick buttered popcorn at her robe like it’s confetti.

**CREMEANS**  (stepping in, gently explanatory)  
It’s…calories. Oil, real butter, pinch of salt. Packs in the density. I use the old skillet. Gets it smoky. Makes her thirsty.

He gestures to a **blender cup**, still half full of something viscous and beige.

**CREMEANS (CONT'D)**  
The smoothies are Whitworth’s recipe. Bengals O-line guy. Protein powder, peanut butter, banana, maple syrup, oat milk. Two gulps, five hundred calories. I did the math.

Jen lifts the cup and toasts.

**JEN**  
To math. And medicinal munchies.

She downs a gulp like it’s whiskey. Wipes her lip with the edge of the robe. Sinks back, eyes half-lidded, *stoned immaculate*.

**JEN (CONT'D)**  
You know… it’s more fun dying slowly when you’re high. Like a sad little Wes Anderson movie. With snacks.

**RUTH**  
That’s morbid.

**JEN**  
You say morbid, I say honest.  Tomato, tomahto.

Kendall swallows the knot in her throat. The kitchen smells like **weed and butter and irreversible decisions.**

**KENDALL**  
I’m sorry I judged him. My husband. Last night.

Jen glances sideways. Doesn’t say anything. Just nods once. Eyes closed now, the robe falling open at the collarbones, revealing nothing but skin and unspoken grief.

**KENDALL (CONT'D)**  
You look like you’re barely here. But you’re still eating.

**JEN**  
That’s the trick. Stay until the last verse.

She crunches another kernel.

### **INT. CREMEANS KITCHEN – MID-MORNING**

The popcorn bowl is nearly empty. A rogue kernel clings to the collar of Jen’s robe like it’s afraid to leave the party. Kendall has finally stopped staring. Jen, now halfway into smoothie number two, raises her pinky like she’s sipping tea at Buckingham.

**JEN**  
“**<font color="#ff0000">Judge not, lest ye be judged</font>**.” That’s in red letters, Kendall. From the Man *Himself*.

**KENDALL**  (nods, chastened but not cowed)  
Fair.

**JEN**  
I figure if I’m gonna get judged, I might as well stack the deck. So, here I am. Preaching…and drinking my husband’s delicious smoothie.

**CREMEANS**  (quietly)

I’ll make calls. I’ll harass state reps. I’ll even brave Reddit. If I can get my student loans forgiven, I can get a cancer patient some weed.

Jeremy is slumped in the backseat, knuckles white around the seatbelt. He’s sweating, skin the color of skim milk.

**JIM**  
You good, Jer? Talk to me.

Jeremy lets out a dry, shaky laugh.

**JEREMY**  
Right now? You remember that Pete Rose quote? “I’d walk through Hell in a gasoline suit to play baseball.” Medium-rare, that’s me. But, uh… not even the Tigers could cook this much sweat out of me.

He wipes his brow, forces a grin.

**JEREMY (CONT’D)**  
Just… get me south of the border and I’ll stop marinating.

Kendall reaches back, pats his knee.

**KENDALL**  
Hang on, hero. I’ll get Jen her card. I’ll even let you skip the next strip search.

Jim cracks a grin, rolling out into the Michigan dusk.

**JIM**  
Next time, we’re doing this legal. And no more singing about Ohio till we hit Findlay.

Kendall lifts her phone, camera ready again.

**KENDALL**  
We lived to tell it. Let’s get this medicine home.

The sun dips low as the truck heads for the highway, three friends lighter by a burden and heavier by a plan.

## **INT. JIM’S TRUCK – I-75 SOUTHBOUND – FINDLAY EXIT – NIGHT**

Neon and flags blur by. The “**Flag City, USA**” sign flashes in the headlights—an accidental punchline after everything they’ve risked and smuggled back from Ann Arbor.

Jeremy is half asleep, head tipped against the glass. Kendall watches the rows of cheap hotels, shuttered diners, and the big “THANK YOU HEALTHCARE HEROES” banner over the hospital parking lot.

She mutters to herself, but Jim hears every word:

**KENDALL**  
Land of the Free, Home of the Brave. But you need a mask to buy a coffee, and you need paperwork to buy a plant that keeps a woman from puking herself into a grave. Some freedom.

Jim’s jaw tightens, but he keeps driving.

**KENDALL (CONT’D)**  
Best they can do for Jen is a goddamn feeding tube. That’s the “solution.” Not the stuff that lets her eat pancakes and bacon. No, that’s illegal. But a plastic port in your gut is *medicine*.

She shakes her head, red hair a wild halo in the dashboard light.

**JIM**  
Feeding tube’s easy to bill, easy to track. Weed? That’s just a story in some committee report.

**KENDALL**  
She wants to taste breakfast again. She wants to eat with her family, not with a pump. And I’m supposed to be grateful it’s legal somewhere?

Jim glances at her, silent agreement.

Outside, the “Flag City” flags shudder in the night wind, bright and pointless, waving for a freedom that stops at the county line.

Kendall pulls the weed tighter against her chest, holding it like a secret. She stares out the window, voice hard:

**KENDALL (CONT’D)**  
If this country really gave a damn about the brave, Jen would be able to buy her medicine at the Kroger, not out of a damn cookie tin.

Jeremy stirs, eyes opening, catching only the end.

**JEREMY**  
Amen to that.

They ride on, past waving flags and locked doors, a little more determined and a lot more furious.
