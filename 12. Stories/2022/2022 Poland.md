**INT. WARSAW – HOTEL CORRIDOR – MONDAY MORNING**

Fluorescents flicker overhead. **Jeff Evans**, fifty—tall, heavy, unhurried—walks down the echoing corridor of a hyper-modern hotel, earbuds in, flight bag dangling. His phone buzzes with another cryptic message from **CD Projekt RED**: *“8:30, Konferencja 3rd Floor. Please bring any field notes from ‘17.”*

He grunts, pockets the phone. The city outside is all glass, cranes, spring haze, trams, and the low burr of traffic. Warsaw’s in bloom but he doesn’t notice.

Jeff’s bored. Not the kind of bored that comes from nothing to do—he’s got emails, contracts, another endless spreadsheet of licensing rights to approve. But the bone-deep, marrow-cracking boredom of a man who’s seen the inside of too many studios, the death of too many friends, and the endless, low drumbeat of “what next?”

He’s here because CD Projekt wants soundscapes—wants him to “consult,” which means: stand in a glass box and tell them their rain isn’t wet enough, their gunfire too thin, their nostalgia all wrong. They’re cagey. They talk about “creative opportunity” and “immersion,” but Jeff’s done this before. He knows what they really want: that old Commuted magic, the feeling no one can quite manufacture but everyone wants bottled and on their product launch.

Commuted 2.0 died last year with Gilley. Four left. No singers. No plan. Just old men and regret, spread out like bad patches in the road.

He rides the elevator up, head full of nothing. The years stretch behind him—a band, a marriage, a grave, a thousand tracks no one will ever hear.

He used to think fifty would feel different. Like maybe wisdom would show up, or at least a second act worth playing. But fifty is just another day; another hotel; another contract for a project that’ll launch and fade and get patched in six months.

What’s the plan for fifty?

He snorts, answers the question in his head:

*Survive to fifty-one. Get his daughter through college, no loans, no drama. Maybe, if God or luck’s in a good mood, keep the house out of foreclosure one more year. Don’t let the family history catch up.*

But musically?

He stands in the hallway as the double doors open—CD Projekt’s producer, young and eager, all English vowels and perfect hair.

Jeff shrugs. *Musically?* He’s got nothing but ghosts.

He’ll make their city sound cold and empty and old. He’ll sell them the ache, the static, the yearning. He’ll give them exactly what they want: the soundtrack of someone who still can’t decide if he’s the last survivor or just the last one who knows how the song was supposed to go.

And that, for now, is enough.

**INT. CD PROJEKT OFFICES – WARSAW – MONDAY MORNING**

Jeff slumps into the padded chair, stylus in one hand, reading glasses perched on the end of his nose. The table is glass, spotless, covered in color-coded folders and a stack of new NDA paperwork. He signs, doesn’t bother reading—it’s all the same in any language.

The producer—thirty, crisp suit, unnervingly optimistic—starts the pitch: *“We are considering remastering The Witcher, first game. You are familiar? There is… room for audio improvement. Your work on Wild Hunt is still revered here. Your candid feedback is appreciated.”*

Jeff’s already flipping through his notes, not looking up.

**JEFF**  
Look, you and I both know this is a 14-year-old game, and if you start now, it’ll be eighteen by the time it ships. You’ll get headlines for nostalgia, and maybe some new money from Americans who can’t remember what they were doing in 2011. But the audio? First Witcher’s sound design was—well—primitive. Not even up to the standards of Witcher 2, much less Wild Hunt.

The producer looks startled, then faintly defensive.

**PRODUCER**  
We know the limitations. Technology, budgets, licensing… you think we should aim for Wild Hunt levels of fidelity?

Jeff shrugs, finally making eye contact—bored, direct, nothing left to prove.

**JEFF**  
Depends. Are you keeping the current creative direction? The Wild Hunt worked because it embraced *its* world—gritty, lush, orchestral, big on folklore. The first Witcher? If you keep that original tone, you’re remastering mediocrity.

The room gets a little quieter. The junior translator stops typing.

**JEFF (CONT’D)**  
If you want advice? Don’t just do a gloss. Play with it. The theme’s loss of memory, right? That’s Geralt’s journey—discordant, alien at first. Let the music and the sound reflect that. Start weird. Broken. Give the player anxiety. Only later, as things fall into place, bring in the familiar themes. Don’t give them all the nostalgia up front—make them earn it.

The producer hesitates, scribbles a note, tries to recover the sales pitch.

**PRODUCER**  
We… hadn’t considered that degree of thematic transformation. The plan was—incremental improvement. Safe.

Jeff leans back, unimpressed.

**JEFF**  
You want “safe,” you don’t need me. You want “memorable,” then use the tech you’ve got now. Play with expectation. Let the player be lost—then reward them with recognition. Memory as a soundscape. Otherwise, you’re just slapping 4K textures on a corpse.

The room is silent. For the first time, the producer looks—almost—excited.

**PRODUCER**  
You will stay for the next round of discussions? We… would prefer you not go home.

Jeff glances at his phone—no new messages from anyone who matters. He sighs, nods once, half-grin slipping out.

**JEFF**  
I’ll stay. But only if you let me play with “discordant.” Otherwise, I’ll just go listen to traffic.

A nervous laugh—relief in the air.

**PRODUCER**  
We… will be in touch.

Jeff settles in, ready for the next round. If he’s going to do this, it’ll be on his terms. He’s too old to be polite about nostalgia.

**INT. CD PROJEKT OFFICES – CONFERENCE ROOM – CONTINUOUS**

The producer, emboldened, tries for small talk:

**PRODUCER**  
So, Commuted—your main project? Are you recording again?

Jeff snorts, shakes his head.

**JEFF**  
No, my main project is blood. Got a daughter in college and a son about to go. That’s my work—getting them through without debt. Not quite rich enough to buy a land grant college yet, but give me time.

The junior dev blinks, scribbles, then leans in, uncertain.

**JUNIOR DEV**  
Land grant? What is—this?

Jeff waves him off, not in the mood to teach American history.

**JEFF**  
Doesn’t matter. Means “public college,” more or less. Point is, it’s expensive, and that’s my real full-time job now.

He settles back, gaze going flat, tired but unashamed.

**JEFF (CONT’D)**  
Commuted? There’s only four of us left. No singer. Kath died in 2012. Gilley last year. Band’s not a project, it’s a support group with instruments.

The room quiets a little; even the interpreter’s tone drops.

**JEFF (CONT’D)**  
Truth is, I miss writing the most vile shit I could think of, calling it “art,” and having people actually *like* it. These days, my day job is being Dad, and occasionally dabbling for video game companies who want a little piece of the old poison.

He flashes a sardonic grin—old wolf’s pride, unwilling to apologize.

**JEFF (CONT’D)**  
You want something brutal, broken, or beautiful, I can still do that. Just don’t ask me for happy endings or comeback tours.

The producer, now understanding the boundaries, nods slowly.

**PRODUCER**  
We want honest. This is… honest.

Jeff grunts, looking out at the Warsaw morning, already moving on.
