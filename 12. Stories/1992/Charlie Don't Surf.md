## **INT. THE RENAISSANCE HOTEL LOBBY – CHICAGO, ILLINOIS – 2:13 P.M. – JULY 1992**

Gilley’s leg bounces like a snare drum on its fifth take. He’s slouched in a red velvet armchair that feels like a leftover from a bad Prince video, but he doesn’t care. He’s got the sunglasses on—indoors, because yes, he’s that guy today—and a sweating glass of Diet Pepsi in one hand. The other holds a cassette tape labeled simply: *“Hilton – Mix 7.”*

Across from him, wedged stiffly into a matching chair, is **MORRIS GARRITY**, mid-40s, trenchcoat journalist type even in July, working on a pad like he’s drafting the Magna Carta. Garrity smells like aftershave and wet paper. He’s been asking the same question, three ways, for the last twenty minutes:

**MORRIS:**  
But what’s the *point*? I mean, sure, it’s catchy, but what are we supposed to take away from a track like “*The Hilton*”? There’s a girl. There’s a riot. There’s—I don’t know, a hotel fire?

**GILLEY (without looking up):**  
You ever fuck someone who lied about their name, their age, and their star sign, and still made you feel like the most honest version of yourself?

**MORRIS (clears throat):**  
I—don’t think that’s relevant to—

**GILLEY:**  
Then you don’t get the song.

**MORRIS:**  
That’s not exactly an answer.

**GILLEY (grinning now):**  
Sure it is. You just want a *thesis statement*. That’s your problem. You think albums are essays.

**MORRIS:**  
They aren’t?

**GILLEY (leans forward, sunglasses glinting):**  
No, sir. They’re *confessions.* And sometimes they’re revenge. *69 Reasons* isn’t some treatise on youth culture. It’s the sound of getting kicked out of your own life and trying to sneak back in through the fucking gift shop.

**MORRIS:**  
That’s very colorful. But—

**GILLEY (cutting him off, almost sweet):**  
Do you know what our sound is about, buddy? Kind sir?

**MORRIS:**  
I’m listening.

**GILLEY:**  
It’s about that day we found out we couldn’t do the score for *The Breakfast Club*. That was the dream, right? That slow-motion shot where the kid pumps his fist and the bass kicks in like God said, “You’re allowed to feel things now.”

**(beat)**  
But nah. Some LA dipshit’s cousin got it.

**(leans back, taps his sunglasses)**  
And then, I went on this bender. Like, full annihilation. Drank three liters of Coke and half a handle of Jack, watching [_Apocalypse Now_](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ePTpg_qJ5Gs) on VHS, thinking, “Okay, fine. Let’s pivot. Let’s *burn* something instead.” But even that gig was taken. So now what?

**(smiles like a bomb under velvet)**  
Now we make music for the in-between. The kids who weren’t in detention *or* the jungle. The ones who fucked up before breakfast and still made curfew.

**MORRIS (scribbling desperately):**  
That’s very… evocative. But how does that tie into the *structure* of the record?

**GILLEY (stands suddenly, towering):**  
Structure? Buddy, [*The Hilton*](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cgk7s9HiGqQ) burns down. You want structure, go read Le Corbusier.

**(pauses, pulls tape from jacket pocket)**  
You want honesty? Here. The scream at the end of “[*After Hours*](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sUJOw5MFVFg)”? That’s real. That’s Kathy in one take. No effects, no overdub. Just heartbreak with a reverb pedal.

**(steps close now)**  
You hear the synth on track nine? Bill did that on a church organ. Barefoot. At 3 a.m. After his mom died. Don’t talk to me about structure.

**(quiet now)**  
Talk to me about *grief*. Talk to me about not getting the movie deal and making the fucking movie anyway—with blood and spit and duct tape. That’s what we did.

**MORRIS (looking rattled):**  
Okay, I think I’ve got enough for the piece…

**GILLEY (sits back down, calmly):**  
You don’t. But you’ll write it anyway.

**(takes off sunglasses finally, eye contact like a blade)**  
And just so you know—*The Hilton* hits number one next week. We’re betting on it. You’ll be writing about us a lot more after that. Better learn the sound, Garrity. Before it eats you.

**CUT TO BLACK.**

**INT. RENAISSANCE HOTEL – LOBBY BAR – CHICAGO – 3:42 P.M. – JULY 1992**

Kathy is trying to smile. She’s failing, but in a quiet, elegant way. Her shirt is oversized plaid, her eyeliner is running from heat and nerves, and her boots are too hot for July. She looks like a girl trying to disappear into a barstool.

Across from her sits **NINA PARK**, arts reporter for the *Chicago Sun-Times*. Sharp cheekbones, a tape recorder, and the poise of someone who’s seen five ingénues too many this week. She wants something—an angle, a quote, a spark. Kathy is giving her… none of it.

**NINA:**  
So, the band is blowing up. And yet—correct me if I’m wrong—you don’t sing lead on any tracks?

**KATHY (V.O.):**  
Correct her?  How about taking a mallet to her face?  She's *never* listened to the record!

**KATHY (too fast):**  
I do too! Just not on the singles.

**NINA (gently):**  
That seems like a waste.

**KATHY (defensive):**  
Not really. I write. I scream sometimes. I’m on “*After Hours*.” That scream’s mine.

**NINA:**  
It’s… a very good scream.

**(beat)**  
But what do you *want*, Kathy?

**KATHY (blank):**  
What do you mean?

**NINA:**  
In the band. In the music. From all this.

Kathy hesitates. Looks past Nina, toward the bar mirror. She sees Gilley in the reflection—standing in a hallway with sunglasses on, still pacing from his own disaster interview. He looks like an unlit firecracker. Sarah Malcolm, the label's line manager, is beside him, lips tight, watching *everything*. Kathy swallows.

**KATHY:**  
I want to play. That’s it.

**NINA:**  
That’s never it.

**KATHY (softly):**  
It is for me.

## **INT. HOTEL STAIRWELL – SIMULTANEOUS**

**BILL DAVENPORT** stands six feet from a red fire alarm switch, looking over his shoulder like a teen vandal. His fingers are calloused, precise. His face is unreadable. There’s a cigarette behind one ear and a torn copy of *Keyboard* magazine in his back pocket.

**SARAH (offscreen, whispering):**  
Now, Bill.

He doesn’t hesitate.

**BILL:**  
Blessed are the peacemakers.

He pulls the alarm.

### **INT. RENAISSANCE HOTEL – LOBBY BAR – CONTINUOUS**

The siren begins. Shrill, immediate, metal-on-glass panic.

Nina flinches. Kathy doesn’t.

**SARAH (sweeping in like a Broadway stage manager):**  
I’m *so* sorry, Nina. We’ve been advised to evacuate. Electrical panel issue on floor 9.

**NINA (already gathering her bag):**  
Are you *serious*?

**SARAH:**  
Bill Davenport is never unserious about sabotage.

**KATHY (murmuring):**  
He does it with style.

**SARAH (to Kathy, low and fast):**  
You’re going to meet her again. At the museum. 6 p.m. Modern wing. No plaid. Say something true this time. And for the love of God, pretend you like being seen.

**KATHY (mumbling):**  
I don’t.

**SARAH:**  
Pretend harder.

### **EXT. HOTEL SIDEWALK – FIVE MINUTES LATER**

Fire trucks are arriving. The band and press are scattered like windblown flyers. Kathy stands alone near a hydrant, hands in her pockets, watching the chaos with a quiet, inward grin. Gilley walks by, still grinning, sunglasses firmly in place.

**GILLEY:**  
You owe Bill a pizza.

**KATHY:**  
But he likes anchovies!

**GILLEY:**  
He’s a complicated man.

She nods, glancing up at the noise and commotion, and for just a second she looks like someone *important*. Someone unforgettable.

She just doesn’t know it yet.

## **EXT. SOLDIER FIELD – CHICAGO – LATE AFTERNOON – JULY 1992**

It’s hot. Midwestern-hot. Thick, immovable air and the kind of sun that gives your sweat lines sweat lines. Soldier Field looms behind them, this towering coliseum of muscle and myth. The grass around the parking lot is burnt to hay. Cars glitter like oil pans under siege.

Kathy’s perched on the low concrete lip of a planter, boot toe worrying a crack in the pavement. Gilley’s standing with his shirt untucked and his sunglasses halfway down his nose like he’s about to deliver a lecture no one asked for. He’s holding a bottle of water like it’s vodka and offering wisdom like it’s contraband.

**GILLEY:**  
How do you handle the press?

**(beat)**  
You have a story.

**(pauses, checking that she’s listening)**  
Have that be your baseline. Something you can say in your sleep. Something they can’t twist even if they try.

**(he leans closer, fevered)**  
And then make shit up.

**KATHY (eyebrow cocked):**  
Make shit up?

**GILLEY:**  
Absolutely. You take a little bit from column A, a little bit from column B, stir, shake—if you want. Not stir, if you’re a James Bond fan. You got yourself a martini.

**(glowing now)**  
Or a bomb.

**(shrugs)**  
Doesn’t matter if it’s true. Doesn’t matter if it happened last week or in a dream or to your cousin. Just *feed the beast.* Give them a little bit of yourself. Keep most of yourself in the pocket.

**(gesturing vaguely toward the stadium, the city, the mythology of it all)**  
We tell stories in our craft, right? In the beat. In the melody. In the lyrics.

**(points to her chest)**  
You gotta do it all over again, sister. In the press.

**KATHY:**  
I don’t know. You’re awful good at this. I’m not.

**GILLEY (sighs, squinting at her like she’s missed the joke):**  
That’s because you’re wearing underwear.

**KATHY (deadpan):**  
Excuse me?

**GILLEY:**  
I said what I said. Try going commando for this upcoming interview. See how that feels. Might inspire you. Loosen you up. Let the breeze blow some truth up your spine.

**KATHY (rolling her eyes):**  
You’re being filthy.

**GILLEY (nodding solemnly):**  
You are correct.

**(beat)**  
But also, I’m not wrong.

**(leans in, conspiratorial)**  
You think they liked me better today because I had great answers? Hell no. They liked me because I was *dangerous*. You—you’re a locked box. And that’s beautiful on vinyl. But out here?

**(he gestures to the press tent, the camera trucks, the vultures with notebooks)**  
You gotta *flash a little lockpick*. Even if it’s fake.

**KATHY (soft, but not dismissing him):**  
You think I can do that?

**GILLEY (grinning):**  
Nope. I *know* you can. The question is: will you?

**(he taps his temple with two fingers, then flicks the sweat from them like he’s anointing her)**  
Also, pro tip: they can’t quote you *exactly* if you whisper it while biting your lip.

She snorts despite herself. He winks. Somewhere in the distance, a guitar riff kicks up from the soundcheck inside the stadium, like a war cry wearing a leather jacket.

## **INT. SOLDIER FIELD – WOMEN’S BATHROOM – 4:12 P.M. – JULY 1992**

It’s cool in here. Not clean, not pleasant, but *cool*—blessed relief from the blast furnace of the asphalt outside. Kathy stands at the cracked porcelain sink, elbows locked, staring at her reflection like it’s a riddle.  Her flannel shirt is wrinkled. Her hair’s doing its own thing. The eyeliner she reapplied in the van is already starting to melt. But it’s not the look she’s worried about.

It’s the underwear.

Specifically: *whether or not to remove it*.

She glances down at herself—not with judgment, not with vanity, but like a general studying the Warsaw Pact. Her jeans are button-fly. The fabric clings from the heat. Underneath: plain cotton panties, purple with a fading waistband. She’s had them since high school. They still do the job.

Except right now, she can’t figure out what the job is.

**KATHY (muttering):**  
If Jeff had said it…

She wouldn’t have hesitated. Jeff’s tone would’ve made it clear—practical, serious, discreet. It would’ve been an order. A code. Take them off. Put them in your purse. Go commando. Let the breeze touch you. Own it.  But it wasn’t Jeff.

It was Gilley.

Which meant: metaphor? Or dare? Or joke dressed in sincerity? He couldn’t order her. He didn’t own her body, didn’t dictate her stage costume or her soundcheck ritual. But still. Gilley *knew* things. Knew the game. Knew how to give the illusion of candor while keeping his real self laminated behind mirrored shades.

So maybe it *was* more than a suggestion. Maybe it was a permission slip.

**KATHY (to herself):**  
Fuck it.

She slips into the stall. Unbuttons. Slides the panties down.  They’re damp with sweat. Ordinary. Not sexy. Not wild. Not anything worth archiving.  She holds them in her palm like a note passed in class.

The door creaks open.

**SARAH MALCOLM (offscreen):**  
That you, Espich?

**KATHY (stall door still shut):**  
Yeah.

**SARAH:**  
I need to piss and I need a moment. Don’t talk to me yet.

**(beat, toilet flushes)**

**SARAH (now at the sink, washing her hands):**  
Okay. What is it.

Kathy steps out. Doesn’t meet Sarah’s eyes. Just holds up the panties.

**KATHY:**  
Gilley said I should go commando.

**SARAH (snorts immediately):**  
Jesus Christ.

**KATHY:**  
But I don’t know if it was a metaphor. Or a bit. Or—

**SARAH (dry):**  
It’s Gilley. It’s *always* a bit and a dare and a metaphor. That’s why it works.

**KATHY (quiet):**  
I don’t know how to do this, Sarah. The press. I can’t fake it like he does.

**SARAH (taking the panties from her hand like it’s the most normal thing in the world):**  
Here. Give me these. I’ll put them in my bag.

**(beat)**  
Listen. Just go out there and try. You’re not going to be comfortable. That’s fine. That’s real. This will seem unnatural. But now? You’ll be more worried about accidentally flashing your pussy than saying the wrong thing.

**KATHY (grimacing):**  
That doesn’t sound better.

**SARAH (shrugging):**  
It’s not. It’s *different*. That’s all we need. Just try it. And if it doesn’t work?

**(she opens the stall, ready to toss the panties into her tote)**  
We try something else next time. We’re making a version of you that they can *understand*. Not swallow whole. Just… chew on.

**(smiles)**  
And hey. Maybe showing a little labia *is* the metaphor.

Kathy stares in the mirror. She doesn’t look freer. Not yet. But maybe she looks *wilder*. Like something unzipped inside her brain. Like the story’s about to begin.

**KATHY (under her breath):**  
Okay.

She walks out of the bathroom.

No panties.  No plan.  Just a purse full of doubt and a whisper of breeze between her thighs.

## **INT. CHICAGO ART INSTITUTE – MODERN WING – INTERVIEW ROOM – 5:52 P.M. – JULY 1992**

It’s one of those half-formed interview setups—soft lighting, plastic chairs, AV club energy. No PR firm, no handler in the corner, just the faint buzz of an HVAC unit and a bowl of off-brand mints. A camera on a tripod. A woman with a notepad.

Kathy walks in on her own. Bare thighs in heavy denim. Hair down. No visible nerves. Just the faint hush of her inner monologue tightening its shoelaces.

**JOCELYN CATES**, arts and features reporter for the *Chicago Sun-Times*, rises to greet her. White linen blouse, perfectly creased. The kind of smile that’s been to war.

**JOCELYN:**  
Kathy. Thanks for meeting with me.

**KATHY:**  
Sure.

**(They sit. The camera clicks on. Jocelyn presses *record*.)**

**JOCELYN (gently, sliding the knife in early):**  
I’ve been listening to *69 Reasons*—really sitting with it. And what keeps striking me is how deeply female it feels. Not just from a musical standpoint, but emotionally. The pain. The anger. It’s… coded.

**KATHY (half-smile):**  
Coded?

**JOCELYN:**  
The scream on “*After Hours*.” The imagery in “[*Payment in Kind*](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KRcd4GEomwc).” There’s a wounded, almost maternal kind of trauma. Can you walk me through what it’s like to write pain into music? From a woman’s point of view?

**KATHY (laughs, short and dangerous):**  
You think pain is *maternal*?

**JOCELYN:**  
I mean—it’s a language. Yours is uniquely feminine. It’s what makes you different in a band full of men.

**KATHY (blinking once, slowly):**  
Different?

**JOCELYN (softly):**  
Yes.

**KATHY (voice low, surgical):**  
I’m different because I bleed?

**JOCELYN:**  
Well—

**KATHY (sharper now):**  
Because I scream in tune?

**(beat)**  
You think the band lets me in because I cry pretty?

**JOCELYN:**  
I think it’s powerful to hear a woman own her trauma—

**KATHY (interrupting):**  
You don’t know a thing about my trauma. And you’re not gonna find it under a microscope labeled *female-coded*.

**(beat)**  
You’re digging for menstrual metaphors in a fuck song. You’re trying to decode a scream that doesn’t belong to you.

**JOCELYN (still composed, but clipped):**  
Our readers appreciate nuance—

**KATHY:**  
Your readers *masturbate* to nuance. You’re asking me to put my uterus in a guitar case and take it on tour so you can write a clever paragraph about “the soul of Midwestern femininity.” You think I scream because I’m *female*?

**(She stands, yanks off her mic)**  
I scream because *you won’t stop asking questions like that*.

**JOCELYN:**  
That’s very—

**KATHY (full volcano now):**  
Don’t. Don’t call me evocative. Don’t say “raw.” Don’t use the word “wound” unless you’re ready to crawl into it.

**(closer now, eyes narrowed)**  
You want a pull quote?

**(pause, voice thick as blood)**  
*Sometimes being a woman feels like being the last fuckable corpse at a goddamn open casket wedding.* There. Type *that* with your manicure.

She drops the lav mic on the chair like it’s a severed ear. The sound rings in the mic like a gavel.

**JOCELYN (still blinking):**  
I need a cigarette.

## **EXT. BEHIND THE MUSEUM – LOADING DOCK – TWILIGHT – JULY 1992**

Chicago’s golden hour slides down the skyline like honey off a spoon. Behind the Art Institute, the alley is half-shadow, half-sunset, streaked with the last gasps of heat and adrenaline. Kathy stands with one boot braced against a loading ramp, arms crossed, heart still pacing like it’s waiting for an encore.

Gilley’s already there. Leaning on a forklift like it’s a chaise lounge. Watching her with that dumb, bright smile that means either he’s proud of her or planning to make it worse.

**GILLEY (genuinely, not mocking):**  
That was fucking spectacular.

**KATHY (narrowing her eyes):**  
You *saw* that?

**GILLEY (pops a piece of gum, grins):**  
I *watched* it. I *felt* it. I almost lit a cigarette halfway through and I don’t even smoke. You went thermonuclear, kiddo. Jocelyn’s probably in a bathtub somewhere writing fan fiction about your death glare.

**KATHY (genuine confusion now):**  
But I… I lost it. I wasn’t composed. I wasn’t professional.

**GILLEY:**  
No, no, no—don’t do that. Don’t kill the myth you just gave birth to.

**(steps toward her, more serious now)**  
You don’t see it, do you?

**KATHY:**  
See what?

**GILLEY:**  
How *much* you contributed to the chaos. You didn’t just survive the trap. You detonated it. That room is part of the lore now. They’ll be whispering about *“The Kathy Tape”* in press lounges for the next decade.

**KATHY (quietly, unsure):**  
But I could’ve done it with more grace.

**GILLEY (tilts his head, mock pondering):**  
Lemme ask you something. Do grown-ass women do media stand-ups with no underwear?

**KATHY (groans):**  
Okay, we’re back to *that*.

**GILLEY (grinning):**  
No, obviously. They wear tasteful slacks and give statements about “artistic journeys.” You? You went in raw. *Chafed,* even. You gave them *you.* No filter. No PR polish. Just fury and fact.

**KATHY (finally cracking a laugh):**  
It *was* uncomfortable.

**GILLEY (finger raised like he’s invoking scripture):**  
*Discomfort pisses excellence!* You think I *like* half the shit I say in interviews? I sweat through my shirt before every one. But I remember that discomfort. That pulse in my throat. That little voice that says, “This could go bad fast.”

**(leans in, almost a whisper)**  
Next time? Don’t skip the panties. But do *remember* the chafe. That burn? That rage? That’s your fuel. You don’t need to undress to be honest. Just let the discomfort do the talking.

**KATHY (softer now, considering it):**  
The horror.  The horror.
