**JEFF**

## **INT. MOTOR LODGE COFFEE SHOP – VANDALIA, OHIO – 7:18 A.M. – OCTOBER 1992**

Pull it off? It's already in motion, Kathy. Remember that contract I ramrodded through two years ago? The one you guys thought was too aggressive? ## **INT. VANDALIA WAREHOUSE – BACK ROOM – 2:47 P.M. – OCTOBER 1992**

The back room of the Vandalia warehouse is a claustrophobic shrine to the road—walls papered with yellowed setlists, a sagging couch exhaling cigarette smoke and stale beer, and a card table littered with Mountain Dew cans, crumpled Marlboro packs, and a dog-eared copy of *Modern Drummer* with Neil Peart’s face half-obscured by coffee rings. Outside, the afternoon sun bakes the parking lot, but in here, it’s a perpetual twilight, the kind of half-light that breeds secrets and tempers.

Alright, Bill, let’s cut the foreplay. Cremeans says we pick the producer, no Island bullshit this time. I’m throwing out Butch Vig—dude’s got *Nevermind* under his belt, knows how to make raw sound huge. We need that for whatever the hell this album’s gonna be.

**BILL**  
Vig’s a safe bet, sure, but he’s everybody’s darling right now. We’d be fighting half the industry for his time, and what do we get? A sound that’s big but predictable. Albini? Man, you’re swinging for the fences. He’d record us in a damn basement with one mic and call it art. I love the grit, but we’re opening for Metallica, not playing CBGB’s. Bill stops pacing, plants his hands on the table, leaning in close enough that Jeremy can smell the coffee on his breath, bitter and black, no sugar to soften the edge. The legal pad crinkles under his palm, a faint rustle in the quiet, the bulb’s hum the only other sound, a low drone that underscores the rising heat of their words. You’re missing the point, Jer. We don’t need a bridge—we need a fucking battering ram. Albini would let us sound like *us*, not Sarah's wet dream. But fine, you want a name? How about Flood? Did Depeche Mode, U2—knows how to make weird sound massive without losing the soul. Or is that too out-there for your mainstream heart?

**JEREMY**  
Flood’s a wizard, sure, but you’re dodging, Bill. You’re throwing out names like you’re reading Cremeans’s Rolodex, but I know you. You’ve got that look—like you’re scheming to steal the keys to the kingdom. Spit it out. You want to produce this thing yourself, don’t you?

I know our sound better than any hired gun. Vig, Albini, Flood—they’re outsiders. I’m blood. I’d carve out every ounce of what makes us Commuted, no filter, no compromise.

Jeremy’s grin widens, but it’s not mocking now—it’s respect, laced with the kind of mischief that comes from knowing you’ve hit a nerve. He leans back against the couch, arms crossed, studying Bill like a chess player sizing up a bold move. The bulb swings, casting his face half in shadow, half in light, his eyes glinting with the spark of a man who loves the fight as much as the win.

**JEREMY**  
Fuck, Bill, that’s ballsy. You’re not just the gear guy—you’re gunning for the throne. But producing? That’s a whole different beast. You’ve got the ear, sure, but you've never sat in the big chair, made the calls that could sink us or send us to the stars.

His shirt’s untucked, hair wild from a nap in the van, but his eyes are sharp, catching the tension like a dog sniffing blood. He pauses, chewing loudly, the sound obscene in the charged silence, then wipes his mouth with the back of his hand, leaving a smear of grease.

**JIM**  
What’s this, a cage match? I could hear you two bickering from the parking lot. Let me guess—producer talk? Bill’s trying to sell you on some avant-garde nutcase who records in a cave?

Bill groans, rubbing his temples, but there’s a flicker of relief in his eyes—Jim’s chaos is a welcome distraction, a chance to regroup. He flips his legal pad shut, as if shielding his notes from judgment, and leans back against the table, arms crossed again, defensive but not defeated.

**BILL**

Jim raises an eyebrow, taking another bite of his burrito, chewing thoughtfully as he crosses to the couch and flops down beside Jeremy, springs squeaking under his weight. The room smells of salsa now, sharp and tangy, mingling with the stale beer and sweat, a snapshot of band life—messy, raw, alive. Jim twirls a drumstick, pointing it at Bill like a conductor’s baton, his voice casual but cutting, the kind of honesty that comes from years behind the kit, watching everyone’s egos collide.

**JIM**  
Bill producing? That’s a change-up, alright. Could be killer—fresh blood, someone who bleeds Commuted. But, man, you’ve never done a full album. Live mixes, sure, you’re a wizard. Jeremy nods, slow and deliberate, his grin softening into something almost conspiratorial, like he’s already plotting how to make this work. He picks up his pick again, twirling it between his fingers, the motion hypnotic, grounding, as he leans forward, voice dropping to a low, persuasive drawl.

**JEREMY**  
Co-produce—now that’s a play. You get your hands dirty, shape the sound, but you’ve got a veteran to catch the fumbles. I’m not sold on you solo yet—too much risk, and we’re already betting the farm on this Metallica tour. But you and, say, Flood? Or even Vig, if we can snag him? That’s a record I’d kill to hear.

Bill’s jaw tightens, a muscle ticking there, his ambition warring with pragmatism. **BILL**  
Co-produce, huh? Fine, I can work with that—for now. But I’m not just tweaking knobs, Jer. I want my name on it, my sound in it. This album’s gotta be us, not some polished turd Island can shove down radio’s throat.

That’s the spirit, Bill. Fight for it, but don’t burn the house down. We’re already half-crazy—this album’s our shot to prove we’re crazy *and* brilliant. Now, who’s got a smoke? I’m out, and Kathy’s probably chain-smoking the last of mine.
