**First Album, First Half: Track-By-Track Breakdown**

**“Sixty-nine Reasons”** as the opener sets the album’s mood with the authority of a guillotine blade, not a greeting. This isn’t a jam band lazily riffing; it’s a fatal demonstration, a warning shot. Let’s break it out, flesh and bones:

---

### 1.**Sixty-nine Reasons (Instrumental Title Track – Album Opener)**

It starts with [[4.1 BILL DAVENPORT character bible|Bill]]:  
That *direful, ethereal synth*—reminiscent of a dying cathedral organ run through ice-cold delay, echoing between piano patches so crystalline you can hear each note’s ghost. Sometimes, it drops to pure, naked piano—no sustain pedal, just the hammers and the cold. You get a sense of mourning and warning, something already lost before the song begins.

[[4.2 JEREMY GILLEY character bible|Gilley]] creeps in with harmonica, playing not for sweetness but for atmosphere, howling through cracked teeth, sharp and almost metallic. It isn’t rootsy—it’s apocalyptic blues. You don’t get comfort; you get premonition.

Then the bass. [[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff]] doesn’t enter so much as *detonate*: Chops that punch holes in the floor, a throbbing, bombastic attack, sometimes so overdriven you think the speakers will fry. He finds the spaces in Bill’s synth and slams them shut, hammering syncopation and pure menace. Every note says, “This is what’s coming. *Run*.”

[[4.6 JEREMY EVANS character bible|Jeremy]]? He’s the madman architect. His guitar chords aren’t simply wicked—they’re calculated sabotage:  He laces the dreamy, floating sections with jagged, angular stabs, never letting the piece rest. Sometimes he syncs with Jeff, and in those moments—skull-splitting, circus sledgehammer—*everything* explodes, the kind of wall-of-sound that makes you clutch your chest and grin in terror. When they land together, it’s not just a climax; it’s a threat fulfilled.

Kath and Gilley provide the glue and the shrapnel—Kathy’s fills are often high, unpredictable, sometimes barely there until she rips a line that suddenly flips the atmosphere from lullaby to terror. Gilley’s harmonica returns not as comfort but as siren: a call to arms, a dare to survive.

Most of the time, the track feels *dreamy*:  It’s that uneasy kind of dream, the one where you’re always one step from the drop. The synths and harmonica lull you, and the bass lurks, ready to bite.

Then comes the *execution*:  No warning, just the two brothers (Jeff and Jeremy) slamming chords together—everything in the mix peels back and *bam*, your skull is open, your brain is on the floor, and the dreamy world is gone, replaced with pure, merciless noise.

The final moments:  A return to the ethereal—Bill’s piano, cold and unresolved, one note hanging too long, then silence.

---

**A Jam Session?**  
Never. This is an ambush, an act of violence performed with surgical precision. The band isn’t noodling—they’re making an example. If you survive “Sixty-nine Reasons,” you’ve earned the right to listen to the rest.

The rest of the album’s filth, humor, and chaos is all the more potent because you’ve been spiritually executed first. You’re not a passive listener; you’re a survivor.

**That’s the track. That’s the statement.**  First-time listeners will *never* mistake this band for another.

### 2. **After Hours**

*Grunge American Primitivism, female vocals, reverb-heavy*  
A scene right out of a Midwest after-dark office, the narrator sneaks into an affair with a married man, knowing exactly what it is and not flinching from the ugliness or the thrill. The lines are blunt—*“we will fuck and hide,”* *“what he needed at home, he found in my arms.”* There’s agency but also raw, unresolved longing, a cycle of regret and pleasure. The chorus is a thief’s confession, torching any sense of victimhood or innocence, refusing absolution. This opener tells the listener: nothing’s off-limits, and if you want romance, get ready for the full price.

### 3. **Not Taken Kindly**

*Grunge American Primitivism, female vocals*  
Here, the sexual dynamic turns blunt and confrontational. The opening is straight-up explicit—*“licking up his shaft, oh, I’m right on track / Need it in my mouth, no way I’m turning back.”* But it’s not porn; it’s indictment. “Post-nut clarity” stings, the narrator finding herself more irritated than turned on, calling her lover out for being a “flyboy so lordly, dumbass in my bed.” The song moves from raw pleasure to that dry-mouthed, soul-scouring dissatisfaction: the post-sex letdown is as much the point as the climax. Vulnerability weaponized as sarcasm, sexuality with teeth.

### 4. **Initiating Connection**

*Grunge, bedroom pop, erotic female vocalist*  
If the first two tracks punch, this one breathes. There’s hope, possibility, and dreamy sensuality: *“Slip your fingers inside me, feel the heat / In this moment, we can’t be beat.”* The explicitness is still there, but now it’s threaded with actual romance and mutual pleasure, heartbeats synced up, city lights and late nights serving as backdrop to two people opening up (literally and figuratively). This is the album’s most optimistic early track: the sex is connection, not just transaction or power play.

### 5. **Eyes Lost in Desire**

*Prog Acoustic Texas Blues, erotic female vocalist*  
This one’s bluesier, the groove slower, the details lush and sticky—sweat, skin, heat, the world falling away. Desire is a fever, something chased and swallowed, the lyrics reveling in every messy detail: *“From your cock on my breasts, to my eager mouth / Every moment with you, it’s what love’s about.”* But again, it’s more than surface—there’s nostalgia, sweetness, and almost a yearning for transcendence through the body. The pleasure here is immersive, total, but haunted by impermanence.

### 6. **The Left Hand**

*Synthwave, energetic, retrowave, erotic female vocals*  
Sex and power become a masquerade. Desire is painted in neon shadows, the protagonist described as a “queen at the gate,” love as a blade that “cuts so deep.” Every line is heightened, mythic, a fever dream of lust, secrecy, and ritual. The eroticism is symbolic as much as literal: “wrapped in crimson silk,” “her fingers weave a tapestry of sin.” It’s a late-night drive through synthy danger, where pleasure is a risk and the body is both temple and battleground.

###  7.**Tangled Up**


*Psychedelic Dream Pop, 80s alternative, Irish chiming guitar, breathy female vocals*  
The soundscape turns brighter, weirder, and wetter—literally. “Feel the squirt, hear your sound,” “I want you to come, I need you to arrive,” the lyrics insist. But it’s not just shock for shock’s sake: this is a celebration of pleasure unashamed, with a hazy lakeside memory, sex in the open, and an open invitation for everyone (the listener included) to stop hiding. Love is messy, sticky, gorgeous, a storm that nobody survives dry. The hook is a chant, a dare, and an exhale all at once.

### 8. **Paradise in Pixels**

*Native chanting, tribal/ambient, female vocals*  
A change of texture: the erotic turns digital, a “selfie” is the altar, and nakedness becomes pixelated, both a confession and a dare. “So here’s my selfie, passion in a frame / In this paradise, I’ll never be the same.” The “don’t show my face” refrain nods to the modern anxiety of exposure—sex, memory, identity all get filtered through the screen. Playful but also honest: this is what modern lovers do, this is how desire gets mediated now. Every verse dances between exhibition and vulnerability.

### 9. **Payment in Kind**

*Psychedelic dream punk, female vocals, pop*  
Back to earth, and back to bed. “Are you hard, Jeff?”—Jodi’s voice pops up as a character, the whole lyric a play on sexual negotiation, shared memory, and the old, ever-weird economics of desire. There’s laughter and boldness, but also the sense that these exchanges—pleasure for affection, body for intimacy—are both games and real stakes. “On my back I paid, was my mouth sublime?” If “Paradise in Pixels” is about how we curate desire, this one is about the cost, the question marks, the aftertaste.

### 10. **The Hilton**

*Lush pastel Synthwave Trance, female vocalist*  
Love in a hotel room: urban, secretive, neon-lit, not pretending to be romance but still full of hope. The narrator wants connection, but it’s all performance and nerves—“Are you hard again? You know I want you so.” Every detail is charged with the tension between longing and routine, between old flame and new spark. The chorus—“Feel the pulse, racing hearts collide, I’m gonna show you everything I hide”—wraps confession in a sugar rush, the sex as much a reclamation as a surrender.

### 11. **I'll Own Your Moment**

*Bedroom shoegaze, 80s alternative, Irish chiming guitar, female vocals*  
A sweetly filthy slow-dance through memory and the here-and-now. “Succubus spread / Anointing your member in my bed,” and yet it’s not all carnality—it’s about trust, ownership, and that terrifying intimacy of letting someone see you want, and be wanted. The ending is as explicit as the rest—“Shooting from your balls, white ropes cascades”—but it lands not as punchline, but as emotional release. The power here isn’t in the act, but in the fact the narrator can claim it, own it, and then ask for more.

---

**In Short:**  
The first half of this record is a defiant, delirious, unblinking journey through sexual experience from a woman’s point of view: power claimed, power given, regret, laughter, anger, endless wanting, and a total lack of apology. Musically, it’s a blender of grunge, dream pop, synthwave, blues, and ambient tribal—matching the chaos and beauty of its lyrical subjects. The sexuality is not just explicit but *owned*, not just performed but claimed and recounted with memory, wit, and self-knowledge. The record doesn’t seduce you; it *grabs* you, shakes you, laughs in your face, and dares you to stick around for Side B.

### 12. **Sweet Memories in a Dive Bar**

*Breakstep Synthwave, female vocals, orchestral soundtrack*  
The night explodes into memory—*“getting naked in his truck, sweet memories, we’re ready to fuck.”* There’s a brashness to the scene: bachelorette wildness, a little Southern Gothic, everything bright, tipsy, and a little dangerous. The chorus is full-throttle freedom and girlhood joy, but every verse knows the thrill comes with a price—what happens in the dive bar *doesn’t* stay there, it becomes a story, a legend, a minor wound and a badge of pride.

### 13. **To Excess**

*80’s Synthwave, sultry female vocals*  
Ambition, hedonism, and the knowledge that every boundary is just another dare. *“We are the phoenixes, rising from ashes… I’m gonna get fucked, let’s do it two times / In the back of this bar, erotica ain’t no crime.”* This is the hangover song that doesn’t regret, the one where pleasure *is* the rebellion. The bar is both sanctuary and danger zone, and the song celebrates every ruined climax and messy victory.

### 14. **It Has a Wow Finish**

*Grunge American Primitivism, female vocals*  
A cinematic trip: Casablanca referenced, wild hearts, daring adventure, and a trio’s tangled craving—*“What if we dance beneath the moonlit sky / Just a glance, just a kiss, we won’t deny.”* But the real magic is in the resignation: “I hope it was worth the wait.” This is the first hint of tenderness creeping through the noise. The wildness is sweeter, the longing more complicated.

### 15. **Next to the Ice Cream Shop**

*Grunge American Primitivism, female vocals, reverb*  
Surreal, dirty, and funny in a way only real debauchery ever is. “Asp massage” is the euphemism, but nothing’s hidden: *“Two hundred for a massage to unwind, in this poisoned promise, our souls entwined.”* There’s an explicitness so complete it becomes almost tender—a transaction becomes ritual, shame becomes a joke, and pleasure is a thing both bought and earned, breasts glazed in herbal oils and lust.

### 16. **Xmas Party**

*Grunge American Primitivism, mathcore, female vocals*  
The supply closet as confessional booth. Every forbidden office-party hookup, every breathless moment of risk—*“Meet me where the shadows lie, in the depths where no one’s nigh, flesh against flesh, a primal scream.”* But there’s violence under the surface, a sense of desperation and the ache of being forced back into “decay” at the end of the night. It’s brutal and a little sad, and it refuses to clean up after itself.

### 17. **Buying a Cookie**

*Alt-country Surf Rock, female vocalist*  
Out comes the mischief, the flirtation, the half-in-joke bravado: “These are fair game, my breasts laid bare / With every touch, I ignite the air.” This is not the cookie you find at the bake sale—this is raw sexuality as an act of self-determination. ### 18. **Snap and Sizzle**

*Grunge American Primitivism, female vocalist*  
The bathroom selfie anthem. “Snap! Got the pic, thrill runs through me,” the lyrics confess. There’s exhibitionism, sure, but also an undercurrent of loneliness, of needing to be seen, needing to be remembered. Lust becomes performance, and the digital world (and last weekend’s memories) crowd in. “Panting, I gasp, can you feel this need?” The rawest parts of yourself, immortalized and sent through the ether.

### 19. **Scientific Method**

*Acid Techno Psybient, female vocals, Cincinnati bass*  
Pleasure is now an experiment, a series of escalating dares. *“Lust is a game we play / With every touch, you lead the way / Sara, you’re my sweetest thrill.”* The seduction here is mutual, deliberate—two partners prodding, testing, chasing the next high, refusing to look back. It’s a game with its own rules, the stakes rising with every chorus.

### 20. **Entry**

*Acid Techno Psybient, female vocals, Cincinnati bass*  
Raw, direct, and almost hymnlike in its eroticism. *“Say my name, whisper low, in the heat, let your semen flow / Came so hard, lost in bliss, feel the pulse, oh, how could I miss?”* Every detail is unvarnished, every climax celebrated—this is a love song that’s not afraid to get sticky. Desire is not cleaned up or hidden; it’s lived out loud, in bright fluorescent light.

### 21. **Come Inside Me**

*Synthwave Trance, Cincinnati bass, female vocalist*  
This is the album’s closing statement—a feverish, hypnotic chant, looping, building, refusing to end with anything but fullness and repetition. *“Do you want, do you want that baby? / Come inside me, feel the warmth of love.”* It’s both a dare and a plea, erotic and oddly sweet, and it ends the record on the most intimate, confessional possible note—sex not as spectacle, but as homecoming, even as it spirals through question and answer, demand and surrender. The pulse never stops; it’s a perpetual motion machine of want and mutual possession.

---

## **Summary: The Second Half of “69 Reasons”**

If Side A was an explosion, Side B is the decadent afterburn. The themes deepen: wildness gives way to reflection, the sex grows weirder and more ritualized, the bravado cracks to let in doubt and sweetness. This side explores transaction, risk, public and private selves, bodies as currency and as home. It closes the loop—no apology, no retreat, only full-throated, honest claim to everything that happened, every squirm and every climax.

The band’s sound keeps mutating: synthwave and grunge and acid-tinged club beats swirl into something that refuses to settle. There’s a pride in mess, a faith in pleasure, and just a hint of bruised, post-party tenderness.
