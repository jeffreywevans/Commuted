*Originally published in NME, April 2016*

**Commuted: "You're Meant to Lose" (Green Rope, 2016)**

**5/5**

By Rhiannon Slate

---

*You're Meant to Lose* dropped without warning. No press tour. No teaser campaign. No two-years-out Spotify bread crumbs. Just a blood-black press release faxed to three outlets with a quote from Jeremy Gilley that read, simply, "We have no interest in nostalgia. The future is failure."

And from failure, they’ve made a fucking triumph.

Let’s get one thing violently straight: Commuted is not trying to make friends. They’re not back to make peace with the industry, their fans, or each other. *You're Meant to Lose* is the sound of a band lighting the fuse from both ends. It’s genius at war with itself. It’s [[4.2 JEREMY GILLEY character bible|Jeremy Gilley]]—who writes nine of the twelve tracks solo—taking the reins with a jaw clenched so hard it broke the bit. [[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff Evans]] polishes the thing like a coroner dressing a body. [[4.5 KATHY ESPICH character bible|Kathy Espich]] is absent, and her absence is *felt*, not just spiritually, but narratively. And in a twist so cruel it tastes biblical, the backup vocalist on the closing track is none other than Dawn Lingerfelt—fired by Kathy in 2011 for getting too close to Jeff. The ghosts don’t whisper on this record; they punch through the drywall.

So what does it sound like?

[[Coma Kiss]] opens the album with slow-syrup dread. It’s a benzo-blessed hymn, begging the pharmacist for salvation. By *[[11.15.3 Paper Doll|Paper Doll]]*, Gilley is dissecting the commodification of the female self, using melodies so cold they chime. *Ghost Signal* is the cry of a man talking to the dead through corrupted electronics. You feel it in the wires.

But it’s *[[11.15.5 Dumb Call (2 AM)|Dumb Call (2 AM)]]* where the record bares its teeth. Allegedly a recovered Kathy demo, finished and re-voiced by Gilley, it turns the drunk dial into ritual exorcism. “Hate myself, love the thrill,” the narrator moans. And you believe them. The sin isn’t in the sex; it’s in the return.

*[[Detuned Television]]* and *[[11.15.9 Asbestos|Asbestos]]* are unrelenting. The former loops addiction and betrayal over screaming drums and synth pulses that feel like seizures. The latter takes the album’s thesis—you’re meant to lose—and detonates it: “Light a match, light a fuse / What you have, you’re meant to lose.” It’s not despair; it’s acceptance.

The quietest devastation comes with *[[Fading In Neon]]*. A portrait of a woman outliving her myth, still dancing, still dressed to be seen, still alone. It’s Kathy, maybe. Or all of them. It might be the best ballad Gilley’s ever written.

And then there’s *[[Thirty Day Supply]]*. The closer. A duet between Gilley and Dawn that doesn’t ask permission from the past. Industrial, furious, alive. “In the depths of all my battles, I find strength to survive / Through the scariest prescriptions, I summon the will to thrive.” It's the sound of Gilley admitting he's never going to be okay. And making music anyway.

Make no mistake: if you bring up 2013's *Be First, Be Smarter, or Cheat*, they’ll walk out. The band has closed that chapter with a blowtorch. *You're Meant to Lose* doesn’t erase it—it buries it. This isn’t a comeback. It’s an exorcism, an execution, and a goddamn resurrection.
