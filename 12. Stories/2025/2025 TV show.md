**PITCHFORK REVIEW — 'Lean In'**

By Lila Mahoney | March 17, 2025 | Score: 8.7

---

There are songs that ache. And then there are songs that apologize.

“Lean In” closes _I Can Take It_ like the last unsent voicemail from a ghost you still sleep with. Sparse, sensual, and terrifyingly still, it’s the latest track in the Commuted canon that doesn’t erupt—it simmers. The emotional climax isn’t a lyric. It’s a pause.

The song was allegedly reconstructed from a damaged cassette Kathy Espich left behind in 2010. Her original vocal was unusable; rumor has it Riley Keough stood in, pitch-shifted and ghosted, at the request of Jeff Evans himself. No one involved will confirm that officially. It doesn’t matter. The vocal lands like breath on glass.

What makes “Lean In” so devastating is what it refuses to resolve. The chord structure threatens a chorus it never fully delivers. The bass line pulses like a heartbeat one breath before heartbreak. The bridge—a hushed lyric-less piano solo—feels like someone leaving a room and closing the door behind them.

It’s also the most feminine song they’ve ever recorded. Not in subject, but in posture: the act of waiting, offering, surrendering without submission. That alone makes it radical within Commuted’s legacy, where power usually comes with teeth.

There’s been debate over whether this track should’ve stayed in the vault. Whether resurrecting it was an act of tribute or exploitation. _I Can Take It_ was already a complicated release. “Lean In” makes it radioactive.

But art isn’t consent. Art is what survives after consent is revoked.

And in that context, “Lean In” becomes something mythic: a posthumous open hand. Soft. Final. Unflinching.

**RIYL:** Mazzy Star, early St. Vincent, A24 grief-core, late-night confessions, that one voicemail you still haven’t deleted.
