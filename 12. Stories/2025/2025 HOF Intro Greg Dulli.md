## **INT. ROCKET MORTGAGE FIELDHOUSE – CLEVELAND, OHIO – ROCK & ROLL HALL OF FAME INDUCTION – NIGHT – 2025**

House lights dim. Spotlights swing across the rafters like searchlights at the end of the world. The industry is here: Bono in tinted glasses, Grohl grinning like it’s a roast, Brandi Carlile in a tailored velvet suit. The teleprompters glow coolly.

Then the voice of the showrunner, godlike and half-bored:

**ANNOUNCER (V.O.)**  
“Please welcome to the stage… frontman of the Afghan Whigs… Greg Dulli.”

Cue the slow, reverent clapping of peers.

## **INT. STAGE – CENTER PODIUM – SAME MOMENT**

**Greg Dulli** walks out in a charcoal suit he probably slept in. Shirt unbuttoned one-too-many. Stubble like regret. There’s a hip flask in his inner pocket—he hasn’t touched it in twenty minutes, which counts as sobriety.

He grips the podium. Leans in. Doesn’t blink.

**GREG DULLI**  
I’m not the obvious choice.

(A beat.)
They could’ve asked anyone. Their producers. Their lovers. Their therapist. Bono, even—he _offered_, for Christ’s sake. But they asked me.  Four kids from Dayton who used to think I was Jesus with a nosebleed. Then thought I was a prick with a god complex. Then thought I was both. And they were right.

So here we are.

(Greg scans the room. Zero warmth. Just reverence and terror.)

I remember hearing _69 Reasons_ for the first time in full. Not a track, not a live bootleg, not that one fabled radio broadcast from WYSO with the gain blown to hell. No—I mean really _hearing_ it.

It was 1992. I was naked in a bathtub in the Hotel Café in Los Angeles. Hungover. Half-hard. Half-sad. The girl had left. The minibar was raided. And someone had left a burnt CD on the bathroom floor—Sharpied: _“You Still Don’t Get It.”_

(Greg laughs, bitter and real.)

So I hit play. And Kathy started screaming.

And I _got it_.

Not in the cerebral way. Not the liner notes, not the charts, not the pitchfork retro-cred bullshit. No. I got it in my body. In my _fucking spine_. Like a lightning rod to the soul.  I got what it meant to be out-sung, out-fucked, out-felt. I got what it meant to be replaced in someone’s mythology.

(Greg glances backstage. Where they wait.)

Because that’s what Commuted was. Myth. And miracle. And menace.

Let me tell you what happens when a band _beats you at your own gospel._ You either evolve—or you die bitter.  I chose both.

(Greg sips water, maybe bourbon. He squints like he’s trying to see 1992 again.)

Commuted wasn’t just good. They weren’t just loud. They weren’t just raw. They were _right_. They were the sound the moment needed before it knew it needed it. They were prophecy, duct-taped together with midwestern fury, menstrual blood, and Pro Tools.

I remember the first time I saw Kathy live. 1994. Somewhere near Toledo. Tiny venue, too loud for its own fire code. She got on stage in a goddamn _funeral dress_ and made twenty thousand people feel like they’d been fingered by a banshee and forgiven by a god they didn’t believe in.

And Jeff Evans? He plays bass like it’s a restraining order. You get close, you get hurt.

Bill Davenport made synths sound like _evidence_. Like something you'd take to court and say: “This is why I cried.”

Jeremy Evans? The kid could stare you down with his guitar like it owed him child support. He didn’t strum. He _accused_.

And Jim? Fucking hell. Jim Brown hits drums like he’s pounding the last nail into your coffin. Precision of a surgeon. Soul of a chainsaw.

And they _never_ asked for permission.

(Greg leans forward now. The room is dead quiet.)

That’s what scared the shit out of us. We were veterans, and they played like prophets with nothing to lose. They broke all the rules we didn’t know we were still following.

They released _That Look of Release_ and went _diamond_ without sucking the industry's dick. Kathy posed nude for the cover and didn’t explain it once. That’s power. That’s not sexuality. That’s _ownership_.

They made us all _look safe_. They made success look vulgar. They made failure look fucking erotic.

And they paid for it.

You think being that good comes free? You think bleeding on a mic and letting the world watch doesn’t cost something?

You saw the fallout. The silences. The fights. The… absences.

(Greg pauses. The room knows. Kathy’s death hangs in the air like smoke.)

I don’t need to say her name for you to feel it.

But I will.

Kathy Espich.

May she haunt every man who doubted her.

(Greg takes a breath that’s not clean.)

Tonight is supposed to be about history. About legacy. About inducting a band into a Hall of marble and memory.

But I say _fuck that._  Tonight is a confession.

I _didn’t_ get it at first. I was too proud. Too threatened. Too old. I looked at them and saw the end of the world.  Turns out—it was just the beginning of a better one.  And now? I’m not just a fan. I’m not just a friend. I’m a _witness._

(Greg lifts his glass. Not water. Not wine. Something darker.)

So raise one with me.  To the band that never begged.  To the sound that never flinched.  To the woman who never blinked.  To _Commuted._  Hall of Fame, sure.  But more than that?

_Hall of Fucking Fire._

(The house erupts. The band steps out. Greg steps back. And maybe—just maybe—he lets go of the last twenty years.)

_Fade to black. Sound of applause like thunder underwater._
