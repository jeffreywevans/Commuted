## **INT. BACKSTAGE PRESS ROOM – ROCK & ROLL HALL OF FAME – LATER THAT SAME NIGHT**

The red curtain behind them still ripples from the band’s exit. The podium smells like old coffee and newer bourbon. Journalists click record. There’s a line of mic flags in front—NPR, Spin, Pitchfork, god help us, even People.

**JIM BROWN** steps up first, already halfway through a granola bar and 100% out of fucks. He wipes his hands on his jeans. Looks directly into the live stream camera like it owes him child support.

**JIM BROWN**  
I am *the* James Brown.

(Laughter. Nervous. But real.)
Y’all can call me Jim.

Just to be clear, I am the *other* member of the Hall of Fame named James Brown.  I like to think I’m a hard worker. But no, I’m not the hardest working man in show business.  That was the *first* guy. The screamer. The cape. I just play drums and try not to lose time when Jeff starts improvising in seven.

(Silence. He softens.)
I want to take a second to acknowledge the two of us in Commuted who aren’t here tonight.

Our original singer, Kathy Espich. That’s Kathy with a K. Hi, Kathy.  
No, we don’t have any blow for you. Sorry.

(He frowns. A flicker of real pain.)
Cremeans says hi. He’s got water. And my close personal friend, Jeremy Gilley. That’s a hard G, media folks. *G-I-L-L-E-Y.*  I spelled it for the A.I. because I’m tired of journalists feeding transcripts to bots instead of sending *humans* to the show.

(He deadpans to the NPR guy in the front row.)
But hey, bygones are bygones, right?

He clears his throat. Sips water. No one breathes.

**JIM BROWN (cont’d)**  
Look, I know this is supposed to be a legacy thing. Hall of Fame. Capital letters. Decades of work.

But for us? This isn’t a closing chapter. It’s… a stubborn bookmark.  We are what happens when six weird kids from Ohio decide not to quit.  And when one of us did, we kept going anyway. Not because we’re tough.  Because we *owe* her. Because Kathy taught us that grief isn’t a weight. It’s a *drum beat.*

Jim stares down. His hand tightens around the mic.

**JIM BROWN (cont’d)**  
And Jeremy Gilley—wherever you are right now, probably smoking in some Parisian dive bar or rewriting our history in a notebook you’ll never show us—

(He taps his chest.)
We saved your seat, motherfucker.

(Then, suddenly—smiling again.)
And now, I think Jeremy Evans has something to say.  If we’re not careful, he might *ramble.*

(Steps back. Chews his granola. Smiles like the only sane man in a burning building.)

**CAMERAS FLASH. JOURNALISTS SHIFT.**

And then…Jeremy steps up. Quiet. Clenched. But glowing just the same.

---

**INT. BACKSTAGE PRESS ROOM – CLEVELAND – ROCK & ROLL HALL OF FAME – CONTINUOUS**

**JEREMY EVANS** steps to the podium like he’s late to church but brought the sermon anyway. A man in a black blazer and beat-up Chuck Taylors. He’s holding a laminated setlist from 1990 in one hand and a trembling water bottle in the other.

He stares at the crowd of media, VIPs, and ghosts.

**JEREMY EVANS**  
I stopped drinking on January the 4th, 2021. Two reasons.

First, because my best friend, Jeremy Gilley, died 365 days before that.

Second, because I was determined to be alive and lucid enough to hear my brother Jeff’s Hall of Fame acceptance speech. I figured it would be full of spite, venom, piss, vinegar, whatever bodily fluid you associate with genius and emotional repression.

(Small laughter. Jeremy doesn’t smile.)
But you know what? I’m gonna let bygones be bygones.

Tonight is probably the happiest moment of my life—behind a *really good* bowel movement in 2003, my 30-year marriage to Charla, and the births of my two kids. You can fight me on the order, but they’re all on the podium.

I’m eternally grateful to every human who ever stopped, paid for a ticket, screamed through a verse, bought a T-shirt, held up a lighter, shook my hand, or said, “Holy shit, you’re Jeremy Evans?”

(Shrugs.)
And to all of y’all who *stole* our album, or streamed it for three-tenths of a cent on Spotify?

You can kiss my ass. Preferably the left cheek. The right’s for legends only.

(The room *howls*.)
But seriously?I couldn’t be happier. I had *fourteen million album sales* before I was legally old enough to drink. Think about that. I was 17 with a buzz cut and a dream. I thought I’d end up running a beer warehouse, you know? Forklift, steel-toe boots, moving pallets from column A to column B.

(Beat.)
Turns out I *did* blow out my back by 40… but I got to play the electric guitar doing it. Instead of delivering kegs.

(He raises the old setlist in his hand.)
And this? This right here?  It’s from Gilley’s garage. June 1990. First show. Six songs. Two of them didn’t even have names. Kathy wrote “fucking ripper” in Sharpie over the title. That became track six on *69 Reasons.*

(Looks up, voice steady.)
Everybody here—y’all know who you are. You know what you meant. You know when you held us up, or held us together, or held us to the fire.

Thank you.

I am grateful. And this might surprise you…

(grins manically.)
…but I wonder if *Bill’s* got anything to say.

(He steps back. One slow nod. Like a man laying down arms at the top of a mountain.)

**CAMERA TURNS.**

**SPOTLIGHT SWINGS.**

**BILL DAVENPORT** rises from the row like a priest who just remembered his sermon’s been written in blood and drum loops.

---

**INT. BACKSTAGE PRESS ROOM – ROCK HALL OF FAME – CONTINUOUS**

The room’s warm now. Lit not by LEDs but by memory, sweat, and three decades of debt coming due. All eyes shift to **BILL DAVENPORT**, who stands slowly like gravity’s been trying to keep him seated. His black jacket is slightly askew. His fingers twitch like they’re still scrolling MIDI, even now.

He steps up to the mic, clears his throat, and doesn’t look at the cameras. He looks at the band.

**BILL DAVENPORT**  
Okay. So I didn’t think I was gonna talk. Which is a dumb thing to think, considering I’m me.

(Crowd chuckles. Bill raises one finger.)
But let me start with this: *extreme* gratitude. I am… sincerely, stupidly grateful.

Grateful for weed.  Grateful for Oregon.  Grateful that I can finally buy a pre-roll without lying to someone about my sciatica.

(More laughter.)
Now look—I’ve been arrested for marijuana possession 14 times over the last 30 years. And if you think that slowed me down, you don’t know what it’s like to produce a #1 record on two hours’ sleep and a court summons in your back pocket.

But hey. Water under the burnt bridge.

He leans forward, eyes sharp behind the glasses he refuses to upgrade.

I’ve made peace with a lot.  Except one thing.

I do wish I had more privacy. Not because I hate the attention—but because sometimes I don’t know who I am unless there’s a synth in front of me. Unless there’s a light in my face.

I’m a private guy who lives in public because the public keeps asking *what key was that in?* and *how did you make that sound?* and *was she really naked on the album cover?*

(He smiles. It’s warm. Almost bashful.)
And you know what? I don’t mind.  I’m just a kid from New Carlisle who *hit it big.*  I still wake up sometimes and wonder if the studio’s going to kick me out. If the tour bus was a prank. But it wasn’t.

He pauses. His voice drops—not shaky, but hollowed out by loss.

And now, look…I wish our girl was here. Kathy. And I wish our first prophet was here too. Gilley.

They should be here. They *built* this thing.  They bled for it. Fought for it. Blew it up, reassembled it, made it louder.

(He looks up. Beat.)
But that’s all right.  There are things that are beyond our control.  And I… I can’t let that bother me anymore.

(He exhales. A long one. Clean.)
Thank you.

He steps back without looking. As if music is calling from just offstage.

**CROWD: SILENT. THEN ROARING.**

---

**INT. BACKSTAGE PRESS ROOM – CLEVELAND – ROCK & ROLL HALL OF FAME – NIGHT STILL NOT OVER**

There’s only one mic left standing. The house is halfway into overtime. The photographers are lowering their cameras, exhausted but charmed. The front row has long since abandoned objectivity. They're leaning forward like they’re at church and the sermon’s just gotten juicy.

And now?

**JEFF EVANS** steps up.

Same signature flannel. Same slightly wild hair. Slightly improved posture. *Very* improved attitude. He’s holding no notes. Just a laminated lyric sheet with a coffee ring and a tuning app on his phone.

He clears his throat and begins.

**JEFF EVANS**  
I’ve been prepared to give this speech for… I don’t know. Let’s say… a decade?

Maybe more.

(A few chuckles.)
It’s fine. You guys got it right eventually. And hey, it’s not like rock and roll is *dying* or anything, right?

(Pause. The crowd feels the wink.)
But sure, I had some feelings. Resentment? Yeah. I had that. Still do, depending on the BPM.

(They laugh. Jeff loosens.)
Anyway.

You want to know my thoughts on marketing rock in the 21st century?  Of course* you do. You’re journalists. You love pain.  So: I’ll give you the *short* version.

(He squints. Then smirks.)
Multimedia.  The *illusion* of connection. With *just enough truth* to make it hurt.

You want to reach kids now? You don’t give them a tour poster. You give them a *narrative arc*.  You give them a streaming show.  You give them *An American Ruin*.

(Crowd erupts. Applause, screams, a woman in the back yells “ADAM!”)

Yeah, yeah. Speaking of which—Adam Driver says hi.

(Beat.)
He also wants to *thank me* for dropping 22 pounds this year. Says he’s a method actor and he really needed the *inspiration* to lay off the donuts for Season Three.

Sorry, Adam. Tough luck.  Season Three? You’re gonna suffer like the rest of us.  That's canon.

(Crowd howling. Jeff shrugs.)
Look, I did kinda let the weight get away from me. *Life happens.* But hey—good lighting and a breakup can do wonders.

(He drops into storyteller mode. Everyone leans in.)
But if you’re just tuning in to the Commuted saga—and you’re wondering what happens at the end of Year Three?

Spoiler alert.

(Silence. A beat.)
…I’m not gonna tell you. Just have two boxes of Kleenex handy. Minimum.

(CROWD: UPROARIOUS.)
Now listen. I’m grateful. I mean that.  I remember this one night. Early days. W.O. Wright’s. We were promised a fifty-dollar bonus if we drew a crowd of 50 people. Fifty bucks! Divided six ways. That’s not a bonus—that’s a burrito and a handshake.

(He leans in, confessional.)
An hour before set time? We counted 48 people. So what do I do? I call my mom.

(Laughter.)
I call her and I say: “Bring my brother. Bring my sister. Just get us to 51.”

Not each.  Not 50 bucks each.  *Fifty bucks TOTAL.*

(Crowd laughing, murmuring, loving it.)
That woman didn’t blink. She *showed up.*  And she didn’t just bring the family—she brought *two hundred* goddamn people from Wright Pat.

Suddenly we’ve got 300 people in the room.

(Crowd: gasping, cheering.)
Did she complain?  Nope. And now you know *everything* you need to know about my mother.  And probably why I write music the way I do.

If you’re baffled by the leaps in logic?  If my metaphors run sideways?  That’s fine.

Just buy the next album.  I explain it there.

(Cracks a grin. Shrugs like a man who’s finally said everything he needed to.)

**JEFF EVANS**  
Thanks.

(Mic drop—not literal. Jeff’s too courteous for that.)
