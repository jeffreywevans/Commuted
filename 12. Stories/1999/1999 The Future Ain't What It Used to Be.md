## **INT. BAND BUS – INTERSTATE 5 NORTH OF LOS ANGELES – 3:04 A.M. – SUMMER 1999**

A battered MCI tour coach barrels north through the void, high beams punching holes in the dark, the engine’s drone a lullaby for insomniacs and addicts. Everyone claims they sleep on the bus. Nobody does. Not tonight.

Kendall is naked, or close enough—a black T-shirt hitched under her tits, a tangle of bedsheet and thigh. The bunk is a cave of rumpled pillows and half-empty water bottles, the air close and sour with sweat, deodorant, and last night’s tequila. Jim Brown is inside her, slow and deliberate, working the rhythm with the concentration of a man who knows every note of her body, but won’t give her the bridge until she begs for it. His hands grip her hips. Not possessive—anchoring. His breath is a slow tide against her ear. Every thrust lands with the authority of a bandleader tapping in the count.

On the other side of the plywood wall: Jeremy Evans, wide awake and radiating *fuck-you* energy through the curtain. The walls are thin as the last layer of self-respect after three weeks on tour. Nobody gets privacy. Nobody’s pretending otherwise.

Kendall tries not to make noise. She fails. A soft gasp, swallowed laughter, the creak of the bunk rails as Jim draws her back onto his cock. He murmurs low—private jokes, instructions, a running commentary that is as much for Jeremy’s benefit as Kendall’s: “Good girl… that’s it, sweetheart… c’mon, let’s show them how it’s done…” Each word a little louder than it needs to be. The bastard loves an audience.

Jeremy finally snaps. The voice slices through the dark like a thrown beer can:

**JEREMY (O.S.)**
God *damn* it, Jim! Will you please stop playing paradiddles with Kendall’s ass? Some of us are trying to sleep!

A silence falls, thick and awkward, pressed between three lives too tangled to untwist. Kendall’s pulse hammers in her neck. Jim pauses—just for effect—and then, in that sonorous, ridiculous baritone, he starts going: “Woo! Woo! Woo! Woo!”—a bastardized *Sympathy for the Devil*, each “woo” a percussive slap against her hip, a challenge, a joke, an act of war.

The bus is awake now. Kathy’s bunk curtain rustles. Gilley mutters something about murder and royalties. Bill, already halfway to a Xanax coma, hurls a shoe at the ceiling.

Kendall starts laughing—helpless, belly-deep, the kind of laugh that sounds like sobbing if you’re two bunks away and too lonely for it. Jim doesn’t stop moving inside her. He speeds up, milking the rhythm, showboating for the ghosts in the hallway.

He plants a hand beside her head, sweat dripping from his jaw, and kisses her, hard. She moans, this time on purpose, shoving her fingers into his hair. She knows Jeremy’s listening—maybe touching himself, maybe plotting arson, maybe both.

Jim bites her shoulder. “You want me to stop?” he whispers, a dare.

Kendall shakes her head. “No,” she mouths, barely a sound. “Louder.”

He obliges. The bus rocks. A bottle rolls across the floor.

**JEREMY (O.S.)**
I’m serious, Jim. You’re a fucking *animal*.

Jim only grins, sweat-soaked and glorious, the devil in boxer briefs, and bellows another round of “Woo! Woo! Woo!” The bus howls with him, every surface vibrating. He fucks Kendall until she’s nothing but breath and bone, until her hands claw the mattress and her legs shake.

After, they collapse in a tangle of limbs, giggling and spent, breathless and wild. The bus smells like skin, like music, like the end of the world and nobody is sorry. Least of all Jim.

Somewhere in the dark, Jeremy pulls his pillow over his head and mutters, “Next stop, I’m buying a goddamn hotel room.” But nobody’s fooled. He’ll never leave.

Kendall reaches for Jim’s hand under the blanket. Finds it. Holds tight.

The bus roars north. Nobody sleeps.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – SOUTH OF SACRAMENTO – 8:13 A.M. – SUMMER 1999**

Sunlight leaks through the filthy curtains, carving gold veins across sleeping bags, crumpled hoodies, a half-eaten bagel on the dashboard. The road shudders under the wheels—three lanes of Interstate running like a scar across the Central Valley. The air is swampy with hangover and recycled AC, too bright for dignity, too early for hope.

Jeremy Evans, groggy and mean-mouthed, elbows his way past the coffee pot. He can’t tell if it’s yesterday or today. His watch says morning. His face says last night. He needs to talk to Cremeans, needs to *do something* about the hell-bus.

**JEREMY** (to no one, but hoping Cremeans hears)
Can we have a word? Seriously, man. We gotta start flying. First class at least. Private, if you’re not bullshitting.

Jeff perks up from the bench seat, glasses pushed halfway up his nose, a Sudoku book open but ignored.

**JEFF**
Private would actually be cheaper, if you consider the hourly cost of replacing Jeremy’s dignity after each bus ride.

Kathy, eyes still half-mast, kicks her socked feet up on the table, slurping what’s left of her orange juice. At the word “private,” her head cocks, animal and sly.

**KATHY**
Private jet, huh? You get me a private jet, I’ll start wearing underwear again.

Jeff looks up, deadpan, not missing a beat.

**JEFF**
No you won’t.

Kathy smiles, hikes her shirt, flashes a bra strap—pink, lacy, an inside joke from Target. Everyone cracks up, the kind of laughter that feels medicinal, like codeine and church at the same time.

**KATHY**
Yeah, that’s true. But hey—let’s just not tell everybody. Wouldn’t want to kill the myth.

**BILL**
Kathy, you want anything harder?  A chaser?

**KATHY**
Nope. Alive is hard enough.

She winks at Bill. Bill, ever the straight man, fills her glass with OJ and doesn’t comment on the underwear situation. He passes her the pitcher and leans into the aisle.

**BILL**
Cremeans, seriously. I mean, I get the bus thing for the East Coast. But these California death marches? L.A. to Sacramento is just sadism with a MapQuest printout. Flying would actually make us hate each other *less*. Or at least more efficiently.

He sets down the juice, stacks his hands like a T.A. prepping for a group project.

**BILL (CONT’D)**
So what’s the P&L say? We can afford it, right? Or are we still hemorrhaging money from the last time Gilley ordered bottle service in Reno?

Cremeans, seated at the dinette with a battered laptop, is already five emails deep into hell. He glances over his glasses, sizing up the band like an actuary about to commit murder.

**CREMEANS** (polite, dry, unblinking)
The P&L says the bus costs less than private. First class, maybe—if you want to check your gear and pray it arrives. Private jet means dropping another eighty grand per month, not counting overtime for load-in and ground transport.  You, individually, are millionaires. The band, collectively, still has to make rent.

He looks at Jeremy.

**CREMEANS (CONT’D)**
If you can guarantee no one gets us kicked off a charter for “inappropriate use of the beverage cart,” I’ll run the numbers again.

Kathy snorts, almost chokes on orange juice.

**KATHY**
What, that happened *one* time.

Bill shrugs, sips his own juice, totally unfazed.

**BILL**
Just show us the spreadsheet. We’ll crowdsource it. Someone in here can do math. Probably not me.

**JEFF**
Or just unionize the rhythm section and threaten a strike. That’s how real bands do it.

Kendall, hair wild, notebook in hand, scribbles it all down. Every word. Every laugh. Every snipe. The sun keeps rising, relentless as regret.  The bus rattles on. Nobody’s asleep. Nobody’s ever asleep. Just hungry, broke, and moving—like always.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – SOUTH OF SACRAMENTO – 9:02 A.M. – SUMMER 1999**

Coffee’s burnt. Nobody cares. Kathy sits at the battered dinette, knees up, flipping through the latest *Elle*. There she is on the cover: **KATHY ESPICH – “THE VOICE OF THE DECADE”**. Cropped hair, smirk sharpened by exhaustion and mascara, lipstick just a little bit too red for morning light. Her face looks back at her like an accusation.

**KATHY** (shouting at the photo, then at the room)
I’m fucking famous! Yes! Yes, I am!

She slaps the cover for punctuation. Bill glances up from his synth case, deadpan as ever.

**BILL**
I believe you. Probably the second most famous female recording artist of the nineties.

Kathy turns on him, arching one brow so hard it could snap a bass string.

**KATHY**
Second? Who the fuck is first?

Jeff doesn’t even look up from his notebook, just mutters.

**JEFF**
Madonna. Madonna is more famous than you, Kathy.

Kathy shoots him daggers, tongue poised between venom and arson.

**KATHY**
Who do you think boys jerk off more to, her or me?

A beat of silence. Bill, not missing a cue, shrugs.

**BILL**
After that Playboy spread in ‘95? I think it’s you, Kathy.

She grins, flashes teeth—hungry and wild.

**KATHY**
Duly noted. I hope she sends me a thank you note. Maybe a fruit basket.

She holds up the magazine again, stares at her own face, and her voice softens just enough to let the tired in.

**KATHY (CONT’D)**
It’s not about fame. Not anymore. Not about fortune, not even about covers or panties or fruit baskets. It’s about us playing 200 fucking nights a year on the road, and being so fucking bushed by Encore that I can’t even remember what city we’re in. We’ve sold 60 million records. We’re rich. We’re miserable. I want to know if we’re selling the damn T-shirts.

Jeff, always counting, always skeptical, sets down his pen.

**JEFF**
How’s the merch doing, anyway? Are we actually moving cotton or just buying pretty ink for storage? Because every time I walk backstage there’s boxes of hoodies nobody wants.

Jeremy, propped against the fridge, eyes bloodshot, grins his mutant lawyer grin.

**JEREMY**
We could just call it “cremains.” Cotton to ashes, ashes to storage. If we ever get a plane ride again, that’s what I want printed on the headrests. “Compliments of your friends at Cremains Air.”

Cremeans, buried in his spreadsheets, interjects, crisp as a judge’s gavel.

**CREMEANS**
We can’t fly American anyway. Because of the incident.

A beat. Everyone looks at Kathy. She shrugs, rolls her eyes.

**KATHY**
But it was *one* time. I was *very* sorry. The people of Iceland forgave us.

Jeff chimes in, voice bone-dry, dead as a graveyard.

**JEFF**
Two years later, they did.

Kathy throws up her hands. The whole bus erupts in laughter, the kind that makes you forget your bruises for thirty seconds. Bill pours more coffee, passes the sugar. Someone puts on a mixtape, and for a minute, the only famous people in the world are the six souls and one overworked bus roaring north to nowhere.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – OUTSIDE DAVIS, CALIFORNIA – 10:19 A.M. – SUMMER 1999**

The bus lurches, engine rattling like a dying refrigerator. The breakfast fog is clearing—Kathy's lipstick now transferred to her coffee cup, Jeff's Sudoku half-finished, everyone awake enough to realize they're just rolling debt with noise and charisma.

Cremeans, the band’s accountant/prophet of doom, sits at the end of the table, fingers tapping at a TI-83 as if the batteries could solve their problems. He's gone red in the face. Sweat stains under his arms. Papers everywhere. His voice could strip varnish.

**CREMEANS**
Yes, yes, you’ve sold sixty million discs. Everyone’s proud. Everyone’s rich. That’s not the point. Here’s the problem—CD sales are down. Last two albums—down. Yes, Jeff, I know you moved fifteen million units but listen to me—**the technology is going to eat us.**

Jeff rubs his eyes, glassy and exasperated, voice rising.

**JEFF**
I fucking *told* you that a year ago, Cremeans! No one believed me! Usenet, Napster, all that shit. I literally stood in this bus and said, “Welcome to the end times,” and you all laughed. Thank you very much.

Cremeans holds up a finger, the universal symbol for "hush, nerd."

**CREMEANS**
Yeah, just hush, buddy. Yes, we're selling well. *No*, we're not going to keep selling like this. The only thing that’s keeping you in wine and roses and red bras is the fact you can still sell out a dome or hit forty venues in ninety days in the winter. *That*—and merch, and licensing. Not discs. Not anymore.

He flips through a printout. Revenue lines, costs, random coffee stains.

**CREMEANS** (CONT’D)
You know what’s not profitable? Touring in 1992 dollars. You know what’s even less profitable? Depending on album sales to pay for bus repairs and bail money. This is what’s coming, kids. *Merchandise*. Licensing deals. T-shirt cannons. Stadiums. Stadiums. Stadiums. You want to eat, you better sell hoodies in bulk. Because every label in the world is about to get burned by their own format.

He shoots a look at the table.

**CREMEANS**(CONT’D)
I mean, a lot of bands had this built-in gimme—sell you the record in ‘76, sell you the CD in ‘86, sell you the “remastered box set” in ‘96. We didn’t get that twice. You know why? Because you never had vinyl.

Bill, sharp as a snare crack, leans in.

**BILL**
Why not? Who says we can’t?

He taps the table like it’s a sampler pad.

**BILL (CONT’D)**
Fuck it. Let's press vinyl now. Limited runs, crazy art, do it like the club kids in Berlin. People want nostalgia? Let’s give ‘em something to fetishize. Sell 'em a slab of wax, signed and numbered, ten different colors. Gilley can draw dicks on the inside jacket for the first hundred copies.

Gilley, grinning, already halfway sold.

**GILLEY**
Hell yes. And we charge a hundred bucks a pop. Include a lock of Kathy's hair, you watch the superfans crawl out of the drywall.

Kathy, mid-laugh, shakes her head, but her eyes say do it.

**KATHY**
Put my panties in the deluxe edition, I dare you. We'll have the weirdest unboxing trauma in history.

Jeff shrugs, but even he’s interested.

**JEFF**
I mean, why not? If the tech’s gonna eat us, might as well sell ‘em souvenirs before we’re dinner.

Cremeans sighs, but his eyes flick to the margins of his spreadsheet—already crunching numbers.

**CREMEANS**
You want to go boutique, you go boutique. But don’t come crying to me when the German post office seizes your shipment for “biological material.” Or when American customs wants to know why your box set smells like lavender and regret.

But he’s already typing up “vinyl profit margins” in his spreadsheet, and for a second, the room is lighter. Nobody’s going broke today. Maybe tomorrow.

The bus keeps rolling. The future is coming. The WiFi’s down, but the merch ideas are up. Nobody sleeps. Nobody stops. The band just keeps selling itself—one sweaty, limited-edition, overpriced artifact at a time.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – OUTSIDE STOCKTON – 10:44 A.M. – SUMMER 1999**

Cremeans—laptop open, mug in hand, posture like a man who’s considered driving the bus into a ravine and decided it’s not quite worth the paperwork. He flips through line items on a screen. The band sprawls around him: Kathy with her *Elle*, Gilley rolling a joint, Bill dissecting a synth mag, Jeff running numbers in his head, Jeremy staring out the window like he’s scanning for an exit ramp to Mars.

Cremeans clears his throat, pinches the bridge of his nose.

**CREMEANS**
Let me spell it out. If you all want to start flying everywhere—first class, private, whatever, with hot towels and champagne—you need **real** revenue. Not rockstar ideas, actual cash flow.

He nods at Kathy, mock-earnest.

**CREMEANS (CONT’D)**
Kathy, your “$50 laundry” is cute. Real cute. But let’s talk numbers. Maybe you push a hundred units a stop, and that’s generous. Eighty stops in six months? That’s 8,000 pieces. At about $13 margin, we’re talking just under ninety grand. Which—wow, do you know what that buys us? Maybe *five weeks* of flying. Maybe. If Gilley doesn’t drink the minibar dry and Jeff doesn’t start a chess club with the pilots.

The table goes quiet. Kathy bites her lip, tries not to smirk. Bill starts to say something, thinks better of it.

**CREMEANS (CONT’D)**
So what’s next, Kathy? Maybe next time I try to get you to a meeting with Target to endorse their “Famous Underwear” line, you actually show up? Or are we too punk for cotton briefs and corporate synergy?

He wheels to face the others, tone sharpening.

**CREMEANS (CONT’D)**
And you—Hollywood. The last time I dragged you into a licensing meeting in LA, you embarrassed me in front of six executive producers and three guys who *literally* shit gold. Every single one of you sitting there, dead serious, taking turns saying, “I’m Batman. I’m Batman. I’m Batman. I’m Batman.” Over and over.

He grinds his teeth, hesitates before the swear, then edits himself:

**CREMEANS (CONT’D)**
Look. Batman is a franchise. DC is a franchise. Michael Keaton didn’t even keep the damn cape. But you? You think it’s funny to snark about “cremains” and clown around while I’m trying to get your music into a movie trailer that would pay for a year of jet fuel.

He sighs, rubs his temple. The room is all ears. No one breathes.

**CREMEANS (CONT’D)**
I’m telling you—if you want the toys, you have to act like grownups. That means making some business decisions. That means showing up. That means letting me license a freaking song to Hollywood without turning it into amateur hour at the Gotham City Improv.

He scans the faces around the table: hungover, brilliant, broke, beautiful. His tone softens just enough to be true.

**CREMEANS (CONT’D)**
I want to give you the world. I do. But you can’t keep spending like you own it unless you start thinking like you built it. The old money’s drying up. You want to fly, you want to play stadiums, you want to be legends? Then you have to let the business catch up to the myth.

He waits. Silence. Even Gilley puts down the lighter.

**KATHY** (pointedly)
So, no to the bat signal on the next album cover?

Cremeans almost laughs. Almost.

**CREMEANS**
Only if DC pays for the plane.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – CALIFORNIA HIGHWAY – STILL 10:46 A.M. – SUMMER 1999**

Kathy, legs curled up on the dinette bench, magazine discarded, hair wild, mood wilder. Cremeans’ lecture still hangs in the air like burnt toast. The rest of the band is quiet, watching her, waiting for the next spark.

She cracks her knuckles, her grin bright and knife-edged.

**KATHY**
You know what? Let’s just fucking call Doc Martin. Get him on the phone, right now. I’ll say, “What’s up, Doc?” and then, hell, let’s do *lunch*. **KATHY (CONT’D)**
And while we’re at it, let’s actually get some product endorsements for shit we *really fucking believe in*, Jeff. I mean, no more boring perfume ads. No more vodka nobody drinks. Let’s get sponsored by Doc Martens. Or Fender. Or fucking Tampax, I don’t care.

**KATHY (CONT’D)**
How about something we *actually* use when we’re bleeding out onstage or getting our amps through customs at Heathrow? Instead of pretending we drink some bullshit imported lager that nobody in this bus would touch sober.

**KATHY (CONT’D)**
Let’s see who calls us back first. Doc Martens or fucking Batman.

The bus erupts in laughter, the tension broken for a minute. Outside, California flashes by, and inside, Kathy knows what she wants: to be a legend and a brand—on her own goddamn terms.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – EDGE OF THE DELTA – 11:12 A.M. – SUMMER 1999**

I guess it’s time to throw a brick in the toilet and see if it flushes.  So—Yamaha’s approached me. They want me to endorse the new keyboard they’re bringing out next year.

There’s a moment of pure, dumb silence. Even the road noise fades. Jeremy twists around in his seat, eyes wide.

**JEREMY**
No shit. You’re kidding.
(I just thought you… got better.)

**BILL**
Yeah, well. Maybe a little of both. But the truth is, I’ve had the prototype for a year. Been using it live, sneaking it into the set since Toronto. Haven’t tracked with it yet—no recordings. But yeah, it’s been on stage.

**JEREMY**
Goddamn, Bill. I just thought you’d been woodshedding.

Bill shrugs, almost bashful in the spotlight for once.

**BILL**
Nah. It gives me a bigger arsenal. New tools, new technology. Lets me make some of those noises you all keep accusing me of running through the PA from hell.

Kathy leans in, skeptical, but not unkind.

**KATHY**
And they’re gonna give you money for this? Just for saying “Bill Davenport uses Yamaha”?

Bill thinks—long enough that everyone wonders if he’ll answer.

**BILL**
No, not like that. I’m not endorsing it. I’m saying I *use* it. If people want to think that’s an endorsement, that’s their problem. I’m not doing a jingle or a photo shoot. But I *will* make sure it’s in the liner notes on the next disc. People should know what’s actually making those noises, not just who’s standing behind the rack.

He shrugs again, understated, already closing the subject.

**BILL (CONT’D)**
I’ll take the tools over the money, most days. But I’ll take both if they offer.

Jeff nods, a faint smile—proud, but also a little awed by the quiet ambition in his friend.

**JEFF**
Put it in the notes, man. If anyone reads liner notes anymore.

Jeremy claps Bill on the shoulder, respect thick in his eyes.

**JEREMY**
Now all I want for Christmas is for Fender to build me a guitar I can play upside down and on fire.

Kathy grins, stretching out, the tension eased by Bill’s honesty.

**KATHY**
Just don’t endorse anything that comes in a spray can, Bill. We have enough weird smells on this bus.

Everyone laughs, even Cremeans, who makes a note in his ever-expanding to-do list: “KEYBOARD: YAMAHA – BILL – LINER NOTES.”  The bus rolls on, slightly lighter. For a minute, the only brand is the band. And that—*that*—still means something.

You know that software company, Apple? You remember them? Used to play Oregon Trail on their machines. “Think different.” Last week, they took my picture. You’ll see me in *Time* Magazine by Christmas.

No fucking way.

**CREMEANS**
Now if only someone could get Gilley to endorse silence.

**KATHY**
Apple? As in, “press play on tape?” Apple? Are you *fucking with us*?

**BILL**
Time Magazine, Jeff? I thought you hated cameras.

Apple, man. I thought they went out of business. You sure it wasn’t a scam? Did they ask you for your social security number?

Here’s to Bill, Pro Tools poster boy, and Jeff, the next star of Apple IIe Monthly.

The mood in the bus is bright and incredulous, everybody a little bit jealous, a little bit proud. In 1999, in a band where everyone’s broke or famous (or both), even a pissant Apple campaign feels like winning the lottery.  The bus rattles on, dreams and rivalries riding shotgun, every ad campaign just another reason to stay loud.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – CALIFORNIA, ALMOND ORCHARDS WHIPPING PAST – 11:23 A.M. – SUMMER 1999**

Bill is still getting razzed about Pro Tools, but the bus has suddenly gotten cooler, a draft of suspicion threading through the stale, coffee-stained air. Jeremy’s not smiling anymore. He leans in, searching Jeff’s face for the punchline.

**JEREMY**
Wait. This isn’t that Think Different thing, is it?

Jeff doesn’t even blink.

**JEFF**
Yeah. Exactly that.

The bus gets so quiet you could hear a Zip disk spin down. Even Gilley, who never stops talking, clamps his jaw. Kathy’s foot freezes halfway to her boot. Everyone knows Jeff’s *different*—that’s old news. But exploited? That’s another animal.

Jeremy’s arms bristle, a flush rising up his neck.

**JEREMY**
Why the hell didn’t you ask me about this, Jeff? They could be using you. That’s a *brand*. That’s… *they’re* the ones who ought to think twice. You’re too fucking smart for that, but—Christ, Jeff—

It’s defensive, not jealous, but it lands in the room like a warning shot. Exploit Jeff? That’s a line even this band doesn’t cross.

Jeff doesn’t get mad. He just cocks his head, dry as static.

**JEFF**
Brother, dear… if I’m being exploited, you ought to check out my bank account. It’s bigger than my dick. They wrote a very large check. I mean, three hours, click-click-click, some photographer with a goatee told me not to smile. Like that’s a problem. I didn’t smile. I never smile. Why would I? I didn’t smile when I cashed the check.

He raises his eyebrows—maybe the closest thing he’s got to a punchline.

**JEFF (CONT’D)**
So, Jeremy, am I being exploited? Or am I just finally getting paid for having the face of a guy who hates cameras?

Jeremy narrows his eyes, does the math in the air.

**JEREMY**
Does the check have five zeros after it or six?

Jeff looks down, voice low, almost shy for once.

**JEFF**
It was about two hundred seventy-five thousand.

A stunned, choked silence—then:

**KATHY**
Holy shit nuggets.

Bill coughs, actually spills his coffee. Gilley just mutters, “Jesus wept.” Even Cremeans is speechless for once.

**JEREMY**
That’s… that’s like dot-com money.

Jeff nods, half a smile finally breaking through.

**JEFF**
Yeah. I guess I’ll think different, too.

The laughter is slow, low, and shell-shocked. Nobody’s ever gotten paid like that for not smiling—especially not someone who means it. The silence is heavy, a mix of awe, envy, and the sudden, terrifying knowledge that Jeff is going to buy something very stupid with that check.

Kathy lifts her mug, voice reverent:

**KATHY**
To Jeff. Our first tech millionaire. Drinks are on you, right?

Jeff shrugs, grinning, finally enjoying the chaos he’s created.

**JEFF**
Maybe. As long as you don’t ask me to smile.

The bus rolls on, full of static, static, static. But somewhere, in a vault in Cupertino, there’s a picture of Jeff Evans not smiling, and the world is about to learn what “different” really looks like.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – I-5, HEAT WAVES OFF THE ASPHALT – 11:28 A.M. – SUMMER 1999**

Jeff’s Apple payday still echoes through the bus, but Gilley can’t let Kathy get the last word. He throws a foot up on the seat, stretching like a man who hasn’t heard the word “modesty” in months.

**GILLEY**
Fuck no, Kathy—I’m gonna be the first peck millionaire in this band. You know what Ask Jeeves is doing these days? That’s the future, baby. Search engines, not synths. I’m just getting started.

Kathy pounces, quick as an alleycat, eyes bright with mischief.

**KATHY**
Well, let’s Ask Jeeves how Ask Jeeves is doing. Bet he’s still waiting for someone to ask him a real question.

The bus howls—perfect timing. Even Bill can’t hold in a grin. Jeff just shakes his head, already in full doomsayer mode.

**JEFF**
It’s a frickin’ bubble. You all see that, right? God, it’s gonna blow up in our faces. Remember that Long-Term Credit Bank thing? The whole system’s built on wishful thinking and bad math.

Bill rolls his eyes, tired of Jeff’s perpetual end-of-the-world routine.

**BILL**
Jeff, shut up. Don’t start. Are you honestly telling us Wall Street’s in a bubble? In 1999? How original.

Jeff’s voice is level, but his eyes are serious—he means it, even if nobody else does.

**JEFF**
Yes. That’s *exactly* what I’m telling you.

Gilley waves it off, arms wide, cartoon confidence on full blast.

**GILLEY**
No way, man. Technology’s only going up. Up, up, and away—like Superman, right? Next stop: gold-plated record deals and dot-com mansions for all of us.

Jeff smirks, can’t help himself.

**JEFF**
You do realize *Superman* was an incredibly cynical song? It’s about a surveillance state, obsession, and a guy so warped by longing he basically becomes a stalker.

Kathy turns, sharp as a switchblade.

**KATHY**
Like you, Jeff? You and me? Should I be worried?

Jeff looks at her—pained, vulnerable, weirdly sincere. His reply is all edge, no heat.

**JEFF**
You would be wrong, Kathy. If I wanted to see you naked, I’d pull out my copy of *Playboy*. I’m not in the mood to see you naked.

Kathy huffs, gives him a look like she’d slap him if he wasn’t so pitiful.

**KATHY**
Jeff, just…shut up.

Jeff blinks, wounded wallaby, searching the faces for backup.

**JEFF**
Am I supposed to shut up now?

Every voice in the bus, in ragged, harmonious unison:

**EVERYONE**
YES.

And for one glorious, impossible second, Jeff actually does. The bus rolls on—dot-coms, divas, millionaires and all—into the weird, unstable future.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – ROLLING INTO SACRAMENTO – 11:34 A.M. – SUMMER 1999**

The bus stinks of orange juice and bragging rights. Jim, slouched at the table with his bare feet up, decides to flex. He cracks his knuckles, looks around at his motley, half-educated band.

**JIM**
I'm the only one in this band that's got a degree.

Kathy nearly spits coffee, eyes flashing.

**KATHY**
Fuck off, Jim. I've got my associate's in psychology. It counts, goddamn it.

Jim tips his invisible cap, a rare show of respect.

**JIM**
OK, I’ll give you that. But I’m the only one here with a bachelor’s in accounting sciences. I actually finished. Seven years, man. *Real* diploma.

Jeff, feet up, voice as dry as I-5 in July.

**JEFF**
You can teach yourself that shit. Once you get rich, I got rich, so I taught it to myself. The bank statement’s just a more honest version of the report card.

Jim grins, undeterred, waving a battered envelope.

**JIM**
I can read a P&L. I can understand what Cremeans is doing back there. I know what Island’s up to, even if they don’t. I can make sense of these damn contracts.

Kathy yawns, stretching like a cat in the sun.

**KATHY**
So what’s your point, Jim? Is this a Discovery Channel promo or are you just showing off for Kendall?

Jim stares straight at Jeff, tone halfway between envy and challenge.

**JIM**
Jeff, how the hell are you keeping up with all this? You hate contracts more than you hate disco.

Jeff just shrugs, eyes cold, deadpan. Then, with surgical precision:

**JEFF**
You ever been related to a brother who can’t move his bowels for a week straight?

They all look at Jeremy. Jeremy shrugs, grins, says nothing, and the bus sniggers.

Jeff keeps going, totally serious.

**JEFF (CONT’D)**
I got in the habit of reading the newspaper since, I don’t know, ‘87. Everywhere we go, they throw a USA Today at us with the hotel breakfast. I read the news. I skip the purple section.

Kathy perks up, eyebrow raised.

**KATHY**
Why? Because I’m in it?

Jeff doesn’t even blink.

**JEFF**
Exactly. Because sometimes you’re in it and I’m not, and that pisses me off.

Gilley grins, absolutely delighted, full Fresh Prince swagger. He eyeballs Jeff’s jeans, hole in the knee, faded as memory.

**GILLEY**
Well, try to be good with people, you sack of shit. Timing, wit, or, I don’t know, *fashion sense*—maybe then you’d end up on A1, C1, whatever the hell the purple section is. Hell, maybe even People magazine. Like me.

Gilley lowers his voice, mock-conspiratorial, eyes darting left and right.

**GILLEY (CONT’D)**
Of course, I only get into People magazine because of the women I sleep with. You, my friend, might want to try something other than Sudoku.

Jeremy laughs so hard he almost falls off his seat. Kathy leans over, pats Jeff’s knee like he’s a rescue dog.

**KATHY**
Don’t worry, Jeff. The *really* interesting people never make the purple section anyway.

Jeff rolls his eyes, cracks a smile, and the bus barrels on—six weirdos, two degrees, and a stack of USA Todays, all headed for the next city and the next page that won’t mention any of them by name.  And that’s rock and roll, baby.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – PAST THE WATER TOWER, SACRAMENTO – 11:41 A.M. – SUMMER 1999**

The mood is riding the edge of mayhem—Jeff’s still smarting from Gilley’s “fashion sense” burn, Jim’s rifling through a folder of ancient receipts, Kathy’s humming a tune from the *purple section* just to annoy Jeff, and Bill is quietly plotting the next passive-aggressive synth solo.

Jeff leans in, drops his voice low and even, which means he’s about to get serious.

**JEFF**
All right, here’s a question—if we’re gonna start endorsing things privately, maybe it’s time for a *band meeting*. You know, an actual discussion about whether we as Commuted accept sponsorships—for tours, for scores, for whatever Cremeans throws our way. Democracy, not anarchy.

Bill, who has made an artform of hating everything about commercial music except the gear, leans back, eyes flashing, then suddenly slaps his hand on the table, voice going full preacher.

**BILL**
I have always *hated* the concept! Hated it! But you know what time it is, boys and girls?

He stands, hands in the air, shouting it like a call to arms:

**BILL (CONT’D)**
It’s time for B-A-N-D democracy!  Kathy, call the motherfucking roll!

Kathy’s mouth splits into a devilish grin. She grabs a napkin, stands up, and whacks it against her palm like a judge’s gavel.

**KATHY**
All right, you degenerates, listen up. We are calling the *roll*. One by one. You want your vote? Say it out loud. You want a sponsorship? You want to keep it pure? You want me to get paid for wearing panties on a magazine cover? State your business.

She eyes Gilley, who’s already giggling.

**KATHY (CONT’D)**
Gilley—yea or nay, first up.

Gilley sits up straight, eyes wild, plays it like he’s testifying at the World Court.

**GILLEY**
I want every sponsorship except for dietary supplements and funeral homes. Unless it’s for a really cool coffin.

Kathy scribbles “Gilley: YES (no laxatives, no caskets except cool ones)” on the napkin.

She points at Jim.

**KATHY (CONT’D)**
Jim?

Jim snorts.

**JIM**
If they pay, I play. But no cigarettes. My father would haunt me.

Kathy notes it down, then turns to Bill.

**KATHY (CONT’D)**
Bill?

Bill sighs, but his grin is all teeth.

**BILL**
If it keeps us in buses, keyboards, and divorce lawyers, I say bring on the cash. But I’m drawing the line at adult diapers.

Kathy winks, then jerks her chin at Jeremy.

**KATHY (CONT’D)**
Jeremy?

Jeremy salutes, dead serious.

**JEREMY**
Endorse anything. I want a guitar with my face on it by Christmas.

Kathy cackles, then points at Jeff.

**KATHY (CONT’D)**
And the big man?

Jeff shrugs, quiet but certain.

**JEFF**
Democracy means we all eat, right? Fine. But we vote on every single deal. One “nay” and it’s out. No sneaking.

The bus falls quiet, the napkin still in Kathy’s hand, now an artifact—dirty, precious, and probably worth $300 on eBay in ten years.

Kathy holds it up, voice echoing through the steel hull:

**KATHY**
So let it be written, so let it be done. BAND DEMOCRACY, motherfuckers.

Bill gives a rebel yell, Jeremy pounds the table, even Cremeans pokes his head up from the back lounge, “Did somebody say *budget*?”

The bus erupts in wild, ragged laughter—endorsements, yes, but only if they survive the circus of democracy. That’s how Commuted rolls: one vote, one circus, one sponsorship at a time.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – SACRAMENTO APPROACH – LATE MORNING, SUMMER 1999**

Chaos, always chaos. Bill’s still riding the high of “band democracy,” Kathy’s still waving her napkin like it’s the Magna Carta, but Jim, forever the drummer, forever the rule-keeper, suddenly slams on the metaphorical brakes.

**JIM**
No, Jeff. Slow down. Hold up. We *had* a constitution. Remember? It was written down on a pizza box—back in ’90, after that gig at the bowling alley. We said if it’s a 4-2 vote, it passes. Four out of six, simple as that.

Jeff leans back, raising an eyebrow.

**JEFF**
Jim, have we ever actually amended our “internal constitution”? You know, the sacred pizza box? Any official edits since ’90?

Jim shakes his head, arms folded, taking his job as parliamentarian for this traveling asylum seriously.

**JIM**
No, we have not. There is no formal way to amend the structure. No articles, no addendum, no “strike the last word.” The pizza box still stands, law of the land.

He points at the napkin, then at Jeff, then at everyone.

**JIM (CONT’D)**
So as a point of order—I state that the last vote was invalid. Because of your little “unanimity” rule. You can’t just *make up* that we need everyone to agree. The only thing that needs to happen is a 4-2 majority.

He trips over the word, fighting his tongue and the caffeine shakes.

**JIM (CONT’D)**
Unam—unanimity. Whatever. Where everyone agrees with each other. That’s not how this works, Jeff. Four out of six. That’s the law.

Kathy, delighted, pounds the table.

**KATHY**
Order in the court! The drummer has spoken! All hail the pizza box!

Gilley’s grinning like a hyena. Bill solemnly raises his mug.

**BILL**
All in favor of respecting the pizza box, say “aye.”

A ragged chorus of “aye”—only Jeff’s silent, stone-faced, still wanting his band democracy to be *perfect* democracy.

**JEFF**
Fine. Pizza box rules. But the next time we’re voting to let Jeremy endorse a line of signature hair gel, I want that box in the room.

Jim nods, smug as hell.

**JIM**
Deal. I keep it in my cymbal case. Never travel without the law.

The band collapses into laughter, half-mocking, half-reverent. And somewhere in the cargo hold, beneath a tangle of wires and drum hardware, the greasy, faded pizza box constitution rides with them, older than fame, worth more than any contract, and always—always—binding.

And that’s how governance works in Commuted: outvoted by cardboard, overruled by tradition, and kept alive by the last crust of memory.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – STILL, SACRAMENTO CITY LIMITS – ALMOST NOON, SUMMER 1999**

The air is hot, a little too full of legacy, orange juice, and parliamentary bluster. Kathy’s still standing, napkin in hand, all the glory of a union boss with a brand-new gavel. The room is primed for another procedural fistfight.

**KATHY**
Well, then, let’s do it this way. All in favor of requiring an *amendment* to the sacred Pizza Box Constitution, for the express purpose of *unanimity* on banned endorsements—say aye.

There’s a real pause. Everyone is suddenly aware of how close this is to being a legal document, except it’s written in marinara and shame.

Jim sits up straight, drums on his knee.

**JIM**
That’s the way you do it, Jeff. I vote aye.

Jeff gives a tiny, surprised laugh, nodding. He’s half-tempted to stand and salute.

**JEFF**
Yeah. Okay. You, uh—you nailed it, Jim. Drawing right from the Articles of Confederation. Maybe even the Magna Crust-a.

Kendall, notebook in her lap, deadpan as a firing squad, cuts him off.

**KENDALL**
Shut the fuck up, Jeff, and let the rest of the fucking band vote.

Jeremy, who’s been chewing a pen cap, flashes Kendall a grateful thumbs up.

**JEREMY**
Thank you, Kendall. I forgot you were here. I vote yes, too.

**KENDALL**
Invisibility grants me credibility, Jer.

Gilley’s got both feet on the seat now, grinning like he’s just won a radio contest.

**GILLEY**
Aye, if only so I can see Jim amend a pizza box with a Sharpie on a moving bus. Historical document, my ass.

Bill raises his hand, as if waiting for permission.

**BILL**
Aye. For posterity. And so we can stop arguing about fucking yogurt endorsements.

All eyes go to Kathy, who slams the napkin down for effect.

**KATHY**

Cremeans, half-smiling from the back, finally pipes up:

**CREMEANS**
Does this mean I can bill you hourly for all constitutional revisions? Asking for my lawyer.

The whole bus groans and laughs, and somewhere in the shadowy storage, the pizza box awaits its next glorious, greasy amendment—now with the added weight of a band that, just for once, managed to agree on something.

The road ahead? Still chaos. But for five minutes, they’re all in the same key.

## **INT. TOUR BUS – CIRCLING DOWNTOWN SACRAMENTO – 12:03 P.M., SUMMER 1999**

It’s an open-air circus, a band meeting with the windows down and the rules re-written on a pizza box. Cremeans (or “Mr. Crummings,” as Gilley likes to call him when he’s feeling formal and snarky) is trying to look official, but he knows better than to think this crew will ever run clean.

**GILLEY**
No, sir, my good friend, Mr. Crummings, sir. All it means is—next time someone wants to slap their logo on our tour, when we’re headlining domes and stadiums, they’ll get a real hearing. We’ll treat you with respect this time. We promise.

Kathy leans in, hand on heart, batting her lashes in mock solemnity.

**JEFF**
Will you wear that leather catsuit, though?

**KATHY**

## **INT. TOUR BUS – SACRAMENTO STREETS, TRAFFIC LIGHTS BLURRING BY – 12:09 P.M., SUMMER 1999**

The meeting has devolved into daydreams and negotiation. Jeremy, chewing the end of a pencil, leans across the table, eyes fixed on Cremeans with the intensity of a man who wants to sound cool but has no idea how anything actually gets done.

**JEREMY**
Cremeans, can you, I don’t know, maybe make my name available to all the guitar companies—Fender, Ibanez, whoever—and tell them I’m not really interested in endorsing anything unless they’re building, manufacturing, and assembling the world’s most perfect Stratocaster? Nothing less.

Cremeans looks up, deadpan, already regretting his professional life choices.

**CREMEANS**
That’s… awfully specific, Jeremy. Especially from a guy who tours with a dozen guitars, and what—maybe two of them are even Stratocasters? So, what gives? Let’s just say, for the sake of argument, Ibanez comes knocking and wants you to slap your name on a signature model. How morally flexible are you?

Jeremy shrugs, not defensive—just honest, for once.

**JEREMY**
I’m not morally inflexible. The guitar I pick serves the song. Always has. Always will.

Cremeans leans forward, steeples his fingers, sounding every inch the devil’s advocate.

**CREMEANS**
Well, then, Jeremy, the guitar you endorse has to serve its *role*, too—which is, let’s be honest, serving your bank account. You can go full mercenary, chase the check, or you can put some real thought into it. Make it mean something. Your call.

Kathy, lips curled up at the edges, jumps in, voice teasing, sing-song:

**KATHY**
If you’re the *smarter* Evans brother—

Jeff cuts her off, dry as sandpaper, not even looking up.

**JEFF**

**KATHY**

**JEREMY**
Yeah. I want something that fits the song. And if it fits the song, maybe it’ll fit me. And maybe then it’s worth putting my name on.

**CREMEANS**
I’ll tell them. No neon. No plastic. Only the world’s most perfect Stratocaster—plus or minus a little common sense. Got it.

Bill mutters under his breath, “So basically a unicorn,” and Kathy laughs, bumping Jeremy’s shoulder.

The bus keeps moving, still alive with arguments, jokes, and the kind of creative integrity you can only buy with two million in Apple money and the promise of one day, maybe, endorsing something that actually means something.

Welcome to democracy, Commuted-style. Even the guitars have to earn their keep.

This is life in the minors, Commuted-style: no shade, no relief, no chance of a baseball team, but the hottest ticket in town anyway.
