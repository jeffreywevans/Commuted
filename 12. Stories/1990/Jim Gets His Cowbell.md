# **THE ANATOMY OF A DRUM KIT**

**[[4.4 JIM BROWN character bible|Jim]] (V.O.)** 
My drum kit wouldn’t impress anyone by professional standards or in a modern music store. But it was mine—assembled, piece by piece, from pawn shops, flea markets, and garage sales. My dad helped once; he brought home the floor tom in his old, rusted pickup truck, treating it like a family heirloom. That meant a lot to me.

The bass drum was a 22-inch Tama from 1978\. I had to fix the front hoop with scrap metal and duct tape, and the outer wrap was peeling like sunburned skin. It looked rough, but when I hit it, the sound was massive—raw, forceful, almost violent. That was the appeal.

The snare drum was a true hybrid: a Pearl shell, a Ludwig throw-off, and mismatched tension rods that never fit quite right. The bottom head was warped, so I kept the snare wires tight with masking tape. It rattled and buzzed, always on the edge—angry, imperfect, and exactly what I wanted.

The cymbal situation was borderline comical. I had a single Zildjian crash with a hairline crack I pretended not to notice. My ride cymbal had once been a crash, until I dropped it on concrete at Gilley’s house. The hi-hats were a mismatched pair—I think the bottom was a Sabian, the top might’ve been from Sears. But when I stomped them just right, they sizzled with an aggressive, greasy energy.

No double bass pedal. Not in 1990\. I couldn’t afford one, and honestly, I didn’t need it for what we were doing. This was punk rock and stubborn faith, not progressive rock excess. The hardware was a mess. One cymbal stand was missing its wingnut, so I used a lock washer from Jeff’s dad’s toolbox. That lock washer actually lasted longer than the cymbal it was holding.

I always tuned by ear. I never owned a drum key that wasn’t half-stripped. Sometimes I’d tighten tension rods with a flathead screwdriver or, once, with a quarter taped to a pencil.

But here’s the real point: When I sat behind that kit—when Jeff locked into a bass groove, Kathy stomped on her distortion pedal, and Gilley howled like he was conjuring every wild spirit in Clark County—that drum kit roared. It was thundering. It was raw ambition. It was my way of telling every kid who believed music needed to be polished: you’re wrong. This was proof that beauty can be built from chaos, and noise can be honest.

# **THE DRUM KIT THAT BUILT ME**

---
## **JIM (V.O.) – Summer 1990, Bethel Township, Ohio**

Most people imagine a drum kit as a single, unified instrument—but in reality, it’s an ensemble, a collective, almost a living archive. For me, it was a polyglot construction, equal parts salvage and invention, resembling a battered but beloved machine you keep running on ingenuity and stubborn faith. What I assembled was a testament to resourcefulness: a kit cobbled together from disparate, cast-off components, held together as much by love and prayer as by hardware. Each piece wasn’t just equipment—it had an identity, a backstory, and a physical record of its journey.

### **The Bass Drum** – “*Old Thunder*”

22-inch Tama Imperialstar, 1978\. The black wrap was peeling away in strips, curling at the edges like a sunburnt snake. I picked it up at a church rummage sale in Huber Heights, which was less a formal purchase and more an act of rescue—I handed over ten bucks, and it was mine. The drum was battered: a split hoop, two lugs missing, the kind of damage that made most people shake their heads and walk away. But I saw potential. I rebuilt the hoop with a busted picture frame from a flea market, using wood screws scavenged from Bill Davenport’s garage. It wasn’t elegant, but it was secure, and that patchwork repair felt more like a badge of honor than a flaw. I tuned it intentionally loose, letting the head breathe and resonate, coaxing out a cavernous, almost primordial boom. The sound it produced wasn’t clean; it was raw, enormous—like war drums echoing out of a concrete culvert, reverberating through your chest. I never muffled it. The overtones and open resonance were a deliberate choice, a tribute to the great Phil Collins—who understood the drama and physicality of an unmuted kick. When I played it, *Old Thunder* didn’t just keep time; it set the atmosphere for the entire kit, anchoring the groove with a presence that was felt as much as heard. It made every rehearsal feel a little bit mythic, as if we were summoning a storm in some forgotten corner of Ohio.

### **The Snare** – “*The Howler*”  

 This one’s a freak. The shell was Pearl Export, maybe early ‘80s, but the throw-off was Ludwig, and the tension rods came from three different brands—whatever I could find that would thread and hold. I bought it out of a milk crate at the Springfield pawn shop off US-68. The guy behind the counter was convinced it was worth forty bucks, but I talked him down to seven by pointing out the dead spider curled inside the shell, plus the snare wires that someone had literally duct taped in place. The drum badge was half-rusted, the strainer only worked if you twisted it just right, but none of that mattered once you heard it.  
 What the drum lacked in pedigree, it made up for in raw personality. Every hit, every ghost note, came with a bit of defiance—as if it resented being forgotten, and chose instead to announce its existence as loudly as possible. There was a brutal, brash crack to that snare; not refined, but urgent, like a door slamming on someone’s last chance, or a warning shot across the bow. Sometimes it would buzz and rattle even after I’d finished a fill, the duct-taped wires never quite staying tight, which only made the drum sound more alive, more on edge.  
 
 I grew to love that chaos. *The Howler* never blended in, it never disappeared into the mix. In a kit made from underdogs and salvage, it was the loudest voice at the table, always eager to jump in, to break the tension or punctuate a groove with its own kind of authority. That snare became the signature sound of every chorus, every breakdown, every time I needed the music to land like a punch and not just a beat. If the rest of the kit was a band of survivors, The Howler was their mouthpiece—imperfect, noisy, and impossible to ignore.

### **The Hi-Hats** – “*Frankenstein’s Dinner Bell*”

The top cymbal was a Zyn, British-made, probably from the 1970s—a relic in its own right, heavy and slightly warped at the edge. The bottom was a no-name piece of sheet metal that likely started life as part of a child’s toy drum kit, barely thicker than a coin and stamped with a halfhearted attempt at a brand. In theory, they shouldn’t have functioned as a pair. The weights were mismatched, the alloys different, the sounds fundamentally opposed. But somehow, together, they made something greater—a sound you could never get from a pristine matched set.

I found them in a cardboard box at a garage sale in Enon, marked “CHINESE STUFF – $3.” The woman running the sale was more interested in clearing out junk than haggling; I offered her two bucks, she threw in a cracked cowbell for free, and the deal was done. When I got them home, I tried to clean the layers of grime and oxidation off with Comet and lemon juice, scrubbing until my hands ached. They never really shined, and I realized quickly they weren’t supposed to. These hats weren’t meant to be pretty—they were meant to speak.

The sound was raw, aggressive, full of character. When I stomped on them, there was a sizzling metallic clash, sharper than anything you’d get from the store shelf. Closed tight, they barked and spat like a warning; played loose, they washed and shimmered with a ragged edge, never quite blending in but always announcing themselves with authority. In rehearsal, that sharp “ksshh” would cut straight through guitars, even if the amps were pushed into the red. And live, if you really leaned into the pedal, you could make the entire room snap to attention. It was the sound of something breaking loose—unrefined, unmistakable, the world ending in stereo every time I dropped my heel.

### **The Crash** – “*Crack Baby*”

Zildjian Avedis, 16-inch, with a hairline fracture starting at the bell. I found it on consignment at the indoor flea market in Urbana, duct-taped to a broken floor lamp, the kind of pairing that seemed both tragic and perfect for its fate. I paid five bucks and promised the vendor I wouldn’t sue when it cut my hand open—a joke, mostly, but I was half-serious, considering the sharp edge where the crack threatened to split further with every hit. Every time I played it, I honestly expected that would be the moment it gave up and shattered for good. But it never did. It survived longer than most of my shirts, weathered more gigs than any other piece of my setup.

At first, I tried to baby it—avoiding heavy crashes, hoping to extend its life. But eventually, I leaned into its flaws. The crack grew slowly, curling outward like a bite taken from the bell, but I rotated it, used the “good” side for most of the song, saving the broken edge for specific accents. That spot had a tone all its own—a quick, trashy burst that would almost stutter, giving a jolt of electricity to any fill. It had a dry, dirty shimmer, the kind you’d get if Phil Collins were playing a street fight instead of a studio session—earthy, unapologetic, always a little dangerous. When the band was at full volume and chaos, that cymbal could still cut through with something sharp and mean. *Crack Baby* became my secret weapon: fragile but fearless, never clean or controlled, but always honest, always uniquely itself.

### **The Ride** – “*Flat Earth*”

20-inch Sabian ride, faded logo, one edge dented from what I suspect was a motorcycle accident. I swapped it off a cousin who’d just been kicked out of his band and needed weed money—another bit of musical inheritance rescued from oblivion. The first thing you notice about this cymbal is that it’s not exactly what anyone would call refined; it’s dull, a little trashy, and covered with fingerprints, tape residue, and the lingering ghosts of a dozen different practice spaces. The dent at the edge isn’t subtle—it ripples in the light, a permanent reminder of some long-ago disaster, and it creates unpredictable harmonics that shimmer and clash against the fundamental tone. Overtones bloom and fade with a will of their own, but I learned to play with those imperfections instead of fighting them.

If you played it gently—just the tip of the stick grazing the bow—it whispered, a thin, silvery sheen just on the edge of the mix. Ride patterns could almost dissolve into smoke, blending with Bill’s synth textures or floating beneath a softer bassline. But if you dug in, really leaned on it, the sound changed completely: suddenly it wasn’t a ride anymore, but a crash with a grudge, a pissed-off dinner plate hurled against the wall. That was the trick with *Flat Earth*—it had two personalities, and you never quite knew which one you’d get in the heat of the moment. The more I used it, the more I started anticipating those quirks, letting its wild character become part of the band’s dynamics. It could anchor a groove or obliterate it in an instant. It taught me to listen, to adapt, and to let the chaos in. That ride wasn’t just a piece of gear; it was a collaborator, unpredictable but always expressive, as much a storyteller as any one of us.

### **The Floor Tom** – “*Low Growl*”

18-inch Ludwig, probably late 70s. I found it sitting next to a dumpster behind a defunct roller rink—one of those post-disco-era mausoleums whose parking lot was more cracked asphalt than anything else. No legs, no tension rods, just a shell and two battered heads, left for dead with the rest of the trash. But I saw the badge and the battered finish and knew it had some history. I bought new tension rods in a blister pack from Hills Department Store—the kind of purchase you only make after rooting around the bins for the last box with all the pieces still inside. The legs? Those weren’t standard-issue. My dad cut them from copper plumbing tubes he had lying around from a half-finished bathroom job, and I jammed them in place with generous wraps of hockey tape, trying to find just the right angle so the drum wouldn’t fall over during a fill.

The shell had obvious water damage, warping and discoloration that would’ve killed its resale value, but I didn’t care. What mattered was the sound: that drum sang in the lower register like thunder rolling through your ribcage—deep, round, and somehow both wounded and massive. At first, the resonance was a problem; I stuffed a pillowcase inside for dampening, but even then it still rang out longer than anything else in the room. There was something about that stubborn sustain that I came to love. It gave the kit its bottom—its earthquake. Every time I crashed down on *Low Growl*, it felt like conjuring the ghosts of roller derbies past, all that energy refusing to die. Still too resonant, still unruly, but it belonged to me—and I wouldn’t have traded it for anything brand new.

### **The Rack Tom** – “*Little Bastard*”

13-inch CB700. It came free when I bought a box of broken pedals from a swap meet in Dayton—a bonus that was more curse than gift at first glance. The shell was scuffed, the hardware ancient, and when I pulled it out of the box, I realized the mounting bracket didn’t match any of the standard fittings in my kit. But I wasn’t about to let that stop me. I rigged a solution using a vice clamp and a stack of washers, tightening it down to a battered cymbal stand until it held steady. The whole contraption looked precarious, but it worked—and if the stand wobbled or creaked under pressure, well, that just became part of the kit’s character.

Tuning this tom was its own battle. No matter what I did with the lugs, it flat-out refused to sing like a modern, pristine drum. Eventually, I gave up on making it “correct” and leaned into its flaws, tuning it low and letting it take on the earthy, boomy tone of a drum circle. The result was a thump that sat right at the edge of melodic and percussive, a sound that seemed to speak in a different dialect from the rest of the kit. Played hard, it had a primal, tribal punch—a guttural voice that filled the space between the snare and floor tom. There was a wildness to it, as if it didn’t care about fitting in with the rest of the drums. If the kit was a band of survivors, *Little Bastard* was the loud-mouthed kid you couldn’t shut up.

On the right night, with the band swirling around me, I’d lay into *Little Bastard* and imagine Phil Collins in a parallel universe, behind a wall of mismatched toms, riding the acid and grinning through every cracked rim. In the hands of someone obsessed with uniformity, it might have been junk. For me, it became a secret weapon—a percussive exclamation point that made our weirdest, wildest grooves sound even stranger and more alive.

### **The Hardware** – “*The Holding Pattern*”

Everything was a mismatch, held together by desperation and whatever was within arm’s reach. One cymbal stand had a tennis ball shoved on the foot to keep it from sliding across the cracked garage floor, its neon fuzz slowly darkening with years of sweat and dust. Another had a coffee can full of pennies taped to the base for balance, rattling faintly every time I launched into a fill, like a homemade rain stick. My hi-hat stand was missing the footboard altogether—I played it barefoot, pressing the rod with my toes like a goddamn jungle monkey, improvising muscle memory every single rehearsal. Sometimes my heel would slip and the whole thing would clatter, but I adapted. The throne? That was a stolen stool from my high school’s typing class, a battered piece of institutional furniture that had survived countless bored teenagers and at least three fires. It squeaked—loudly, relentlessly, sometimes even in rhythm with my left foot. I let it stay. The noise, the instability, all of it became part of the kit’s voice, woven into every beat and groove until it was inseparable from the music itself.

### **Sticks** – “*Mismatched, Like My Soul*”

I used whatever I could find. Vic Firths, sure, if I found ‘em in the sale bin at the music store, but those were a rare treat, not the rule. More often than not, I grabbed random sticks from the ‘free box’ at Heartland Music—sticks with chewed-up tips, tape residue, and someone else’s initials still fading from the butt end. Sometimes I’d use castoffs that Jim Huber’s marching band friends had snapped during practice, scavenging the least-damaged halves and making the best of their odd lengths and weights. When those ran out, I got creative: I’d build hot rods out of bamboo skewers from the grocery store, held together with strips of duct tape so they wouldn’t fly apart mid-song. I even experimented with oddities—once I played an entire week of rehearsals with a turkey baster in my right hand, just to see if I could. It wasn’t elegant, but it worked fine, and in a weird way, the unpredictability of the tools made the sound more alive. The mismatched, improvised sticks became another layer of the kit’s personality—quirky, adaptable, and totally unrepeatable from one gig to the next.

## **INT. GILLEY’S GARAGE – SUMMER MORNING, 1990 – PRE-PRACTICE**

The air is dense with summer heat, sunlight slicing through floating dust and painting slow-moving patterns across the battered floor. [[4.6 JEREMY EVANS character bible|Jeremy]] leans into a folding chair, guitar case at his feet, gripping a Gatorade bottle like a sacred offering—hydration before the ritual.

Jim crouches by his kick pedal, methodically tightening a lug with a worn wrench. Bandana tied back, bare-chested, sweat tracing down his shoulders and arms. The scene carries a quiet reverence; the ritual of setup is nearly as important as the music itself.

**JEREMY**  
Hey, can I ask you something?

**JIM**  (doesn’t look up)  
You have my attention, my son.

**JEREMY**  
I mean, what’s the point of all this? I don’t just mean today’s rehearsal. I mean the kit—the whole mess.  
(He gestures at the drum set: a collage of mismatched shells, scavenged hardware, metal bent from years of use.)

**JIM**  (grinning, finally turning to him)  
You mean her.

**JEREMY**  
You actually gave your drum kit a name?

**JIM**  
Not one name. Each component has its own. Every drum, every cymbal, every piece of hardware has a history—a story, a reason it’s here. This isn’t just equipment; it’s an archive. The Island of Misfit Toys, but built with purpose.

**JEREMY**  
But you’re just the timekeeper, right? You hold the beat so [[4.2 JEREMY GILLEY character bible|Gilley]] can belt, so I can solo, so [[4.5 KATHY ESPICH character bible|Kathy]] can play at full roar. Why does it matter if your hi-hat comes from Sears or was fished out of a dumpster?

**JIM**  (cocks his head, sits back on his squeaky throne, leans in)  
 That’s the difference between functional rhythm and true musical identity. What sets apart a merely competent drummer from an exceptional one is the leap from pulse to personality. Why do people recognize Phil Collins in three notes? Why does “[*In the Air Tonight*](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YkADj0TPrJA)” hit you in the chest before your brain catches up? It’s not about speed or flash—every piece of his kit is chosen with intent. Every timbre, every resonance, is a decision. Nothing is random.

**JEREMY**  
But we’re not Genesis. We’re not a prog band. Hell, we’re not even signed.

**JIM**  
Exactly. Which is why I can’t afford for anything to be arbitrary. Every detail, every salvage job, every scrap is a way to make our sound matter. It’s not about just keeping up. It’s about making it count.

## **INT. GILLEY’S GARAGE – MID-MORNING – SUMMER 1990**

Sunlight slants through a high side window, casting sharp lines across the dust and the bare shoulders of Jim, crouched low by his kit. The garage feels less like a practice space and more like a cathedral: coiled cables, gear cases, a lawn mower exiled to the corner, and the steady whir of an oscillating fan fighting the heat.

Jeremy Evans, gangly and sweat-soaked in a faded White Zombie shirt, wipes his hands on cargo shorts and eyes the drum kit—a spaceship built out of junkyard scrap.

Jim is tuning his floor tom by ear, bent low, tapping and listening. Bandana like a field medic, no shirt, gym shorts. The air is stultifying.

**JEREMY** (half to himself)  
I still don’t get why you need five drums and a trash can full of cymbals just to go boom-tssk-boom-tsssk.

Jim doesn’t flinch. He keeps tuning, but a faint grin tugs at his mouth.

**JIM** (gently)  
Say that again.

**JEREMY**  (grinning)  
Boom-tssk-boom-tsssk.

Jim carefully sets down his drum key, and stands up slowly.

**JIM**  
You’re not wrong. That’s the job, most of the time.  

(He studies Jeremy—not judging, just gauging how much to reveal.)  
But you asked the wrong question.

**JEREMY**  
Okay… what’s the right one?

Jim lights up—not loudly, but fully. For once, he has an audience.

**JIM**  
The right question is: why do I choose five drums and a trash can full of cymbals?  Come here. I’ll show you.

Jeremy steps closer, realizing this is reverent, not sarcastic.  Jim crouches beside the bass drum, taps the head gently with the side of his hand.

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
This here’s *Old Thunder*. 1978 Tama Imperialstar. Found it under a church stage in Huber Heights—ten bucks. The hoop was split, so I rebuilt it with plywood from a picture frame. When I hit it—  

(He stands, foot on the pedal; thump—a low, resonant boom fills the room.)  
 —it doesn’t just keep time. It tells you what’s coming. You feel that in your ribs?

Jeremy nods, wide-eyed.

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
That’s the pulse. The sound of the world turning.

(He moves to the snare.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
This bastard? That’s *The Howler*. Three snares in one: Pearl shell, Ludwig throw-off, got it out of a milk crate for seven bucks—came with a spider carcass for luck.  

 (smiling like a kid)  
 It cracks. It barks. Listen.  
 
 (He sits, snaps a crisp note.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
You can’t ignore a snare like that. It’s the seatbelt of the band. Keeps you strapped in when the ride gets stupid.

**JEREMY**  (laughing)  
Okay, okay. What about this one?

(He points to the floor tom.)

**JIM**  
Ah, *Low Growl*. Pulled her from a dumpster behind a roller rink. Shell warped, legs copper plumbing and duct tape. Heavy, stubborn, but when I hit her—  

(He thumps it with a mallet; the boom is deep, like something waking up in a cave.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
That’s the sound of doubt. The moment before the drop.

Jeremy exhales, surprised.

**JEREMY**  
Okay, so… what about these?  

(He gestures to the hi-hats—mismatched and scratched.)

**JIM**  
That’s *Frankenstein’s Dinner Bell*. Top’s British, bottom’s maybe Sears. Shouldn’t work, but when I stomp ‘em—  

(He does. The metallic ksshhh slices the air.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
That’s the whisper between punches. Your handshake with the audience. It says: I’m here. I’m listening. Are you?

Jeremy just stares. He’s hooked.

**JEREMY**  
Okay, but… where’s Phil Collins in all this?

Jim straightens, like a priest whose favorite hymn just got name-checked.

**JIM**  
Phil? Phil’s the North Star. Everyone wants to sound like Dave Grohl’s dirty laundry. That’s fine. That’s fashionable. But Phil Collins? Phil’s engineering. Geometry. Three notes and he summons wrath. That gated snare in “In the Air Tonight”? That’s the universe accusing you of something.

**JEREMY**  (grinning)  
That’s dramatic.

**JIM**  
So is life.  

(He hits the snare again. The sound lingers.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
You think I bash. But I don’t. I sculpt. I’m painting with tension, air, wood, wire. Every piece of this kit has to work with me—not for me. That’s the secret.

**JEREMY**  (softly)  
Dude, you really love this.

Jim shrugs, glowing.

**JIM**  
It’s the only place I don’t stutter. That’s why I picked drums. They don’t talk back. They listen.

**GILLEY (O.S.)**  
If you nerds are done spooning the floor tom, we got rehearsal\!

**JIM**  (quietly, to Jeremy)  
That’s okay. They’ll hear it soon enough.

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
Let’s make noise.

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
Now this little bastard—  

(He taps a small tom perched awkwardly on a crooked arm jutting from a cymbal stand.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
Literally. That’s his name. *Little Bastard.* Thirteen-inch CB700. Got it for free when I bought a box of broken pedals at the Dayton swap meet. The mounting hardware didn’t match any known species, so I vice-clamped it to a cymbal stand and prayed.

**JEREMY**  
That’s… safe.

**JIM**  (grinning)  
Not even slightly. But she sings when she wants to. Tuned just above conversation level. Sounds like a trash can getting offended. I use it to interrupt people.  

(He gives it a quick roll—agitated, sharp, declarative.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
Tells the audience: shut up and listen.  

(He pivots, pointing to a cracked but defiant cymbal with tape on the edge.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
*Crack Baby.* Sixteen-inch Zildjian Avedis. Paid five bucks. The crack’s from the bell outward. You play around it, you get this nasty, gnarled decay—like something got hit too hard and never forgot.  

(He strikes it. It sounds like metal trying to scream politely.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
I only use it in songs where we mean it.  

(Then he gestures to a big, dull ride cymbal next to it.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
That’s *Flat Earth.* Twenty-inch Sabian, dented edge, probably rolled out of a van in the '70s. Swapped a cousin for it when he needed gas money and weed. It’s not sweet. It’s not polite. But if I ride it soft, it whispers. If I ride it hard…  

(He smashes into it and lets it ring.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
It’s a goddamn confession. Sounds like thunder apologizing to no one.

**JEREMY**  
Jesus, dude. These aren’t instruments. They’re characters.

**JIM**  
Exactly. Each one has a job. A voice. You treat ‘em right, they talk. You hit ‘em wrong, they talk back.

**JEREMY**  
Okay, but… (gesturing around) none of this matches. Nothing’s name-brand. Half of it looks like it was salvaged from a barn fire.

**JIM**  (grinning wider)  
That’s the point. I didn’t buy a kit—I earned one. Every piece was a deal, a haggle, a prayer. I got this whole setup for maybe… thirty-two dollars and some lunch money. It’s not pretty. But it’s alive.  

(He raps his throne with a stick.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
This here? My throne? Typing stool from my high school. Found it in the dumpster. Squeaks like a rat with asthma, but it spins.

(Jeremy sits briefly. It spins. Loudly.)

**JEREMY**  
That’s obnoxious.

**JIM**  
But musical.  

(He picks up a pair of wildly mismatched sticks.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
Sticks? Never bought a new pair in my life. One’s a Vic Firth 5A I found under the bleachers. Other’s a marching stick from some band kid who quit. I sanded the tip down with a rock. Works fine.  

(He flips them into his hands like pistols.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
The point is, you don’t need to be shiny. You need to be true. Every hit on this kit is something I chose. Something I felt. That’s why it matters.

**JEREMY** (sincerely)  
I didn’t get it before. I thought you were just… banging on stuff.

**JIM**  
Most people do. Until they realize I’m conducting time itself from a throne that smells like dittos toner.

(They both laugh. The fan hums.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
One day I’ll upgrade. Maybe. But this kit? This is my origin story. It’s the sound of seventeen and not knowing how to talk to girls but knowing how to hit back at the world.

**JEREMY** (nods)  
So when Gilley starts yelling about tempo, I should tell him to respect *The Howler*?

**JIM**  
You should tell him to get a metronome and a clue.  

(Beat.)

**JIM**  (softly)  
But yeah. Respect *The Howler*.

## **EXT. LOWER VALLEY PIKE – NIGHT – JULY 1990**

Gilley’s Pontiac Grand Am crests a rise on the two-lane blacktop, headlights sweeping over endless cornfields and sagging mailboxes. Windows are down, stereo blaring—Ratt, maybe, or early Chili Peppers—something reckless and alive.

Jeremy rides shotgun, hair tousled by the wind, arm out the window like a makeshift sail. Gilley drives one-handed, the other gripping a Big Gulp, tank top and jean shorts, radiating the kind of restless, magnetic energy that signals either disaster or inspiration.

They pass a dark stretch lined with a sloping fence, the faint scent of bullshit drifting through the night air, thick and familiar.

**GILLEY** (grinning sideways)  
Hey. Have you ever tipped a cow?

**JEREMY** (deadpan)  
No. Because I was raised by humans.

**GILLEY**  
C’mon. Don’t be such a Jehovah Witness about it. It’s Lower Valley Pike. This is tradition.

**JEREMY**  
Pretty sure trespassing and harassing livestock isn’t a tradition. That’s… criminal. Or at least stupid.

**GILLEY** (staring ahead)  
Could be both.

The car coasts past a wooden sign: **COPEY’S FAMILY RANCH – PRIVATE PROPERTY**

**GILLEY (CONT’D)**  
Besides… what if one of us found a real cowbell?

**JEREMY** (squinting)  
Wait. You’re serious?

**GILLEY**  
Imagine the face Jim would make. We show up to practice with a real, actual cowbell. The kind that’s seen things. Tasted alfalfa. Rung with authority.

Jeremy can’t help but grin. The idea is both idiotic and undeniably compelling.

**JEREMY**  
You think he’d use it?

**GILLEY**  
He says no. But deep down? That little Phil Collins blood pudding inside him? It’s gonna start itching.

**JEREMY** (grinning now)  
You’re the devil.

**GILLEY**  
Nah. Just the rhythm section’s muse.  

**GILLEY (CONT’D)**  
So… you in?

Jeremy looks out at the fence posts flickering past in the dark. The moon is low. The air is thick with grass, diesel, and the promise of bad decisions.

He sighs.

**JEREMY**  
God help me, let’s get a goddamn cowbell.

## **EXT. COPEY RANCH – PERIMETER FENCELINE – NIGHT**

The Pontiac Grand Am is tucked behind a tree line, engine still ticking from the climb. Gilley and Jeremy crawl under a slack section of barbed wire, shoes squelching in the damp earth. It’s quiet. Too quiet. No lights. No dogs. Just the low insect buzz of a summer night and the distant breath of cattle somewhere unseen.

**JEREMY** (whispering)  
I feel like we’re gonna get shot with rock salt and buried in silage.

**GILLEY**  (grinning)  
You feel that way because you lack vision.

They creep along a disused gravel lane that cuts behind the main feedlot. Half-hidden in a tangle of pokeweed and brush is an old barn—leaning, rust-streaked, like it’s trying to fall into its own grave. The wood slats are gray with time. One side has caved in completely. A rusted pulley hangs from the roof beam like a gallows ornament.

**JEREMY** (staring at the barn)  
That thing wanted to collapse in 1957 and just never got the courage.

**GILLEY**  (grinning)  
Perfect. Nobody tears down a building that dangerous. They just stop noticing it exists.

## **INT. COPEY RANCH – OLD BARN – NIGHT**

They push through the brush and duck into the barn through a yawning hole in the siding. Inside, it smells like rust, manure dust, and a history nobody recorded. There’s broken tack on the walls. A splintered cart wheel. Rotting hay bales.

**JEREMY**  (low voice)  
This place is haunted.

**GILLEY**  
Yeah, by tetanus.

They shine their flashlights around—slicing through spiderwebs, illuminating half-collapsed rafters. In the back, nearly buried under a pile of moldy burlap and busted crates, something glints.

**JEREMY**  (quietly)  
No…

**GILLEY**  
Yes.

They approach. It’s a metal bell, the kind once hung around a draft ox or workhorse. Heavy. Dented. Edges curled like a dead tulip. The clapper still rings when Gilley jostles it, echoing a dull, ancient clang.

**GILLEY (CONT’D)** (lifting it gingerly)  
This is older than Phil Collins’ dick.

**JEREMY**  
That’s… almost holy.

**GILLEY**  
It’s not a cowbell. It’s pre-cowbell. This is Genesis. Not the band. The book.

**JEREMY** (laughing softly)  
Jim’s gonna hate it.

**GILLEY**  
Or he’s gonna fall in love.  
  
**GILLEY (CONT’D)**  
Either way, we bring this to rehearsal, and something changes.

They both look at the bell. It’s stupid. It’s perfect. It’s exactly the kind of relic that finds you when you’re too young to deserve it and just stupid enough to take it anyway. They nod—unspoken agreement. As they turn to leave, the flashlight beam catches a set of initials carved into the stall post: **B.C. – 1948**

Neither of them notices.

Best five-finger discount of my life.

## **INT. GILLEY’S GARAGE – LATE AFTERNOON – DAY OF REHEARSAL**

The garage radiates heat and humidity, saturated with the scent of dust, sweat, and the faint tang of burned-out amplifiers. Jim is bent over his drum kit, intently tuning the snare—focused, methodical, almost meditative. He’s immersed in his own ritual, oblivious to the mischief about to unfold.

Gilley and Jeremy step inside, carrying themselves with a deliberate nonchalance, as if they hadn’t just pulled off a low-key heist for the sake of music history. Jeremy clutches something concealed under a towel.

**GILLEY**  
Jimbo.

**JIM**  (grunts)  
You’re late.

**JEREMY**  
But we brought you a present.

Jim turns. Sees the towel. His eyebrow arches in suspicion.

**JIM**  
What… did you do?

**GILLEY**  
We liberated it.

**JEREMY**  
From entropy.

**GILLEY**  
From obscurity.

**JEREMY**  
From a collapsing barn full of ghosts.

(Beat. Jeremy reveals the object: an old, heavy cowbell, its surface dulled by time. The patina speaks of a hundred years of neglect and survival.)

Jim steps forward, wordless. He runs his thumb across the surface, studying the aged steel before leaning in to inspect the inscription.

**JIM** (reading softly)  
“William Brown and Sons. 1867\. Dayton, O.”

(He exhales, as if something long held in his chest finally lets go.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
You… assholes.

**GILLEY**  
You’re welcome.

**JIM**  
Do you have any idea what this is?

**JEREMY**  
Nope.

**JIM**  
This is cast iron—likely forged at the old foundry downtown. This bell predates the radio, maybe even electric light. It could’ve hung from a team of cattle hauling timber during Reconstruction.

**GILLEY**  
So… it’s vintage?

**JIM**  
It’s more than that. It’s righteous.

(Jim moves to his kit, almost in a trance. He mounts the bell onto a cymbal arm with a wingnut—a combination that shouldn’t work, but does. Raising a stick, he prepares to strike.)

**JEREMY**  
Wait—wait. You’re actually going to—

**CLANG**.

(The bell detonates through the garage—a resonant, metallic tone halfway between a fire alarm and a temple gong. It doesn’t just clang. It proclaims.)

Gilley and Jeremy wince, then break into laughter. Jim just grins.

**JIM**  
Happy birthday to me.  
(Beat.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
We start the next song with that.  
(Beat.)

**JIM (CONT’D)**  
Every show.

## **2025 – JIM BROWN – MODERN DRUM KIT REVIEW EXCERPT**  
*Published in Modern Drummer, April 2025 issue*

**“The Future in Front of Me. The Past at My Left.”**  
by Jim Brown, Commuted

Let’s start with the bones. I play a 2025 Roland hybrid rig, custom-built to withstand three-hour stadium sets, airport conveyor belts, and whatever hell Bill does to the in-ear mix when he’s feeling cocky. The shells? Carbon fiber. The heads? Low-profile mesh, embedded with pressure sensors and tactile feedback spanning the full frequency range. The tom mics are AI-balanced in real time, with every nuance digitized and translated instantly. The rack is milled titanium—lightweight, road-proof, impossible to kill. I can trigger four samples per pad and adjust haptic pushback on the fly.

It’s not a drum kit. It’s a spaceship on legs.

But if you glance at the stage left—just past the ride, above the second floor tom—you’ll see one piece that doesn’t match. It’s old. Rusted. Bolted to a plate I hand-welded, because I don’t trust anyone else to mount it right. There’s no MIDI-mapping. No cable to the desk. And it sure as hell doesn’t come in chrome.

It’s a cowbell.

Not a sample. Not a branded boutique reissue. The cowbell. Stamped: “William Brown and Sons, 1867, Dayton O.”

Gilley and Jeremy dragged it out of a collapsing barn back in 1990\. We were just kids. I didn’t even want a cowbell then—Phil Collins never used one, nor did Larry Mullen Junior. But I hit it once—just once—and it sounded like a hammer thrown by God.

Thirty-five years later, it still does.

I could replace every other piece of my kit tomorrow: shells, pads, cymbals, triggers, throne, sticks. The manufacturers would send replacements in bubble wrap and call it progress.

But that bell?  That stays. Because the bell reminds me: every time you strike something, it matters. Every sound has a price. And every part of your kit should be something you chose—not just something you bought.

The future may be carbon fiber and microsecond latency. But the soul of my kit? It was forged in Dayton in 1867. And it still rings true.
