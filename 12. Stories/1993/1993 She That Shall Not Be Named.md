# **SHE THAT SHALL NOT BE NAMED**

---
##  **Flashback: Spring 1991, Springfield, Ohio—Jeremy Evans’ First Wedding**

The church is half full—nervous aunts in homemade dresses, [[5.1 Tim Wright (Tim)|Tim]] Wright in a corduroy jacket that still smells like last night’s weed. The bride is already radiating “problem” energy, brittle and self-important, the kind of woman who counts calories at her own reception and glares when the band jokes about the open bar. Jeremy’s hair is already thinning, but he’s young, thin, sharp in the way only desperate guitarists are. [[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff]]—Best Man—can’t stop fidgeting with his tie, smirking like he’s going to bolt any second.

[[4.5 KATHY ESPICH character bible|Kathy]], standing in the back with [[4.2 JEREMY GILLEY character bible|Gilley]] and [[4.1 BILL DAVENPORT character bible|Bill]], has a red Solo cup of wedding punch and a predator’s grin. She leans into Gilley, voice pitched just low enough to be dangerous:

**KATHY (V.O.):**  
God, what a mistake. She’s going to eat him alive and spit out the stubble. Did you see how she glared at me during the rehearsal dinner? Like I was going to drag him off to a lesbian commune. Maybe I should’ve. Jesus, at least then he’d get a good meal and a blowjob that didn’t come with a fucking prayer circle.

**GILLEY:**  
You wanna go in there and say something?  

**KATHY:**  
Babe, if I went in there, I’d just offer her my condolences. Or hand Jeremy my room key and a Xanax.  

**BILL:**  
He’s not that dumb. He knows what he’s doing.  

**KATHY:**  
No, he doesn’t. He knows what his dick is doing, and that’s about it.

---

Later, at the reception, as “[Dancing in the Dark](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=129kuDCQtHs)” plays (the DJ’s idea of romance), Tim Wright corners Jeremy at the bar, eyes wild:

---

**TIM:**  
You don’t have to do this, man. You really fucking don’t.  

**JEFF (arriving, tie askew):**  
Seriously, Jer. Last chance—Gilley and I can have you in Tijuana by sunrise. They won’t even miss you until the cake’s cut.

**JEREMY:**  
Jesus, you assholes. It’s fine. She’s fine.

**KATHY (over Jeff’s shoulder):**  
You’re not fine, you’re fucked. You better keep your guitars locked up. She looks like the type who’ll pawn your Telecaster for a new set of drapes.

---

**KATHY** (to Jim, later)
You know what it is? He needs to do it. Needs to ruin himself once, so he knows what the rest of us already figured out—love is the opening act, regret is the encore. He’ll come crawling back, and I’ll be there with a bottle and a list of all the things she didn’t let him do in bed.

**JIM** (mild, toying with his ring)
You’d really take him on?  That sounds pretty damn reckless, Kath.

**KATHY**
I’d fuck him out of spite, and then I’d write a song about it. “*She That Shall Not Be Named*.” God, it writes itself.

---

**Postscript:**  
Jeff and Gilley really did have a plan. They’d mapped out the drive, even had cash stashed in the glovebox, just in case Jeremy sobered up mid-reception and decided to bolt.  He didn’t, of course. He made it almost two miserable years.  

Kathy never liked the wife, never bothered to learn her favorite drink, but she kept the story alive for years—whenever someone in the band started dating a control freak or a wet blanket, Kathy would just lift her glass and say, “Don’t Jeremy this, okay? Don’t marry the first pretty disaster who agrees to fuck you sober.”  

Kath was ruthless, and she was right.

## **“Free Return Trajectory – November 1992”**

The air is wet and grim with November, the kind of Ohio cold that seeps into your teeth. Jeremy’s new place is a rental on the edge of New Carlisle, one-story, forgettable, the walls painted the color of cigarette ash. Gilley and Jeff have just driven back from watching a gig in Dayton, Gilley's Ford Ranger rattling with old cassettes and fresh dread, and they’re here because they don’t trust him to make it through a night alone.

The first thing they notice: **no cups.**  

The fridge—empty except for two cans of malt liquor and a pack of Marlboro Reds wedged beside the icebox light.  The freezer: ice cube trays, half-full, not even a bag of peas for show.  

In the drawers: nothing but a single bottle opener and some crumbs. No plates. No evidence anyone here has eaten since summer.

**JEREMY** (answering the door, Marlboro between his fingers, jeans half-zipped):  
Hey. Thought you were cops.

**GILLEY** (kicking the fridge shut):  
You wish, man. You look like you’re living in a crime scene.

**JEFF** (surveying the room, the unfurnished bleakness):  
You planning on dying in here, or just killing time till you can move back in with Mom and Dad?

**JEREMY** (shrugging, smirking, voice thin):  
Depends how long it takes for the cable to get hooked up.

They sit on the dusty carpet, the three of them—no chairs, so it’s just bodies, knees to chest, hands wrapped around malt liquor bottles, passing smokes like communion. There’s a pizza box in the corner, but it’s empty except for the crust.  The silence grows, thick as the nicotine haze.

**GILLEY**:  
Got any cups?

**JEREMY**:  
Who needs cups?

He drinks straight from the bottle, wipes his mouth with the back of his hand.  Kathy calls and surprised her by answering.

**KATHY (via payphone, her voice electric over the static):**  
You idiots taking care of him? Don’t let him start writing poems. That’s when you know he’s really fucked.

**JEFF** (covering the receiver):  
She says you’re not allowed to go full Morrissey in here. She’ll mail you a shot glass if you promise not to write another breakup song.

Jeremy cracks a smile for the first time all week. It’s ugly and hopeful.

The night wears on. Jeff puts on a cassette—one of the early Commuted demos, rough and fast and full of too much energy.  They play it loud enough to piss off the neighbors. Gilley raps his knuckles against the bare wall, keeping time. Jeremy stubs out a cigarette, exhales a shiver.

**GILLEY:**  
You know you can come back, right? To the band, to the couch, hell, to my mom’s lasagna if you want.

**JEREMY:**  
I know.

Outside, it starts to rain again, hard enough to erase the last bit of road dirt from the van.

That night, the three of them drank, coughed, listened, and survived. No wife, no cups, no reason left except the music. Sometimes the only thing you need is an empty fridge and a song you can scream at the ceiling.

Kathy called back later:  *He’s got to fail on his own terms. Just don’t let him drown in the kitchen sink. Tell him I’ll write him a new song. Call it “No Cups in Ohio.”*

The next morning, the bottles were gone and the band was back together, not because anything was fixed, but because nothing else made sense. True as whiskey and twice as hard to swallow.

## **“Tattoo Parlor Gospel—December 1992”**

**Day after Thanksgiving, Springfield, Ohio.**  

Jeremy’s got the look of a man who’s just realized surviving divorce is a kind of haunting. He hasn’t shaved; his clothes are on inside out, or at least look it.  He’s got his last thirty bucks cash crumpled in his jeans and an idea for “a mark, something to make this mean something.”  

Gilley and Jeff are with him—they’ve been with him all week, afraid to leave him alone long enough to start alphabetizing his record collection by heartbreak.  Jeff's worst habits might be infectious, and all. Kathy is on a media tour, but she calls in twice, drunk, insisting he “get something that’ll make the next girl ask questions.”

The tattoo parlor is in a strip mall next to a shuttered Payless Shoes and a Chinese buffet that smells like wet dogs.  The kid with the needle has a blond goatee and a Guns and Roses t-shirt with a rip at the nipple.

**GILLEY:**  
You sure you don’t want something cool?  

**JEREMY:**  
No.  

**JEFF:**  
How about the band logo?  

**JEREMY:**  
Fuck no.  

**GILLEY:**  
How about Kathy’s phone number?  

**JEREMY (dead serious):**  
That’s just dangerous.

So he opens a napkin, scrawls out the **yin/yang symbol**—except he draws it upside down. The dark on top, the light on bottom, both circles off-center. He hands it to the artist like it’s a sacred text.

**TATTOO ARTIST:**  
You know this is backwards, right?  

**JEREMY:**  
Story of my fucking life.

The needle bites his shoulder. He winces, but doesn’t flinch. Gilley films it on his shitty Hi8 camcorder, narrating:

**GILLEY (off camera):**  
Here we are, recording Jeremy’s descent into imbalance and bad decisions.

Jeff snorts, but it’s affectionate. Jeremy stares at the ceiling tiles, lips moving, silent prayer or new lyrics.

**TATTOO ARTIST:**  
There. You’re officially unique.  

**JEREMY:**  
Or cursed.

**AFTER:**  
He stands in the mirror, shirt off, bandage on his shoulder, flesh angry-red around the tattoo, black ink shining like wet oil.  He looks smaller and stronger, like someone who’s been through something and lived.

**GILLEY:**  
You gonna tell people you did it on purpose?  

**JEREMY:**  
Hell yes.

They go for Skyline chili dogs after, and Jeremy doesn’t say much, but he sits straighter.  Later, Kathy leaves a message:

**KATHY (voicemail):**  
About time you branded yourself, Evans. Yin and yang, upside down—like your marriage, like your brain. Maybe now you’ll finally write a song that’s not in 4/4.

---

**ARCHIVIST’S NOTE:**  
That upside-down tattoo showed up in every dressing room mirror for the next decade—a reminder that sometimes the balance you need is the chaos you choose.

No wife, no cups, but now he had a permanent badge. And every new lover would ask him about it, and he’d always say: “It’s on purpose.” And the band would laugh, and the story would live.

## **“Jeremy’s Upside-Down Mark”**

**December 1992, Vandalia warehouse, cold as a crypt, the gear sweating with condensation from the last cold snap.**  

The band assembles for the first practice post-Thanksgiving, amps humming, everyone expecting Jeremy to be a wreck or a revelation. He’s neither—he’s just there, wired, grinning, tugging his shirt down off his shoulder.

**JEREMY (loud, to the room):**  
Check this out, motherfuckers.

He peels off his flannel. The tattoo is angry, raw, inked high on his left shoulder: yin/yang, but flipped—the black toppling over the white, both dots drifting off axis like someone knocked the world sideways.

**KATHY (immediately):**  
Why the fuck is it upside down? You been hanging from the ceiling or just going through it backwards?

**JEREMY (with a devil’s smirk):**  
It’s for when I’m sixty-nining someone.  

(Pauses, deadpan.)  
So she can appreciate the cosmic balance while I’m working.

The room falls *dead silent*—then everyone starts squinting, twisting, doing math with their eyes. Jeff, usually unflappable, looks like he’s just tried to divide by zero. Bill tilts his head. Gilley tries to mime it, then almost falls off the amp.

**BILL:**  
So… wait, you’re… under? Or… she’s—  

**KATHY:**  
I mean, I appreciate the ambition, Evans, but unless she’s got a mirror on the ceiling, she’s just gonna see your wrinkly ballsack.

**JEFF:**  
Dude, you could have just gotten “open here” tattooed on your thigh and saved everyone the geometry lesson.

**GILLEY (laughing, then wheezing):**  
What kind of girls are you pulling who want a philosophy lesson mid-orgasm?  

(He cackles.)  
God, you’re such a fuckin’ poet.

**JEREMY (defiant, grinning):**  
Hey, at least it’s not the band logo.

**KATHY (mutters to Jim):**  
Next time I’m on top, I’m bringing a compass and a protractor.

Practice is a write-off for ten minutes.  Every time Jeremy tunes his guitar, someone snickers, someone mutters “balance,” and Gilley keeps trying to look at his own shoulder upside down, almost giving himself whiplash. When they finally play—new song, heavy riff—Jeremy rips his shirt all the way off. The ink shines in the fluorescent light, the mark of a man who has survived, fucked up, and intends to keep fucking up, on purpose.

**ARCHIVIST’S NOTE:**  
The tattoo was never about sex, or karma, or cosmic order. It was a sign:  “I survived. I’m upside down, and I’m still here.”  But God, did they all wish they’d he'd never thought of it.

## **“First Test of Jeremy’s Yin/Yang”**

**Warehouse, Vandalia, December 1992.** 

Practice ended an hour ago. Most of the band is gone—Bill off chasing a synthesizer high, Gilley already texting a girl from Dayton, Jeff chain-smoking out by the van. Kathy lingers, draped on a drum case, watching Jeremy coil cables, still shirtless, tattoo glowing in the halogen bruise of midnight. She’s holding a battered geometry compass, twirling it in her fingers, eyes sharp and mischievous.

**KATHY (drawling, low):**  
You said it’s for women to appreciate? Prove it.  

(She stands, stalks over, presses the cold metal legs of the compass gently to the swollen lines of his new tattoo, tracing circles with clinical, almost obscene, precision.)

**JEREMY (tensing, grinning):**  
You gonna grade me on symmetry, teach?

**KATHY:**  
This is some shoddy sacred geometry, Evans. Yin’s drifting west, yang’s got a flat tire.  

(She runs a thumb just below the bandage, gaze dark, almost considering.)

**KATHY (after a beat, looking him up and down):**  
So, you gonna invite me over, or am I just gonna stand here critiquing your ink?

**JEREMY (suddenly sheepish):**  
Uh… I don’t have sheets. Or, like, a bed. Or… anything soft.  

(Bare honesty, the real scar tissue showing.)

**KATHY (half laughing, half furious):**  
God, you are such a fucking disaster.  

(Louder, for the rafters:)  
I’m not fucking you on bare vinyl, Evans. I have standards. Bare minimum: sheets. And not the ones that smell like your ex-wife’s shampoo.

A long pause. He shrugs, trying to look unbothered, but he’s clearly embarrassed.

**KATHY:**  
Come on.  

(Grabs her coat, tosses him his shirt.)  
We’re going to Walmart. You’re getting sheets. And a pillow. And you’re not allowed to buy anything with cartoon characters or NASCAR logos.

---

**Midnight, Walmart parking lot.**  
Fluorescent lights hum. Kathy leads him through the aisles with military precision—sheet sets, cheap but soft, navy blue; a pillow that promises “cloud support”; a jug of detergent and two condoms, which she throws in the cart without breaking eye contact.

**KATHY (deadpan):**  
If you ever have a girl over who brings a protractor, run.  

(Turns, grins.)  
But if she brings a compass? That’s your sign.

At checkout, Jeremy tries to pay, but she smacks his hand away, slaps down a twenty, daring him to argue.

**Back at his apartment.**  She supervises him making the bed—sloppy but passable—then inspects it like a drill sergeant. Runs her palm over the sheets, checks the corners. Sits. Looks up at him.

**KATHY:**  
There. Now you’re halfway civilized. 

(She lies back, fully clothed, stretches out, and points at his shoulder.)  
Show me the universe, Evans. But keep your pants on ‘til I say.

She pulls him down beside her, head on his chest, compass resting on the nightstand, cold November wind rattling the single window.

**ARCHIVIST’S NOTE:**  
A clean bed, a friend at his side, and the first real laugh he’s had since he left New Hampshire.  The tattoo is warm against her cheek. Kathy falls asleep first, shoes on, compass under her hand.  That’s the thing about sacred geometry: Sometimes, you have to buy new sheets before the stars align.

## **“The Walmart Tryst”**

It’s just past 2 a.m. in Jeremy’s new rental.  The air is thick with fabric softener and the wild hope of starting over.  Kathy, sprawled on new navy sheets, hair fanned out, looks at Jeremy—shirtless, raw, tattoo gleaming, half-smiling in the moonlit fug of his first real privacy in months.

She’s already kicked off her panties.  The compass is on the nightstand, a totem of absurdity and precision, pointing nowhere but at them.

**KATHY (quiet, but sharp):**  
Condoms, Evans.  

(Pauses, taps her belly with the edge of her palm)  
IUD’s for the guys I trust. You? You’re still detoxing from ex-wife cooties.

**JEREMY (pulling a condom from the box, hands shaking just enough to show):**  
I wouldn’t trust me either.

**KATHY (smirking, tugging him down):**  
Just don’t get lube on the tattoo. It’s supposed to heal, not mutate.

It’s awkward at first—laughter and nerves, old aches in new positions, the sound of plastic crinkling under their knees. The sheets smell chemical and sharp.  But it doesn’t matter. They fuck like they’re staking a flag in lost territory, claiming this sad, empty room for the living.

He lets her on top—she rides him slow, pinches the compass between her knuckles and traces the upside-down tattoo with her tongue.

**KATHY (panting, words broken by motion):**  
See? Now the universe makes sense.  

(She laughs, dark and low.)  
I’m the fucking balance.

He groans, arches up, hands full of her hips, both of them dissolving into sweat and shared anger at everything that came before.  She bites his shoulder—right on the black dot—leaves a mark on the mark, just for her. They finish messy and giggling, the condom knotted and flung into the trash, proof that old wounds don’t keep you from new joys.  She lies against him, spent and untamed, and for a second, neither of them is alone. For a second, the world is in perfect, dirty symmetry.

**KATHY (sleepy, into his neck):**  
I’ll leave the compass. Next time, we try for pi.

**ARCHIVIST’S NOTE:**  
Trust is a slow build, but the barriers are real.  You don’t buy new sheets because you plan on falling in love; you buy them because you’re done bleeding on someone else’s.  She Who Shall Never Be Named is gone, but her shadow’s still in the room, and Kathy—pragmatic, sacred, wild—knows better than to fuck without a shield.

That’s how healing starts: navy sheets, latex, a little geometry, and a night where nobody pretends to be safe.

## **“The Fist Bump Epiphany”**

Morning is pale through frosted glass, cold on bare feet, and the navy sheets are already rumpled, half-pulled from the mattress, flecked with crumbs from a sleeve of crackers they split at dawn. Kathy wakes first—always does—and stretches, hair wild, mouth tasting of sleep and salt and late-night laughter. Jeremy’s still dozing, tattoo half-hidden under her thigh, face open and untroubled for the first time in months.

They fuck again, lazily, with daylight slanting in and the world outside silent except for the occasional rumble of a delivery truck. The second condom gets used, and it’s easier this time, gentler—a kind of affirmation, less hunger, more healing.  Kathy laughs when he comes, low and surprised, and kisses his chest before rolling off, arms flung overhead.

**KATHY (propping herself up, voice half-mirth, half-philosophy):**  
Look at you. You’re practically human again. I’d almost say you’re happy, except your hair still looks like you lost a bar fight with a squirrel.

**JEREMY (grinning, reaching for her hand):**  
Best night I’ve had since… 

(pauses, shrugs)  
…hell, since high school.

She studies him a long moment—how the dark has drained from his eyes, how the lines around his mouth have softened, how he’s stopped channeling Jeff’s perpetual gloom. There’s relief in her, too, a lightness. Something that isn’t hunger or regret.

**KATHY (suddenly, almost startled at herself):**  
You know, Evans… I think I figured something out.

He looks at her, waiting.

**KATHY (soft, real):**  
You’re a friend. Not a lay.  

(beat)  
This was—  

(smiles wryly)  
—well, not a mistake. But maybe not necessary.

(pauses, looking away, then back)  
I spread my legs in error.  

(grins, reaching out her fist) 
How about a fist bump instead of an apology?

He doesn’t hesitate. He bumps her knuckles, both of them snorting laughter, easy, honest, nothing between them but understanding.  She tosses him a pillow, starts making the bed—badly—while he boils coffee in a saucepan.  On his fridge, in lipstick, she writes:  *FRIENDSHIP IS AN UPSIDE-DOWN YIN/YANG*.

They eat crackers, share bad coffee, and for once, nobody feels used.

Sometimes, you need to fuck it out to find the boundary.  Kathy’s never one for regret, but she knows now: sometimes a good friend is worth more than the best lay, especially when the world is upside down and you’re both trying to figure out which way is north.  The fist bump is a contract. The friendship outlives the sheets.
