# **INT. BACK HALLWAY – AFTER THE SET – NIGHT**

The door slams like a cymbal choke and the air out here is meat-warm, a corridor of breath and ductwork and the slow drip of a busted A/C. [[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff Evans]] steps into it backward, bass still slung, cable dragging like a wounded tail. He’s twenty-two and radiant with ruin, pores leaking champagne salt, hair damp, eyes caught between laughter and a prayer. His forearms are welted where the strap bit. He’s mouthing a thank-you to the nothing that comes after noise, a superstition he keeps—every gig, the same little liturgy: Thanks for letting me survive it.

I trail him like static, the band’s shadow that writes it down. The room’s a cheap cathedral—stained concrete floor, graffitied vending machine humming in C-sharp, damp flyers curling off cinderblock. The lights are the sick yellow of a gas station and the only sanctified thing is the bass, that battered black mirror against his hip. The instrument still vibrates faintly like a lover who hasn’t finished. He thumbs the E-string and it answers back with a low animal vow you can feel in your knees.

**JEFF** (half to himself, half to me)  
That last chorus was a cliff I jumped and then the cliff jumped after me.

He grins, then winces, then grins harder, because pain is just applause with its mouth closed. The others are still inside shaking loose the last yards of thunder, but the bass player’s job is to hold everything together, and it’s always the spine that gives first. Sweat runs down the V of his chest, a tributary map. His T-shirt is gray now, not black, and there’s a constellation of little salt stars dried on it from a tour that doesn’t end, not really, just gets handed from night to night like a bad habit.

He leans his forehead against the wall. It’s cold, blessedly, and he groans like he’s having a minor religious experience. Twenty-two is the age when a groan still sounds like a hymn. He peels the bass off, holds it by the neck, and the shape it leaves across his torso is like a tan line from a different life: the ghost of a thing that owns him. I can smell him—copper, laundry, beer, that sweet ozone you get when speakers have been hot all night, the church incense of fried electricity. Music leaves residue; he’s lacquered in it.

Bill hustles by in a comet of adrenaline and wrist tape, muttering about the kick mic, and Jeff gives him a lazy salute with the headstock, saint of low end blessing a passing pilgrim. Jeremy floats past like a sparrow with a guitar case, precious and careful, talking about a voicing he didn’t nail in verse two as if the audience kept a ledger. Kathy is in the doorway still, wringing light out of her hair, smiling like she’s just been let in on the universe’s private joke. Jim—say his name and people look at me—sticks his head out long enough to shout something about merch, then vanishes, that difficult captain. They all orbit around the calm in Jeff, because every ship needs a keel and he’s the keel, however much he pretends to be just another wave.

He finds a milk crate and sits. His knees bounce. The tremor that comes after the roar is its own rhythm, a metronome set by God or amps. I sit cross-legged on the concrete across from him, recorder off, notebook open. He hates the red light of the recorder; he wants to be taken down in graphite, slow, human errors intact. He takes off his wristband, wrings it out, and a small rain hits the floor.

**JEFF**  
You know that kid front left? Pink hair, crutches?

**[[5.5 Kendall Weatherby|KENDALL]]**  
Saw him. Yelled every word like he’d written them.

**JEFF**  
He did. Tonight he did. I just borrowed them back.

He says shit like this without blinking. Twenty-two and already a grand thief of meaning. He drinks from a bottle that’s more backwash than beer and wipes his mouth with the hem of his shirt, exposing a slice of stomach, the diagonal bruise blooming there where the bass rode him like a bull. My thighs press together; I’m not a journalist right now and he knows it. We exist in a fevered truce: I get the truth if I don’t pretend not to want the boy he was before the stage bit him and made him devout.

He watches me watch him and lets it ride. There’s always a power struggle, and it’s almost sweet here, this gentle tug between the archivist and the archive. He refuses to be posed and I refuse to look away. He looks at his hands. Calluses like topography, the pale moons of them proof he’s been digging graves for silence. He flexes, shakes out the snare fire in his forearms, then smiles, embarrassed by his own body’s applause.

**JEFF**  
I thought I was gonna pass the fuck out halfway through “*Probably Not*.” Saw the light. Turns out it was just the monitor.

He laughs, a crack of clean thunder, then coughs, then laughs again because vulnerability and victory have the same aftertaste. The door behind us opens and a gust of night sneaks in—wet concrete, street sausage, the city’s nocturnal piss-and-rose perfume. He closes his eyes and breathes like he’s taking the smell into storage for a lonely future. Twenty-two-year-olds hoard sensation without meaning to; their senses are thieves with pockets they don’t even check.

Some kid from the opening band wants a setlist, wants advice, wants to be knighted by proximity. Jeff hands him the gaffer-taped paper and says, “Tune lower than you think.” He could say anything and it would be gospel now, and you can tell he hates that, the way a good man hates being made into a statue when he still wants to move. The kid leaves haloed in gratitude, and we are back in the acoustics of the pop machine.

**KENDALL**  
Tell me what it felt like. The last two minutes.

**JEFF**  
Like—okay. Like running downhill so fast the hill disappears. Like kissing someone and realizing they’re the one who’s been kissing you your whole life. Like falling through the floor and landing on the exact note you meant to play.

He laughs again at his own ridiculousness and eyes me with that private-boy mischief. He is exhausted and exhilarated, which means his defenses are butter and his honesty has teeth. His mouth is cut on the inside where he bit it in the bridge; a tiny ruby sits in the corner like punctuation. He licks it away, absentminded, then looks briefly, brutally young—twenty-two in the way of summer jobs and sunburned noses—before the stage-mask resettles and he is the myth again, the boy who tames earthquakes with four strings.

He stands, and I rise with him. He rotates his shoulder, grimaces, then slings the bass back like a promise. He doesn’t even put it in the case; cases are coffins and we are not burying anything tonight. He fishes a crushed cigarette from his pocket, twirls it between his fingers, considers, then tucks it behind his ear like a talisman he refuses to light. He’s trying to be good, that’s the joke, in a profession that pays you to be holy and filthy at once.

**[[4.5 KATHY ESPICH character bible|KATHY]]**  (offscreen)  
There you are. We’re signing shirts. Come be handsome.

She grabs his elbow. He lets himself be led but glances back at me like we’ve interrupted a confession. I shake my head: we’ve preserved it. That’s my trick—catch the heat before it hardens into a story and cools into a lie.

We get to the little table by the loading dock. The pen he’s handed is one of those thick metallic numbers that makes your name look like a logo. He hates it. He trades it for a battered Sharpie and writes like a person instead of a brand. His signature is small, almost shy, first letter emphatic, the rest a quick river. He asks names and repeats them, folds them into the ink. “To Luis.” “To Rae.” “To the kid with the crutches—what’s your name? Liam? To Liam.” He draws a little bass clef smile where a smile should be.

He is a genius without humility, except he’s not—it’s more complicated; it’s the humility of a craftsperson who knows his worth so completely he doesn’t have to drape it in false modesty. He knows the exact distance between his fingers and glory and he can cross it on instinct. He also knows there are nights when the animal won’t come and he will have to fake it with posture and grit. Tonight, blessedly, the animal came, rolled over, and purred.

From the corner, I watch him flirt like a lighthouse: steady, never promised. Some woman with eyeliner like a battlement leans in to whisper something that makes him blush. He looks at the floor. He’s a philanderer in theory, a monk in practice, his romance more with the thing that roars at 60 Hz than with the warm catastrophes of actual bodies. But the way he holds the bass—god, the way he held it onstage, straddling it, hips synced to drum and throat, playing like he was trying to make the floorboards ovulate—don’t tell me there isn’t sex in that sanctity. The instrument is both icon and partner; the sacred and the obscene meet on his fretboard and shake hands.

**[[4.1 BILL DAVENPORT character bible|BILL]]** (returns, claps him)  
Stop making them love you and help me get the road case up the ramp, Romeo.

**JEFF**  
They only love me because I’m the one who keeps them from drowning.

**BILL**  
You don’t keep anybody from drowning. You just make drowning sound bad.

They grin at each other, the old argument—who’s saving who. He sets down the Sharpie, hoists the case with that same sore-shoulder gallantry, and the tendons in his neck jump like bass strings. The ramp is slick with spilled beer; he moves slow, deliberate, as if careful were a kind of prayer. He and Bill maneuver the coffin toward the mouth of a van that has lived six hundred nights like this, and when they shove it the last inch, Jeff stumbles, catches himself with a palm on the bumper, and laughs, that ridiculous sunlight laugh again.

I’m supposed to be careful about him, and I am, but precaution is not abstinence. I write what his body tells mine: that he is invincible for exactly the length of a set and then nothing but mortal meat again, that he believes in rhythm the way other people believe in their mothers, that his arrogance is actually devotion to a low frequency most of us ignore. He turns and catches me noting the way his shoulder blade rides under the skin, that winged bone, and he rolls it with theatricality, a little shrug-dance like he’s undressing a moment of pain for me alone.

**JEFF**  
Put this in your sacred scrolls: I’m never gonna play that bridge that well again. Tonight was the fat kid at the carnival throwing a perfect strike and winning the biggest stuffed animal. That was me. I want that bear forever.

**KENDALL**  
You can’t keep the bear. The bear belongs to the night.

**JEFF**  
Then I’ll steal it back tomorrow.

He drops down on an amp and massages the place where the strap dug in, that angry red neighbor of his heart. I think about how memory is a bruise we press to make sure it’s real. He looks up and the backstage fluorescents put halos in his eyes. He is all pulse and grin and trembling quiet, the storm finished with him, leaving him threaded with secret lightning. He is twenty-two and the bass is taller than his future but he carries it like a cross and a kite.

The hallway fills with the end-of-night chores: gaffer tape peeling like snake-skin, setlists abandoned like used condoms, the slow diplomacy of “good set, man,” and “nah, you, really,” and the settling of receipts. Someone hands him a slice of pizza that’s mostly cardboard. He eats like a prayer anyway, because hunger after a resurrection is honest. Grease moons his lips and he wipes them with the back of his hand, then licks the grease because he’s a hedonist with a monk’s budget.

I move closer, recorder still off, pencil dull. I want to write down everything, the way his knee bounces, the exact pitch of his post-show hum (between B and Bb), the grammar of his fatigue, the blessed illegibility of his hair. I want to write that when the stage spits him back out he looks like a kid sneaking back into the house at sunrise, his pockets full of the night he’s not supposed to keep. And I do. I write it. That’s the job. I write that [[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff Evans]], twenty-two, bass on his thigh and city on his breath, walks off into the machinery of the band like a saint who let the blood rush to his head and found God there. I write that he is not humble, not really, because humility is for people who think they can fail safely. He fails like a cliff collapses: loudly, gorgeously, on the nights when it happens, and then he rebuilds by the next soundcheck.

He finishes the pizza. He stands. He adjusts the bass like a guilty pleasure. He looks at me as if I’ve been kind or cruel, he can’t decide which.

**JEFF**  
You got it?

**KENDALL**  
I got it.

**JEFF**  
Make it sound better than it was.

**KENDALL**  
It was exactly what it had to be.

He smiles, pleased and skeptical, and wanders toward the door that leads to weather and strangers who will say the same five things that feel brand new every time. He pauses in the threshold, bass silhouetted, boy and instrument cut from the same unruly night. He taps the headstock against the doorframe—another tiny superstition, wood against wood, promise against portal—and steps into the alley’s wet breath. The door swings back on its hinges, a cymbal closing, and the hallway inhales the silence he leaves behind, already starving for the next low note.
