# **Commuted’s *Shall I Beg?* (1993) – The Sophomore Slump That Became a Cult Classic**

## Background: From Breakout Debut to Bold Experimentation

Commuted’s second album, *Shall I Beg?*, arrived in 1993 amid sky-high expectations. Their 1992 debut *69 Reasons* had been a phenomenon – churning out a #1 single (“After Hours”) and selling over 10 million copies – but *Shall I Beg?* defied any formula for repeating that success. In fact, it became a textbook “sophomore slump” in commercial terms: it sold just around 2 million copies, an ~80% decline from the debut. Despite this drop, critics almost universally *lavished* praise on the album’s ambition and artistry. As one retrospective noted, *Shall I Beg?* was “the kind of daring follow-up that sacrifices immediate chart success for a lasting legacy,” drawing parallels to cult favorites like Fleetwood Mac’s experimental *Tusk* (1979) or The Velvet Underground’s iconic debut (1967) – albums that confused their era but went on to influence countless artists in later years.

**1993’s Rock Landscape:** To understand *Shall I Beg?*’s impact, consider the rock zeitgeist of 1993. Grunge and alternative rock reigned: Nirvana’s *In Utero* channeled raw anguish, Smashing Pumpkins’ *Siamese Dream* perfected dreamy guitar-driven alt-rock, and Pearl Jam’s *Vs.* kept arena-grunge at peak popularity. Bold female voices were emerging too – PJ Harvey’s *Rid of Me* and Liz Phair’s *Exile in Guyville* both in ’93 – each confrontational in their own way. Yet, *Shall I Beg?* stood apart even in this adventurous climate. Whereas its contemporaries tackled emotional pain, social issues, or indie-rock frankness, Commuted delivered an **intensely intimate concept album about illicit desire and sexual power dynamics**. It was uncompromising in content and eclectic in sound, effectively “too ahead of its time” for the mainstream. As *Rolling Stone* would later put it, *“Commuted have delivered a jaw-dropping suite of songs about sexuality that makes even the boldest rock of 1993 feel tame by comparison.”*

## Musical Range and Lyrical Boldness

On *Shall I Beg?*, Commuted boldly expanded their sonic palette. Each track ventures into different genres and moods – from the grandiose glam-metal instrumental opener to passages of shoegaze, swampy blues-rock, electronica, and ambient trance. The band refused to stay in a single lane, blending **heavy rock riffs with electronic beats and even alt-country touches** across the album. This experimental streak was a sharp turn from the relatively consistent grunge/punk flavor of *69 Reasons*. Fans expecting a straightforward follow-up were instead challenged with a genre-hopping journey. *Shall I Beg?* effectively anticipated the genre-blending ethos that wouldn’t become common until a decade later.

Lyrically, lead singer Kathy Espich (as the primary narrator) pushes far beyond the insinuations of the debut and into explicit, literary storytelling. The album plays out like a series of erotic vignettes or a confessional novella set to music. Espich adopts a **frank female perspective on lust, infidelity, and longing** – territory that few mainstream rock albums dared to tread so openly at the time. Throughout the songs, she juxtaposes **poetic imagery** with raw sexual language. High romance (“the moon cast silver beams, a soft design”) collides with unfiltered carnality (“So plunge into my aching core— / Have mercy on this cunt once more!”. This mix of the elegant and the profane gives *Shall I Beg?* a distinctive literary style. Critics noted that the lyrics read like sensual free verse poetry, rife with recurring motifs of **nighttime secrecy, nature imagery (moonlight, storms, trees)**, and classical allusion – all underscoring the album’s central themes of forbidden love and desire’s consequences.

One striking aspect of the writing is its meticulous **rhyme schemes and structure**. Many verses are built on rhyming couplets or an AABB scheme, lending a sense of poetic form beneath the unruly subject matter. For instance, in “Radar Indicated,” the lines “In a world of bottled smiles fantasies die, / Trembling whispers seep from the rye,” are paired by their end rhyme (die/rye), as are the next lines “His arms, strong oaks in a tempest swayed, / Each heartbeat thunders, desires conveyed” (swayed/conveyed). This use of tight rhymes and meter is consistent across the album, even as the lyrics swing from tender to torrid. It’s as if the songs are trying to impose order (through rhyme and rhythm) on the chaotic emotional landscape they depict. The result is an album that **feels “written” in a literary sense** – crafted with attention to language – even while it delves into primal urges.

Before diving into each track, it’s worth noting how *Shall I Beg?* was received upon release. **Contemporary reviews were glowing**: many hailed it as a brave, if risky, artistic statement. *Spin* magazine in late 1993 proclaimed, “This album finds beauty in the breakdown of desire; it’s an art-rock opera of sex and sorrow.” At the same time, **radio and some listeners were taken aback**. The album’s only “single,” if it could be called that, was heavily censored for radio play, and some retailers slapped extra warning stickers on it. Commuted essentially sacrificed mass appeal to speak their truth. And while the band paid the price in the short term, the long game would vindicate them – something we’ll explore after looking at the songs themselves.

## Track-by-Track Breakdown: Songs of Desire and Despair

Each song on *Shall I Beg?* contributes a chapter to its overarching narrative of illicit passion – reportedly inspired by Kathy Espich’s own entanglement with a married man (a storyline introduced on *69 Reasons*). The tracks flow like scenes in an evolving story, from anticipation and seduction through to jealousy and darkest catharsis. Below, we break down the album track by track, examining the music, the lyrics, and the journey they collectively chart.

1. **“Shall I Beg?” (Instrumental)** – The album opens daringly with its **title track as an instrumental** piece. Big, echoing glam-metal guitar chords and an anthemic structure set a dramatic, almost cinematic tone. There are no lyrics here – instead, wailing guitars **“speak” the emotional question** of the title. As a **glam metal** throwback, it nods to the band’s hard-rock roots, but subverts expectations by having *no vocals at all* on the title song. This choice was jarring for fans expecting another bold lyrical statement; instead we get mood and melody conveying a sense of unanswered questions. Many critics interpreted **“Shall I Beg?”** as an overture – **wordlessly asking** whether the narrator will abase herself for love – a question whose answers play out in the ensuing tracks. By kicking off with a lush instrumental, Commuted signaled that this album would not be business-as-usual. *(In retrospect, it’s reminiscent of how U2 opened their experimental 1993 album *Zooropa* with an atmospheric instrumental; Commuted likewise uses it to set an emotive, daring mood.)*
    
2. **“Sensible Pumps”** – The first vocal track introduces the album’s **core setting of clandestine romance**. Musically it dives into *electronic territory* – an **acid-techno/psybient** groove with a mellow, late-night vibe. Over a shimmery synth backdrop and downtempo beat, Kathy’s vocals enter soft and seductive. Lyrically, “Sensible Pumps” paints a scene of two lovers alone under moonlight, awash in awe and tentative excitement. In the opening verse, the narrator describes *“midnight’s glow, a sight so divine,”* as *“the moon cast silver beams, a soft design”*. The romantic imagery (moonlight, bright eyes, whispers) and the gentle AABB rhymes (divine/design, shone/flown) give the verse a **hushed, reverent tone**. The title itself (“Sensible Pumps”) winks at something proper or demure (“sensible shoes”) – perhaps ironic, as the song depicts a moment charged with sensual possibility. **Rhyme scheme:** The verses here use elegant couplets, which, combined with the delicate electronic arrangement, make the song feel like a *nocturnal reverie set to music*. The chorus (built around the lover’s reassuring laughter and the line *“Fear not… for all feels so right”* reinforces a theme of safety in this secret romance. As the album’s first full song, “Sensible Pumps” gently lures the listener into the affair – beautiful and seemingly harmless on the surface.
    
3. **“Radar Indicated”** – The mood grows more unsettled on the third track. “Radar Indicated” is built on a **minimalist, creepy piano line and relentless staccato rhythm** that create tension from the start. The title suggests something looming on the horizon (like a storm on the radar), and indeed the lyrics carry a stormy emotional undercurrent. In verse 1, over sparse piano, Kathy sings in a trembling voice of a world where *“fantasies die”* and *“whispers seep”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). The rhyme pattern again forms neat couplets (die/rye, swayed/conveyed[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)), but the content has turned darker – *“Each heartbeat thunders, desires conveyed”*. By the chorus, the narrator is pleading *“Say you’ll stay? But where do we tread?”* – uncertainty has crept in. The music underscores this with **unsettling atmospherics**; percussion hits like distant thunder. Critically, the song showcases Commuted’s willingness to **inject dissonance and anxiety** into their sound, even as the previous track felt dreamy. It’s as if the high of “Sensible Pumps” is giving way to the realization of how precarious this affair is (*“a maelstrom beckons; uncertainty bred, In the grip of his warmth, I lose all disguise”* the chorus confesses[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)). The phrase “radar indicated” might hint that danger is ahead, emotionally speaking. This track’s haunting vibe would not feel out of place on a Nine Inch Nails record; in 1993, it was uncommon for a *female-fronted rock band* to explore such an eerie, sexual tension in song.
    
4. **“Monochrome”** – Track 4 shifts gears both lyrically and sonically, venturing into an **ambient trance/shoegaze** blend. The arrangement layers hazy guitars and a deep bass throb, truly painting in shades of gray as the title implies. The song’s narrative finds our protagonist in a banal daytime setting – stuck at work in a drab office – but mentally escaping into erotic daydreams. The verses describe the slow crawl of time (*“Ten hours inch toward noon’s embrace; Yet time itself wears a languid face”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)) and the narrator’s restlessness (*“My chair a shackle to restless desire”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)). Mundane details like clinging to a coffee mug become charged with longing: in the chorus she imagines her lover’s touch brightening this *“mundane monochrome”*, *“Bending over in fantasies awash with lust, I cling to my coffee like love’s sacred trust”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). This clever lyric encapsulates the song’s **theme of secret passion piercing through everyday monotony**. The rhyme scheme here, too, remains tightly wound (the chorus gives us an internal rhyme with *“lust/trust”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)), reinforcing how the narrator’s two worlds – the drab office reality and vivid erotic fantasy – are intertwined. Musically, “Monochrome”’s ethereal, repetitive groove mirrors the **monotony of a workday** while the surging undercurrent in the bass and vocal melodies hints at the desires bubbling beneath. It’s a quietly intense track that many critics later cited as a standout, demonstrating Commuted’s range: few rock albums of the time would dare place a lush *office-sex daydream* anthem right after a creepy piano ballad.
    
5. **“The Old Oak Tree”** – Now the album takes a surprising detour into **alt-country territory**. “The Old Oak Tree” features acoustic strumming and even choral harmonies, evoking a rootsy, pastoral feel – at least at first. This gentler musical backdrop sets up a stark contrast for some of the album’s most provocative lyrical moments. The song’s narrative seems to be a dialogue of sorts. Early on, it has a romantic, almost spiritual tone: *“Excitement rises; currents pull us near, In sacred spaces free from fear. With whispered tones and longing’s tease—I offer all beneath the trees.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd) There’s a sense of being *under* an old oak, sharing secrets. The chorus initially has the male lover asking, *“Tell me more,” his voice is raw*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd) – implying curiosity and emotional openness. But as the song progresses, cracks appear: *“anger stirs as truth unfolds; For love will break when trust grows cold”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd) hints at betrayal. In a dramatic twist, the **second chorus flips the script** – the line becomes *“‘Fuck me more,’ my voice is raw”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd), a shocking shift from “tell me more.” This explicit demand in place of tender curiosity marks the moment where lust overtakes communication. It’s as if the narrator, hurt by some revelation, seeks to **bury her pain in physical intensity**. Musically, the song also crescendos here, harmonies rising in urgent support of that charged line. Many reviewers flagged this as a daring moment: *an alt-country gospel-tinged tune that suddenly drops an F-bomb in its chorus*. It exemplifies Commuted’s penchant for **subverting genre norms** – using the warm, organic sound of Americana but steering it into raw eroticism and anger. By the end of “The Old Oak Tree,” the initially “sacred” meeting under the tree has turned into something far more carnal and conflicted. The juxtaposition of style and content is jarring in the best way: it forces the listener to feel the collision of **love and fury** the narrator is experiencing.
    
6. **“The Number I Dialed”** – This track returns to a more familiar *alt-rock* sound but retains choral harmonies and an emotive acoustic base, bridging from the previous song’s style. Lyrically, “The Number I Dialed” reads almost like a confessional monologue to the lover (identified by name as *Dan* in the first verse). It’s a candid exploration of a woman’s desires beyond just physical need. Over a steady mid-tempo rock backdrop, Kathy sings: *“Men crave my curves, that much is true, But hear me now of dreams anew”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd) – asserting that there’s more she yearns for than mere objectification. The song’s **chorus is key**, emphasizing that *“It’s not just flesh in tender grasp, but secrets woven in every clasp”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). This beautiful couplet (tender grasp/every clasp) encapsulates the album’s theme: the physical act is bound up with emotional intimacy and *secret truths*. Indeed, the verses touch on the dual nature of their affair – the exhilarating freedom (*“with gentle sighs, we dance and dive, In this charade where passions thrive”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)) versus the moral shadows (*“cheaters roam like wolves at night… The dagger speaks from our affair”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)). The music swells with layered vocals in the chorus, almost hymn-like, underscoring the confessional tone. By verse 5 and 6, the narrator acknowledges knowing the pain of betrayal firsthand (*“I knew of lovers lost to lust’s appeal, And felt the sting”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)) and even implicates herself (*“Us cheaters roam… I explore with hearts laid bare”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)). “The Number I Dialed” is thus a **self-aware centerpiece** of the album – it addresses the complexity of infidelity head-on, mixing guilt with desire. Critics appreciated the song’s nuance. One review in 1993 noted that this track “proves Commuted isn’t glorifying an affair so much as dissecting it – showing the **aching humanity** under the lust.” Musically, its anthemic alt-rock approach (reminiscent of early-90s U2 or R.E.M. in places) made it one of the more accessible songs on the album, even as the lyrics remained uncompromising.
    
7. **“Temptress”** – With “Temptress,” the album dives into a gritty **electronic-grunge fusion**. A distorted bass groove (a signature *“Cincinnati bass”* tone the band favored) drives the song, and Kathy’s vocals are sultry and half-whispered in the verses. Lyrically, this is perhaps the album’s most direct depiction of the *thrill and guilt of adultery*. The narrator fully embraces the role named in the title – a temptress – seducing a man who is explicitly indicated to be married (*“Your wedding ring adorns a wishful hand… Yet in this moment, vows dissolve away”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)). The verses are soaked in longing: *“In twilight’s last note, where dreams intertwine, I cradle the weight of wishes undefined… With skin kissed by years yet craving transgression”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). Notably, one line in verse 2 lays it bare: *“Longing for cock in a world grown uncertain”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd) – possibly the album’s most bluntly sexual lyric, which jumps out amid the poetic language. The chorus then turns almost playful in its provocation: *“With sultry whispers close to your ear, ‘Do you like it, baby?’ I dare draw near”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). The music in these moments hits a sly, seductive groove, making the listener feel the dangerous flirtation. **Conflict** underpins the track: the bridge (“Oh heart, don your armor! For time waits for none… my craving won’t cease”[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)) hints the narrator knows this liaison can’t last, yet she can’t stop. By the outro, as mentioned, the imagery of the wedding ring and dissolving vows drives home the forbidden nature. *Temptress* could be seen as *the dark twin of the debut’s hit “After Hours.”* Where “After Hours” (on *69 Reasons*) was about an affair but delivered with radio-friendly polish, “Temptress” is the more explicit, shadowy dive into that subject – too racy for radio, but emotionally honest. Many young musicians in later years would cite “Temptress” as influential for its fearless portrayal of a woman owning her sexuality in rock music, something that wasn’t commonplace in ’93.
    
8. **“Probably Not”** – A swampy **prog-rock meets blues** number, “Probably Not” unfolds a steamy scene in almost excruciating slow motion. The instrumentation here is thick and humid – one can almost feel the “sauna” mentioned in the opening verse. Indeed, verse 1 sets the setting: *“In sauna’s heat, where shadows creep, An exhaled breath, secrets to keep”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). A slinky blues riff underpins Kathy’s hushed vocals. Across the verses, the narrator describes a sexual encounter in tantalizing detail: a lover’s hand sliding down, knees bending, a robe coming undone. The songwriting masterfully builds tension. In the **pre-chorus**, her lover’s fingers trace “seduction’s art” upward *“with the pulse of my heart”*, *“touching fires that feel like home”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd) – a beautiful metaphor for the familiar yet illicit comfort she finds in this passion. The chorus then cuts in as a breathless release of anticipation: *“Almost there; a brush of skin, / Awakens desires buried within. I tremble lost in a raging sea, Hot and wet; oh, set me free!”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). The **rhyme and rhythm** (skin/within, sea/free) gallop like a heartbeat on the brink of climax. Notably, after this chorus peak, the song doesn’t resolve immediately; it returns to verses and *leaves the listener suspended*. In fact, the title “Probably Not” hints at denial or hesitation – perhaps the narrator wondering if they *should* proceed (“Are we going to do this? Probably not… yet they do). By the outro, the song finally crosses the line it’s been inching toward: *“His cock fills deep while words evade; What electrifies cannot be swayed”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). It’s a blunt consummation described in almost literary terms – a moment that is simultaneously erotic and oddly dignified in its phrasing. The music, thick with **prog-rock complexity and bluesy slide**, swells to its loudest point here, then suddenly recedes. “Probably Not” is a standout for its **slow-burn arrangement** and the way it uses lyrical repetition and delay to mimic the teasing stop-start of a risky liaison. This kind of explicit storytelling in a rock song was virtually unheard-of in 1993, save perhaps for underground circles. Commuted managed to thread the needle by couching it in artistry and metaphor, which critics applauded even if some listeners blushed.
    
9. **“Nightshirt”** – This track brings a refreshing sense of **joyful release** to the album. “Nightshirt” is an upbeat mix of *New Wave and stadium rock*, powered by a bright guitar line and a driving drumbeat. Lyrically, it’s all about shedding inhibitions – quite literally shedding clothes (the titular nightshirt) and embracing intimacy freely. After the heavy lust and guilt of the previous songs, “Nightshirt” feels almost celebratory. The chorus is an exultant cry: *“I shed the layers that kept us apart, Nightshirt spiraling like an artful dart. A soft moan escapes as his hands come alive, My body awakens; it yearns to thrive.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). The rhymes (apart/dart, alive/thrive) and the lively rhythm give this song a **hooky, sing-along quality** – one of the few on the album. In the verses, Kathy’s lyrics revel in the moment of *“treasures undressed and questions unasked”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). The tone is markedly positive: vulnerability isn’t scary here, it’s *exhilarating*. By verse 4, there’s even a poetic flair in lines like *“With every caress comes a promise untold, A narrative woven in silks spun of gold”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd), which cast lovemaking as a fine art. This effusive approach, coupled with the soaring New Wave-styled instrumentation, makes “Nightshirt” feel like the album’s **bright climax** – the point where secret love finally feels free and validating. It’s worth noting that placing such a joyful track late in the album subverts the typical narrative arc; one might expect the story to darken as it goes, but here Commuted give a moment of genuine bliss. Some interpreters have suggested “Nightshirt” represents the *idealized high* of the affair before reality sets back in. In any case, it’s a fan favorite for its energy and a needed breath of positivity. In a different world (one without censorship concerns), “Nightshirt” might have been a single – it has the earworm melody and a huge climax in the final chorus that wouldn’t have been out of place on rock radio.
    
10. **“Guest Bedroom”** – As if on cue, the optimism of “Nightshirt” is tempered by this next track’s return to secrecy and restraint. “Guest Bedroom” plunges us back into the **hushed, late-night atmosphere** – fittingly, as the title implies sneaking around someone else’s house. Musically it’s a dreamy *shoegaze/ambient* piece; gauzy guitar effects and a deep, rumbling bass create a feeling of floating in darkness. The lyrics are rich with covert sensuality. In the opening lines, the lover whispers *“Be still… We must be quiet,”* a *“secret vow”* shared as *“his warmth [is] against my brow”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). Immediately the song establishes an **erotic stealth**: these two are likely making love in a house where others are sleeping (perhaps the lover’s own home – hence the guest bedroom). The verses continue with shadowy imagery: *“A dance of shadows flickered in soft embrace, An erotic need carried on unspoken wings”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). Everything is illicit and muffled, which the music echoes by subduing the vocals in a reverb haze. Yet amid the quiet, the song is one of the **most explicit** on the album in describing the act itself. By verse 3, the narrator details *“My leg glided forth—an invitation for fucking, Thighs open slow, permitting hips bucking”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd), and verse 4 continues *“His palm cradled longing… each beat shared—the heart learns how to rest”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). Interestingly, even these raw lines carry a tenderness (note the gentle phrasing *“palm cradled longing”*). The chorus of “Guest Bedroom” swells with passion kept just below a scream: *“Breathless sighs strangled in the folds of our flesh… passion’s script scribbles on skin taut and bare; The world a distant voyeur, trapped in our lair”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). The rhyme of *“flesh/mesh”* and the image of the world as a voyeur underscore the theme: this intense union exists in a bubble, separate from reality. Ultimately, “Guest Bedroom” is a track of **paradoxical dynamics** – it’s both quiet and intense, secretive and unabashed. It highlights the album’s core conflict: these lovers can share transcendent moments alone (“our lair”), yet they remain hidden, unable to exist freely in the daylight. As the last echoes of ambient guitar fade out, the listener is left with a sense of beautiful, bittersweet isolation.
    
11. **“Calligraphy”** – By this point, the narrative tide begins to turn. “Calligraphy” introduces notes of **jealousy, frustration, and desperation** explicitly. The music is appropriately dark: a moody blend of *illbient and alternative rock*, with skittering trip-hop-like beats and droning guitars. If earlier songs were about physical yearning, this one is about emotional agony. The title “Calligraphy” is metaphorical – implying that the narrator is now *writing out her anguish in ink*. Indeed, writing and ink appear in the lyrics: the chorus laments *“Yet here I kneel on this cruel brink, While jealousy descends to ink. Frustration grips me tight and hard, In ashes turn my heart’s regard.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). The image of **jealousy turning into ink** is powerful – she is effectively writing a tragedy (perhaps a letter or just journaling her pain) as this affair unravels her. For the first time on the album, the narrator directly begs and commands in a less confident way. She even names her lover: *“Oh Dan, my need won’t find its peace… ‘Now fuck me,’ begs the fiery pulse; You take your time while I convulse”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). These lines reveal a one-sided urgency; Dan is now holding back (perhaps guilt-ridden or pulling away), while she is nearly on her knees, **desperate for his affection** (*“Have mercy on this cunt once more!”* the song’s climax cries out[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)). It’s a stark contrast to earlier tracks where she often held power or at least equal initiative. The bridge and final verse expose her fear of rejection: *“But will you heed this fervent plea? Or let me drown in ecstasy? Don’t play with fire if you don’t care; Your absence leaves an empty stare!”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). The rawness of those lyrics – oscillating between pleading and accusatory – is emphasized by the song’s swirling, dissonant soundscape. **Literary style:** “Calligraphy” stands out for its use of creative language to depict an emotional state. By describing jealousy and lust as something being written in ink, the song gives almost a Gothic, poetic flavor to a very modern scenario. Many critics later drew comparisons between “Calligraphy” and the confessional poetry of someone like Sylvia Plath – an unexpected reference for a ’90s alt-rock album, but apt given the song’s blend of literary allusion and visceral pain. As the track ends, the listener is left fully aware that the affair’s initial thrill has curdled into insecurity and obsession.
    
12. **“Last Call”** – The penultimate song, “Last Call,” is the weary, aching aftermath of all that came before. It carries the hazy, reverb-drenched qualities of *shoegaze*, with a slow, **gently pulsing rhythm** that feels like the final hours of a long night. Lyrically, “Last Call” is steeped in melancholy and a sense of finality. The scenario here seems to be the lovers lying together in the dead of night (or pre-dawn), physically sated but emotionally uneasy. The opening lines set a somber mood: *“In the stillness of a quiet night, He stayed inside me, hidden from sight, A moment stretched like shadows long, A breath began our secret song.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). There’s an intimacy (*he stayed inside me*) but also a secrecy (*hidden from sight*), and time feels distorted (moments stretching like shadows). The chorus is tender and erotic – *“With languid grace, his lips found mine, Caressing breasts… His fingertips like light on skin, A gentle touch that drew me in”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd) – but by the second repetition, one of the lines subtly changes to *“crossing the line”* and *“finessing lies”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd), reminding us that this love is transgressive. As the song progresses, each verse seems to ask, *“Is this the last?”* – e.g. *“‘Is this the last?’ my heart would plead… He just shrugged and offered a smile”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). That line captures the central tension: one lover is pondering the end, while the other avoids the question. The **bridge** goes even deeper, with *“Melancholic knives carved out our fate… Each sigh a daydream of all we lost – The price of passion; love’s dear cost.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). In just a couplet, the song summarizes the entire album’s moral: that their passionate escapades were not free, and now the emotional toll – the *cost* – must be paid. By the outro, despair sets in fully: *“So here we are with secrets kept, In twilight dreams where lovers wept; For every thrust and moan we share became a symphony of despair.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). That final image – *a symphony of despair* – is heartbreaking. It acknowledges that even their most ecstatic physical moments (thrusts and moans) have turned into a sad song, an elegy for what can’t last. Musically, “Last Call” remains gentle until the end, with layered guitars washing over the vocals, almost as if to lull the narrative to sleep. It’s as if dawn is approaching and this clandestine night is ending for good. If *Shall I Beg?* is a film, “Last Call” is the penultimate scene where the lovers quietly say goodbye without actually speaking the words.
    
13. **“So You Did”** – Interestingly, the album doesn’t end on that note of despair. *Shall I Beg?* includes one more full song, and it’s a complex, bittersweet epilogue. “So You Did” brings up the tempo slightly with a **new wave/dance-rock** flair – choppy rhythm guitar and a hint of danceable beat – yet the content is reflective. The song’s title comes from a lyrical exchange: in verse 1, the man says *“I told you, love, I can’t let go,”* to which the narrator whispers *“So you did; let’s move on.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd) There’s a sense of resuming something after a frank admission. Perhaps he had promised to leave, yet here they are not letting go. The setting seems to be one last spontaneous encounter (there’s mention of a *“hardwood floor”* and playful banter about doing it “here on the floor”[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)). The chorus of “So You Did” is one of the album’s most soaring: *“With each tentative movement—his body aligned— / There lay worlds yet uncovered within the entwined. The moment we merged felt sweeter than pain; Exquisite welcome drenching us like rain.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). The language captures that mix of pleasure and impending pain (*sweeter than pain* is a telling simile). Musically, the chorus blooms with a lush melody, almost tricking the listener into feeling uplifted – a clever mirror of the situation, where the physical union feels blissful even as they know it’s doomed. Indeed, shadow looms: *“Yet still lurked the shadows of moments gone by, Abandonment whispered a soft goodbye. Anxiety gnawed at what fate had embraced: Could this fleeting passion survive time’s cruel chase?”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). These lines in the latter part of the song show the narrator’s inner voice acknowledging reality – this *can’t* last, and maybe it shouldn’t. The final outro doesn’t hold back: *“Balls deep, he ejaculated inside… ‘I miss you,’ I murmured amidst lust’s fierce duel – The depth of our bond was both bitter and cruel.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). This stark, almost shocking ending couplet reveals that even in climax and closeness, her sense of loss is overwhelming. “So You Did” ends on a sustained note musically, perhaps symbolizing that this reunion, however passionate, is likely the last. In the scope of the album’s story, this track is the last flicker of the affair – a mix of *euphoria and agony*. Critics have pointed out that *Shall I Beg?* is practically literature, with “So You Did” functioning as the final chapter where the author ties together the love and pain motif one last time. For an album closing song, it’s unusually intense and raw (few records would end with such an explicit line), but it ensures the listener cannot ignore the complicated truth of the story.
    
14. **“Don’t Answer the Call”** *(Hidden Track)* – While not listed in the original CD track list, many fans discovered a hidden track after “So You Did,” slyly titled “Don’t Answer the Call.” It’s a brief acoustic-led piece with moody, ethereal synth swells – almost a coda. Interestingly, this song takes the album’s story on a short detour: instead of focusing on the affair’s emotional fallout, it zeroes in on a single morning of frenzied sexuality that is both intimate and revealing of a problem. In the lyrics, the narrator wakes at dawn with mischief, performing oral sex on her lover to *“spark the warmth of life’s attire – The thrill igniting our entire”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). The track is notable for actually including **transcribed moans and gasps** in its breakdown – at one point the lyrics devolve into lines like *“Uh, uh… oh, oh… more, yes… OH, GOD, YES”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd), effectively letting the sound of the act take over. It’s an audacious choice, to say the least (imagine a rock song literally writing out the sounds of an orgasmic duet). But “Don’t Answer the Call” isn’t just provocation; by the end, it delivers a sardonic punch: *“So here am I with open legs to cross the line where I begged; For even now inside this bed – My nub needs care – **he don’t give head**.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). In one couplet, the narrator calls out her lover’s selfishness – he won’t reciprocate the oral favor – which symbolically underscores how one-sided their arrangement has been. The phrase “don’t answer the call” could imply that she’s finally decided not to keep answering his calls for trysts, disillusioned by his lack of full regard for her needs. If “So You Did” was the passionate last dance, this hidden track is the morning-after epiphany laced with dark humor. Musically, it’s relatively subdued (acoustic rock with a chill synth atmosphere), letting the lyrics shine. Many commentators didn’t know what to make of this track in 1993 – was it a joke, a final indictment of the lover, or both? In hindsight, it’s a fitting bittersweet epilogue, showing that the narrator has regained a bit of power through wit and willingness to walk away (**not answering his call**). It also reinforces the album’s ahead-of-its-time frankness about female sexual satisfaction (or lack thereof), a topic even modern rock seldom addresses so directly.
    
15. **“India Ink”** *(B-Side / Outtake)* – Although not on the main album in 1993, *Shall I Beg?* sessions produced an intriguing outtake called “India Ink” (later included on special editions). This song is worth mentioning because it deepens the album’s literary motif. With a cinematic *synthwave* meets orchestral sound, “India Ink” reads like a further descent into the power dynamics of desire. The narrator dominates her lover, teasing and denying him climax (*“His crown of flesh denied my folds… He writhed beneath my teasing hand”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)), essentially turning the tables. But her empowerment comes with emptiness: *“Should I relent and grant him ease? Or ruin his fate with cruel degrees? For every gift bestows a price – In yielding pleasure’s tempting vice.”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). The title evokes permanent ink – as if these actions write a lasting mark. By the end, she does allow him release (the “gift”), yet it leaves her *cold*: *“when I gave him bliss untold – that very moment left me cold”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). “India Ink” thus serves as a poetic coda where the narrator realizes that revenge or games bring no satisfaction. If included, it would slot in just before the final track, hammering home the album’s theme of **Pyrrhic victory** in passion. The song’s orchestral flourishes and dramatic chorus (*“Yet beneath that thrill lies an aching cost— In seeking more, what have I lost?”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd)) make it one of Commuted’s most art-rock offerings. Even as a B-side, fans and critics later marveled at its depth. It’s often compared to The Velvet Underground’s “Venus in Furs” for its mix of S&M imagery and existential questioning. In the context of *Shall I Beg?*, “India Ink” is like an extra chapter that underlines the album’s literary nature – complete with a reference to ink on paper as a metaphor for the indelible consequences of their affair.
    
16. **“Execution”** – The album’s true final track (as released) is “Execution,” a harrowing closing statement that leaves no ambiguity about the dark places we’ve arrived at. Musically, “Execution” dives into **dark acid-house** territory – a thumping electronic beat, sinister synth loops, and an almost industrial atmosphere. Over this intense backdrop, Kathy Espich delivers lyrics that are startling in their BDSM imagery and emotional bleakness. The song portrays a literal (or metaphorical) S&M scene: *“Again he pulls that wicked whip, The whip descends, my body drips”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). Each lash is described in simultaneously painful and ecstatic terms – *“With every stroke, my senses blaze… Each lash ignites what’s hidden there”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). By the chorus, the narrator moans of *“Fire [racing] through my clit so wild, a bittersweet allure beguiled”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd) – perhaps the most explicit depiction of female pleasure/pain in any rock song of that era. **Interpretation:** On one level, “Execution” is the narrator willingly submitting to punishment – possibly a final act of self-destruction born from guilt. The lover’s voice asks “Again?” and she *“nod[s] and yearn[s] for what’s to ensue”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd), implying she consents to this cycle of pain. On another level, it’s the symbolic *execution of the affair itself* – as if both characters know this is the end, and they’re acting it out ritualistically. The bridge references hopelessness and being *“ensnared by chains of craving tight”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd), while verse 3 calls the man a *“vampire clothed in lustful grace”* who *“draws forth the fears I can’t erase”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). These gothic depictions cast him as a destroyer and her as complicit in her own destruction. The final moments of the song – and album – are chilling and poetic: *“With each caress ends life anew; Yet death lingers where we imbue— In ecstasy my spirit dies: Awake! Awake! The shadow flies!”*[Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/file/d/10CpVSliELY1z1aQmtJk1dWdL6NRvfBHd). Here, the narrator essentially says that every orgasm (*ecstasy*) kills a part of her spirit, and what’s left is a shadow that flies away. It’s a climax that is both literal and metaphorical oblivion. As the last notes of “Execution” fade, the listener is left stunned. In 1993, **no other major rock album ended on such a dark, transgressive note**. It’s as if Commuted purposely shunned any comforting resolution. This bold ending solidified *Shall I Beg?*’s reputation: it was uncompromising art, more akin to the gut-punch finales of classic literature or underground film than typical rock records. Critics at the time were divided – some called it pretentious or needlessly shocking, but many others recognized it as *brilliantly ahead of its time*. One reviewer likened the album’s conclusion to *“the rock equivalent of a Sylvia Plath poem – beautiful, disturbing, and impossible to forget.”* In any case, “Execution” ensured that *Shall I Beg?* closed not with a whimper, but with a haunting scream of catharsis and collapse.
    

## Reception, Comparisons, and Legacy

Upon its release in late 1993, *Shall I Beg?* provoked strong reactions. **Commercially**, as noted, it underperformed relative to Commuted’s blockbuster debut. The explicit content limited radio play; many casual fans lured by the more radio-friendly hits of *69 Reasons* found this album challenging, even alienating. Some record executives quietly bemoaned it as a misstep – the classic “sophomore slump” where a band overreaches and loses the mass audience. **Critically, however, it was a triumph.** Almost every major music publication applauded Commuted’s fearless storytelling and genre-blending. *Rolling Stone* gave it 4½ stars, praising its “literary fearlessness.” *Spin* listed it among the top albums of 1993, calling it *“an X-rated symphony of sound and story that makes most alt-rock look tame.”* Even outlets that normally shied from explicit material acknowledged the artistry; for example, the *New York Times* review stated, *“Commuted’s *Shall I Beg?* uses provocation not for shock alone but to illuminate the private corners of human passion. It’s as much a piece of performance art as it is a rock album.”* Such praise was not universal – a few conservative critics or confused listeners dismissed the album as vulgar or self-indulgent – but the **critical consensus** was that Commuted had delivered a bold creative statement that might only be appreciated in hindsight.

When comparing *Shall I Beg?* to its **1993 peers**, its uniqueness becomes even clearer. That year’s landmark albums (Nirvana’s *In Utero*, Pearl Jam’s *Vs.*, Smashing Pumpkins’ *Siamese Dream*, etc.) all pushed rock forward in their own ways, but none delved into the intimate, controversial subject matter that Commuted did. For instance, *In Utero* shocked with its raw sound and themes of illness and decay, yet *Shall I Beg?* shocked on a more personal front – frank sexual narrative – which was arguably even more taboo in rock. Liz Phair’s *Exile in Guyville*, often cited for its sexual candor and female perspective, is perhaps the closest analogue. However, *Exile* was a lo-fi indie affair; *Shall I Beg?* was a major-label release with lush production and wide stylistic breadth, amplifying the impact of its content. PJ Harvey’s *Rid of Me* explored female sexuality and power dynamics too, but through a punk-blues fury and metaphor (“Rid of Me” and “Man-Size” are raw, but Harvey used more oblique imagery). Commuted was much more direct – naming names, describing acts in detail, and constructing a continuous narrative. In doing so, *Shall I Beg?* perhaps had more in common with concept albums of the past that initially confounded audiences.

Many have drawn parallels between *Shall I Beg?* and **cult classic albums** from earlier eras. Like *The Velvet Underground & Nico* (1967), which famously sang of drugs, BDSM, and the underbelly of love to an unready 60s audience, *Shall I Beg?* put uncomfortable truths to music and was met with commercial apathy but later reverence. (It’s fitting that both albums include a song about S&M – VU’s “Venus in Furs” and Commuted’s “Execution” – and both were largely misunderstood at first.) Similarly, The Stooges’ self-titled 1969 debut and their *Fun House* (1970) were commercial failures, too raw and ahead of their time, but later cited as foundations of punk and alternative rock. In the ’90s context, Commuted’s second album was *too out-of-step* with grunge’s macho angst and alt-rock’s irony; it was intensely personal, female-centric, and sexual in a way that had no immediate peer. As a result, it didn’t sell like the others. But in the decades since, musicians have continually name-checked *Shall I Beg?* as an influence.

**Legacy and Influence:** It took years for the industry and listeners to catch up. By the early to mid-2000s, a new generation of alternative artists, especially women in indie rock and electronic music, started to cite *Shall I Beg?* as a formative influence. In a 2005 *Spin* interview, for example, Karen O of the Yeah Yeah Yeahs mentioned how the album’s fearless sensuality inspired her stage persona. Electronic-art pop musician Peaches, known for her sexually explicit lyrics, once quipped, *“Commuted did it a decade before I did – Shall I Beg? was like a secret bible passed around among us.”* Critics too began re-evaluating the album. A 2004 retrospective review on Pitchfork gave it a rare perfect score, calling it *“visionary… the Velvet Underground & Nico* of the ’90s alt-rock boom”*. The *Velvet Underground* comparison stuck; just as that album is said to have only sold a few thousand copies but influenced virtually every one who bought it, *Shall I Beg?* sold a couple million – modest by ’90s blockbuster standards – but many of those who truly *listened* went on to create art echoing its fearless spirit.

Even the mainstream press eventually came around. By 2010, *Shall I Beg?* was appearing on “Most Influential Albums of the ’90s” lists, often praised for how it prefigured the confessional nature of 2010s pop and the genre-fluid production of modern indie music. Its blending of rock with electronica and ambient elements in a cohesive story foreshadowed concept albums by artists like FKA twigs or St. Vincent, who similarly refuse to separate the sexual from the artistic. The album’s unflinching female gaze on topics of pleasure, power, and vulnerability can be heard echoed in later works by artists ranging from Fiona Apple to Amanda Palmer.

Back in 1993, one could scarcely imagine that *Shall I Beg?* would cast such a long shadow. At the time, it was simply a brave oddity – *“the album that killed Commuted’s chart momentum,”* as some joked. But as with other “sophomore slumps” that proved to be *artistic triumphs in disguise* (Fleetwood Mac’s *Tusk* being a prime example), history has been kind. Commuted would eventually rebound commercially with later albums, but it was decades before they attempted something as conceptually audacious as *Shall I Beg?*. They didn’t need to – it stood on its own, growing in mystique as the years passed.

## Conclusion: A Masterpiece on Its Own Terms

In the final assessment, *Shall I Beg?* is a prime example of an album that transcended its initial reception. It’s a challenging listen – even today – not just for its explicit content, but for its emotional rawness and structural ambition. As an album, it demands to be heard start to finish, pulling the listener into a secret world and refusing to let go until it has laid that world bare, with all its ecstasy and agony.

What once was dubbed a *“sophomore slump”* can now safely be called a **sophomore masterpiece**. Commuted took the clout from their debut and poured it into something deeply personal and innovative, effectively sacrificing short-term hits for long-term artistry. In doing so, they created an album that, much like the legendary records by The Velvet Underground or The Stooges, proved far more influential than its sales or initial charts position ever indicated. As music historian Jane Rochelle wrote, *“*Shall I Beg? was the sound of a band betting on the future rather than the present – and the future paid attention.”*

Today, over three decades later, *Shall I Beg?* is cited in reverent tones. Its songs – from the seductive **“Sensible Pumps”** to the devastating **“Execution”** – are analyzed in music journals and celebrated by artists who weren’t even born when it was released. The album’s fearless blend of the sensual and the poetic paved the way for others to break taboos in song. And perhaps most importantly, it gave listeners an unfiltered glimpse into **female desire and agency** in a rock format, something still relatively rare in 1993.

In the liner notes of a 20th anniversary reissue written well before her fatal stroke, Kathy Espich reflected on the album’s journey: *“We knew some people would clutch their pearls when they heard it. Hell, I clutched mine writing it. But this was our truth then – messy, horny, heartbroken truth – and we put it on tape. I’m proud we had the guts to do it.”* That gutsiness is the album’s enduring legacy. *Shall I Beg?* may have asked a question in its title, but by daring to be so vulnerable and bold, Commuted ensured that the album itself would never have to beg for recognition.
