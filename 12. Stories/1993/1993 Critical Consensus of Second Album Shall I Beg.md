***Shall I Beg?* (1993) – Commuted’s Fork in the Road**

**Commuted’s sophomore album, *Shall I Beg?*, is the sound of a band at war with itself.** Released in 1993, the album pushed the band’s creative ambitions forward, yet failed to capture the lightning-in-a-bottle success of *69 Reasons*. Critics lauded its depth, but audiences—perhaps expecting something more radio-friendly—did not embrace it in the same numbers. Over time, however, it has become a cult favorite, appreciated by those who favor Commuted’s more intricate and emotionally raw work.

**Sound & Style: A Clash of Vision**

From the opening notes, *Shall I Beg?* presents itself as a more layered and texturally rich album than its predecessor. While *69 Reasons* had a raw, infectious energy, *Shall I Beg?* finds the band pulling in two distinct directions. **Bill Davenport and Jeremy Evans** lead the charge into experimental territory, incorporating unconventional time signatures, dissonant harmonies, and complex song structures. Meanwhile, **Gilley and Jim Brown** push for accessibility, anchoring the album with straightforward rock hooks and anthem-like choruses. The resulting tension creates an album that is both compelling and uneven—a record of a band simultaneously pushing boundaries and pulling against itself.

**Track Review: "*Sensible Pumps*" – Commuted’s Unexpected Psybient Odyssey**

Opening *Shall I Beg?* with a sonic detour into acid techno and psybient soundscapes, ***"Sensible Pumps"*** immediately sets the album apart from *69 Reasons*. A hypnotic and texturally rich track, it is both daring and restrained—a contradiction that seems intentional.

**The Sound: Experimental Restraint in a Hypnotic Pulse**

The track leans heavily on electronic elements, with **Jim’s steady drumming** providing an organic counterpoint to the layers of programmed percussion. **Bill Davenport’s xylophone fills** are a surprise highlight, offering an unexpected contrast to the deep, undulating synths. His experimental flourishes seem to drift between worlds—halfway between the avant-garde and the deeply melodic.

**Jeremy and Gilley** hold back here, providing only the faintest guitar textures, while **Jeff’s bass work glides effortlessly** between smooth, jazz-like phrasing and subtle, controlled pulses. The whole arrangement feels like the band engaging in a careful, cerebral push-and-pull—never allowing any one element to dominate.

But perhaps the boldest choice is **Kathy Espich’s electronically altered vocals**. Her voice—typically raw and emotive—is filtered into a **deep, smoky resonance**, almost androgynous at times. This creative decision was met with **a mixed public response**, as some fans found it compellingly atmospheric while others struggled with the emotional distance it created.

**Lyrics: Subtle Seduction and Playful Tension**

Lyrically, *"Sensible Pumps"* is steeped in **suggestion rather than overt passion**. The song captures the *before* moment of desire—the electric tension, the lingering glances, the slow, almost agonizing build-up to something more.

The verses are **poetic and flirtatious**, emphasizing body language, whispered words, and the weight of anticipation:

*At midnight's glow, a sight so divine,  
The moon cast silver beams, a soft design…*

Kathy’s delivery—made huskier by the production—only adds to the **sultry, nocturnal vibe**. There’s a conscious effort here to **avoid clichés**, instead painting the scene with *curious grins, blushing exchanges, and playful restraint*.

The **chorus, however, is a moment of warmth**:

*His laughter—a melody that stirred in the night,  
“Fear not,” he said softly, “for all feels so right.”*

It’s a contrast to the **underlying nervous energy of the verses**. The lover’s laughter is reassuring, breaking the tension in a way that *could* be comforting—or could be hiding something. This ambiguity adds to the song’s allure.

**Public & Critical Reception: A Divisive Opener**

As an album opener, *"Sensible Pumps"* is unexpected. It introduces **Shall I Beg?** as something far removed from *69 Reasons*’ radio-friendly appeal. Instead, it challenges the listener with an eerie, shimmering, yet restrained experience.

Some critics **praised the song’s production and sonic risk-taking**, calling it *“one of Commuted’s most hypnotic and layered compositions.”* Others felt it **lacked the emotional immediacy of their previous work**, particularly pointing to Kathy’s altered vocal tone as an odd creative choice.

In retrospect, *"Sensible Pumps"* is now recognized as an **early indicator of Commuted’s willingness to experiment**, a prelude to their later sonic shifts. **It remains a cult favorite among die-hard fans** but is often overlooked in mainstream discussions of the band’s early years.

**Final Verdict**

**A mesmerizing, texturally complex opener that signaled Commuted’s evolution—subtle, electronic, and seductive.** It didn’t grab the masses, but it planted the seeds of the band’s artistic ambition.

⭐⭐⭐⭐☆ (4/5) **— A bold choice that aged better than its initial reception suggested.**

**Track Review: "Radar Indicated" – A Midwest Gothic Nightmare**

If *Sensible Pumps* lured listeners into a smoky, pulsating haze, **"Radar Indicated"** slams them into a relentless, claustrophobic storm. The second track on *Shall I Beg?* is an **aural horror film**, a song designed to **disquiet, unnerve, and hypnotize** in equal measure. With its violent, staccato violin, hauntingly minimalist piano, and Kathy Espich’s **deliciously evil vocal delivery**, this track captures the terror of **the Midwest sky at its most apocalyptic.**

**The Sound: Unrelenting Staccato and Trephination by Violin**

Who knew *Gilley* could play the violin? Not a damned soul—until this song. His **vicious four-note staccato attack** never lets up, drilling into the listener’s mind like a relentless storm siren refusing to be ignored. Even when he steps back to pluck eerie, brittle notes, the tension remains suffocating. **The modified piano**, reminiscent of John Carpenter’s *Halloween* theme, is equally unsettling. Bill Davenport has altered its timbre to something almost inhuman—*metallic, dry, and hollow*, striking like an axe against bone.

With **bass and drums stripped back**, the song leans into its oppressive atmosphere, amplifying the violin and piano’s power. **Jeff’s bass is sparse and skeletal**, **Jim’s drumming is more heartbeat than rhythm**, allowing the instrumentation to **feel like a living, breathing force of nature rather than a traditional song structure**.

The **music video** reinforces the track’s terror, showing **footage of flattened small towns, split tree trunks, and wind-lashed fields under roiling, tornado-laden skies**. Gilley, ever the Midwestern fatalist, delighted in reminding people that *the sky tries to kill you here*—and this track serves as evidence that he wasn’t being metaphorical.

**Lyrics: Love as a Stormfront**

Lyrically, *"Radar Indicated"* is a masterful metaphor that, on the surface, **feels like a sensual push-and-pull between lovers**, but beneath that, there’s something darker—something **indifferent, cold, and violently inevitable**.

*With each careful breath that sweeps like a fire,  
I inch closer to need that spirals—entire.  
In agony sweet as he hovers so near,  
Hints of possession darken my smear.*

There is **no safety in this song, no gentle embrace, only forces that act upon the narrator, drawing her deeper into a passion that feels more like an impending natural disaster**. The **prechoruses** reinforce this unease, filled with imagery of walls crumbling and unspoken chasms.

The **chorus** suggests that the song’s narrator knows she is **standing on the precipice of something uncontrollable**, but whether she welcomes it or fears it remains unresolved:

*Say you’ll stay? But where do we tread?  
A maelstrom beckons; uncertainty bred,  
In the grip of his warmth, I lose all disguise,  
His stormed gaze shrouded in passionate skies.*

Like a tornado ripping across the plains, the track makes it clear: **nature doesn’t care about your feelings**. The tension in the song **isn’t just erotic—it’s existential**.

**Kathy’s Vocal Performance: A Villainous Seduction**

Kathy Espich’s vocals **steal the show**, heavily processed to **sound deeper, richer, and almost inhuman**. Unlike the smoky allure of *Sensible Pumps*, here she **sounds demonic, wicked, like a trickster reveling in destruction**. She draws out syllables with **a creeping, taunting cadence**, and her delivery of the **bridge** is genuinely chilling:

*Each movement a step on this fragile ledge—  
A dance without music—a finely tuned edge.  
Just one slip away from my fevered delight:  
Where pleasure ignites—yielding screams into night.*

Her voice stretches and **contorts the word “screams” into something feral**, like the wind through a shattered barn window. By the outro, she’s nearly whispering, **as if standing right behind the listener, daring them to turn around**.

**Public & Critical Reception: A Cult Classic in Terror**

At the time of release, *"Radar Indicated"* **confounded mainstream listeners**, but critics immediately hailed it as *one of Commuted’s most terrifyingly brilliant compositions*. The **music video cemented its legacy**, turning the track from unsettling to outright *unholy*. Over time, it has become a **fan favorite among those who prefer Commuted’s darker, more experimental side**.

This song **isn’t about catching your breath—it’s about making you hold it until you break**.

**Final Verdict**

**"Radar Indicated" is a landmark in Commuted’s catalog—one of their most disturbing, evocative, and unforgettable songs.** It doesn’t try to be approachable, but it *dares* you to sit with its menace.

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (5/5) **— A relentless, horror-infused masterpiece that perfectly captures the Midwest’s brutal indifference to human life.**

**Track Review: "*Monochrome*" – Sensual Trance in a Corporate Cage**

By the time ***"Monochrome"*** drifts into play, it’s clear that *Shall I Beg?* has fully abandoned the punchy, anthemic rock energy of *69 Reasons*. **This is Commuted in full ethereal, shoegaze-laden trance mode—sprawling, hypnotic, and deeply intimate.** If *Radar Indicated* was about the raw terror of nature, then this track is its counterpoint: a slow-burning, *sensory-driven longing wrapped in corporate monotony*.

**The Sound: Subdued, Hypnotic, and Mesmerizing**

Bill Davenport’s **languid, droning synths** form the song’s backbone, casting a soft, undulating atmosphere that never fully resolves—always hovering in tension. Jeff Evans' **deep, resonant bass swells** under the mix, subtly shaping the song’s rhythm in a way that is *felt* more than heard.

Jim’s **distant, precise percussion** keeps everything restrained, like a slow heartbeat, never rushing, never demanding attention. Gilley? In perhaps the **strangest textural experiment of the album**, he **uses sandpaper to create an organic, swishing effect**—a grainy, unsettling contrast to the track’s otherwise fluid ambiance. The sound is unreplicable live, forcing Gilley to substitute **maracas**, which lack the same tension but keep the sonic motif alive.

**Kathy’s Vocals: An Erotic Daydream Drifting in a Shoegaze Haze**

Kathy’s voice here is **soft but smoldering**, submerged in layers of reverb, making her words feel like they *drift* rather than cut. She doesn’t so much *sing* as *breathe the lyrics into existence*, mirroring the *weightless yet inescapable* feel of being trapped in an office, lost in fantasies that make the hours bearable.

**Lyrics: Office Ennui as Erotic Fantasy**

Lyrically, this is **one of the most intimate and personal songs on the album**, reflecting Kathy’s own experiences working in a bank before Commuted’s rise. It’s a song about **being trapped—not just in a physical place, but in time itself.** The corporate environment, with its monotony and interruptions, is juxtaposed against a **private, smoldering desire that keeps the protagonist sane.**

*In the stillness of hushed office air,  
I linger, trapped in thoughts laid bare,  
Ten hours inch toward noon’s embrace,  
Yet time itself wears a languid face.*

This **mundane setting is transformed into something deeply sensual**, each glance, movement, and coffee sip infused with unspoken desire. The **prechorus** escalates the tension:

*My chair a shackle to restless desire,  
A tempest within like a smoldering fire;  
The desk becomes more than wood and steel,  
The world fades away—only you feel real.*

The **chorus is the song’s heart**—a mantra-like repetition of longing:

*Imagining fingers, their patient roam,  
Your touch ignites this mundane monochrome;  
Bending over in fantasies awashed with lust,  
I cling to my coffee like love’s sacred trust.*

It’s the *ultimate contrast*—the tension between **corporate restraint and raw, personal hunger**. The phrase *"clinging to coffee like love’s sacred trust”* is an evocative image, capturing how **every sip, every idle moment, is an anchor in a sea of desire.**

As the song progresses, the daydream intensifies:

*With legs spread wide across the stark tabletop,  
A sip of warmth as daydreams interlock;  
Breasts pressed against where ambitions collide,  
In this office temple where yearnings reside.*

There’s **something powerfully defiant about this verse**—it takes the sterile, corporate setting and transforms it into a *sanctuary of forbidden pleasure*. The **prechorus** suggests that the protagonist’s desires are *a well-kept secret*, adding an *exhilarating sense of risk*:

*If only they knew this sweet secret kept tight—  
That between the banter lies heat sparked by night;  
This table I grip transforms into flesh;  
Each curve craves your name when ambition is meshed.*

**Public & Critical Reception:**

Fans who were expecting **stadium rock anthems were utterly blindsided by this song.** Some called it **“self-indulgent and aimless,”** while others found it **“a stunning, immersive dreamscape.”**

Many listeners **didn’t catch the personal connection at first**, assuming it was simply a sensual track—until interviews later revealed Kathy’s past job in banking. Suddenly, the song **took on a more grounded, even bittersweet meaning**—it wasn’t just about desire, but about the **power of fantasy to make mundane existence bearable**.

Over time, *"Monochrome"* has **aged into a fan favorite**, particularly among those who prefer Commuted’s **more atmospheric and abstract material**. In later years, **ambient electronic artists have cited it as a major influence**, with some **sampling its basslines and vocals in chillwave remixes**.

**Final Verdict**

This is **one of the album’s most immersive and hypnotic moments**, proving that *Shall I Beg?* wasn’t interested in playing by commercial rules. It takes **a deeply personal experience—feeling trapped in a 9-to-5 job—and turns it into an ambient trance dream**, rich with sensuality and escapism.

⭐⭐⭐⭐☆ (4.5/5) **— A mesmerizing, intimate masterpiece that rewards patience and deep listening.**

**Track Review: *"The Old Oak Tree"* – A Slow-Building, Alt-Country Masterpiece**

By the time ***"The Old Oak Tree"*** begins, *Shall I Beg?* has already thrown every expectation out the window. But even knowing that this album is *not* the second coming of *69 Reasons*, **this song still blindsides listeners.**

What begins as **a sparse, fragile piano ballad between Bill Davenport and Kathy Espich** unfolds into a stunning alt-country anthem. **The song’s structure is one of its greatest strengths**, with the band exercising **an almost painful amount of restraint**, holding back **until the exact moment their explosion is needed**. The result? **A career-defining moment.**

**The Sound: Pure Restraint, Then Pure Release**

For the first two verses, **it’s just Bill Davenport on piano, stark and unembellished, accompanying Kathy’s raw, emotional vocals.** There is no trickery, no layering—just **pure, open space**, making every note and syllable feel monumental.

**The rest of the band doesn’t play a single note until the third verse.** Even then, their presence is subtle, creeping in like a distant storm on the horizon. **Only after the fourth verse does the song explode,** releasing all the built-up tension with a cathartic, soaring chorus.

This track is **a masterclass in patience**. **Jeff, Jeremy, and Gilley hold back in a way that feels unnatural for Commuted**, almost as if they’re suffocating until the exact moment they can *breathe*. When they do, the release is *euphoric*—**a choral, alt-country burst of harmonies, acoustic layers, and emotional weight.**

The **sheer discipline** shown in this arrangement suggests that this song was **engineered for radio play**. The fingerprints of **Gilley and Brown are all over it**—their tendency to keep songs structured, digestible, and emotionally immediate.

**Kathy Espich’s Final Evolution to Piano?**

One of the most **widely speculated turning points in Commuted’s history** is tied to this song:

**Did *"The Old Oak Tree"* end Kathy Espich’s tenure as a guitarist?**

The sheer beauty of her **voice paired solely with Bill’s piano** is rumored to have been the **moment she fell in love with the soundscape that piano allowed**. If this track was the catalyst for her transition to **piano as her primary instrument**, it **forever altered the band’s sonic identity.**

**Lyrics: A Journey Through Lust, Trust, and Destiny**

Lyrically, the song **begins with warmth, hope, and wonder**, but quickly takes a turn:

*Excitement rises; currents pull us near,  
In sacred spaces free from fear.  
With whispered tones and longing's tease—  
I offer all beneath the trees.*

This **painted moment of idyllic romance and promise** quickly sours as **anger and distrust take hold**:

*Yet anger stirs as truth unfolds;  
For love will break when trust grows cold.*

The **chorus** is perhaps the most evocative line in the song:

*“Tell me more,” his voice is raw—  
As destiny whispers its silent law;  
With curiosity burning bright as fire—  
We chase our dreams upon lust's wire.*

This **duality of desire and consequence runs through the entire song.** The final version of the chorus, however, shifts from request to *demand*:

*“Fuck me more,” my voice is raw—  
As destiny whispers its silent law;  
With curiosity burning bright as fire—  
We chase our dreams upon lust's wire.*

There’s **an escalation of power, a defiant embrace of raw passion** that adds a dangerous, reckless energy.

The **bridge** takes the song into **something almost mythic**, speaking to **the inevitability of mistakes, deception, and wandering paths**:

*This journey vast is yours and mine—  
Through tangled thoughts our hearts align!  
And when our course goes again astray—  
Let's craft a tale where lies hold sway!"*

There is **acceptance here**, not just of passion, but of imperfection. Love is **a gamble, a negotiation, a dance with trust and deceit**.

**Public & Critical Reception: A Shock That Paid Off**

If there was one song on *Shall I Beg?* that had the best **chance at commercial success**, it was *"The Old Oak Tree."* It was the **most digestible, the most structured, and the most emotionally direct.**

But **critics and fans were still stunned.**

**"Is this even a rock band anymore?"**

That was the reaction from some corners of Commuted’s fanbase. A song **so devoid of rock elements for its first half** felt like a betrayal to listeners expecting *anything remotely resembling* the sound of *69 Reasons.*

Yet, in time, ****"The Old Oak Tree"*** became **one of the most respected tracks in the band’s catalog.** It is **often cited as a career-defining artistic risk**, a song that **proved Commuted’s willingness to abandon expectations in pursuit of something greater.**

The track's **radio-friendly run time of 3:29** also **helped it find favor on alternative stations**, particularly in **college radio circuits** where alt-country crossovers were beginning to take hold.

**Final Verdict**

A song that **should not work as a Commuted track—but absolutely does.** This is **the moment where restraint became a weapon** for the band, where they **trusted silence and space more than sound itself.**

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (5/5) **— A devastatingly beautiful moment of control, release, and emotional resonance.**

**Track Review: *"The Number I Dialed"* – A Haunting Alt-Rock Confessional**

By the time ***"The Number I Dialed"*** arrives, *Shall I Beg?* has firmly established itself as an album **unconcerned with mainstream rock conventions**, yet this track offers a brief return to **the band's 80s roots—albeit drenched in the creeping influence of the grunge explosion.**

This song carries **a distinct DNA of 80s alternative rock**, but it’s **warped, stretched, and slowly suffocated** by the weight of the emerging Seattle sound. The **piano-heavy arrangement** continues the album’s obsession with stripped-down, emotionally raw compositions, a choice **borrowed from fellow Ohio band The Afghan Whigs**, according to later interviews with the Evans brothers.

Yet, despite its **melancholy and brooding verses**, the song is **not without playfulness**—it **fakes out the listener with a false ending at the three-minute mark**, before suddenly returning with **a giggle from Espich,** leading into an **unexpectedly upbeat conclusion.**

**The Sound: Nostalgia Meets Grunge-Tinged Reality**

If ***"The Old Oak Tree"*** was a slow burn of patience and release, ***"The Number I Dialed"*** takes a more traditional **alt-rock route—but subverts it in small, brilliant ways.**

- The **piano remains central**, **but it never feels delicate—it’s weighty, a grounding force** rather than an ornament.
- **Jeff’s bass is deep, steady, and full-bodied**, reinforcing the song’s sense of forward motion.
- **Jim’s drumming remains restrained**, his hi-hats and snares keeping everything precise, **never allowing the song to spiral out of control.**
- **Jeremy and Gilley’s guitars enter subtly, almost reluctantly,** as if they were **afraid of overwhelming the song’s stark beauty.**

But the real trick? **The false ending.**

At **3:00**, the song **abruptly fades**, as if it has wrapped up, only to be **broken by Espich’s soft, mischievous giggle.** This moment is **so uncharacteristic for Commuted** that it feels almost jarring—a breaking of the fourth wall, a signal that the song is about to shift. And it does.

**The final two verses brighten the mood, pushing the tempo ever so slightly forward.** It’s as if the song **refuses to stay somber**, revealing that beneath its confession and regret, there is still **pleasure, indulgence, and a willingness to embrace the moment despite its consequences.**

**Lyrics: A Sultry Confessional Wrapped in Deception**

Lyrically, ****"The Number I Dialed"*** is one of the most **intimate and vulnerable songs on the album.** It walks the line between **sensuality, regret, and self-awareness, painting a picture of illicit passion and its inevitable consequences.**

It opens with a **tense but quiet confession**:

*In Friday’s hush, I spoke so soft,  
To Dan, whose breath was ragged loft.  
"Men crave my curves, that much is true,  
But hear me now of dreams anew."*

The protagonist is **fully aware of her power, her desirability**—but **this is not a boast; it’s a burden.** She is **framing her desire as something deeper, something searching.**

*A yearning stirs beneath the skin,  
Not just the chase, but deeper within.  
Oh Dan, you must understand my plight,  
How women thirst for warmth at night.*

**The chorus is poetic but deliberate:**

*It’s not just flesh in tender grasp,  
But secrets woven in every clasp.  
With gentle sighs, we dance and dive,  
In this charade where passions thrive.*

This isn’t *just* about physicality—it’s about **the emotional weight behind desire**, the hidden motivations that pull people toward **dangerous, thrilling connections.**

By the third verse, the song **introduces betrayal and consequence:**

*For cheaters roam like wolves at night,  
With masquerades of soft delight.  
While we explore with hearts laid bare,  
The dagger speaks from our affair.*

It is here that **the song becomes a confession not just of indulgence, but of guilt**. There is **no illusion of innocence—only acceptance of what has already transpired.**

*You see these hills, this tender land?  
Each curve a canvas by nature planned.  
Underneath the moon’s soft glow we find,  
Our bodies speak—our hearts aligned.*

But after the **false ending, Espich’s giggle, and the song’s unexpected continuation**, the lyrics shift **from passive regret to active participation.**

The **fifth and sixth verses flip the perspective**:

*Yet trust can flicker like a dying flame—  
When shadows whisper sweet deceitful name.  
I knew of lovers lost to lust’s appeal,  
And felt the sting—an aching reel.*

The song **fully acknowledges the cycle of betrayal**, but **there is no denial, only acknowledgment.**

*Us cheaters roam like wolves at night,  
With masquerades of soft delight.  
While I explore with hearts laid bare,  
The dagger speaks from our affair.*

In its **final moments, the outro plays almost like a sigh,** a final realization that the characters in this story are **bound by their own choices**:

*Dan knows my whispers too wild to tame;  
Enraptured by echoes calling my name.  
We’re floating on pulses through fragrant spells—  
In this confessional tale where lies softly dwells.*

It’s **one of the most evocative endings on the album**—it neither condemns nor justifies, instead **allowing the listener to sit in the unresolved tension of desire and deceit.**

**Public & Critical Reception: A Conflicted Favorite**

Much like ****"The Old Oak Tree,"*** this song **immediately stood out as one of the most commercially viable** tracks on *Shall I Beg?*. The **nod to 80s alternative rock**, combined with **its intimate storytelling and a clever, attention-grabbing structure**, made it a **radio favorite in certain markets.**

However, **the song divided listeners.**

Some **loved its honesty and playfulness**. The **giggle from Espich** and the **fake ending were hailed as brilliant choices**, making it one of the band’s **most memorable experiments.**

Others **felt it was too deliberately structured for airplay,** calling it **“a calculated emotional maneuver.”** Some die-hard fans of Commuted’s earlier work **disliked the overt polish of the track**, seeing **Gilley and Brown’s radio-minded instincts as a dilution of the band’s raw essence.**

Over time, ****"The Number I Dialed"*** became **one of the more debated tracks in Commuted’s catalog**—both **loved and criticized for the same reasons.**

**Final Verdict**

A **haunting, sensual confessional wrapped in classic alt-rock stylings**, but **with a knowing smirk and a refusal to fully wallow in its regret.**

It’s one of the **most carefully structured** songs on *Shall I Beg?*, engineered **to land with emotional impact**—a move that **some found too deliberate, but many found masterful.**

⭐⭐⭐⭐☆ (4.5/5) **— An emotional gut-punch, elevated by a clever structural trick and a vocal performance that lingers long after the final note.**

**Track Review: *"Temptress"* – A Cincinnati-Fueled Grunge Inferno**

After the somber and confessional tone of ****"The Number I Dialed,"*** Commuted shifts gears *hard* with ***"Temptress"***, a **distorted, groove-heavy, adrenaline-fueled grunge storm** that solidifies *Shall I Beg?* as **one of the band’s most stylistically adventurous albums.**

This track is **a fast mover**, clocking in at just over **three minutes, with vocals wrapping up by 2:45** before descending into **a chaotic 30-second grunge-and-synth outro**. It’s a **midwestern fever dream**, built on a **massive, Bootsy Collins-inspired bassline courtesy of Jeff Evans**, proving that **funk and grunge aren’t enemies—they’re secret lovers.**

**The Sound: Cincinnati Bass Meets Grunge Fury**

- **Jeff Evans doesn’t just hold down the low end—he *dominates* it.** The bass is **thick, sludgy, and relentless**, borrowing from the deep grooves of Cincinnati’s funk scene but distorting it into something **gritty, filthy, and undeniably modern.**
- **Jeremy, Kathy, and Gilley’s guitar work isn’t just grunge—it’s *playfully aggressive*, weaving in and out like a pack of hyenas circling their prey.**
- **Jim Brown digs into his bag of tricks with his signature gated drum sound,** making every snare hit feel like a *controlled explosion.*
- **The outro is a beautiful mess.** Distorted synths and guitar feedback **collide in a final act of defiance**, leaving the listener **drenched in sonic sweat.**

This is **one of the few moments on *Shall I Beg?* where the band fully embraces their rock DNA**, but **it’s mutated into something different—grungier, dirtier, and more experimental.**

**Lyrics: Seduction, Guilt, and Reckless Desire**

"Temptress" is **as much about power as it is about lust.** The protagonist isn’t just longing—she’s **in control, unapologetic, and dangerously alluring.**

*In twilight’s last note, where dreams intertwine,  
I cradle the weight of wishes undefined,  
A symphony of heart in quiet confession,  
With skin kissed by years yet craving transgression.*

There’s **a poetic weight to these lines**—the sense of **a woman both reflecting and acting in the same moment.**

Then comes one of the most **explicit, brutally direct lines on the album:**

*Longing for cock in a world grown uncertain;  
New life unfurling beyond forbidden curtains.*

There is **zero subtlety here—just raw, primal hunger.** It’s a declaration that, in this moment, **moral hesitation has no place.**

**The chorus** turns the song into a *slow-burn seduction ritual*:

*With sultry whispers close to your ear,  
“Do you like it, baby?” I dare draw near;  
As worlds collide in this fleeting dance,  
A symphony played in a stolen glance.*

The protagonist **dares her lover to admit his own weakness**, **turning desire into a game—one where she holds all the cards.**

The **bridge** plays almost like a challenge to fate itself:

*Oh heart, don your armor! For time waits for none,  
In the cadence of breath, life’s pulses run—  
Yet brimming with fervor, my craving won’t cease;  
As spirits connect and obtain release.*

There’s **a sense of inevitability here**—that whatever has started **cannot be stopped.**

But the **outro takes a sudden emotional turn.**

*Your wedding ring adorns a wishful hand,  
My heart speaks louder than what I had planned,  
Yet in this moment, vows dissolve away;  
An aching thrill that beckons me to stay.*

Here, **guilt creeps in**—not enough to stop the affair, but **enough to acknowledge its consequences.** The **final lines** capture **the fleeting nature of lust and the depth of what’s been lost:**

*I melt beneath your gaze, youthful and bold,  
With wide blue eyes reflecting tales untold.*

It’s **the perfect closing sentiment**—one that leaves the listener **unsure whether to celebrate or mourn what just transpired.**

**Album Analysis: *Shall I Beg?* – Commuted’s Daring Triumph and Commercial Compromise**

**Overview: The Follow-Up That Defied Expectations**

Released in **1993**, *Shall I Beg?* was **a massive critical and industry success** but **a commercial enigma**. It **sold two million copies in its first year**—an incredible number for most bands, but **a steep drop (80% lower) compared to *69 Reasons*, which had dominated 1992’s airwaves and cultural landscape.**

What happened?

- **Did Commuted lose their magic?**
- **Did they push too far artistically?**
- **Was it inevitable that their follow-up to *69 Reasons* would struggle to maintain its audience?**

The answer is **all of the above**—and yet, **it’s more complicated than that.**

---

**Artistic Evolution: A Band Unwilling to Repeat Itself**

Commuted had **two choices after *69 Reasons*:**

1. **Play it safe, refine their sound, and deliver *69 Reasons Pt. 2* for an audience that would eat it up.**
2. **Burn the formula to the ground, embrace artistic risks, and challenge every expectation.**

They **chose the latter.**

Unlike *69 Reasons*, which **thrived on immediacy, anthemic songwriting, and massive hooks**, *Shall I Beg?* is **a sprawling, experimental, deeply personal record that deliberately alienates casual listeners.**

**Musical Risk-Taking**

- The album **pulls from an insane mix of genres**, blending **shoegaze, swamp blues, acid house, alt-country, breakstep, and orchestral elements.**
- **Certain songs ("Sensible Pumps," "Guest Bedroom," "Calligraphy") feel like intimate diary entries**, designed for headphones rather than stadiums.
- **Other songs ("Execution," "Don't Answer the Call") are deliberately antagonistic**—not just sexually explicit, but composed in ways that make them impossible to program on mainstream radio.
- **The sequencing is chaotic on purpose**—the album **never settles into one tone for long**, keeping the listener **off balance at all times.**

It’s **the ultimate “fuck you” to radio formats**, and while **critics and insiders loved it**, the average listener had a **much harder time digesting it.**

---

**Lyrical Themes: From Playful to Disturbing**

Where *69 Reasons* balanced **sensuality with accessibility**, *Shall I Beg?* **pushes deeper into obsession, control, and emotional dissection.**

**Recurring Themes**

1. **Sexual Power & Submission**

- **"Calligraphy"** turns sex into a **psychological battle.**
- **"Execution"** plays with **themes of dominance and fear** in a way that borders on horror.

3. **Intimacy vs. Alienation**

- **"Guest Bedroom"** and **"Monochrome"** are **almost painfully quiet**, capturing **small moments of vulnerability.**
- **"Nightshirt"** is **the outlier—one of the only moments of pure joy.**

5. **Jealousy, Regret, and Power Struggles**

- **"India Ink"** and **"Don't Answer the Call"** turn desire into **something destructive, something that leaves the protagonist colder rather than fulfilled.**
- **"So You Did"** plays as **an upbeat, radio-friendly track that hides its transgressive nature in the outro.**

These aren’t **songs about love**—they’re **songs about control, loss, dominance, and consequence.**

For **critics and industry insiders**, this was **fascinating, fearless songwriting.**

For **the mainstream audience**, it was **often too much to process.**

---

**Why *Shall I Beg?* Struggled in the Long Term**

The album **sold an impressive 2 million copies in 1993**, but **sales fell 80% below *69 Reasons* within a year.**

**1. Lack of Radio Support**

- *69 Reasons* **was everywhere** in 1992, with **four huge hit singles.**
- *Shall I Beg?* **only had one mainstream hit—*"Nightshirt"***—and even that **was an anomaly.**
- Songs like **"So You Did"** and **"Calligraphy"** could have been massive… *if* **radio stations hadn’t immediately banned them.**

**2. MTV & Industry Politics**

- *69 Reasons* **benefited from heavy MTV rotation.**
- *Shall I Beg?* **had one video in rotation ("Nightshirt"),** but many of its best songs were **too explicit, too artistic, or too long for MTV.**
- The **band’s refusal to self-censor** meant **labels were hesitant to push the record as hard.**

**3. Casual Listeners Were Alienated**

- *69 Reasons* was **a rock album that made sense to mainstream listeners.**
- *Shall I Beg?* **was weirder, darker, and more fragmented**, making it **harder to engage with on a mass scale.**
- Many **casual fans who bought it expecting *69 Reasons Pt. 2* simply didn’t know what to do with it.**

**4. Live Shows Became Legendary… But Not Always Accessible**

- **"Don't Answer the Call" became a myth among fans**—no one knew if the band would dare play it live.
- **"Execution" turned some performances into borderline religious experiences**—but it also **alienated certain fans who expected a more traditional setlist.**
- The album’s **intensely sexual and transgressive themes made some venues hesitant to book them.**

---

**Legacy: A Masterpiece That Needed Time to Find Its Audience**

While *Shall I Beg?* **was a financial success, it didn’t have the lasting mainstream impact of *69 Reasons*.**

**But over time, its reputation grew.**

- **Industry insiders still hail it as one of the boldest follow-ups to a mainstream rock album ever recorded.**
- **It became an underground classic**, beloved by **fans who appreciate its audacity and complexity.**
- **Certain songs ("Execution," "India Ink," "Calligraphy") developed cult followings,** with live performances becoming **legendary events.**

Ultimately, *Shall I Beg?* was **too challenging, too unsettling, and too experimental to match its predecessor’s sales.**

But **it was never meant to.**

---

**Final Analysis: The Album That Defined Commuted’s Future**

If Commuted had **stayed on the same trajectory as *69 Reasons*, they might have become a household name.**

But *Shall I Beg?* proved that **they weren’t interested in following trends—they were interested in making history.**

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (5/5) **— A bold, uncompromising masterpiece that alienated casual listeners but solidified Commuted’s place as one of the most fearless bands of their era.**
