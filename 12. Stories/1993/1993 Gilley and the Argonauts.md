# **GILLEY AND THE ARGONAUTS**

---

# **SYMPLEGADES**

---


**EXT. INTERSTATE 675 SOUTH – EARLY AFTERNOON – JANUARY 1993 – LOW CLOUDS, BARE TREES**

A skinny ribbon of brand-new asphalt pulls the car toward Dayton like a black belt cinching America’s waist. The shoulders are chalked in salt like a bad haircut. The median grass is winter-brown, stiff as wire. Jeff’s got the heater on low and the fan ticks at the third notch because the fourth one rattles like someone whispering through foil.

He has driven this corridor in a hundred moods and ten different decades of himself—late to class, late to practice, late to everything—but never like this. He keeps catching the thought like a glint in the corner of his eye and then letting it go: everything around him is new. 675 isn’t even old enough to be jaded. The green exit signs look freshly scrubbed, the reflective sheeting too clean, as if a storm hasn’t yet taught them humility. The concrete seams on the overpasses are pale and uncracked. Even the Nutter Center—a low, pale spaceship squatting off the Wright State campus—is new enough to smell like wet gypsum and promise.

He checks the clock in the dash. The plastic buttons feel cold and hollow, the kind of engineering that promises to last exactly as long as the loan. 2:07 PM. Soundcheck at 3:30. Of course he will be early. Jeff has always treated time as a tuning key.

His right hand taps the steering wheel in a pattern no one else would recognize as music. Thumb, two, three, four; thumb-and-index like a ghost hi-hat he isn’t supposed to own. The bass line for the opener runs under his ribs like a subway. He hears where it wants to bloom in the room—low E swelling against new concrete, then falling away in a clean 2.2 seconds if the builders didn’t screw up their math. The Nutter Center will teach him its secrets in five notes. He’ll ask politely. He’ll demand the rest.

The radio is off. He tried it at the on-ramp and caught a Soundgarden promo—someone’s voice on a tin can saying “tonight,” saying “Dayton,” saying it like a rhyme—but he killed it. He doesn’t need the reminder. They’re opening for the big cats from Seattle; the air is already piney with secondhand myth. Let the others chase adrenaline. Jeff hoards something quieter: focus like a blade he’s sharpening against his teeth.

A sign flashes by: N. FAIRFIELD RD – NEXT RIGHT. He could make this exit with his eyes closed. He has, once, when he was eighteen and stupid and the snow came sideways. Not tonight. Today he is a man with a map in his bones. Today he is a professional coming home.

The Midwest is allergic to newness. They build things to look like they’ve always been there, then apologize for the fresh paint with a joke about taxes. But here—675 smooth as a record that hasn’t lived yet; the bridge shoulders chalk-white; the arena glass reflecting a sky the color of dishwater—here the future went ahead and hatched anyway. Anomaly. Miracle. Mistake. Take your pick. Jeff smiles to himself and downshifts a thought he’ll use later if anyone asks him how it felt: Like opening a shrink-wrapped Bible. Like a promise you can still smell.

He flicks the turn signal—not because anyone is near him, but because rules are rhythm, and rhythm is how you keep a band out of a ditch. The car slides down the ramp toward North Fairfield. The mall is new. The chain restaurants are new. Even the poles that will eventually hold a banner that says WELCOME RAIDERS look like they were bolted in last night. He feels a prickle under the skin at the thought of his high school friends taking their kids to Disney stores here someday. He is not sentimental, or so he tells himself. He is merely observant in a way that sometimes hurts.

At the light he drifts his eyes toward campus, curtain rods of winter trees framing the pale bowl of the arena. He can already see the loading dock in his mind, the black maw where all rock shows go to become real: yellow lines, longshoremen of Ohio in faded jackets, a forklift that coughs like it smokes. He imagines the crew cases—black coffins on casters with white stencils: COMMUTED in block letters, SOUNDGARDEN in a font that looks like it chews metal. He imagines Jim hopping the wheel chocks like a kid. He imagines Bill guarding a rack like it’s nuclear. He imagines Jeremy’s shoulders taking on that pre-show set, nerves dressed as swagger. He imagines Kathy icily unwrapping her voice. He imagines Gilley tapping a cigarette from a too-flat pack, then not lighting it, then lighting it anyway because the ritual is the point.

He doesn’t have to imagine the thing no one has said out loud yet, because Gilley told him on the phone last night, low and almost shy: “I want to do the Beatles for my old man.” No smirk, no dare. Just the straight line of it. Jeff said okay in a voice that didn’t betray relief. Okay because if you start saying no to sincerity, you end up alone in a pretty house with a trophy case and a bad taste in your mouth.

He takes the service road that loops behind the arena. The snow in the grass is the dirty kind, the kind that memorizes footprints and equipment dollies and then freezes them. Two campus cops watch him pass with the bored hostility of people who have to wear uniforms to watch other people have fun. Jeff finds the security gate. He leans out into the cold to tell the guard his name and watch recognition travel the man’s face like heat under snow. Oh. That band. That boy. Home. The gate clanks open. The civic machine approves.

## **INT. NUTTER CENTER – LOADING DOCK – 2:31 PM – BREATH FOG**

It’s a cathedral of concrete and reverb back here. Every sound has a twin that returns to apologize for itself a second later. A forklift beeps time. A coiled cable looks like a sleeping snake. Jeff backs the car into the spot the road manager texted him to take and kills the engine. The fan keeps spinning another second, a faithful little soul, then dies.

He sits with his hands on the wheel for one breath longer than a reasonable man would. It’s not nerves; it’s gratitude mixed with the sourness of the price tag. He can feel the years compressed into this one dock: the trembling hands in the garage, the first bar stink, the lies they told to get the van out of the impound lot in ’90, the too-fast tour eighteen months ago where the interstate stopped and the shoulders were mud. He remembers how 675 was a rumor then. And now he is here, early, in a city that knows his name for something other than his brother’s GPA. He lets himself smile once, briefly, like an unguarded photograph.

Then the door opens and the air bodies him: January cold with a tang of new paint. He shoulders his bass in its case—he prefers to carry the weight himself; it teaches respect—and clacks up the dock ramp, nodding at the IATSE guys like officers passing.

Inside the bowl, the lights are half-on, the big house lamps that make everything look like a hangar. The stage is half-built and still enormous. The risers are an urban development of black plywood squares. A drum rug sprawls red as a tongue. There’s a low murmur of crew dialect: Where’s the snake? Left snake. No, the other left. Power at stage right is clean? Clean-ish. This is church to him.

He sets the bass case down stage left and stands at the lip. The seats stair-step into a dim galaxy. You can feel 8,000 people before they’re here—the idea of them has a weight, a heat. He tests a simple shape with his hand: the first five frets on the E string ghosted into the air by memory alone. In his head, the notes slosh out to the ceiling and back like tidewater in an empty swimming pool. He will need less on the low EQ. He will want the side-fills at sternum height, not throat. He will make a request, and then he will do it himself because he can.

A shout snaps the illusion: “**JEFF**!” It’s Cremeans, coat zipped to the collar, clipboard held like a shield. He doesn’t hug; he never does. He nods like a man who can smell a tax write-off. “You’re early. Good. We’ve got fifteen on stage power and twenty on line check before the big boys want the deck.” He tips his head toward the Soundgarden crew hauling a black sea of cases like ants around a kill.

“Works,” Jeff says. It always does.

## **MONTAGE – THE OTHERS ARRIVE**

— **JIM** barrels in with his duffel and an expression that says what if we start the set with a felony? He grips Jeff’s shoulder in passing and doesn’t slow down. His breath makes steam banners.

— **BILL** comes with the reverence of a man escorting an organ donor. He cracks the latches on a rack case and touches a patch bay like a priest putting two fingers to a neck to feel the pulse. “Hum is beautiful,” he says to no one, meaning there isn’t any.

— **JEREMY** (Evans) walks with that admirable quiet that always reads like planned. He taps a knuckle on the body of his guitar in the case, four clicks, and listens. Jeff could cry at how much that noise means they are going to be okay.

— **KATHY** appears at the far wing in an army jacket too big for her and a thermos that might hold tea or dynamite. She nods at Jeff with the gravitas of the one person in the room who can make him rewrite a chorus with a sentence. “New place,” she says. He says, “New place,” back. She likes that they don’t waste language.

— **GILLEY** last, of course, stepping out of the bowl seating like he’s just been hatched there by applause. He’s in a black T-shirt under a leather he doesn’t need in this temperature and glasses that catch every house light and fling them back like coins. He saunters to the mic and then stops shy of it, head bowed one beat. He looks up at Jeff and the look means: that thing we said. Jeff gives the smallest nod a human face can perform. That is consent. That is arrangement.

## **INT. STAGE – 3:29 PM – LINE CHECK**

The PA coughs awake and the whole building gathers itself. A monitor engineer with a pencil behind his ear is muttering the rosary of channels. “Kick. Snare. Hat. Rack. Floor. Bass DI. Bass mic.” Jeff plugs in, twists the volume with the care you use for someone else’s baby, and hits an open E.

There it is. The Nutter Center tells him its truth. New rooms tell faster; they aren’t ashamed yet. The note goes out full and round, collides with concrete, returns younger than it left. He can work with that. He trims a hair on the onboard EQ. He asks for a little less 200 in the wedge, hand flat and tilted to show the angle. The monitor guy gets it in one movement. Love.

Jim answers the world with a snare crack that sounds like ripping denim in God’s kitchen. Bill opens a synth pad like daybreak slipping under blinds. Kathy steps forward, says “check” once, and half the crew glances up because even a syllable from her has story. Jeremy tests a clean line, then dirties it on purpose, then grins like he stole something and will never be caught.

Gilley lifts the mic. “Okay,” he says to the room, and only Jeff and maybe Kathy hear the little quake in that. He doesn’t grandstand. He doesn’t announce. He just looks toward the far bowl where Section 213 will be later and breathes. “Let me have a little plate,” he tells the front-of-house, and the FOH tech gives him a tasteful drizzle of echo like memory itself.

Gilley sings a line. Not theirs. Not yet.

*Your day breaks, your mind aches…*

Jeff feels the lyric slide across the fresh concrete like a skate. Kathy’s harmony joins in without being asked. It is tender and not weak. It is dangerous because it is honest. Bill takes his pad down to air. Jim brushes. Jeremy plays a counterline almost too quiet to hear, a heartbeat the arena leans in for.

The sound guy looks at Jeff like you knew about this? Jeff tips his head like I know about the weather. Section 213 is empty now but the ghost of a man in a winter coat is already looking down, and Jeff imagines what it will do to Gilley to meet that gaze at full volume. He thinks of fathers. He thinks of the cost of standing on a stage and telling the truth while everyone expects a knife fight. He thinks, with his jaw set, that he will cut the set in half himself if anyone mocks the moment. He thinks of Ohio as a long-held breath.

They finish the verse. No one claps. This is soundcheck. But the room holds something, like a cupped match.

“Okay,” Gilley says again, this time with a grin that looks like home and trouble. “Now let’s make them forget their names.”

The rest unfolds the way it always does when they’re ready: Jeff and Jeremy pick up the spine, Bill hangs lights on it, Jim drives it through the neighborhoods, Kathy straps dynamite under the front steps, and Gilley knocks on the door with both fists. The monitors settle. The front-of-house blooms. The room tames itself around their noise until the noise is the room.

## **EXT. NUTTER CENTER – DUSK BLEEDING IN – 4:12 PM**

They step out to the dock for air. It smells like brine and exhaust and a bakery somewhere doing its best. The newness is already dulling under the day. That’s what days do.

Cremeans is running down times. “Doors at seven. Curfew hard at eleven. You’ve got forty. They want you off at thirty-eight.” He looks at Jeff, then at Gilley. “Do what you’re gonna do. But do it on time.”

Jeff nods. He always does. He looks back at the building, at the bridge gleaming pale, at the brand-new interstate sliding past like someone else’s good idea. He feels the cables of his life pull snug: boyhood to this; garage to this; all their almosts to this.

“Home,” Kathy says, not really to him.

“Home,” he echoes, because sometimes repeating the word is how you teach it to believe you.

Down in the parking lots, cars nose in like cattle to water. Fans in thrift flannel and varsity jackets and brand-new Soundgarden shirts tramp up the stairs. Somewhere, in one of those cars, an older man checks his watch and tells his wife not to worry about the crowd because their boy will take care of them. Somewhere a girl from their high school is already crying and she doesn’t know why yet.

Jeff rolls his shoulders, feeling the ordinary miracle of a back that still does what he asks. He looks at his hands, always a little haunted by what they can conjure, and flexes them, slow. Rhythm is rules. Rules keep you alive. And life—here, now, against a building that didn’t exist when the dream was declared—is brand new. Even in the Midwest. Even in January. Even for them.

He goes back inside to tune. Soundcheck at 3:30. On time. Ready. The kind of ready that turns concrete into memory and memory into a note that doesn’t decay until you tell it to.

## **EXT. NUTTER CENTER PARKING LOT – JANUARY 19931 – LATE AFTERNOON**

The Ohio cold sits heavy, twenty-four degrees and falling, the kind of cold that makes Bic pens snap in your pocket. The sun is already dragging its fat belly toward the horizon, a dull orange behind the skeletal trees. The parking lot is half ice, half gravel, and already filling with Dayton’s faithful—kids in flannel, in leather, in varsity jackets with Commuted scrawled in black marker across the back.

By the tour bus, Jim Brown and Bill Davenport are hunched over the hood of someone’s Camaro, Sharpies in hand, signing cassette inserts and wrinkled ticket stubs. Two girls—Dayton Tech sophomores with chipped nail polish—giggle as Jim scrawls his name across one’s bra strap with a casual authority that would get anyone else slapped. Bill, steady and sardonic, signs Bill D. in precise block letters on the other girl’s spiral notebook, as if she might file it alphabetically later.

They are doing the public work of rock stars, but their voices are pitched low, urgent, conspiratorial, belonging not to idols but to conspirators plotting a small coup.

**JIM** (firm, like he’s rehearsed it in his head already)
“They gave us forty minutes every night this tour. Forty-two if they were loose. Tonight they want us off in thirty-eight.”

**BILL** (flat, incredulous)
“Thirty-eight.”

Jim shakes his head. His curls are damp from the cold and his breath fogs like smoke.

**JIM**
“Fuck that. This is Dayton. This is the Nutter Center. They added a second night here, Bill. Do you know where else Soundgarden gave a second night? Houston. That’s it. Houston and us. That tells you everything.”

Bill caps his Sharpie, leans his squat frame against the Camaro, and folds his arms.

**BILL**
“So what are we talking here? We going to press it?”

Jim’s eyes burn. He doesn’t even hesitate.

**JIM**
“No. We’re not pressing anything. We’re taking it. This is our turf. This is our land. This building didn’t even exist when we started touring eighteen months ago. The highway didn’t exist. The bridge didn’t exist. All of this was corn and gravel. Now it’s brand new and so are we. You think I’m giving that back because Soundgarden wants their smoke machines on schedule?”

Bill grins slow. He likes when Jim’s voice goes hard like that, like steel dragged against flint.

**BILL**
“Alright. We take it. But it’s still math. ‘Come Inside Me’ is eleven and a half minutes. That’s a quarter of the set. You want it?”

Jim stares down the ice-slick blacktop, toward the arena rising like a spaceship over Wright State.

**JIM**
“We need it. That song is us. And if they don’t like it? They can play tighter. Let them shave their encore. We sold this place out, Bill. Twice. Memphis can’t even move half their tickets. You know why? Because kids here believe in us. That’s leverage. And leverage is everything.”

Bill nods, still leaning, still quiet, but a grin creeping onto his lips.

**BILL**
“Leverage is everything. Yeah.”

They pause as another trio of kids shuffle over with posters and cheap disposable cameras. Jim signs in big, quick loops, a general bestowing his mark. Bill doodles a tiny keyboard next to his name, a private joke.

When the kids leave, Bill shakes his head and mutters like he can’t let it go.

**BILL**
“Hey Jim, how the fuck do you end up with a road out here called Colonel Glenn Highway? A Marine colonel in an Air Force town. Next to Wright-Patt. Next to W.O. Wrights.”

Jim snorts, exhales a ghost of laughter into the frost.

**JIM**
“Beats the fuck out of everybody. But I don’t care what they call the road. They call it forty minutes tonight? I call it forty-five. Maybe forty-six. We play our show, not theirs.”

The Sharpies squeak again, autographs handed off like contraband. The crowd swells in waves as the sky deepens purple. Inside, the soundcheck ghosts still hum on the risers. Outside, the band’s two most dangerous strategists finish their plotting with nothing more than a nod.

They’ve decided. Tonight belongs to them.

## **INT. BACKSTAGE – NUTTER CENTER – LATE AFTERNOON – JANUARY 1993**

The backstage hallway smells like dust and piss, that particular American arena perfume of cinderblock sweat. Everyone’s nerves are splitting into different shapes: Bill pacing with his head down, Jim cracking his knuckles like pistons, Jeff already coiled into quiet fury. And Gilley—Gilley’s a fire alarm in human form, glasses fogging, jaw clicking, wound up and pacing.

But Kathy? Kathy’s a tuning fork vibrating itself apart. Her army jacket is off and balled in a corner, her black shirt damp already under the arms. She’s chain-chewing a piece of gum like it’s the only thing anchoring her to earth. Her eyes are wide enough to swallow the room.

**GILLEY** (leaning in, half-serious, half-fucking with her)

“Are you actually fit to play? Because you look like you’re halfway to a seizure.”

**KATHY** (breathless, sharp)
“I just need a shot. Or a liter of vodka. Either one.”

**GILLEY** (shaking his head, pacing in front of her)
“No, that’s not going to do. That’ll just spin you harder. We need a wicked idea. Something you can grab onto. Otherwise this whole thing goes down in flames, and it’s our homecoming. Your mom’s in the crowd, my dad’s in the crowd, this is the night. We can’t just hammer through the set like it’s Cleveland.”

Kathy rubs her hands on her thighs, trying to bleed some of the electricity out through denim. She snaps her gum, eyes flashing like she just thought of fire.

**KATHY**
“Okay. Okay. Listen. Your dad’s a U2 fan, right? Like old school? I’ve got the utmost respect for him. Why don’t we do a deep cut?”

**GILLEY** (lifting his head like a dog catching a scent)
“How deep are you talking?”

**KATHY**
“Off *Wide Awake in America*. The live version of *A Sort of Homecoming*. Not the floaty one from *Unforgettable Fire*. I’m talking about the one with shaved balls on it.”

She says it with a manic little laugh, snapping the word shaved like a snare hit.

**GILLEY** (stopping dead, grinning so wide it looks dangerous)
“Shaved balls on it. Christ, that’s the hottest thing I’ve heard in days.”

(beat, leaning closer, lowering his voice like a dirty secret)
“I had sex with twins last night.”

Kathy barks out a laugh, too loud, too sharp, adrenaline spilling into the air like battery acid. She almost tips over from it, hands braced on her knees.

**KATHY**
“You’re deranged. And I love it. We should fucking do it.”

Gilley straightens his glasses, slicks his hair back with both hands like he’s sealing a deal.

**GILLEY**
“Yeah. We’re gonna do it. We’ll drop Homecoming right in the middle of the set. Make the whole arena remember where the fuck we’re from.”

Kathy’s hands stop shaking. Just a little. Her eyes still burn, but now the fire has shape, a thing to follow. She bites the inside of her cheek until it stings, grounding herself, and looks at Gilley with something like worship, something like complicity.

**KATHY**
“Let’s burn it down.”

And in the cinderblock shadows of the brand-new arena, they both know: they’ve just put gasoline on the night.

## **INT. BACKSTAGE – NUTTER CENTER – JANUARY 1993 – PRE-SHOW**

The cinderblock corridors hum with pre-show static—roadies shouting, amps groaning as they’re rolled into place, the sting of gaffer tape ripped too fast. The band is in their little war council huddle, jackets off, nerves raw. Kathy’s pacing like a cat on a radiator, Gilley prowling beside her, mind spinning angles. And now he pulls Jeremy aside, the brother-in-arms, the one who always half-smiles like he already knows the answer.

**GILLEY** (grinning, too eager, eyes sharp behind his glasses)
“Jeremy, how well can you emulate The Edge?”

Jeremy tilts his head, gives him that look—half pity, half affection, like watching a puppy eat its own tail.

**JEREMY** (flat, mocking)
“Come on, man. You wounded me. You think I can’t? The fuck do you take me for?”

Gilley barks a laugh and spins toward Jim, who’s been lurking, arms folded, waiting to throw gas on the fire.

**GILLEY**
“Alright, Jim. We got The Edge, you gonna be Larry Mullen? Senior, Junior, whichever the fuck. You can do Larry Mullen Jr. on demand, right?”

Jim pops his knuckles, dead serious.

**JIM**
“Try me.”

Gilley’s teeth flash.

**GILLEY**
“Okay then. What about *A Sort of Homecoming* in the middle of the set?”

Jeremy’s face hardens, instant. He shakes his head like swatting a fly.

**JEREMY**
“Hell no. Wrong. No.”

Gilley blinks, caught off guard.

**JIM** (leaning in, testing the water)
“Hold up, man. The idea’s kind of appealing. We’re already doing one cover. And it’s Dayton. We’re about to play in front of a fucking adoring crowd. Might be perfect.”

Jeremy jabs a finger at both of them, eyes blazing.

**JEREMY**
“No. Wrong again. We don’t bury it in the middle. We open with it. You hear me? We open with it. We walk out and baffle the fuck out of everybody. We hit them upside the head with it and claim the place before Soundgarden even gets their gear tuned. Nutter Center’s ours.”

There’s a pause. Then Gilley leans forward, glasses catching the fluorescent buzz.

**GILLEY**
“So what you’re telling me… is this is territorial pissing.”

Jeremy and Jim glance at each other. Their grins explode in unison.

**JIM** & **JEREMY**
“Fuck yeah.”

Gilley slaps Jeremy’s shoulder, laughing like a lunatic, already sold.

**GILLEY**
“Alright. Here’s what we do. We get the announcer to bring us onstage like a local prizefight. *Live from W.O. Wright’s down the street—It’s Commuted!*”

Jim throws his head back, loving it.

**JIM**
“I love it.”

Jeremy’s already pacing, rehearsing riffs under his breath, his hands twitching like strings are under them.

**GILLEY**
“And when we’re done—when *Come Inside Me* finishes and we leave them bleeding—Kathy steps up to the mic and invites the whole goddamn crowd to W.O. Wright’s for the afterparty.”

The idea hangs in the stale air like lightning about to strike. Kathy stops pacing, hears it, and her eyes widen like she just found another gear.

This is no longer a show. It’s a siege.

## **INT. BACKSTAGE – NUTTER CENTER – GREEN ROOM – MINUTES BEFORE DOORS**

The plan hangs in the air like cigarette smoke. Kathy’s still vibrating, barely tethered, when she realizes her name just got volunteered for the grand finale. She freezes, blinks once, then cuts them off before Gilley can even start explaining.

**KATHY**  
“Shut the fuck up. I’m in. Don’t even finish the sentence. I’m in.”

Her voice is jagged, sure, almost giddy. Gilley grins—he knew she’d leap before looking. But the real problem is still looming: Bill and Jeff.

They pull the circle tighter. Jeff’s slouched in the corner, legs sprawled, tuning his bass low, lazily, like he’s got better things to think about. Bill’s perched forward on a folding chair, hunched over his rack like a surgeon laying out knives, testing switches with the quick, delicate taps of his fingers.

**GILLEY**  (almost ceremonious)  
“Alright, brothers. The plan: we open with *A Sort of Homecoming*. Wide Awake in America cut. Straight Edge worship. We plant our flag before Soundgarden even smells the stage.”

Jeff lets his bass note die in the air, unimpressed.

**JEFF**  (flat, half-bored)  
“Adam Clayton? Come on, man. That’s not a challenge. That’s not even bass work. That’s just repeating one idea until your knuckles seize. You really want me to lock into that?”

Kathy whips around, eyes blazing, riding the adrenaline hard.

**KATHY**  
“Jeff. It’s not about *you* tonight. It’s about *home*. About my mom in the fucking crowd. About Gilley’s dad. About telling this arena it belongs to us before we even start our set. You bored? Good. You’ll play bored. Just make it sound like God’s heartbeat and then pout about it later.”

Jeff blanches, doesn’t commit, but doesn’t walk either.

Bill, though—Bill’s fingers are twitching. He’s staring at the keys like he’s already hearing the reverb trails, already seeing the timing grids in his mind.

**BILL**  (measured, calculating)  
“If you gave me a day, I’d show up with precision. Whole racks programmed. Delays stacked. Something rich enough to melt concrete. A day, and I’d have it locked.”

**KATHY**  (leaning in, almost rabid)  
“You don’t have a day. You’ve got twelve minutes. And you’re Bill Davenport. If anyone on earth can set up an impossible wall of sound in twelve minutes, it’s you. You live for this.”

Jeff chuckles, throwing the bait in heavier.

**JEFF**  
“I don’t think there’s a keyboard player on earth who could take an impossible deadline like that, no prep, no safety net, and nail it on the first strike. No one. Except maybe…”  

(he lets it hang like a lazy prick )  
“You got me, Bill?”

Bill stops fiddling. Looks up. His pupils are sharp pins. For a heartbeat, silence. Then:

**BILL**  (sworn like an oath)  
“It’ll fucking rule.”

The room exhales. The coup is complete.

## **INT. NUTTER CENTER – STAGING CORRIDOR – 6:56 P.M.**

The house lights drop just enough to make the crowd swell in one long, shivering roar. Then Van Halen’s “Jump” detonates through the PA. The synth line is pure 1984 neon, buzzing off the rafters, bouncing across the concourse, and the whole arena sings it back like they’ve been waiting since birth.

Three minutes, forty-five seconds. That’s all Commuted has before the cue is theirs.
