**INT. KOTOKA INTERNATIONAL AIRPORT – ACCRA, GHANA – MIDDAY – JUNE 1996**

Customs is chaos. The air, thick with jet fuel, fried plantain, and sweat, sticks to your skin. Gilley gets flagged for “suspicious equipment”—the pedal board’s a tangle of cables and handwritten lyrics. Jeremy tries to bribe a customs agent with a promo cassette.  

Kathy?  

She doesn’t say a word. She just stares straight ahead, hair wild, tank top streaked with the dust of three continents. If you’d asked her, she’d say she wasn’t nervous. If you’d listened to her breathing, you’d know she was lying.

**EXT. LABADI BEACH – AFTERNOON**

Hotel pool glistens under a violent sun.  Bill’s reading *Things Fall Apart* in the shade. Jim and Jeff are bickering over snare tuning, arguing in language only drummers and brothers understand. The band’s been on the road for months—skinny, sunburned, irritable, horny, brilliant.

Tonight is their first African stadium show.  Forty thousand tickets.  Half the gear didn’t make it through Lagos.  Half the band wants to kill each other.  No one wants to admit they’re afraid.

---

**INT. NATIONAL THEATRE – BACKSTAGE – EVENING**

A wall of heat and noise. Ghanaian crew moving at their own tempo, beautiful and precise. There are fans outside who have waited since noon, chanting “Commuted!” in accents that make every song sound brand new.  

Kathy sits cross-legged on a cracked linoleum floor, writing setlists in black marker on her thighs.  Jeff asks the local promoter if there’s any whiskey. The promoter laughs. “You want palm wine or you want to remember tonight?”

Gilley is high on adrenaline and mosquito repellant, flirting with a British travel reporter. He dares Kathy to open with “*Not Taken Kindly*.” She double-dares him to wear only a dashiki on stage.

**SOUND: Drums thundering through cement. Bill dialing in a synth patch with the engineer, who says, “Play it loud, the ghosts are listening.”**

**SHOWTIME – NIGHT – ACCRA SPORTS STADIUM**

The crowd is wild—alive in a way that London never was, honest in a way New York never dared to be. The band launches into “Victory Monument.” Kathy’s voice, raw, full of sex and anger and prophecy, slices through humid darkness.  Kids climb the stadium fences.  Gilley loses his shirt on the second song, throws it into the crowd.  Jim breaks a stick, keeps going, blood on his snare.  Jeff’s bass rumbles like a threat.  Bill triggers samples of traditional Ghanaian percussion, and the place explodes.

Third encore—local rappers storm the stage.  Nobody knows what’s happening.  Security can’t stop it.  It turns into a massive, multilingual jam: “Glass Hotel” with verses in Twi, Kathy howling, crowd surging, everyone in the band grinning, desperate, more alive than they’ve ever been.

**AFTER THE SHOW**

The afterparty spills into the streets. Palm wine, grilled fish, bodies dancing, laughing, forgetting.  Kathy kisses a girl in a yellow sundress.  Gilley disappears with the travel reporter, comes back at dawn with a photo of a Sankofa bird in his hands.  Bill and Jeff end up on the beach with a borrowed acoustic, playing until their hands hurt.  Jim passes out on a plastic chair, dreaming of drums and home.

**INT. HOTEL ROOM – DAWN**

Sunrise over the Gulf of Guinea, the band collapsed together in a tangle of sheets, gear cases, and empty bottles. No one speaks. No one needs to.

Kathy finally breaks the silence:  “If we can do this, we can do anything.”

And for one night—maybe the only night—they all believe it.

**Legend says** there are still kids in Accra who learned English from Commuted lyrics.  
Legend says a tape of that show made it to pirate radio, and every copy is a little more distorted, a little more holy.

You want *world domination*?  It tastes like sweat, fear, palm wine, and the electricity of a band at the absolute edge of itself—giving everything, taking nothing for granted, burning so bright the world couldn’t help but notice.

**INT. HOTEL BATHROOM – LABADI BEACH HOTEL – DAWN AFTER THE SHOW**

Jeff stands shirtless, sweat and dust streaked on his chest, staring down a cracked porcelain sink. He’s brushing his teeth with bottled water—imported, overpriced, and already running out. He hates the city’s pipes, the metallic tang of fear in every tap, the way the water stains his hands with the memory of what’s at stake.  But he loves the city. God, does he love the city.

He loves the way the crowd sang his name, twisting “Evans” into something new and holy.  
He loves the street vendors hawking roasted plantains and the kid with a broken Strat copy begging him to teach “*Victory Monument*.”  

He loves the sound of Ghanaian highlife leaking out of open windows, the way his bass lines feel like a foreign language, the way nobody in Accra cares about Billboard charts or what Rolling Stone thinks.

**Backstage that night:**  He confides to Bill, voice low, heart open for once:  

**JEFF:**  
“You ever get the feeling the real world is just… more real here? Like we’ve been sleepwalking and these people—these kids—are wide awake?”  

Bill just nods, hands him a bottle of palm wine.  

**BILL:**  
“You wake up in Accra, man. Whether you want to or not.”

He spends half the afterparty arguing with a local sound engineer about EQ curves and talking shit about the hotel plumbing.  

**JEFF:**  
“How does anyone live like this? It’s barbaric.”  

**ENGINEER:**  (grinning)  
“You just have to want it more than you want to be comfortable.”

*And Jeff does.*  By 4 a.m. he’s got blisters on his fingers, a stomach full of street food, and a mind humming with new rhythms.  He sits out on the balcony, watching the city wake up, watching the fishermen pull their nets from the Gulf, and for once—just once—he doesn’t want to fix anything.  He just wants to *feel* it.

In the weeks that followed, he wrote some of his wildest, weirdest, most hopeful basslines. “Reduit” and “Burn the Harbor” both start as sketches in his Ghana notebook.  He’d tell everyone later it was the water that nearly killed him, but it was the city that resurrected him.

**The real truth?**  Accra is where Jeff learned how to belong somewhere foreign, and how to trust a band of strangers to be his family—if only for one impossible, perfect night.  
It’s why he kept coming back, chasing that alchemy, chasing a world where the only thing more dangerous than the water was the *music* itself.

**INT. ACCRA – HIDDEN OFFICE – AUGUST 1998**

Jeff’s back in Ghana. Low profile. No entourage, no band. Just a duffel bag, a laptop, and a bank account fattened by three years of international royalties. The kind of secret trip that would get him murdered by PR if anyone in North America caught wind.

He meets with local engineers, not music execs. No photo ops. No press. Just Jeff, wild-eyed and quietly furious, grilling them over infrastructure plans and sustainable filtration.  
He bankrolls a test system himself—silent partner, no strings, no vanity plaque. Every time he comes back, he inspects the work personally, refusing to let “good enough” slide. More than once, he walks the slums incognito, stubble, sweat, no security, no translator, just a notebook and the memory of that first hotel sink.

When the first tap opens—cool, clear, safe water in a neighborhood that hadn’t seen it in a decade—he doesn’t stick around for thanks. He catches a cab to the airport, tips the driver triple, and writes a new bassline on the back of a boarding pass.

**Not a word to Rolling Stone. Not a word to Billboard.**  Band doesn’t know. Label oesn’t know.  It’s not a brand.  It’s penance, and proof he could still change something for the better.

Backstage, the only clue anyone ever saw:  A Ghanaian water utility receipt folded into his wallet, soft with age, replaced every year like a talisman.  Kathy found it once, years later, in a bundle of receipts and sketches.  

**KATHY** (waving it at him):  
“You buying property now, Mr. Rockefeller?”  

**JEFF** (shrugs, deadpan):  
“Just wanted to make sure the water’s good next time we play there.”

And that was it. No confession, no lecture, no self-congratulation.  Just the truth, quietly done, the way Jeff always believed the best things should be:  Unrecorded. Unrewarded. Unfinished. And absolutely, fiercely real.

In Dayton, in New York, even in Paris—Jeff Evans is the weird, gnomic bassist who always has his passport half-packed, who misses a soundcheck every August with some excuse nobody quite buys. But in Accra? He’s the white ghost in flannel and work boots who shows up for every annual board meeting of the *Trust*, still anonymous, still dead-serious, still cursing about maintenance contracts and chlorination cycles, correcting the minutes by hand.

---

They wanted Bono, they got the Clean Water Act—tattooed on a battered notebook, recited by heart, wielded like scripture at sunrise board meetings. No charity selfies. No “visionary founder” portraits in the annual report. Just a band guy with sweat stains under his arms, reading EPA code and grilling civil engineers about NTUs and backflow valves, daring anyone in the room to know the law better than him.

**INT. TRUST BOARDROOM – ACCRA – LATE MORNING, AUGUST 2008**

The boardroom: high ceilings, cheap fans, plastic chairs, every window cracked to keep the room from boiling. The community’s elders and engineers in crisp shirts, hands folded, waiting for the show. They’re expecting a guitar. Maybe a prayer. Instead, they get Jeff, notebook open, sleeves rolled up, pen between his teeth, pages flagged and creased.

He stands.  

**JEFF:**  
“Ladies and gentlemen, this is our bible: Title 40, Chapter I, Subchapter D, Part 131. Water Quality Standards. United States Environmental Protection Agency. We beat these standards, we beat anyone. Ghana, Europe, fuckin’ Ohio. You want clean? You want safe? You want to keep your kids alive and your crops watered and your pipes free of rot? We win here. We win everywhere.”

(He flips a page, eyes the engineer at the end of the table.)

**JEFF:**  
“I move that Section 6, Addendum C, be amended to include quarterly testing for dissolved solids and trace metals. All in favor?”

Nobody speaks for a second—then a smile creeps around the table, because this is not the speech they were expecting, and now everyone’s awake.

**LOCAL ENGINEER** (grinning):  
“I second the motion.”

Jeff nods, tucks his pen behind his ear, leans back like a man who just dropped the best solo of his life. Minutes later, he’s scribbling the vote tally, not looking up as someone whispers,  “I thought he was the singer. Or the leader. Or…”

Someone else cuts in:  “He’s the one who knows what keeps you alive.”

After the meeting, the local chair shakes his hand—hard.  
“You’re not like the others. You stay for the hard part.”

**JEFF:**  
“I don’t sing. I keep time.”

**He didn’t give them a savior. He gave them a system. He gave them rules. He gave them water that will outlast him.**

And then, quietly, he caught the next flight home. Missed the press. Missed the thanks. Missed the myth.  Just left the city a little cleaner than he found it, and a country full of engineers who know exactly what “Part 131” means, and why it matters.

---

**INT. CREMEANS’ OFFICE – LATE SUMMER, 2010 – EVENING**

Cremeans sits at his desk, pen poised over a tour schedule, phone pressed to his ear.  
Jeff is on the other end, line fuzzy, a riot of horns and traffic in the background.

**CREMEANS:**  
“Jeff, you’re in Ghana again, aren’t you?”

**JEFF:**  
“Board of directors meeting. Gotta vote on a new pump system.”

**CREMEANS:**  
“You run a water charity now? What the hell do you know about wells?”

**JEFF:**  
“I know you start at the bottom, you get the right investors, you hire local. You don’t cut corners. Roberts Rules of Order, Cremeans. There’s a whole process.”

**CREMEANS:**  (sighs)  
“I thought you hated bureaucracy.”

**JEFF:**  
“I do. That’s why I run it.”

**CREMEANS:**  
“Just don’t forget we have load-in at Union Transfer next week.”

**JEFF:**  
“I’ll be there. Unless the water’s dirty again.”

**CREMEANS:**  (mutters, but smiling)  
“Saint Evans of the Chlorinator. I’ll light a candle.”

*Jeff hangs up, and for a minute, Cremeans just stares at the receiver, grinning like a man who knows he’ll never get the full story out of him. Nobody ever does.*

**In Accra, ten years in:**  The Trust is solvent, the systems are robust, and the community votes him honorary chairman every year—he declines the title, but always brings a new notebook and a crate of school supplies for the neighborhood kids.

There are rumors, of course. A few band superfans on Ghanaian music blogs connect the dots, whispering that “Evans” is the foreigner who built the pipes.  But in the U.S., the only clue is Jeff’s annual  late August radio silence, and his stubborn refusal to play anywhere but clean venues when he’s back in Ohio.

**Bill once asks him, drunk, late night:**  
“Why Accra? What’s left for you there?”

**Jeff shrugs:**  
“The water’s better now.”

**And that’s all he’ll say.**

---

**Weapons grade autism**—oh, yes. Jeff Evans, diagnostic codes be damned, runs on a kind of focus that makes other overachievers look like napping cats. Give him a regulatory loophole, a missed test, a false positive on manganese, and he goes nuclear. He’s not there for the photo ops. He’s there for the war.

**INT. ACCRA – DISTRICT WATER BOARD – YEAR TWO, THE INCIDENT**

The meeting is supposed to be routine. The city’s mayor shows up for his annual handshake, some low-grade pomp and circumstance—maybe a chance to snag a photo with “the American musician” for local TV. Jeff, already three days into jet lag, with a migraine and half a notebook’s worth of water quality readings, sits through the pleasantries with the patience of a shark in a koi pond.

Then he sees the numbers.  He checks the printout, runs the math again, and feels that cold, surgical click behind his eyes—the autistic snap where the world narrows to *problem* and *solution*.

The mayor starts his speech, beaming: “We’re proud of our progress. The system is working. Clean water for all.”

Jeff interrupts. Doesn’t raise his hand. Doesn’t wait for the pause.

**JEFF:**  
“Excuse me, Mr. Mayor. According to these readings, your section C output last quarter exceeded the EPA maximum for lead by 18 micrograms per liter. That’s a violation of both local and international standards.”

The mayor, taken aback, tries to laugh it off.  
“We’re making improvements—”

**JEFF (voice cold, loud, unstoppable):**  
“No, you’re making excuses. This isn’t just a ‘progress update.’ You’re serving poison. And if you keep hiding the test results, I’ll make sure every funding partner, every health ministry, every fucking news station in the capital knows you tried to pass off half-assed compliance as public safety.”

The room is dead quiet.  The mayor tries to protest, sweating now, looking for an ally.  
Jeff flips the EPA manual open, slaps it on the table.

**JEFF:**  
“This is not negotiable. You fix the intake, replace the filters, and rerun the tests by next month. Or I pull the plug. On everything. And I do it in writing. Roberts Rules of Order, Section 14: If you can’t prove compliance, you’re out.”

Someone tries to calm him down. It doesn’t work.  Jeff stands, all angles and fury, that surgical focus cutting through the posturing like a knife.

**JEFF:**  
“You think this is about charity? This is about survival. You’re not getting away with this while I’m on the board. Second the motion, someone. Or I’ll second myself.”

Local engineer—head down, hiding a smile—seconds it.  The motion passes. The mayor, humiliated, storms out.

Minutes later, Jeff is alone in the boardroom, hand shaking just a little, the adrenaline making his writing nearly illegible as he enters the new compliance schedule into the minutes.

He texts Cremeans from the airport:  **“Had to light a fire. Back in two days. Don’t move my amp.”**

**Weapons grade. Not just autism. Accountability. No mercy.**  And when the water runs clean a month later, nobody gives him a parade.  He doesn’t want one. All he wants is the next set of test results.  And maybe, if he’s honest, a city that never needs him again.
