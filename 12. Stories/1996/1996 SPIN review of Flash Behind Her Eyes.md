**FLASH BEHIND HER EYES**  
_Commuted_  
**(Island, 1996) ★★★★½**

By the time Commuted dropped their fourth album this spring, the Midwest quartet had already established themselves as genre-agnostic provocateurs with a cult following among college radio programmers who weren't afraid of the FCC. But _Flash Behind Her Eyes_ feels like the moment where all that restless experimentation—the Irish rock detours, the synthwave flirtations, the post-hardcore aggression—finally coheres into something dangerous and fully realized.

This is an album about female desire that refuses every available script. Not the coy innuendo of mainstream pop, not the riot grrrl rage-against-patriarchy stance, not even the confessional singer-songwriter vulnerability that's been Lilith Fair's stock in trade. Instead, lead vocalist/lyricist Kathy Espich delivers seventeen snapshots of lust, agency, ambivalence, and aftermath with a bluntness that would make Liz Phair blush. When she sings "*I grow my own power, on my back at my place*" on opener "*Manchester Hall,*" she's claiming territory that rock music—even alt-rock—rarely lets women occupy without judgment.

The sonic palette is gloriously unhinged. "*Speeding*" pairs "Cincinnati bass" (whatever unholy frequencies that means) with American Primitivism guitar work, creating a highway sexual encounter that's equal parts **Sonic Youth** and **John Fahey**. "*Cuyahoga Valley Scenic Railroad*" slams glam metal against electronica with post-hardcore fury—a song about fucking by Lake Erie that sounds like if Helmet covered Garbage. The crossover hit "*County Fair Confession*" works precisely because it smuggles its blue-ribbon-breast-contest narrative inside propulsive Southern Rock that could slide onto CMT if programmers weren't actually listening to the words.

But the album's true genius emerges in its second half, where the confidence curdles into something more complex. "*Decisions, Decisions*" introduces doubt with its electric banjo and jail-cell imagery. "*Gasping For Clarity*" finds its narrator "shuddering violently" on a well-worn sofa, anxiety tugging at pleasure's edges. By the time we reach "*Why Her and Not Me?*"—the 5:55 epic that's already a fan favorite—we're deep in messy emotional territory, a "seven o'clock confession" where sexual power games intersect with genuine heartbreak.

And then there's "*Despair's Own Hand*," the album's deranged closer. Over dark acid house rhythms, Kathy confronts apocalypse and mortality mid-coitus: "*When did lust become despair's own hand?*" It's genuinely unsettling, the sonic equivalent of staring into an abyss that's also an orgasm. Peak Bill Davenport, indeed.

What makes _Flash Behind Her Eyes_ more than just a provocative stunt is how it refuses to moralize about its narrator's choices. She's proud and uncertain, empowered and trapped, joyful and afraid—sometimes within the same song. The album never asks whether she _should_ be doing any of this; it simply documents what it feels like when you do. In an era when female sexuality in rock still largely exists to be either celebrated or punished, Commuted's refusal to choose either path feels genuinely radical.

The production occasionally overreaches—do we really need both synthwave AND glam metal in the same track?—and some of Jeremy Evans's guitar solos meander when they should strike. But those feel like quibbles when held against an album this fearless and this fully inhabited.

_Flash Behind Her Eyes_ probably will crack the Billboard 200 wide open, but MTV will never touch some of it. But in twenty years, people will cite this as the moment someone finally said the quiet part loud about what women actually think about while everyone else was still parsing Alanis metaphors. Commuted has made an album that sounds like nothing else in 1996, and said things that still aren't supposed to be said. That's worth four and a half stars in any universe.

**RIYL:** Liz Phair's _Exile in Guyville_, PJ Harvey's _Rid of Me_, the sexual frankness you wish more bands had the guts to attempt.
