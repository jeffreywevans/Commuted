# **Cremeans Doubts**

This is why [[5.2. JEFF CREMEANS MODERN REVIEW|Cremeans]] prays. He is well aware of his own shortcomings and his own sins, and completely believes that he is a sinner who is hellbound absent the purchase of his soul by Christ Jesus' death on the cross. 

Because his faith isn’t posturing.  It isn’t PR.  It isn’t a costume he wears to be the “good guy” in the band’s orbit.

It’s the one place he tells the truth.

He prays because he knows what he’s capable of.  He prays because he believes he deserves hell.  And he clings to grace not as comfort—but as desperation.

That means every quiet word, every bowed head, every still moment with him at a breakfast table or backstage or next to a dying friend is charged.  Not with smugness.  
With surrender.

And here’s what’s devastating:  He may be the only one in the band who believes he’s actually not worthy.  And that is where the real holiness lives.

By the time the flight left Oslo, Cremeans had already counted the lies he wouldn’t tell.

## **INT. FIRST CLASS CABIN – NIGHT**  
A hush rides the cabin like a sermon half-whispered. Soft blue overhead lights. A glass of still water, untouched. Cremeans doesn’t drink anymore. Not for years.

The seat beside him is empty. The flight attendant had smiled gently when she saw his solitary name on the manifest. No one in the band flew with him this time. Jeff had chartered something separately. Madeline was staying behind to “handle optics.”

Cremeans doesn’t handle optics.  He handles silence.

He takes out the prayer book he’s had since 1992\. The spine is cracked. Dog-eared pages flutter against the air vent. One Psalm marked in yellow. Another verse scrawled in fading red ink.

> **Romans 7:19** – <font color="#ff0000">*For I do not do the good I want to do, but the evil I do not want to do—this I keep on doing.*</font>

He stares at that line for a long time.

A flight attendant stops, asks him if he needs anything. He says no, thank you, with a soft Appalachian drawl and a weary kindness. She looks at his hands. No wedding ring.

She moves on.

## **EXT. SKY – HOURS LATER**  
The plane cuts across the Atlantic like a slow confession.

## **INT. FIRST CLASS – LATER**  
Cremeans watches the map crawl west. Oslo shrinking. JFK rising. He shifts. Stretches. Then pulls out a small, worn photograph from his Bible. It’s his wife, Jen, in 1996. Laughing. One of the band’s old backstage passes around her neck. He’d kept it. Tucked away.

He stares at it.

"You were better than I deserved," he says, quietly.

He folds it shut and sets it in the seat pocket.  Then he whispers a prayer—not the kind he says when people are listening. Not the kind that sounds like strength. But the real kind.

> [!help]
> “Lord, you knew me when I was young.  
> You know what I did.  
> You watched me lie by omission.  
> You let me love her anyway.”
> 
> “You let me keep my daughters.  
> You let me bury her in peace.  
> You didn’t take my ministry.  
> And for some reason… you let me keep this job.”


He stops. He breathes. The air is dry. His eyes sting.

> [!help]
> “I don’t ask for peace.  
> I ask for memory.  
> I ask for the right to remember without shame.  
> And if I can’t have that…”


His voice breaks. Only slightly.

> [!failure]
>  “…then I ask that I not see her face the moment I open my eyes in Hell.”


The engine hums. He closes the Bible. Turns out the light. Leans his head against the window. Stares at stars he doesn’t name anymore.

## **FLASHBACK – BRIEF, SILENT**  
A hotel hallway.  A closed door.  A woman whose name he never said aloud.  One night in a life built on vows.

## **BACK TO PRESENT**  
He doesn’t cry. Not anymore.  But when he sleeps, he dreams in her voice.

## **INT. FIRST CLASS CABIN – NIGHT**

The seat beside Cremeans is empty.

The leather is soft, the cabin lights dimmed, and the whine of the engines is low, steady, familiar—like the hum of a church air conditioner during a summer sermon. He doesn’t sleep on flights. He reads. He prays. He watches the map.

From Oslo to JFK, eleven hours.  Thirty-one years since he had his one and only drink: a half glass of red wine, with his wife Jen, during the toast at Jeff’s wedding.

Jen had smiled, nudged his elbow gently, and whispered, “It’s grape juice with a promotion. One and done.”

He’d smiled back. She always had a way of making things easier. Now she was four years gone.

He adjusts the tray table slowly and opens his old, worn Bible.  The binding is soft from years of use. The page marked with a ribbon today is Psalm 51.

    “Have mercy upon me, O God, according to thy lovingkindness.”

He mouths the words silently.

The flight attendant asks if he’d like something to drink. He asks for water, then clarifies: no ice. She offers a smile, gives him space.

---
## **HOURS LATER**

He shifts in his seat. Pulls out an old photograph tucked in the back of his Bible.

Jen, in her early thirties, sitting on a picnic blanket with their girls. Sunday after church, back when he still led the men's group and she organized potlucks like military operations. She was laughing in the photo, looking off-camera, hair caught by the wind.

He closes his eyes. “You were the only thing I never second-guessed,” he says softly.

He opens his Bible again and pulls out a folded scrap of paper. A prayer list. Some names are written in thick, confident strokes. Some are shakier. Some have been prayed for so long the ink has faded.

Near the bottom, written smaller than the rest:  Forgiveness (mine)

## **INT. FIRST CLASS CABIN – LATER**

He looks at the seat beside him and whispers like it’s a confessional.  “You would’ve hated the silence, Jen. You always wanted to talk on long trips.”

He leans his head back. Not to sleep. Just to be still.  “I sinned once. Not with my hands. But with my will.  I walked past that woman’s hotel room and paused. That was enough.”

“I prayed. I fasted. I buried it deep.  I never told you. And that was the real sin.”

He opens to Romans. Reads it by heart.  “*All have sinned and come short of the glory of God*.”

He doesn’t believe grace is cheap.  He believes it was bought in blood.  Not just Christ’s.  
His own, too.

**INT. FIRST CLASS CABIN – FINAL MOMENT**

The cabin is still. Flight attendants dim the lights further. Someone snores quietly in 3C. A baby cries far away.

Cremeans bows his head.

> [!help]
> “I’m not praying to be clean.  
> I’m praying to stay faithful.  
> Because I know what I could be, Lord, if You weren’t holding me back.”


He doesn’t cry. That’s not how he grieves. He just keeps his head bowed.  Somewhere, far below, the ocean stretches black and endless.  Above, stars.  Inside, peace.  And a page still marked with a ribbon.

**INT. FIRST CLASS CABIN – DESCENT**

Cremeans watches the seatbelt sign flick on. He doesn't move.

The plane starts to descend, and with it comes that familiar tightness in his chest. Not fear. Not turbulence. Conviction.

Stephen, Lord… you know I’ve thought about him more than I should.  Not because I want to be brave.  But because I wonder if I’d have stood there, like Saul, and said nothing.

His hands rest folded over his Bible. It’s not open. It doesn’t need to be. He knows the words.

*“And Saul was consenting unto his death.”*

He thinks about every moment in thirty years when he looked the other way. When he let Jeff walk into pain alone. When he said nothing to the label execs. When he stayed just silent enough to preserve the band… but not the truth.

“I didn’t throw the stones, Lord.  But I passed out the cloaks.”

The landing gear hums beneath him.

“Don’t call me a martyr. I’m barely a servant.  I just want to get home without lying again.”

---
## **EXT. JFK – NIGHT**

The cab smells like vinegar and old gum. The driver doesn't talk much, which Cremeans appreciates. The skyline appears in the windshield—sharp and wide and glimmering, like temptation poured in steel and neon.

He’s supposed to go to the hotel. It's just a few blocks from where Sarah died. Just a few miles from the studio where Avril nearly did. The band thinks he’s in town to stabilize things.  He knows he’s here to see what’s left of himself.

The cab turns off FDR Drive. The driver asks if he wants to reroute due to traffic. Cremeans opens his mouth to say no.

Instead—“89 Broadway.”

**DRIVER**:  
“Church?”

Cremeans nods once.  In a storm, any port will do.

## **EXT. 89 BROADWAY – TWENTY MINUTES LATER**

It’s not Baptist. It’s Episcopalian. Stained glass. Ivy. Cold steps. He doesn’t flinch. He’s not here for the label on the door.  He’s here because his soul is rattling in his ribcage.

Inside, the church is dim, quiet, and open. No one greets him. No one needs to. He finds the fifth pew from the front. Sits with his hands in his lap. Doesn’t take off his coat.

There’s no music.  No sermon.  Just silence. He doesn’t kneel. That’s not his habit.  But his head bows.

“Lord, I don’t need clarity.  I need ballast.”

“This city’s loud, and I’m tired.  The people I love keep trying to set themselves on fire.”

“If I see another executive try to sell pain as art, I may throw something.”

“Keep me from saying what I want to say.  And let me stay in the background long enough to do some good.”

He looks up at the cross. It’s ornate. Not his taste.  But it’s still the cross.  And that’s enough.

“Jesus… you already bought me. I remember that.  I just forget what that’s supposed to feel like sometimes.”

His fingers tighten around the wooden rail in front of him.  He whispers, almost angrily:

“I want to go the rest of my life without disappointing anyone else.  I know I won’t.  But I want to.”

## **INT. CHURCH – LATER**

He lights a candle.

He doesn’t believe it carries his prayer anywhere.  But he believes it reminds him that he has one. He leaves nothing behind in the pew. But something in him stays there.

## **INT. HOTEL SUITE – JUST BEFORE DAWN**

[[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff]] Evans sits by the floor-to-ceiling window of his downtown Manhattan hotel room. The lights are low. A mug of black coffee sits untouched on the sill. His acoustic guitar leans quietly against the far wall. He hasn’t touched it yet this morning.

Outside, the city shifts.  Streetlights blink.  Steam hisses from vents.  Garbage trucks make their declarations.  Somewhere, a jogger cuts through the pre-dawn mist like time means something again.

Jeff rubs his temples, then his jaw. He’s barely slept. Didn’t even try.  He just watched the skyline inch toward color, like a bruise healing in reverse.

“This damn city,” he mutters. “Doesn’t sleep. Doesn’t let me either.”

His phone buzzes—two unread messages. One from Madeline. One from Jeremy. He ignores both.  Instead, he presses his forehead against the cool glass.  And watches.

A coffee cart opens its shutter. A doorman stretches on the stoop.  Some guy in a Mets hoodie yells at a cab.  And Jeff breathes it all in.

He doesn’t feel ready.  He doesn’t feel rested.  But the band is in town. The press is waiting. The album is done. The label’s teetering. The wounds are still raw. And none of that gives a damn about his fatigue.

He thinks about Cremeans.  About Jodi.  About Kathy, always. And then he mutters: “Time to go be the dickhead again.”

He stands. Cracks his neck.  Looks out once more at the city as it slips into full roar.

“Alright,” he whispers to himself. “Let’s see if I remember how.”

## **INT. HOTEL LOBBY – MORNING**

Jeff’s coming back from his walk. Coffee in hand, hoodie pulled low over his eyes, guitar pick still in his pocket from the night before. He’s not ready to speak to anyone yet. Least of all himself. The elevator *dings*.

Cremeans steps out.

Bible tucked under his arm.  Still in yesterday’s pressed shirt.  Eyes red—not from tears, but from wrestling with God in an unfamiliar city. They stop when they see each other.  Two men. Thirty years of history in a half-second of stillness.

**JEFF:** (mildly surprised)  
Cremeans?

**CREMEANS:**  (blinking)  
I’ll be. Didn’t know you were here.

**JEFF:**  
Same. I thought you were up in midtown.

**CREMEANS:**  
Was.  
(long pause)  
Detoured.

They both know that word means more than geography.

**JEFF:** (sips coffee)  
Get any sleep?

**CREMEANS:**  (shakes his head)  
Didn’t ask for any.

They stand in silence for a moment. The lobby bustles around them—guests checking out, staff wheeling carts, the world moving too fast.

**JEFF:**  
I watched the sun come up. Thought it’d mean something.  Didn’t.

**CREMEANS:**  
Didn’t have to. You showed up to see it. That’s enough for today.

Jeff exhales through his nose—half amusement, half fatigue.

**JEFF:**  
You always this optimistic before breakfast?

**CREMEANS:**  
Only after prayer and stubbornness.

Another beat.

**JEFF:**  
You want coffee?

**CREMEANS:**  
If it’s not a metaphor.

**JEFF:**  
It can be both.

## **INT. HOTEL CAFÉ – MOMENTS LATER**

They sit in a quiet corner. No band, no media, no noise.  Two men. Two burdens.  One table.  One fragile peace. Not to fix anything.  Not to explain anything.

## **INT. HOTEL CAFÉ – LATE MORNING**

The coffee is warm. The room is quiet. The world is moving again outside, but here it’s suspended—just for a moment.  Jeff leans forward, elbows on the table.  Cremeans stirs his coffee, even though he takes it black. Just something to do with his hands.

**JEFF:**  
I was thinking about Kathy this morning.  Didn’t want to.  Did anyway.

Cremeans nods, not speaking yet.

**JEFF (CONT'D):**  
She’d hate most of this.  The awards. The circus.  Avril.  Hell, probably me not consummating with Avril.

**CREMEANS:**  
Nah.  She’d love you.  Hate how you punish yourself.

Jeff looks down, smiles wryly.

**JEFF:**  
I keep thinking if I carry it long enough, it makes it worth it.

Cremeans finally speaks, voice quiet and rough.

**CREMEANS**:  
You ever carry something so long, you forget it was never yours to hold?

Jeff looks up. Their eyes meet.

**JEFF**:  
Sarah?

**CREMEANS**: (matter-of-fact)  
Every day.

He takes a breath.

**CREMEANS (CONT'D):**  
I spent so long trying to be the one who held it all together…Turns out I was just good at hiding the cracks.

**JEFF:**  
Nobody saw it.  I didn’t.

**CREMEANS:**  
That’s the part that still keeps me up.  Not the sin.  The silence.

They sit in it for a while. The weight. The memory. The ghosts.

**JEFF:**  
She died thinking you were whole?

**CREMEANS:**  
She died believing in grace.  I’m still trying to catch up.

Another pause.

**JEFF:**  
That night she died…I played her voicemail twice.  She told me she loved you more than anything.  Said she was proud. Of how you held her. Of how you stayed.

Cremeans blinks quickly. Looks away. Rubs his thumb along the rim of the cup.

**CREMEANS:**  
I don’t deserve that.

**JEFF:**  
None of us do.  That’s why it means something.

They sit quietly. Two men. Not broken. Not whole.

## **INT. HOTEL CAFÉ – CONTINUED**

The clink of ceramic. The low hum of the city outside. A breeze touches the curtain by the window.

Cremeans sits with his fingers steepled, staring into the swirl of his cooling coffee. Jeff watches him a long moment before speaking again, voice low and even.

**JEFF:**  
We bought her a jukebox.  Custom-made. Not retro junk—real build. Steel, weight, sound you can feel in your ribs.

Cremeans blinks. Looks up slowly.

**JEFF (CONT'D):**  
Me and Bill filled it with every track she ever smiled at.  Songs she sang in college. Choir stuff. Hair metal. Gospel. A few real bad country songs we let slide. Fuckin' Garth Brooks.  
Even found some garbage from those cassette mixes she made in the '90s.

**CREMEANS:** (choked laugh)  
She still like that Prince bootleg?

**JEFF:**  
Had it on loop her last week. Said if the morphine kicked in hard enough, he’d show up in person.

They both smile—small, real.

**JEFF (CONT'D):**  
Peaches. For breakfast. Every day.  Said if God made anything better, He kept it in heaven.

Cremeans exhales, half-laugh, half-lament.

**JEFF (CONT'D):**  
And sex. Every day, till she couldn’t.

Cremeans looks at him. There’s no judgment. Just a deep, aching respect.

**JEFF (CONT'D):**  
It wasn’t graceful. Or sad. Or poetic.  It was weird. And funny. And loud sometimes.  
But I watched it. I stayed.  Held her hand when the music stopped.

Silence.

Cremeans nods once. Then again.

**CREMEANS:**  
That’s what love is.  It ain’t about staying clean.  It’s about staying in the room.

Jeff leans back, eyes glassy but unbroken.

**JEFF:**  
Sometimes I think that was the last real thing I ever did.  The rest of this winter has been noise.

Cremeans reaches over—not for drama, not for show—and sets his hand over Jeff’s forearm.

**CREMEANS:**  
Then let the noise be in her key.

A beat.

Jeff nods. And there it is.  Two men—grieving, faithful in their own ways, bound by music, memory, and grace.  Not redeemed.  But walking toward it.

## **INT. HOTEL CAFÉ – STILL MORNING**

The sunlight slants sharper now, tracing long shadows across the table. The city’s fully awake. Neither of them flinches.  Jeff stares at the last sip of his coffee. Cold. Forgotten.

Cremeans breaks the silence, quiet but certain:

**CREMEANS:**  
I’ve lost faith in the job.

Jeff looks up.

**CREMEANS (CONT’D):**  
Not the Lord. Not the call.  But this machine. The thing we built. The thing we keep feeding like it matters.

He exhales slowly. Like he’s rehearsed this.

**CREMEANS (CONT’D):**  
There’s too much noise. Too much pain being polished and sold.  And I’m tired, Jeff.  
Tired in the soul.

**JEFF:** (quietly)  
You thinking about walking?

Cremeans nods.

**CREMEANS:**  
I’ve got enough money to disappear ten different ways.  Could vanish clean. Build houses. Go fix up a Bible college. Or just sit in a quiet place and wait for my grandkids to ask me why I ever thought rock and roll mattered.

Jeff takes a long breath. Stares out the window.

**JEFF:**  
You think you’re done because you lost the mission?  Or because you lost the audience?

Cremeans doesn’t answer at first.

Then:

**CREMEANS:**  
I think I’m done because…I’ve seen what we all look like up close.  And I’m still not sure if we saved anybody.

Jeff shifts in his seat. Not angry. Not surprised.

**JEFF:**  
You saved me.  A couple times.

Cremeans meets his eyes. He nods once. A thank you, without needing the word.

**CREMEANS:**  
I’m not asking permission.  I’m just… saying it out loud. For the first time.

**JEFF:**  
So what now?

**CREMEANS**:  
Don’t know.  Maybe I don’t leave tomorrow. Maybe I leave at the end of the tour.  Maybe I leave when I know you’re steady.

Jeff laughs softly—dark and knowing.

**JEFF:**  
So never, then?

Cremeans grins. Then sighs.

**CREMEANS:**  
We’ll see.  But if I go…I want to go out, not bitter.  Not cynical.  Just…clean.

They sit with that.

**CREMEANS (CONT'D):**  
I don't enjoy cutting my lawn anymore.  I don't get peace volunteering cutting the lawn at my church, either.

## **INT. HOTEL CAFÉ – MOMENTS LATER**

Jeff sits straighter, suddenly alert. Not angry yet—concerned. The way you get when someone who’s always been a constant starts speaking in past tense.

**JEFF:**  
Hold up. You’re saying you’re not even getting peace cutting your own lawn?

**CREMEANS:** (nods)  
Not even at church.  That little tractor used to be the only time I felt useful without talking.  
Now it’s just noise.  Another task. Another silence I can’t fill.

Jeff frowns, his voice low, steady, but there’s fire under it.

**JEFF:**  
You mean to tell me…You’re this close to walking out of the only thing we ever built that lasted, and the thing replacing it is yard work?

**CREMEANS:**  (slight smile)  
Didn’t say I had a plan.

Jeff’s eyes flash.

**JEFF:**  
This isn’t about a plan.  This is like a captain taking one last walk across his ship before they scuttle it.  And you’re not even waiting to hear if the enemy’s still out there.

**CREMEANS:**  
Jeff—

**JEFF (CONT’D):**  
Don’t “Jeff” me.  You check out now?  You’re not just leaving a stage.  You’re leaving us on that boat.

Cremeans looks down. Folds his hands. The silence says he knows Jeff’s not wrong.

**CREMEANS:**  
I didn’t come to burn it all down.  Just…to see if it was okay to stop carrying it.

Jeff breathes hard through his nose. He pushes his coffee away.

**JEFF:**  
You can stop travelling with us. Hell, you can stop managing.  But if you walk away from the work, the real work, you’re gonna be miserable.  You weren’t built to sit still. You were built to serve.

Cremeans looks away, jaw tight.

**CREMEANS:**  
Maybe I’m just tired of being everybody’s conscience.

**JEFF:**  
No.  You’re tired because you’ve been trying to carry the cross and the stage at the same time. But you don’t have to disappear to lay one down.

Another pause. Jeff softens.

**JEFF (CONT’D):**  
You’ve been our shepherd for thirty years.  Just because the flock bit you a few times doesn’t mean the call’s gone.

Cremeans closes his eyes. For a second, he looks like a man on the edge of tears. But he isn’t. It’s just too heavy to cry.

**CREMEANS:**  
If I stay…I need to know it still matters.

Jeff leans in.

**JEFF:**  
Then stay long enough to see it does.

## **INT. HOTEL CAFÉ – CONTINUED**

Always liked that about him.

A beat.

**JEFF (CONT’D):**  
You stepping away?  That’s your call.  But don’t rewrite the history in your head.

I didn’t always know what I was doing.

No one does. But you made us believe we could pull it off.  You gave us a compass when the money started flying and the ground started shaking.

**CREMEANS:**  
You think it’s okay if I don’t want to be the compass anymore?

Jeff sits back. Looks out the window. The city roars.

You’ve earned the right to rest.  Just don’t forget, you’re still on the ship.  Even if you hand off the wheel.
