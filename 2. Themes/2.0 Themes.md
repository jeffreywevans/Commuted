## Core Themes of *Commuted*

**1. Fame corrupts differently depending on gender.**
[[4.2 JEREMY GILLEY character bible|Gilley]] can be a mess and still be celebrated. [[4.5 KATHY ESPICH character bible|Kathy]] has to be perfect just to be tolerated. Male dysfunction is seen as part of the package; female power is seen as a threat. The show interrogates what success costs when you're not born with permission.

**2. Identity is forged under pressure.**
The garage isn't just a place — it's a crucible. Each character enters with a version of themselves and is forced to confront whether that version can survive, evolve, or be destroyed. Authenticity isn't discovered. It's hammered out under heat.

**3. Friendship is not unconditional.**
The band begins with shared passion but fractures under ego, jealousy, and betrayal. Loyalty is negotiable. Bonds are tested. The show asks: do they love each other, or just need each other to be heard?

**4. Sex is power. And a weapon. And a currency.**
Characters trade, withhold, weaponize, and misunderstand sex constantly. Consent and autonomy are not always clear. The show doesn't moralize — it exposes. The erotic is never clean; it's a language with too many dialects.

**5. Art is rebellion — and responsibility.**
Every show, every song, every riff is a statement of survival, rage, hope, or vengeance. But once you create something, you no longer control what it means. Art changes the artist and the audience — sometimes brutally.

**6. Silence speaks volumes.**
The adults say little, but loom large. Mr. Gilley, in particular, represents a generational pressure valve — the voice of disappointment, resigned wisdom and ultimate approbation. In a world of noise, what isn't said can define everything.
