# **How to add external link in Obsidian**

In Obsidian, you add external links with the same Markdown syntax used elsewhere, just with the full URL. There are a couple of ways to do it:

---

**1. Basic inline link:**

```markdown
[Google](https://www.google.com)
```

That will show up as **Google**, clickable, and open in your browser.

---

**2. Naked link:**

```markdown
https://www.google.com
```

Obsidian will auto-detect it and make it clickable.

---

**3. With a title/tooltip (hover text):**

```markdown
[Search Engine](https://www.google.com "Click to open Google")
```

When you hover over it, the tooltip pops up.

---

**4. If you want it to open in the default browser instead of inside Obsidian:**

Go to **Settings → Files & Links → External Links** and set it to “Open in default browser.” Otherwise, some may open inside Obsidian’s own window.

A fun trick: you can even link to a specific heading in a web page by copying the URL fragment (like `#section-title`) if the site supports it.

---

Since you asked, here are some link hacks in Obsidian that might scratch your power-user itch:

- **Alias links:** You can make an internal link display however you want.

  ```markdown
  [[MyNote|Cool Display Text]]
  ```

  That links to *MyNote* but shows “Cool Display Text.” Great for weaving citations into prose without looking like a database nerd.

- **Links with queries:** If you use the [Dataview](https://blacksmithgu.github.io/obsidian-dataview/) plugin, you can link to dynamic tables or lists that auto-update, so one link can show “every note tagged #Kathy.” Pure sorcery.

- **Callouts that *contain* links:**
-
  ```markdown
  > [!info] Reference
  > Check this [interview](https://example.com) for context.
  ```
 
  Makes it look slick, like you’re running your own wiki.

- **Hidden embeds:** You can embed external content like this:

  ```markdown
  ![Google](https://www.google.com/)
  ```

  Which basically pulls in little icons or images right next to your text.

The danger is falling down the rabbit hole of “optimizing” instead of writing. The best hack of all is knowing when to just slap the link in and keep moving.

---

**1. Faux liner notes inside a scene**
You can make your draft read like the band is holding the actual insert from the CD booklet.

```markdown
> [!quote] *Liner Notes – 2002 Pressing*
> ![[Kathy-Lyrics-Draft.jpg]]
> "Recorded in a week of sweat, blood, and no air-conditioning. Dedicated to the girls who taught us the cost of looking cool under stage lights."
```

Now your writing looks like it’s interrupting itself with ephemera. It’s a prop that *feels real*.

---

**2. Embedding album covers as chapter headers**

```markdown
# Face Down, Ass Up (2002)
![[FDAU-Cover.png]]
```

Every time you transition to a new album-era scene, you drop the cover art right into the note. It anchors the mood visually, just like flipping a record sleeve.

---

**3. Scene-appropriate photos**
Draft a scene in Tokyo ’94? Inline a street photo. Drafting in OTR in 1998? Drop a burned-out storefront image.

```markdown
![[Cincinnati-OTR-1998.jpg]]
INT. BILL’S STUDIO – OVER-THE-RHINE – NIGHT
The radiator ticks like a metronome set too slow.
```

Suddenly your scene isn’t just described—it’s collaged.

---

**4. Artifacts as narrative misdirection**
The real mind-bender: embed fake but “archival” looking material mid-story. Like a page of Kathy’s diary, a bootleg setlist, a court transcript. The reader in Obsidian (or you, rereading later) gets jolted: *wait, am I reading fiction or a recovered document?*

```markdown
![[Bootleg-Setlist-1997.png]]
```

Followed by the scene: *“Jeff swears they never played ‘Thirty-six Degrees Fahrenheit’ that night. The setlist proves otherwise.”*

---

That’s where you stop just writing and start world-building with props. And because it’s your vault, you don’t even need the artifact to be polished—just the presence of it embedded changes the brain’s reception.
