## **Subject: I’m Done**

Jeff, Jeremy, Jim—

This isn’t some feel-good farewell. I’ve had it. I’m sick of the bullshit, the egos, and the circus this band has become.

[[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff]], let’s start with you. Every goddamn decision revolves around your dick. [[4.5 KATHY ESPICH character bible|Kathy]], Ha-Yoon, Jodi, Kirsten, and whoever comes next—you’ve turned our band into a revolving door of drama because you can’t keep it in your pants. We used to be about music, about pushing boundaries, about fucking art. Now? It’s just damage control after your latest meltdown.

[[4.6 JEREMY EVANS character bible|Jeremy]], you’re too busy chasing ghosts of past glories to notice how stale we’ve gotten. You call it “preserving the legacy.” I call it refusing to move the hell on. You’re clinging to songs we wrote 30 years ago like they’re life rafts, and it’s sinking the whole ship.

And [[4.4 JIM BROWN character bible|Jim]]…Christ, Jim. You’re the glue holding this mess together, but maybe you should stop pretending everything’s fine. Your silence makes you complicit.

You know what kills me? I love this band. I gave everything to Commuted—every sleepless night, every broken relationship, every ounce of creativity I had. And what’s left? A hollowed-out version of what we used to be, with me trying to hold together a foundation that’s crumbling because nobody else gives a damn.

I’ve been working on my solo projects, and you know what? They remind me why I fell in love with music in the first place. No bullshit. No drama. Just creating something pure, something that matters. And that’s what I need now. Not this endless spiral of self-destruction.

So, I’m done. I’m stepping back. Figure it out without me.

[[4.1 BILL DAVENPORT character bible|Bill]]

## **Scene: No Carrier**

Bill sits in the middle of nowhere, surrounded by towering pines and the faint hum of a nearby creek. He’s perched on the tailgate of his truck, laptop balanced on his knees, typing furiously. The crisp mountain air and absolute silence contrast with the heat and venom in his words.

When he finishes typing, he breathes heavily, staring at the “Send” button. He clicks it—and nothing happens. A little red notification pops up in the corner: “No internet connection.”

He stares at the screen, curses under his breath, and slams the laptop shut. The frustration lingers, but so does a flicker of relief. The email becomes a weight he chooses not to discard but to carry as a reminder—a scar from a moment when he almost walked away.

## **Scene: Tall Trees**

Bill slammed the tailgate shut, the metallic thud echoing in the stillness of the forest. He tossed his laptop bag onto the passenger seat of his dusty SUV and climbed in, his boots grinding against the worn floor mats. The engine roared to life with a low growl, the sound breaking the tranquil silence.

He pulled onto the narrow gravel road, tires crunching against the rocks, and eased into the winding path. Towering evergreens flanked both sides of the road, their dense canopy blotting out much of the midday sun. The light filtered through in fractured beams, painting the dashboard with flickers of green and gold.

Bill gripped the steering wheel tight, his knuckles pale. The words he had written echoed in his head. “Hollowed-out version of what we used to be.” He bit the inside of his cheek. Why did saying the truth always feel like swallowing glass?

The road curved and climbed, revealing glimpses of moss-covered rocks and occasional breaks where the forest opened to distant ridges. The trees whispered in the breeze, their rhythmic sway a stark contrast to the storm still raging in Bill’s chest.

“This place should calm me down,” he muttered, his voice breaking the spell of silence. He glanced out the window at the endless green. It was the kind of green that seemed to stretch on forever, vibrant and alive. He used to come here to think, to write music when the band’s chaos became too much.

But today, the forest wasn’t enough to quiet his mind. His thoughts circled back to Jeff, to his unchecked arrogance, to the way he bulldozed through everything—relationships, decisions, respect. It wasn’t fair. It wasn’t right. And yet, somehow, they always let him get away with it.

Bill exhaled sharply, the tension easing just slightly. The forest had a way of doing that, sneaking in under your anger like a balm. He turned down the volume on his inner monologue and focused on the road ahead.

The trees blurred together as he picked up speed on a straight stretch, the hum of the tires and the engine filling the void left by his silence. He thought about the resignation email again, his chest tightening. He’d meant every damn word. But the more he thought about it, the more it felt like a declaration made in anger, not truth.

Maybe he’d send it. Maybe he wouldn’t. The forest didn’t care, and for some reason, that was comforting.

As the road began to descend, the sunlight grew stronger, breaking through the canopy in patches. He spotted the familiar bend in the road ahead. Around the curve, there’d be the old country store—a squat, weathered building with a hand-painted sign advertising ice, beer, and “World’s Best Jerky.”

And, if he remembered right, a 5G signal.

He slowed as the store came into view, its gravel lot empty except for a battered pickup parked near the entrance. The sight was almost enough to make him smile. Almost.

Bill turned into the lot and killed the engine, the silence rushing back in. He sat there for a moment, gripping the wheel, letting the forest breathe around him. Maybe the trees had done their work after all.

Or maybe they just gave him the space to do it himself.

## **Scene: Americana**

The screen door creaked and slammed shut behind Bill as he stepped into the store. The smell of cedar and faintly stale chips greeted him, mingling with the cool bite of the air conditioning. The fluorescent lights buzzed softly, and a radio tuned to a classic rock station played low in the background.

Behind the counter, the store’s owner, Maxim, looked up from his crossword puzzle. A wiry man in his sixties, with a neatly trimmed white beard and a trucker cap that said Timberline Supply Co., Maxim’s sharp blue eyes widened briefly before he composed himself.

“Afternoon, Bill,” Maxim said casually, setting down his pen. “Good to see you again.”

Bill nodded, his lips twitching into the faintest of smiles. “Maxim.” He walked past the counter toward the back, where a small refrigerator hummed softly, stocked with local brews.

Maxim leaned on the counter, watching Bill with a mix of respect and concern. He never pried. That was why Bill kept coming back. In this little pocket of Oregon, he wasn’t a rock star or a sound engineer extraordinaire. He was just a guy who liked beer, jerky, and quiet.

Bill grabbed a six-pack of a pale ale from Fort George Brewery, the condensation already cool against his fingers. He moved to the aisle with snacks, finding his usual bag of peppered beef jerky, and made his way to the ice chest near the door.

As he hefted a bag of ice onto the counter, Maxim tapped the six-pack with a knowing grin. “Good choice. That brewery’s been killing it lately.”

Bill snorted softly. “Yeah, well, it pairs well with jerky and staring at trees.”

Maxim chuckled, ringing up the items with practiced ease. “You camping out at your usual spot?”

Bill hesitated, then nodded. “Yeah. Needed to clear my head.”

Maxim handed him a receipt but didn’t let go of it right away. He looked Bill square in the eye, his tone gentle. “You know, whatever’s eating at you, this place has a way of putting things in perspective.”

Bill blinked, taken aback by the sudden earnestness. “What makes you think something’s eating at me?”

Maxim raised an eyebrow. “Son, I’ve been running this store for thirty years. Seen all kinds walk through that door. You’ve got that look—you’re carrying something heavy.”

Bill exhaled, leaning on the counter for a moment. “Yeah. I guess I am.” He looked out the window, where his SUV sat dusted with road grime, parked against a backdrop of endless pines. “Sometimes I wonder why I keep doing it. The band, the music, all of it. Feels like I’m holding something together that’s already broken.”

Maxim nodded slowly, scratching his beard. “Maybe it’s not about holding it together. Maybe it’s about finding a new way to make it work. You wouldn’t keep coming back to these woods if you weren’t looking for something—peace, purpose, whatever it is.”

Bill stared at him, the words sinking in. “You ever think about writing fortune cookies?”

Maxim barked a laugh. “Nah, too many rules. I like keeping it simple. But seriously, whatever you decide, you’ve got this place. No one’s going to bother you here.”

Bill slid the receipt into his pocket, his fingers brushing against his wallet. He nodded, a flicker of gratitude passing across his face. “Thanks, Maxim.”

“Anytime.”

As Bill pushed open the door, the screen creaking again, Maxim called after him. “And hey—if you find some peace out there, let me know where to look.”

Bill didn’t answer, but the faint smile that touched his lips as he walked to his SUV said enough.

## **Scene: Failure to Send**

The tires kicked up small clouds of dust as Bill turned back onto the gravel road, the six-pack of pale ale and the bag of beef jerky secure on the passenger seat. The ice, already sweating inside its plastic, sat wedged in the cooler in the back. The forest enveloped him again, the road winding through a sea of green that stretched endlessly toward the horizon.

He tapped his fingers on the steering wheel, staring straight ahead. The email lingered in his mind like a half-remembered song. He replayed the words he’d written, the anger behind them simmering just beneath the surface.

“Hollowed-out version of what we used to be.” His own phrase echoed, sharp and bitter. It felt true when he wrote it, but now? Now it felt like a punch he’d thrown in the heat of a fight, only to regret it later.

He shook his head, his jaw tightening. “Why didn’t I send it?” he muttered, the hum of the engine and the crunch of gravel the only response.

Because you don’t really want to, a voice in his head answered, quiet and insistent.

Bill pressed harder on the gas, the SUV speeding up slightly as the road straightened out. He’d told himself he forgot to send it. He knew that wasn’t true. The damn signal was out at the campsite, but if he’d really wanted to send it, he could’ve found a way. He always did.

His grip on the wheel loosened as he let out a long breath. Maybe he didn’t want to send it because, deep down, the email wasn’t the solution. Leaving wouldn’t fix the cracks in the band, and it sure as hell wouldn’t bring him peace.

He glanced at the passenger seat, where the beer and jerky sat. “Maxim’s got it easy,” he muttered. “Just sit in a store all day, hand out advice like some small-town oracle.” He smirked faintly, the edges of his anger softening.

The forest thinned slightly as the road dipped into a shallow valley, the sunlight breaking through the canopy in wide beams. The warmth spilled into the car, and for a moment, Bill let it settle over him.

He took a left at the fork, steering toward another campsite he’d used before. It wasn’t as secluded as his usual spot, but it had a view of the river and a wide clearing perfect for sitting and thinking. Maybe that was what he needed—something different.

“New spot, new perspective,” he said aloud, as if trying to convince himself.

As the SUV climbed a hill, the river came into view, its waters glinting in the afternoon sun. The road narrowed, and Bill slowed, navigating carefully over the uneven terrain. The anger he’d carried earlier felt lighter now, dulled by the quiet beauty around him.

He pulled into the clearing, killed the engine, and stepped out. The river’s steady murmur greeted him, joined by the rustle of leaves and the distant cry of a bird.

Bill stood there for a moment, the email still a weight in the back of his mind, but not as heavy as it had been. He grabbed the cooler and beer from the back and made his way toward the edge of the river.

“Let’s see if this place has the answers,” he muttered, setting up his chair and cracking open a beer. The forest seemed to nod in agreement, its silence both a challenge and a comfort.

## **Scene: Peace by the River**

Bill settled into his camp chair, the faint creak of the fabric giving way under his weight. He propped his boots on a flat rock and leaned back, beer in hand, as the river stretched before him. The water glided over smooth stones, its surface shimmering in the golden light of late afternoon.

The steady rhythm of the flow was hypnotic, an endless motion that seemed to erase the edges of his anger. Bill stared at the current, watching the ripples catch the light, and felt the tension in his shoulders ease. He took a slow sip of his beer and let out a breath he hadn’t realized he was holding.

This. This was why he came out here.

The forest and the river didn’t care about his frustrations or the storm he carried inside. The water just flowed, oblivious to the rocks in its path, carving its way forward without complaint or resistance.

He thought about that, the way the river moved—relentless, yet patient. It reminded him of something he’d read once about Stoicism, how it wasn’t about controlling others but controlling your reaction to them.

“You can’t stop the current,” he muttered, “but you can figure out how to float.”

He thought about Jeff, his impulsiveness, his inability to separate his personal desires from the band’s greater good. Bill had spent so much time trying to fight Jeff’s behavior, to push back against decisions that grated on him. And yet, what had it changed? Jeff was still Jeff.

Maybe that was the problem. He’d been focusing too much on changing things outside his control. The Stoics had a name for that—externals. Jeff’s choices, Jeremy’s nostalgia, even the band’s direction… they were externals. Worrying about them only drained energy better spent on his own actions.

Bill took another sip of beer, the bitterness balancing the warmth of the sun on his face. If he couldn’t control Jeff, then what? He could control how he responded. He could choose patience instead of frustration. Understanding instead of anger.

Not that it would be easy. Jeff could be infuriating. But maybe if Bill stopped letting Jeff’s choices dictate his emotions, he’d find more peace—like this moment by the river.

The water continued its journey, unhurried and unstoppable. Bill let the thought settle as he leaned back further, tipping his hat down to shield his eyes from the setting sun.

“Flow like the river,” he said softly, the words barely audible over the sound of the current.

For the first time in a long while, he didn’t feel the need to fix everything. He didn’t need to send the email. He didn’t need to decide anything today. The river would keep flowing, and he’d figure it out in time.

## **Scene: Futility of Fury**

Bill sat back in his camp chair, his eyes fixed on the river as it flowed relentlessly past him. The steady murmur of the water was a comforting contrast to the storm of thoughts swirling in his head. He took another sip from his beer, letting the bitter liquid settle in his chest as his gaze followed the current, never ceasing, always moving forward.

He thought about the band. About Jeff. About Jim. About how, in his fury, he'd allowed himself to become as stagnant as the frustration he felt toward them. He had been trying so hard to change things that were outside his control—especially Jeff, with his impulsive decisions and emotional wreckage.

But then, there was Jim. The quiet one. The steady heartbeat of the band, or at least the one who kept it from collapsing entirely under the weight of its own dysfunction. Bill’s frustration with Jim hadn’t been about the man himself; it was about what Bill needed from him, what he expected. He wanted Jim to take charge, to confront Jeff’s reckless behavior, to speak up when things felt out of balance.

But Jim was… well, inert. He wasn’t loud, he wasn’t confrontational. He didn’t want the spotlight. Bill had always resented that about him. Hell, he'd spent years wondering why Jim never took a more vocal stand, why he never pushed back when things were spiraling. It had felt like Jim was complicit in everything, standing by quietly, waiting for the storm to pass.

But that was the problem. Bill had been looking at Jim all wrong. He had been expecting Jim to be someone he wasn’t. Maybe if Bill had spent more time appreciating Jim’s role—steady, unwavering, always there—he would have seen it earlier.

There was strength in Jim’s silence. Strength in his refusal to play the game of ego and posturing that the rest of them did. The more Bill thought about it, the more he realized that Jim wasn’t just sitting back in passive acceptance. Jim had his own way of holding the band together, of protecting what they’d built. He wasn’t running from conflict. He just wasn’t going to engage in it unless it truly mattered. And that, Bill thought, was probably for the best.

Bill took a deep breath, letting the air fill his lungs as he let go of his resentment. What was it the Stoics said? Accept what you cannot change, and change what you can. Bill had spent too much energy trying to change Jim—expecting him to be something he wasn’t. The only thing he could control was his response. And maybe, just maybe, he could find peace in that.

Jim wasn’t going to be the hero of the band. Hell, Jim wasn’t even going to be the loudest voice in the room. But that didn’t mean he didn’t matter. Jim had always been there, silently keeping the rhythm, holding the rest of them together. Bill could stop fighting him for what he wasn’t and start valuing him for what he was.

Maybe that was the compromise Bill needed. Maybe that was the answer to the tension that had built between them. Jim wasn’t going to change, and Bill didn’t have the power to make him. But Bill could choose to meet him where he was—acknowledge his role and, for once, let the pressure of expectation go.

The river flowed on, unstoppable. Bill watched it for a long moment, the soft rush of the current somehow aligning with the calm he was beginning to feel within himself. There was no point in fighting the current. He couldn’t change the way the river moved. But he could choose to be at peace with its course.

Maybe, just maybe, he could do the same with Jim.

## **Scene: Evans the Younger**

Bill leaned back in his chair, the soft crunch of gravel beneath his boots the only sound in the stillness. The beer had warmed slightly in his hand, but the burn of the alcohol was still there, providing a kind of blunt edge to his thoughts. He looked out across the river, where the light was beginning to fade, casting long shadows over the water. The natural world, the rhythm of the earth, had always been Bill’s solace. It helped clear his mind, gave him space to breathe. Out here, he didn’t have to worry about all the noise—the noise of the band, of Jeff’s reckless decisions, or the internal arguments that seemed to surface every time he tried to make sense of it all.

There was something grounding about nature, about the way the world just was. The trees didn’t care if he was angry. The river didn’t care about his disappointments. They just flowed, just grew, just being. And for the first time in a while, Bill didn’t feel the need to fight against everything that was out of his control.

The beer helped, too.

But when he thought about Jeremy—well, that was different. It wasn’t that Jeremy was out of control like Jeff; it was that Jeremy was… stuck. He was clinging to an image of the past, a legacy that Bill wasn’t sure even existed anymore. Jeremy always talked about legacy—how the band had once been revolutionary, how they’d changed the game, how they were the game. It was as if Jeremy was trying to keep them all locked in that moment, that mythical place in time when they were gods of the stage, bigger than life itself.

But Bill knew better now. He could feel it every time Jeremy launched into one of his rants about the good old days, about recreating what was lost. Bill had spent years holding onto those memories, too. But reality had set in. The world had changed. They’d all changed. And Jeremy? He was still caught up in the myth.

The problem wasn’t that Jeremy wasn’t talented—hell, Jeremy was still a beast on the guitar. It wasn’t even that he was stuck in the past. It was that he was dragging the rest of them down with him. His nostalgia had become a weight around their necks, keeping them from moving forward, from adapting, from evolving. And every time he brought it up, every time he used legacy as a shield against new ideas, Bill felt the pull of frustration threatening to rise up again.

Bill sighed, tossing the empty bottle into the cooler. He could feel the knot in his chest tightening again, but he forced it down. It was a natural reaction, a reflex to Jeremy’s stubbornness. And if Stoicism had taught him anything, it was that his reaction was the one thing he could control. Not Jeremy’s fixation on the past, not the band’s collective inertia. Just his response to it.

He took another slow breath.

Maybe the answer with Jeremy wasn’t to try to convince him to let go of the past. Maybe it was just to meet him where he was, to acknowledge his need for legacy and give him space to feel that. But also, to challenge him—not to recreate something they couldn’t go back to, but to build something new. They didn’t have to erase the past; they just needed to stop worshipping it.

Bill knew Jeremy wasn’t the kind of guy who would listen to reason easily. He was passionate, sometimes too much for his own good, and when he got on a roll about the band’s legacy, it was like trying to stop a freight train. But Bill could speak the language of legacy, too. He could remind Jeremy of the risks they’d taken, the innovation they’d embraced when they were young. And maybe, just maybe, he could challenge Jeremy to be brave again, to take risks not just with his music but with his vision of what the band could become.

There was a time when Bill had been on that same track, clinging to an image of what the band was supposed to be, the ideal version of the group they had once been. But that time had passed. And he had to make Jeremy see that. It wouldn’t be easy. But nothing worth doing ever was.

Bill stood, stretching his arms toward the sky. The sun was dipping lower now, casting an amber glow over the river. For a moment, he just watched the water flow, steady and unrelenting, moving forward without hesitation.

Maybe it was time to bring that forward motion into the band. He couldn’t change Jeremy’s attachment to the past, but he could force them to confront the future. They had to move forward. The question was: Could they all get on the same page? Could Jeremy let go of his nostalgia long enough to build something new?

Bill wasn’t sure. But he was damn well going to try.

And maybe that meant having a conversation with Jeff—a real one, not just the passive-aggressive remarks and silent judgment. But the thought of it made Bill’s stomach twist. Maybe the peace he had found by the river wasn’t a permanent state, maybe it wasn’t about being completely free of anger. Maybe peace was about learning to face the shit that came his way—learning how to deal with it, how to move forward with it, and how to carve out space for himself amidst the chaos.

And right now? He needed to respond with clarity.

## **Scene: Bill’s Perfect Morning**

Bill woke up to the soft light filtering through the trees, the scent of wood smoke still lingering in the cool morning air. For a moment, he simply lay there, eyes closed, taking in the peaceful quiet. The river’s steady flow was the only sound, and the weight of yesterday's frustrations had faded into the distance, like the fading stars overhead.

He rose slowly, stretching his arms overhead, and the sense of clarity he'd gained by the river yesterday remained with him. He had a plan. He knew what he needed to do.

The fire from last night was still smoldering, the coals glowing red beneath a layer of ash. Bill knelt by the campfire, poking at the embers until they flared back to life. He took his time, setting up his small cooking gear and laying out the ingredients: eggs, a few strips of bacon, and, of course, a thick handful of his favorite peppered beef jerky.

He cracked the eggs into the pan, their yolks breaking perfectly as he fried them sunny-side-up. The bacon sizzled beside them, the aroma filling the crisp morning air. But it was the jerky that made it. The peppered jerky—salty, smoky, with just the right bite—was what elevated the whole meal. He sliced it into small pieces and scattered it over the eggs. It wasn’t just breakfast; it was a celebration of simplicity, of the moment. The best part was the jerky, he thought. The crunch and the spice, bringing everything together.

As he ate, Bill leaned back against a nearby rock, his plate resting in his lap. He looked out over the river again, taking it all in—the fire, the food, the warmth of the sun just beginning to climb over the treetops. He didn’t have to rush. There was no pressure here. Just peace. And for once, he didn’t feel guilty for enjoying it. He snapped a quick photo of the scene—the fire, the coffee cup, the half-eaten breakfast plate with the jerky glistening on top.

Bill smiled to himself, staring at the picture on his phone. It wasn’t just a photo; it was a reminder of the calm he had found, of the clarity he had fought so hard to achieve. This moment would stay with him. He wasn’t letting it go.

## **Scene: Molar Teeth**

By mid-morning, Bill was back in the SUV, the road stretching out before him. The familiar bends and turns of the forest road felt less like a path and more like a return to something important. He felt lighter today, more settled, more sure of himself.

When he pulled into the lot outside the country store, he felt a small surge of gratitude. This was one of the few places where he was truly just… Bill. No fame, no expectations—just a guy getting some jerky and a beer.

Maxim was at the counter when he walked in, his usual crossword puzzle abandoned for a moment as he glanced up. The door jingled behind Bill as he stepped inside, a fresh gust of wind sweeping through the room.

“Morning, Bill,” Maxim greeted him, his voice warm but measured. Maxim had always known how to give Bill space.

Bill pulled his phone from his pocket, showing Maxim the photo he’d taken by the river. “Here’s where to look,” Bill said, his tone casual but with an undertone of something more—something final.

Maxim raised an eyebrow, his gaze flicking to the photo before meeting Bill’s eyes. “Looks peaceful,” he said, his voice steady. He paused for a beat, then added, “I’m guessing you’ve found what you’re looking for.”

Bill nodded, a small, contented smile forming on his lips. He didn’t need to explain further. Maxim had been there before.

“Yeah,” Bill replied, the weight of the words settling comfortably in his chest. “I think I have.”

With that, he turned, walking to the counter. He bought another six-pack, another bag of jerky, and a fresh bottle of water. Maxim rung him up, the sound of the register ringing in Bill’s ears as if marking the end of one chapter and the beginning of another.

As Bill grabbed his bag and turned to leave, he felt lighter than he had in months. He didn’t look back
