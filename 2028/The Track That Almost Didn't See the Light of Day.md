# **The Track That Almost Didn’t See the Light Of Day**  
*Kendall Weatherby*

---

If you asked any of the guys—off the record, back when they were still doing the polite thing and pretending they might do press again—they’d all tell you the same thing.

Avril wrote it.  Track sixteen. *Blind.* That was hers.  They said it the way a ship crew points to the north star. With certainty. With muscle memory. With the kind of certainty that announces the conversation’s already over.

Ask again, and Jim would laugh and say, “Go ask her.”  Bill would just nod.  Jeremy would grimace, as if hearing the question physically offended him.  Jeff? Jeff had a rotation of responses, each sharper than the last. His favorite was: “That’s a stupid question.”

Because of course Avril wrote it. Who the fuck else would?

*“All these days watching from the inside…”*  
*“Now she’s here, lying in the casket…”*

It had Kathy’s ghost stitched into every line. But it didn’t ache the way Jeff’s songs did, didn’t boil like Jeremy’s or eulogize like Bill’s. It floated. Controlled. Intentional.

It sounded like someone trying to be respectful in a room full of survivors.  And that’s what made it work.

See, this wasn’t a band interested in revisiting *Be First, Be Smarter, Or Cheat.* Not even in oblique ways. That album was a trauma scar, and no one liked touching it. They didn’t perform the songs. They didn’t stream the sessions. They didn’t talk about it. Sarah Malcolm, still the most unshakable thought leader in modern rock, in 2014 issued the only line that has ever stuck: *“It speaks for itself.”*

But by 2028, Avril had earned things no one said out loud. She’d made five records with them, held her own onstage and in-studio, took punches and handed them back. She stopped asking to be let in and just stayed. What she hadn’t done—until sixteen—was reach *backward.*

“*Blind*” wasn’t an attempt to steal legacy. It was the opposite. It was the sound of her *giving it back.* In their language. In Bill’s pacing. In Jeff’s verbal geometry. In Kathy’s ghost key.

And they let her.  They recorded it without fuss. Played it live regularly. Never once explained it.

There are two ways to tell that story:

1. That it was Avril’s victory lap.

2. That it was the last test, and she passed.

The truth’s probably in the third answer no one gave:  It wasn’t about her at all.

She just said what they couldn’t. Still. After all that time.

Sixteen is the song that almost wasn’t—because the wound never closed properly. But Avril found a way to dress it without reopening it. And when the band finally sang it with her, they weren’t forgiving her for prior transgressions. They were *acknowledging* her. As a voice. As a member. As someone who understood, finally, what silence was made of.

She didn’t break the rules. She honored them.  Predictably, they just didn’t know how to say thank you. So they cut the track inst
