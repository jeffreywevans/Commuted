**January 24, 2012 – 3:48 A.M.  Location: Back alley behind a recording studio in Cincinnati**  

Kathy’s barefoot. Gilley’s holding her shoes. The snow is filthy. The world is ending. 

---

## **EXT – COLD ALLEY – NIGHT**

Gilley blocks the exit, coat half-zipped, breath clouding in the air. Kathy’s pacing in circles like a woman who’s too alive, too electric, too broken to touch. Her hair’s wet from sweat and sleet. Her teeth chatter between screams.

GILLEY:  
Kat, please—  Just come inside.  You’re not wearing shoes. It’s fucking snowing.

KATHY (whipping around):  
Oh now you’re my fucking handler? What’s next, Gilley, huh? You gonna strap me to a gurney and tell me I’m "a risk to myself"?

GILLEY:  
You’re high as hell and three blocks from collapsing in a gutter.

KATHY:  
So what? So what?  You want a medal for showing up with a blanket?

GILLEY:  
I want you to sleep. Somewhere safe. That’s it.

KATHY (screaming):  
I don’t deserve safe! I don’t get the fucking cot!  You think I didn’t hear them?  “She used to write real shit.” “She’s a waste now.” “She peaked when Jeff broke down.”

GILLEY (stepping forward):  
No one said that.

KATHY (getting in his face):  
They thought it.  Even you. Even Saint fucking Gilley, with his guitar and his endless second chances.

GILLEY:  
I’d give you a thousand second chances.  Because you’re worth it. Even when you’re like this.

KATHY (shaking):  
Don’t pity me.  Don’t rescue me.  You wanna help? Buy me a bump and fuck off.

GILLEY (quiet, shaking now too):  
I loved you when your songs could burn houses down.  I loved you when you were just noise and fire.  And I still love you.  But I don’t know how to hold you anymore.

KATHY (voice cracking, eyes wide):  
Then don’t.

She shoves him. He doesn’t move.

KATHY (quiet, trembling):  
Then don’t.

GILLEY (soft):  
I’m still gonna leave the door unlocked.  And I’m gonna put your shoes on the stairs.  If you want them.

Kathy doesn’t speak. She just turns, walks a few feet, and screams into the snow—  a sound like a song that broke its bones.  She doesn’t follow him.  Not yet. But she doesn’t walk away either.

**January 25, 2012 – 6:12 A.M.**  **Location: Bill Davenport’s Studio, Over-the-Rhine, Cincinnati**

## **INT – STUDIO LOUNGE – MORNING**

The door creaks open just after sunrise.

Gilley’s jacket is still hanging on the coat rack. Kathy enters like a shadow, still barefoot, one heel clutched in her hand, the other missing.  

Her mascara looks like war paint. Her lips are cracked. She’s not shaking anymore—she’s past the edge of rage. Now it’s just the fall.

The studio is silent. Only a low hum from the fridge. A red “record” light blinks quietly in the main booth. Bill never sleeps.

---

## **INT – STUDIO LOUNGE – CONTINUOUS**

Kathy moves past the mixing boards, doesn’t glance at the piano.  She doesn’t need a bed. She doesn’t need water.  She just needs the couch.

It’s threadbare. Probably smells like beer and compressor dust.  But it’s warm.  And for now?It’s enough.

She curls into the corner of the couch like a broken needle in a vinyl groove.  She doesn’t cry. She doesn’t think.  She just exhales.

Offscreen, someone sets a blanket on her—  probably Bill.  Definitely not Gilley.  Gilley already gave her space.  Now someone else gives her grace.

January 25, 2012 – 8:30 A.M.  
Location: Bill Davenport’s Studio Lounge – Over-the-Rhine, Cincinnati  

Kathy’s still on the couch. Bill has had exactly zero hours of sleep, one black coffee, and too many thoughts.

## **INT – STUDIO LOUNGE – MORNING LIGHT FILTERING THROUGH SECURITY BARS**

Kathy’s just waking up. Her hair looks like shit. Her breath tastes like battery acid. Her back hurts. She doesn’t remember if she dreamt anything or if she was the dream.

She sits up.

There’s a Starbucks cup on the table next to her.  Black. No sugar. No bullshit.

And Bill, standing across the room, arms folded, eyes red from caffeine and silence.

BILL:  
You gonna drink that?  Or snort it?

KATHY (groggy):  
Fuck you.

BILL:  
Love you too, dork.

Beat. Kathy took the cup. Sips.

BILL (calm, like a sniper):  
So when are you sobering up and checking into rehab?

KATHY (deadpan):  
You trying to get rid of me?

BILL:  
Trying to keep you alive.  So I can keep calling you a fucking dork.

She doesn’t reply. Just stares into the coffee like it might have answers.

BILL (sitting now):  
You’re a genius, Kat.  But you’re not a martyr.  And if you die for a girl who ghosted your ghost, I swear to God I’ll mix your last vocals through a Fisher-Price microphone and call it art.

KATHY (hoarse):  
You’re such a prick.

BILL:  
Good. Pricks get results.  And I need you sober, so we can finish the record, bury the decade, and maybe get one clean breath before the tour eats us alive.

Beat.

BILL (quiet):  
You don’t gotta get better for all of us.  Just for you.

Kathy stares at him.  No comeback.  Just the first sip that doesn’t burn.  And the slowest nod she’s ever given in her life.

**January 25, 2012 – 9:03 A.M.  Location: Alley behind Bill’s Studio – Over-the-Rhine, Cincinnati**  

Kathy’s inside, asleep again—caffeine and adrenaline finally crashing.  Jeff’s just arrived. Bill’s been waiting.

## **EXT – BACK ALLEY – MID-MORNING, BITTERLY COLD**

Bill stands by the door, arms wrapped around his thermos like a priest holding a flask full of bad miracles. Jeff pulls up in his usual beat-up sedan, eyes hidden behind sunglasses, as if the weight of reality is just one more light he can dim.

He climbs out. Doesn’t say anything. Just leans against the brick wall like gravity hates him personally.

BILL:  
She’s inside.  Drank half the coffee. Called me a prick. So, better than yesterday.

JEFF:  
I figured she was either here or dead in a parking lot.

BILL (flat):  
Wasn’t a coin toss.  It was a slow collapse with a clear trajectory.  You’ve been watching it, same as me.

JEFF (defensive):  
I didn’t start her spiral.

BILL:  
No.  But you kept handing her shovels when she needed a ladder.

Jeff says nothing. Jaw tight. Hands buried deep in his coat pockets like he's afraid they’ll tremble.

BILL (calmer):  
Look, I’m not saying it’s all your fault.  I’m saying she fell in love with someone who fell in love with someone else.  And then got left with the mess.

JEFF (quiet):  
Sherry wasn’t the problem.

BILL (cutting):  
She was your out.  Kathy was your mirror.  You tried to swap the one that showed you too much for the one that said nothing at all.

Jeff flinches. Slight. Real]

BILL (continuing):  
And now Kathy’s unraveling and you’re too numb to stitch anything.

You want my advice?

Be useful.  Don’t make her ask for help.  Just be there. Like you used to be.  Before everything got complicated and you stopped being the guy she could trust.

JEFF (voice cracking slightly):  
What if she doesn’t want me there?

BILL:  
She doesn’t.  But that’s not the point.

They stand in silence for a beat. The alley’s quiet. The city groans around them.  Inside, Kathy sleeps like a woman being hunted by her own heartbeat.

**January 25, 2012 – 9:09 A.M.  Location: Alley behind Bill’s Studio – Over-the-Rhine, Cincinnati**

Tension: Cuttable. Pain: Old. Words: About to get bloody.

## **EXT – BACK ALLEY – CONTINUOUS**

Jeff lights a cigarette with hands that look steadier than they are. He exhales like it’s the only thing he’s in control of.

JEFF (quiet, sharp):  
I don’t rescue women anymore.

BILL (turning):  
The fuck does that mean?

JEFF (flat):  
It means it always ends the same.  You pour yourself in, they drink you dry, and then they leave.  It’s betrayal by design.

Bill goes still. Really still. The kind of still that only comes from someone choosing not to break something.

BILL (measured):  
You say that shit to me again, and I will plant you into that wall like ivy.

Jeff says nothing. Smokes.

BILL (tighter):  
If I were ten years younger,  
we’d be brawling right now.  
Because that sentence?That sentence makes you a coward.

JEFF (shrugs):  
Maybe.  Or maybe I’m just out of romantic illusions.

BILL (calm, volcanic):  
This isn’t about romance.  It’s about showing up.  And I swear to Christ, Jeff, if you don’t walk back in there and act like a human being who remembers what loyalty looks like,  I’ll forget my own name and knock some into you.

Silence. The cigarette burns low.  Jeff doesn’t apologize. But he doesn’t walk away either.

BILL (final):  
She’s not asking for a rescue.  She’s asking for someone who remembers her before the spiral.  You used to be that guy.

Bill heads back inside.  Jeff flicks the cigarette into the slush and stares at the door like it might bite.

**January 25, 2012 – 9:12 A.M.  Location: Alley behind Bill’s Studio – Over-the-Rhine, Cincinnati**  

The cold is seeping in deeper now. The coffee’s gone. The gloves are off.

## **EXT – BACK ALLEY – CONTINUOUS**

Bill’s halfway to the door when Jeff lets it out—voice low, like he’s trying to convince himself more than anyone else.

JEFF:  
She won’t listen to me.  She hasn’t in years.  Only time Kathy gives a shit what I think is when she wants a number one or a dick.

Bill turns. Slowly. Face blank. Neutral. Too neutral.

BILL (dry):  
You done?

JEFF:  
No.  I’ve been done since 2010, but my heart forgot to get the memo.

BILL:  
You’re exaggerating.

JEFF (eyes burning):  
Am I really, though?

**Silence. Heavy. The kind of silence where Bill wants to swing. Wants to scream.  But he doesn’t.

He just stares at Jeff like he’s reading a suicide note written in eyeliner and cigarette ash.

BILL (soft, but flinty):  
That woman would cut out her own tongue before she told you how often she listens to the demos you leave on the shared drive.  She’s addicted to your chord changes.  She stalks your lyrics like they owe her rent.

She doesn’t ignore you. She just can’t afford to need you again.

Jeff flinches, just slightly.

JEFF (bitter):  
So we’re all just pretending this isn’t a fucking loop?

BILL:  
It is.  But it’s the only loop keeping her from falling through the floor.  And whether she hears you or not?  You're still the echo she checks for when it gets dark.

Beat. 

Bill heads back inside without another word.  Jeff leans against the wall again.  He doesn’t smile.  But he doesn’t light another cigarette either.

**January 25, 2012 – 9:27 A.M.  Location: Bill Davenport’s Studio Lounge – Over-the-Rhine, Cincinnati**

## **INT – STUDIO LOUNGE – MID-MORNING LIGHT THROUGH SOUNDPROOF GLASS**

Jeff walks in like he’s stepping onto a battlefield that’s been his home for a decade.  He doesn’t slam the door. He doesn’t sigh.  He just walks over to the couch, stands there for a moment.

Kathy doesn’t move.

He crouches.  Still no reaction. So, he speaks.  Quiet.  Measured.  Deadly fucking serious.

JEFF:  
Get up.

No response.

JEFF (firmer):  
Get up, Kathy.  Get sober.

Because if we can do that—  If we can remember who we are when we’re not bleeding into microphones or snorting history— Then maybe it’s time. Time to go to L.A.  Time to sit down with those Netflix assholes.  Time to stop being a tragedy with a discography.

She still doesn’t move. But her breathing changes.  Slower. Tighter.

JEFF (softening just a touch):  
They want a docuseries.  Eight episodes. Us. The songs. The real shit.  They want to put our wreckage on screens.  And maybe… maybe it’s time we let them.

He sets down a protein bar next to the coffee. The wrapper crackles.

JEFF:  
You’ve got until noon.  If you’re vertical and not wired, I’ll book the flights.

If not… We’ll both keep rotting in high definition.

He walks away. No drama. No noise.  Just the click of the door behind him. Kathy doesn’t open her eyes.  But she unwraps the blanket.  And reaches for the coffee.

**January 25, 2012 – 9:41 A.M.  Location: Vocal booth mirror – Bill’s Studio, Cincinnati**

## **INT – VOCAL BOOTH – DUSTY LIGHT, STILL AIR**

Kathy stares into the mirror inside the booth.  Her face looks older than yesterday.  Her eyes are red, but not from sleep. She opens her mouth like she’s about to sing.  Closes it again.

And then:

KATHY (muttering):  
It’s Kathy’s unworthy.  Kathy the waste.  Kathy who couldn’t keep a leash on a bassline or a bedmate.

She presses her forehead to the glass.

KATHY:  
Kathy wants her next hit.  Kathy wants the Sonic Land of Cocaine.  Neon harmonies and click tracks that make your gums itch.

She pulls out a notebook. Scribbles:

*“Don’t give me love.*  
*Give me volume.*  
*Give me that sonic white lightning.*  
*That mirrorball mania.*  
*That fake applause in my veins.”*

She whispers it again, voice cracking into something just short of a scream:

KATHY:  
Don’t give me love.  Give me volume.

She stares at the mic like it might bite her back.  Because deep down, she knows: Kathy doesn’t want to be saved.  Kathy wants to be loud enough that nobody notices she’s disappearing.

Passengers: Jeff, Kathy, Gilley. Destination: Netflix Headquarters. Mission: Maybe tell the truth. Maybe destroy themselves trying.

## **INT – PRIVATE JET CABIN – 33,000 FEET OVER KANSAS**

Kathy’s got one leg tucked under her, sunglasses still on despite the dim cabin lighting. She’s picking at a protein bar like it insulted her family. Across from her, Jeff scribbles in a leather notebook. His third since they left Dayton. Gilley is reclining, boots up, scrolling through his phone, periodically grinning.

KATHY (dry):  
So what are we calling this little press junket?  “The Addicts’ Anthology”? “We Swore We’d Never Talk, Now Here’s Eight Episodes”?

JEFF (not looking up):  
I want to start at the beginning.

KATHY (tilting head):  
Like… our first show? That high school gig with the amp that blew?

JEFF (sharper):  
No. After that.  August ‘89. The garage. The moment it stopped being a hobby and became a contract.

Gilley exhales hard. Low whistle.

GILLEY:  
You really wanna dig that up on camera?

KATHY:  
Of course he does.  Jeff wants pain with structure. He wants trauma with a timestamp.

JEFF:  
Because that’s where it started. The why. The first spark.  Everyone’s seen the fallout. Nobody’s seen the fuse.

Kathy finally peels her glasses off, sets them on the armrest.

KATHY:  
And what if telling the truth ruins us?

JEFF (firm):  
August 1989.  That garage outside Donnelsville.  When it stopped being a jam and turned into a signature. That’s when we became Commuted.  That’s the beginning.

[Gilley snorts.]

GILLEY:  
I remember your hands shook so hard you almost signed in Sharpie on the toaster oven.  And Kathy threw up in the sink after her third wine cooler.

KATHY (dry):  
Because someone—Jeff—called our signing “a kind of spiritual deflowering.”

JEFF (still calm):  
It was.

Silence stretches. The hum of jet engines. Turbulence somewhere deep in the past.

KATHY (removing her glasses, watching him now):  
Again, what if telling the truth ruins us?

JEFF:  
Truth’s already ruined us.  This is just… setting the camera at the right angle.

GILLEY (grinning, not looking up):  
If we’re gonna self-destruct, might as well get the Netflix residuals.

KATHY (whispered):  
They don’t know what it was like.  The basement. The heat. The… urgency of it.  They’re not ready.

JEFF (finally meeting her eyes):  
Then let’s get them ready.

## **INT – CONFERENCE ROOM – MIDDAY**

Jeff’s seated, notes out. Gilley’s spinning a pen. The producers are waiting for the pitch—expecting post-grunge nostalgia and maybe some Behind the Music tearjerker shit. Kathy’s standing, pacing slow and deliberate in boots that cost more than the camera equipment.

KATHY:  
You want a series? Then here’s what you get. We give you the rawest, most graphic, most inhumanly honest depiction of sex, drugs, and rock-and-roll ever filmed.

Not a memoir.  A fictional lens.  Built from our world. Our ruins. Because the truth?  The truth is too radioactive to survive eight episodes intact.

[The producers blink. One of them—young, bright-eyed, clearly a fan—starts typing.]

KATHY (continuing):  
We fictionalize it.  Names are changed. Timelines are blurred.  But everything else? Uncensored. We show the coke streaks on the console, the hotel room where Gilley got head from two people who didn’t speak the same language, the night Jeff and I broke a bottle and a rib. We show what happens when a band becomes a battlefield and a family and a suicide pact nobody signed, but everyone honored.

JEFF (quietly, without looking up):  
We show what it costs.

GILLEY (grinning):  
And how fucking good it sounds when it works.

Along pause. The room is silent except for the hum of the ceiling fan. The assistant stops typing.

KATHY:  
So, Netflix. You still want us?

## **INT – NETFLIX EXECUTIVE CONFERENCE ROOM – CONTINUOUS**

The silence after Kathy's pitch is thick, charged, electric. The Netflix producers glance at each other—awkwardly, cautiously. One of them, a thirty-something man in a sport jacket and a curated beard, finally speaks.

**NETFLIX PRODUCER 1:**  
This is… intense.  But what we’re really looking for is a documentary.  You know—archival footage, interviews, narration, that kind of thing.  Something grounded. Real.

[Kathy blinks slowly. Jeff’s pen stops moving. Gilley groans out loud and throws his head back.]

JEFF (deadpan):  
So you want the funeral, not the resurrection?

**NETFLIX PRODUCER 2 (defensive):**  
No, we just want to tell the story as it actually happened.

JEFF (leaning forward, eyes dead serious):  
Then make a mockumentary. Or a docudrama. Or whatever the fuck you want to call it.  Be inventive.  Don’t you guys already have a label for this?

(beat)  
“Based on a true story.”

KATHY (cold, razor sharp):  
The truth doesn’t need to be verifiable.  It just needs to hit.

GILLEY (smirking):  
Besides, half the real stuff we did, no one would believe if we showed it to them straight.

**Another pause. One of the producers looks very much like a man rethinking his career. The other one, younger, more dangerous, is clearly… intrigued.**

## **INT – HOTEL SUITE, LOS ANGELES – 1:12 A.M.** 

A single lamp on. The suite is spacious, over-air-conditioned, and haunted by old music. Jeff is on the couch with a yellow legal pad. Kathy sits on the bed with her legs crossed, a bottle of sparkling water in one hand, and a Sharpie in the other. They’re drafting the outline of their own reckoning.

JEFF:  
What if we do it in magical realism?  Like—surreal edges. Memory logic.  Like when time folds in on itself, and one scene bleeds into a decade.  Make it feel like how it actually felt to live it.

KATHY (raising an eyebrow):  
Yeah, or we could tell a story that people can follow without taking a tab of acid first.

JEFF (half-smiling):  
I’m just saying—real life didn’t feel linear. It felt like a fever.  We blinked and woke up famous and broken.

Okay. So, where’s the line between documentary and drama?

It’s not a line. It’s a vibe.  We play it straight until the straight-line snaps.  And when it does, that’s where the audience learns they can’t trust anything… except the emotion.

JEFF:  
How curated is this gonna be?

KATHY (shrugging):  
Include everything.

JEFF (looking up, pointed):  
Even you and the cocaine?

Pause. Kathy sips the water. Smiles. Doesn’t blink.

KATHY:  
Maybe we… dial it back a little.  For the public’s consumption.

JEFF (flat):  
Right. Like calling the asteroid that killed the dinosaurs a “weather event.”

KATHY (grinning):  
Exactly.  Let them think I was just “energetic.”

They both laugh—real, unguarded, relieved laughter.  Not because it’s funny. But because they’re finally talking like the world didn’t end.
