## **INT. BOSTON HOSPITAL – MORGUE CORRIDOR – NIGHT – NOVEMBER 1, 2012**

The hallway is painted a color that was supposed to be cheerful, once. Pale yellow, now sickly under buzzing fluorescents. The hum is constant, fluorescent light eating the silence. The floor gleams too clean, disinfectant sharp enough to sting the throat.

Jeff walks first. Jim follows half a step behind, slower, like each foot weighs fifty pounds. Neither of them has spoken since the elevator doors closed. They don’t need to.

At the end of the corridor, a **steel door with no window**. The placard reads: MORGUE – AUTHORIZED STAFF ONLY. A nurse in pale scrubs and paper mask pushes it open. She doesn’t meet their eyes. She doesn’t want to see what’s about to happen.

---
## **INT. HOSPITAL MORGUE – NIGHT**

Cold hits first. A sharp slap, like walking into a meat locker. Thirty-six degrees. Jeff feels his glasses fog for a second. Jim exhales and watches his breath hang. The air is wet, metallic, wrong.

The room is bare except for steel drawers stacked like filing cabinets for the dead. A single gurney waits in the middle, draped with a white sheet.

The nurse whispers something about *you’ll have ten minutes*. Neither man answers.

[[4.3 JEFF EVANS character bible|Jeff]] steps closer. His hand trembles. He hates that. He wants to be precise, composed, the one who always remembers details. But his hand shakes anyway.

He grips the sheet and pulls it back.

[[4.5 KATHY ESPICH character bible|Kathy]].

Except not Kathy.

Her skin is waxen, stretched, bruised in places that speak of medical violence. Tubes pulled, tape still stuck to her wrist, lines of adhesive tearing hair from her arm. A catheter mark like a wound. The surgeon’s stitches that look less like mercy and more like butchery. Her lips pale blue, parted. Her jaw slack.

Jeff’s stomach lurches but he does not retch. He refuses. He counts. One, two, three—he makes himself catalog. The way her hair mats with dried blood at the scalp. The bruising along her clavicle. The way her hands, always clenched into fists on stage, lie open now. Empty.

Jim can’t. He steps back, pressing one fist against his mouth like he’s going to punch himself. His blue eyes are wet. He mutters *fuck* once, barely audible. Then again, louder.

Jeff doesn’t move. He stands over her, glasses fogging, heart a stone. 

**JEFF (whispering)**
Thirty-six degrees.

Jim blinks, startled.

**JEFF** (louder)
Thirty-six degrees. That’s what it is in here.

**JEFF** (faltering)
She’s cold, but not frozen. They don’t freeze you. They keep you right above. Just cold enough.

It’s how he holds himself together. With mere numbers sometimes. With precision, always.

Jim sinks to the floor, back against the wall. 

**JIM** (muttering)
*Fuck-fuck-fuck…*

His drummer’s hands shake. He won’t look at her again.

Jeff doesn’t stop looking. He forces his eyes to stay on every bruise, every seam, every place where life was ripped out. He makes himself memorize it. He knows one day he’ll write it down, because it’s the only thing he can do.

---
Ten minutes pass. The nurse clears her throat.

Jeff leans forward, whispers something none of them can hear, and pulls the sheet back over her face.

The cold lingers in his bones as they leave. Thirty-six degrees. It will never leave.

## **INT. HOTEL SUITE – BOSTON – NIGHT – NOVEMBER 1, 2013**

The suite reeks of stale coffee and takeout no one ate. Bill’s fiddling with a laptop. Gilley’s sprawled on the couch, chain-smoking like it’s still 1995. Jeremy’s sitting at the table with his guitar, quietly running scales, nerves, avoidance, denial.

The door slams. Jim barrels in. Hoodie, boots, eyes red-rimmed, reeking of cold air and cigarettes.

**JIM**  (voice sharp, ragged)  
Where the fuck were you?

No one answers.

**JIM**  (shouting)  
**WHERE. THE. FUCK. WERE. YOU?**

Jeremy blinks, startled. Bill looks up, frowns. Gilley smiles, a nervous tic, not actual humor.

**JIM**  (spitting each word)  
Me. Jeff. The morgue. Thirty-six goddamn degrees. That’s where we were. Standing over her body while you fuckers sat here jerking off your grief.

Silence.

**JIM**  (to Jeremy)  
You. You couldn’t put down the guitar for ten minutes? Couldn’t face her when she couldn’t sing back?

Jeremy’s lips tighten. He says nothing.

**JIM**  (to Bill)  
And you. Mister precision. Mister *engineer.* Couldn’t engineer your ass out of this suite? Too scared of a goddamn refrigerator with drawers?

Bill sets his laptop down, jaw clenched. Doesn’t answer.

**JIM**  (to Gilley)  
And you. The mouth. The charmer. The one who could talk his way into any girl’s bed, any bar, any label office. Where was that mouth when she was cold on the slab? Where was the fucking charm then?

Gilley’s frown drops. He looks down. Ash falls from his cigarette onto the carpet. Jim’s chest heaves. He looks at each of them in turn.  Then he turns, slowly, to Kendall. She’s standing near the minibar, notebook in hand, silent.

**JIM**  (low, dangerous)  
And you. What’s the story, Kendall? Scribbling down our pain for your fucking archive? You think this is a *scene*?

Kendall stiffens, stares back. Doesn’t answer.

**JIM**  (voice breaking now, not anger but grief)  
She deserved better. She deserved us. All of us. Not just me and Jeff in that freezer.

Silence. Thick enough to choke on. Jim wipes his face roughly with his sleeve. Grabs a bottle of bourbon off the counter. Doesn’t bother with a glass.

**JIM**  (quiet, bitter)  
Next time one of you grows a pair, let me know.

He storms out. The door slams behind him.

## **INT. HOTEL SUITE – SAME NIGHT – LATER**

The room is still heavy from Jim’s eruption. The bourbon bottle is half-drained, left on the table like a weapon.  Bill sits with his head in his hands. Jeremy picks one muted note on his guitar again and again, like a pulse he can’t find. Gilley stares out the window, cigarette forgotten between his fingers.

Kendall sits in the corner, notebook unopened on her lap. Guilty. Burning.  She hears Jim’s words echoing in her skull: *Where the fuck were you. What’s the story, Kendall? You think this is a scene?* Her pen doesn’t move. For the first time since she was named their archivist, she cannot write.

Finally, Bill breaks the silence. His voice is small, shaky, stripped of the bravado he usually cloaks himself with.

**BILL**  
We…we couldn’t have done it. Not what they did. I couldn’t see her like that.

Jeremy finally looks up. Eyes bloodshot.

**JEREMY**  
None of us could. Don’t you get it? Jim thinks it’s about courage. It’s not. It’s about…survival.

Gilley exhales smoke through his nose, bitter.

**GILLEY**  
Tell that to him. He doesn’t want survival. He wants martyrs.

Silence again.

Kendall feels the weight of their eyes flicker toward her. She knows what they’re thinking but won’t say: *Write this down. Make it mean something. Tell the story.* But she can’t. Not yet.

The door crashes open again. Jim’s back, bourbon fire in his eyes, wild hair damp from the night air. He stares at them all.

**JIM**  (hoarse, furious)  
You think this is survival? Hiding? Cowards! You couldn’t even stand there when she was laid out, thirty-six degrees, skin like wax. You think you loved her? You didn’t love her enough to look at her when it counted.

Bill flinches. Jeremy grips his guitar like a shield. Gilley shrinks back into his smoke.

Jim points a shaking finger at them all, then lands on Kendall.

**JIM**  
And you. Archivist. *Mythographer.* Pen saint. Where’s the story? Huh? Where’s Kathy’s truth? You’re supposed to be the one who never blinks.

Kendall can’t meet his eyes. She closes the notebook.

Jim laughs, bitter, cracked.

**JIM**  
That’s it then. Take it up with Jesus. ’Cause when it mattered, when Jeff and I had to stand in hell and see what was left of her, you didn’t. None of you did.

He slams the door so hard the mirror on the wall trembles.

Silence.

## **INT. JIM & KENDALL’S HOME – KITCHEN – TWO WEEKS AFTER THE MORGUE**

The kids are asleep. The dishes are stacked in the sink. The whole house hums with the faint refrigerator buzz,  domestic echo of that thirty-six degrees.

Jim sits at the table, a half-drained glass of whiskey in front of him. Kendall stands at the counter, notebook open but untouched. She’s been trying to write, but every time the pen hovers over the page, it stalls.

**KENDALL**  (quiet, almost a plea)  
Say it. Whatever you’ve been carrying since Boston. Just say it.

Jim doesn’t look up. He swirls the glass.

**JIM**  
I’ve already said it.

**KENDALL**  
You screamed at everyone else. You barely looked at me.

He lifts his eyes now. Blue, sharp, tired.

**JIM**  
I don’t scream at you.

**KENDALL**  
Then what? What do you do?

He leans back, exhales.

**JIM**  
I forgive you.

Kendall stares, heart stuttering.

**KENDALL**  
You…forgive me?

**JIM**  
Yeah. You chose your limits. I can’t hold that against you. You didn’t sign up to see bodies in drawers. You’re not me.

He takes a long drink. Sets the glass down harder than he means to.

**JIM**  
***But***.

The word is a hammer.

**JIM**  
My opinion changed. That’s all.

Her face flinches like he slapped her.

**KENDALL**  
Changed how?

He shrugs, eyes flat.

**JIM**  
It’s hard. Seeing your spouse come up short in a time of need. Makes you…recalibrate.

Silence. The refrigerator hums louder, somehow.

**KENDALL**  
So what does that mean? For us?

Jim pushes the glass away. Looks at her, softens just a fraction.

**JIM**  
It means I won’t put you there again. I won’t ask you to go outside your limits. You won’t have to stand in the cold with me, not ever.

Kendall feels the weight of it, a mercy, and a sentence. She’s protected. She’s excluded.

She nods, though her throat burns.

**KENDALL**  
Thank you.

Jim rises. Puts a hand on her shoulder, heavy but not cruel.

**JIM**  
Don’t thank me. Just…live inside what you can do. And I’ll live outside it.

He walks out, leaving her with the notebook. Blank still.

## **KENDALL’S JOURNAL – UNDATED, UNSIGNED – FOUND YEARS LATER IN A BOX OF TAPES**

I stood in the hallway. Thirty-six degrees behind that door. Jeff’s glasses fogging, Jim’s fists clenched, both of them ready to face her. And me? My legs wouldn’t move. My pen might as well have been lead.

I thought of my kids. I thought of therapy. I thought of what seeing Kathy’s body, not Kathy, but what was left, would do to me. I thought about waking at 3 a.m. with her bruises stitched across my dreams, the smell of antiseptic bleeding into my children’s bedrooms. I thought: if I go in there, I will not come out the same.

So I stayed out. I chose to fail.

Every archivist has one story they don’t write. Mine is that room.  I told myself it was cowardice. I told myself it was protection. I told myself it was survival. All of it was true. None of it was enough.

And here’s the part that damns me: I thought I’d be judged by the world, by the fans, by history. I thought they’d say: Kendall flinched. Kendall couldn’t hack it. Kendall blinked.

What I didn’t expect was to be judged by my husband. By Jim.

He looked at me like I’d spat on her grave. Like I was worse than Jeremy’s silence, worse than Bill’s trembling, worse than Gilley’s smoke. Because I had a pen. Because my job was to record. Because I was supposed to be the one who never turned away.  And he’s right.
