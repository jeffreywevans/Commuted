# **THE HEART OF IT ALL**

---

# **CHAPTER ONE**

---

There are cycles in lust and genius—tight spirals like breath held too long underwater, until the pressure breaks both skin and sanity alike. In Cincinnati, in the heat-piss haze of July 2008, it wasn’t the sun that scorched the walls of Commuted’s recording studio. It was Jeff inside Kathy. Kathy on top of Jeff. Kathy under Jeff, on her knees and on the floor and on his tongue—fucking like possession, fucking like blasphemy. And then rising—panting, bruised, throbbing—to make art from the wreckage.

The band didn’t write songs. They survived them.

Commuted was a machine powered by combustion, and at the heart of it beat two filthy, gifted lunatics: Jeff with his hard jaw and hollow stare, his voice like iron pulled from the ground—deep and unsmiling—and Kathy, all rust-stained mouth and hips that moved like sin, all voice and bone and appetite. She sang in ragged gospel, moaned in harmony. Every time she opened her mouth, something burned.

This was not a romance. It was true crime.

No comfort. No satin. No pillow talk or studio rules or polite applause. Just the endless, rolling ache of need tangled in the desperate architecture of creativity. The fucking came like storms, always preceded by thunder, always followed by silence thick with ozone and the animal stink of bodies that had nothing left to give. That was when the real work began.

They’d be hunched over keyboards, bass strings, coffee mugs gone cold. Then a lyric. Then a riff. Then a sound like a door breaking open inside the chest. That was Jeff—slinging his guitar on like a war drum, sweat crusting in the hollow of his throat, snarling out something half melodic, half obscene. That was Kathy—ripping off her headphones to scream a harmony that came from somewhere too deep to name, voice cracked and thighs still damp, fingers still reeking of him.

The cycle had rhythm. Friction, detonation, echo. It was biological, geological, ecclesiastical.

When it began, it began with glances. With the glint of challenge in Kathy’s eye, and the click in Jeff’s throat when he locked it down, when he said nothing and said everything with a single look. He was all restraint, practiced tension, a man honed by defying abstinence and eschewing discipline—but when she set her mouth just so, when she bit down on the corner of a lyric and spat it back bleeding—he unraveled quietly and completely.

They'd argue over tempo. Over phrasing. Over whether the pain should rhyme.  She’d accuse him of cowardice. He’d accuse her of melodrama. And then it would crest—the music would stop, the air would go thick—and he’d grab her by the wrist like she was a weapon he’d just remembered how to use.

He never kissed her first. He backed her against the studio wall. She dared him to try. She laughed in his face, and then she bit his lip when he slammed her against the mixing board.

The first time, they’d broken a mic stand. The next, it was a synth. After that, Gilley stopped keeping track of the damage.

When they fucked, it was loud. Nothing polite. Nothing sealed behind closed doors. It started in the writing room, or the stairwell, or the van. Sometimes even mid-take, headphones flung, amps still hissing. His mouth would be on her neck, hers on his jeans, then his fingers in her—wet and fast and unforgiving—her breath stuttering as he curled them inside her and said, low and gravel-dry, “You gonna finish that verse now, or you need to come first?”

“Both,” she’d pant, nails clawing down his chest, gasping into the space between them. “You first.”

Their fucking was percussion. Rhythm and climax, climax and crash. Her legs around his back, heels digging in, bruises the shape of headphones and songbooks and urgency. She’d ride him on a leather couch slick with spilled beer and heat, every bounce a retort, a chorus, a line shouted into his mouth.

Her voice—god, her voice—wouldn’t quit. She'd moan like she was scoring the moment: “Hnnnh—ahh—uhfuckfuckfuck—”  Jeff would grunt, low and brutal, hips snapping, hands buried in her hair, pulling her down hard enough to grind bone against bone.  And when she came—when she finally shattered—it wasn’t a sound. It was a revelation.  He’d feel it in the tightening of her cunt, the fierce buck of her hips, the hiss between her teeth. He’d chase it, spit out a curse against her throat, and come with her name bitten into her shoulder, his teeth the only punctuation that mattered.

Afterward, they didn't collapse into anything like tenderness. They sprawled on studio floors with cracked tile biting their backs, or slumped in torn-velvet chairs, skin glazed with sweat and silence. No endearments. No cleanup. Just the humming awareness of what they'd done—what they'd summoned—and the knowledge that something worth keeping had passed between them. She’d reach for a guitar with fingers still slick, murmur a line that came out broken and perfect, and he’d follow it with a chord that might have been holy, if they believed in that kind of thing.

The band didn’t speak of it. Not directly. But everyone knew when the room turned feral. Knew when Jeff emerged with a bite on his neck and a new melody bleeding from his fingers. Knew when Kathy’s voice hit the studio mics sounding like she’d been choked by the gods.  Or Jeff, hard to tell in the dim light two days before the Apocalypse.

Gilley would grin—“the kids fucked again,” he'd mutter, always half-right, always proud of the fire they sparked. Bill would sigh and press Record before the heat cooled. Jim scribbled BPMs like he was chronicling a plague, trying to catch the disease before it mutated. And Jeremy kept his head low, fingers silent on strings, the steady current beneath the chaos that always, always knew exactly when to come in.

It wasn’t sustainable. Of course it wasn’t. But no one ever wanted it to last. Longevity was for cowards and accountants. What Jeff and Kathy made was explosive, perishable, erotic in its decay. Their music smelled like sweat and tasted like blood. It was made with aching limbs and bitten lips, with bruises hidden under denim, and moans baked into the reverb.

When the door shut behind them, they left ghosts in their wake. The room went quiet not because the noise had ended—but because it was still echoing in walls, soaked into carpet, trembling through the mics. They built each track like a crime scene. They recorded over the sounds of Kathy crying out—“Aaah—fuck, Jeff, deeper—deeper—don’t stop—don’t fucking stop!”—and layered basslines on top of her climax so that only those who knew would ever hear it, like sacred filth buried in the mix.

And Jeff? He’d walk out bleeding sometimes. Sometimes she'd punch him for real. Sometimes he liked it. And when he picked up his guitar again, the notes came cleaner. Feral. More exact.  The songs were the aftermath. The music was the altar. The fucking was the sacrifice.

They weren’t in love. They were in rupture. In ritual. In the fucking marrow of the storm.  And no one—not Gilley, not Jim, not even Kathy herself—could have told you whether what they did to each other was sex or salvation.

They didn’t care.

They made a record. And the record made legends. And every song on that album bore teeth marks and spit, came out of Kathy’s throat with his taste on her tongue, came out of Jeff’s fingers with her breath still drying on his cock.  They weren’t trying to last.  They were trying to burn.
