# **The Theology of *Come Inside Me***

Every band has its myths, but Commuted’s myth begins with a dare and a confession scrawled into melody. *Come Inside Me* is one of their oldest songs, written in 1990 when they were barely more than high school graduates hauling gear into VFW basements and Bogart’s sticky floor stage. It dates to within five months of Jeff and Kathy’s first sexual encounter, which means it is less “composition” than “documentation.” The band hadn’t even been discovered yet, but they were already setting their gospel to music.

What does the song do? It names what they had already begun to learn: sex between Jeff and Kathy was never casual. It was not friendship. It was not safety. It was not philos. It was lust, appetite, obliteration—ἔρως in the rawest Greek sense. The song’s lyrics were never coy about this. Kathy demanded union, Jeff answered in action, and the chorus bled the hunger for fusion out into sound.

The label, years later, panicked at the honesty. They censored the lyric about “a plopping sound, like sweet delight.” They thought it was obscene, silly, or crude. But that line was the entire theology of the song. The Greeks would have understood instantly. Because the *plop* is the tragedy of ἔρως: the moment of separation, the loss of unity, the void after fullness. The sound is sacred because it names the truth—oneness collapses back into twoness, and the ache begins again.

Commuted never built *Come Inside Me* to be romantic. They built it to be cyclical. The structure itself—long, trance-like, uphill for eleven and a half minutes—embodies the grammar of their sex: steady escalation, prolonged unity, explosive climax, collapse into silence. It’s not a ballad, it’s a ritual. The crowd chanting with Kathy at Athens in 2000 were not hearing a love song. They were participating in liturgy.

The theology is cruel but consistent. *Come Inside Me* is not agápē—it has no selfless forgiveness. It is not philos—it has no safe companionship. It is not storgē—it has no familial steadiness. It is ἔρως, through and through. It is hunger as holiness, lust as language, climax as communion. And like ἔρως itself, it leaves its worshippers trembling, undone, wanting again.

That it dates back to 1990 matters. Before contracts, before MTV, before Greece, the pattern had already begun. Jeff and Kathy had discovered each other’s bodies, discovered the way union and absence carved them open, and they wrote it down. The song is not just performance; it is artifact. A fossil of the moment they realized sex was not recreation for them but revelation.

To this day, *Come Inside Me* is less a track in their catalog than a creed. It explains why Jeff and Kathy could never walk away clean. It explains why their bond is both holy and destructive. It explains why the cycle repeats.

If you want to understand Commuted, don’t start with the fame. Start with the VFW hall, the sweat, the new lovers who put their truth into song before they knew anyone would hear. *Come Inside Me* is their Genesis, their scripture, their proof.
