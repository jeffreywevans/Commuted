**Cinematic Language & Visual Rules –** ***Commuted***

**Overall Style Mandate:**  
*Commuted* is not nostalgia porn. Its aesthetic must *feel* lived-in, cracked, hot to the touch. The visual approach mirrors the characters’ emotional volatility — sometimes lyrical, sometimes clinical, often harsh. Every frame is subjective: the camera is a conscience, not a neutral observer.

---

**1. Camera Movement:**

- **Garage Era (1989–1990):** Handheld, intimate, jittery. Camera breathes with the characters — stumbles when they do. Feel like we’re inside a home video no one meant to watch.
    
- **Peak Fame (1993–1999):** Locked frames during performances. Controlled tracking shots in business and media scenes. The band is being *packaged* now — and the camera reflects that false sense of order.
    
- **2029 Flashforwards:** Wide, static, emotionally void. Almost surveillance-style. Frame what they’ve become, not how they feel.
    
**2. Composition Rules:**

- **Kathy’s POV:** Slightly wider lenses. More physical space between people. Her emotional isolation should be visible in the framing.
    
- **Jeff’s POV:** Tight, over-the-shoulder shots. Mirrors. Unflattering angles. He’s always looking at himself through others.
    
- **Band Conflict Scenes:** No master shot. Just cuts between mismatched coverage, heightening chaos.
    
- **Music Moments:** No slow motion. No music video artifice. If it’s shot like a concert, it *is* a concert — shot real-time, sweat and all.
    
**3. Color Theory & Lighting:**

- **Early Years:** Sodium vapor warmth, deep shadows. The light feels heavy. Garage scenes lit with one source: fluorescent, yellow, intrusive.
    
- **Peak Fame:** Desaturated pastels mixed with strobe colors. Overlit in media scenes. Artificiality is the point.
    
- **2029:** Cool blues and lifeless grays. Exception: scenes where a character creates something alone — those glow.
    
**4. Texture & Film Stock Simulation:**

- **Seasons 1–2:** 16mm grain simulation. Grounded. Sweat, rust, grease.
    
- **Seasons 3–4:** Polished digital with grain overlay — a false clarity.
    
- **Season 5:** Mixed stock. Scene dictates tone. Flashforwards = cold digital. Flashbacks = smeared memory.
    
**5. Visual Motifs:**

- **Mirrors and Reflections:** Misidentification, split identity.
    
- **Wires and Cables:** Connection vs. entrapment. Frequently lit like veins.
    
- **Cigarette Smoke:** Presence of unspoken pressure. Smoke curls when tension spikes.
    
- **Ceiling Fans and Flickering Fluorescents:** The mundane given menace.
    
**6. Sound Design Philosophy:**

- No wall-to-wall score. Music competes with ambient realism.
    
- Feedback, amp hum, tape hiss — not polished. Sonic grit is emotional honesty.
    
- When music comes in fully, it must feel earned — like release, not relief.
    
**7. Editing Style:**

- **Touchstone Moments:** Long takes. No coverage. Let the actor drown or fly.
    
- **Fights and Betrayals:** Cut mid-line. Don’t let dialogue resolve before shifting POV.
    
- **Sex Scenes:** No coverage. Always from one character’s subjective lens.
    
**8. Visual Rulebook for Directors:**

- If it looks clean, it’s suspect.
    
- If it looks staged, it better be — and you better be commenting on it.
    
- If a scene could exist without sound, we’ve failed.
    

---

**Director’s Key:**  
This show’s soul lives somewhere between Verhoeven’s provocation, Fincher’s precision, and Andrea Arnold’s intimacy. No homage for homage’s sake. The aesthetic should always sharpen the emotional blade, not dull it.
