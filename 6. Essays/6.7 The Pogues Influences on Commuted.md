# **THE POGUES INFLUENCES ON COMMUTED**

---

The Pogues—boozy, blistered, and unapologetically Irish—were a spectral influence on Jeff Evans during Commuted’s early years. Not overt. Not namechecked on stage. But they haunted his songwriting like the aftertaste of whiskey on a Monday morning. Jeff didn’t just *like* The Pogues; he revered them, especially in the early '90s, when he was wrestling with rural Ohio’s emotional repression and needed models of lyrical combustion. Shane MacGowan gave him permission to be literate *and* filthy, poetic *and* self-destructive. “Rain on My Parade” was reportedly drafted the same week Jeff binged *Rum Sodomy & the Lash* on repeat while nursing a broken finger and a panic disorder. That album taught him how to wield a melody like a shiv.

Gilley, meanwhile, *loathed* The Pogues. Called them “drunk leprechaun war crime music” during a van ride through Indiana in 1993 and refused to sing anything in triple meter for months afterward. The disdain was visceral. Gilley saw himself as Elvis gone glam, not Shane with a hangover. Pogues-style folk-punk made him twitch. He said the tin whistle sounded like a car alarm in hell. And every time Jeff slipped a 6/8 rhythm into a demo, Gilley would glare, mutter something about “celts with accordions,” and sabotage rehearsal by swapping chords for surf-rock licks until Bill threatened to replace him with a fiddle-playing cousin from Toledo just to make a point.

But here’s the fucked-up twist: the tension worked. Jeff would sneak Pogues cadences into bridges, Kathy would pick them out on piano and reshape them with velvet claws, and Gilley—sweating, gritting, furious—would wrap them in barbed-wire charm just to prove he was better. That clash, that dissonance, *that* was Commuted. The Pogues were the grit in Jeff’s engine, and Gilley’s hatred was the friction that made it combust.
