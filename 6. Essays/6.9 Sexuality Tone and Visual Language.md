**SEXUALITY: TONE & VISUAL LANGUAGE –** ***Commuted***

**Mandate:**  
Sex is not a subplot in *Commuted* — it is a primary language of power, connection, rebellion, and survival. It is treated with the same narrative gravity and creative intentionality as dialogue, music, and violence. There is no fade-to-black. There is no suggestive shorthand. Sex is explicit not to titillate, but to excavate emotional truth.

---

**1. Philosophy of Depiction:**

- **Sex is sacred.** Even when casual, exploitative, or ill-advised, every sexual moment matters because it reveals or distorts something core about the characters.
- **Sex is bodily memory.** Every scene must be rooted in *felt physicality*. The viewer should remember what it’s like to be kissed badly. To be breathless. To ache after. To want more and get less.
- **Sex is story.** We depict sex even if it does not drive character or theme. If a scene can exist without its sexual element, it has a potential place in this show.
    
---

**2. Core Elements Required in Realistic Portrayal:**

- **Foreplay** – the decision to engage is as important as the act.
- **Intercourse** – not stylized, but weighted. Variations in rhythm, initiation, pacing.
- **Orgasm** – may or may not occur. Its presence or absence is always a beat.
- **Aftercare** – what follows matters. Eye contact? Withdrawal? Shame? Laughter?
   
If these elements are skipped, the absence must be intentional and meaningful.

---

**3. Bodily Sacred Memory Activation (BSMA):** This is the target tone. Every sex scene should evoke a *visceral sense memory* in the viewer: the tremble, the scent, the post-climax dizziness, the awkward sock, the sticky hands. Not pornographic — *recognizable*. Honest. Physical. Lived-in.

---

**4. Messy Physical Realities – Required, Not Optional:**

- Sweat, scent, breath 
- Smudged eyeliner, uneven socks
- Cum, stickiness, fluids, damp bedsheets   
- Unintended noises (a phone buzzes, a belt clinks, a laugh escapes)   
- Bodily awkwardness: missed buttons, tangled jeans, knee cramps

If it would happen in real life and deepens the emotional or psychological beat — it belongs.

---

**5. Visual Rules for Sex Scenes:**

- **No standard coverage.** Shoot entirely from one character’s emotional POV. If necessary, switch POV halfway through to reflect a power shift.
- **No montage, no music overlay.** Real-time. Let silences stretch. Let breath be the score.
- **Lighting:** Use practical light sources — a bedside lamp, a passing car, fluorescent flicker.
- **Blocking:** Must feel accidental, not choreographed. Sweat smears. Sheets cling. Rhythm stutters.
- **No objectivity.** The lens must always be aligned with a character’s emotional interior — lust, grief, desperation, euphoria, numbness.

---

**6. Tone Guide (Directors, Writers, Actors):** Sex is:

- Sometimes divine
- Sometimes transactional
- Sometimes confusing
- Never neutral
- Always revealing

If a sex scene makes the audience feel *comfortable*, it’s wrong. They should feel what the characters feel — vulnerable, hungry, overwhelmed, powerful, exposed.

---

**7. Meta-Narrative Function:** Sex in *Commuted* evolves:

- In early episodes: discovery, rebellion, awkwardness
- In mid-seasons: power plays, self-destruction, artistic use of sex as currency
- By 2029: memory, regret, longing — the cost of what was once careless
   
Every act of intimacy leaves an echo. The body remembers what the mind forgets.

---

**Final Rule:** If the audience forgets it five minutes later — it was a lie. If they carry it in their stomach for the rest of the episode — it was true.
