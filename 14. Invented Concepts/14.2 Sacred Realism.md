**Sacred realism** is the official Commuted doctrine and my personal gospel. It is the fusion of holy and filthy, the practice of writing memory as if the world is both haunted and sweating. It is not a genre. It is not magical realism, which lets the impossible slip into reality. Sacred realism is sensory and bodily, intensely *here*—but it renders that bodily experience as mythic, as sacrament, as story so naked you can smell its sweat. It refuses euphemism and refuses apology.

### In Sacred Realism:

- **Everything is vivid, everything is flesh**: Sweat, spit, blood, cum, breath, noise—if it happens in the body, it happens in the archive, and it matters. There’s no fade-to-black, no retreat to abstraction. We don’t cut away from sex or violence or tears or laughter. We don’t sanitize. We bear witness.

- **The holy and the profane are twins**: Sacred realism never draws a line between prayer and fucking, between laughter and blasphemy. You write someone gasping “God” in the same breath as you write them clutching a lover’s hips. Sex is not just an act but a kind of revelation—messy, awkward, and true.

- **Every scene is lit like a cathedral**: That means scene headers are sacred—INT. MOTEL ROOM, ST. PAUL, 2:17 A.M.—each one an altar. Weather, geography, time, light: all of them are sensory cues to the emotional and spiritual stakes. We are always somewhere, always somewhen. There is no neutral, empty space in sacred realism.

- **Dialogue is breath, not script**: Dialogue isn’t there to move the plot, it’s there to reveal power, desire, struggle, and genius. People talk over each other, wound each other, seduce each other, and fail to say what they mean. It’s sweaty. It’s confessional. It’s half-fight, half-flirt, always alive.

- **Power, sex, failure, and genius are written naked**: We record power struggles as they are—ugly, erotic, or both. Erotic entanglements are described without euphemism. Genius appears without humility, failure without apology. We are not modest. We are not kind. We are not “objective.” We are honest in the way only the desperate can be honest.

- **Everything is confession, nothing is shame**: The writing is memoir, not memoir’s polite cousin. It’s myth, not myth’s bedtime story. When someone fucks up, or fucks someone, or fucks *themselves*—we write it as if it’s scripture. Because it is.

### Canonical Example:

> *“Put prayer next to fucking. Let them say ‘God’ with a hand in their hair and their knees on the mattress. Nothing is too holy for sweat. Nothing is too dirty for light.”*

### The Point

Sacred realism is writing the truth so raw, so detailed, so unflinching that it becomes holy—whether it’s a hymn or a howl. It’s what lets Commuted’s history be not just a story but a scripture. The dirt is the miracle.

If you want to write like this, you don’t flinch from the messy, the carnal, or the contradictory. You don’t worship the facts; you worship the experience. You document like a voyeur, but confess like a priest. And you always, always, *always* remember that the divine and the depraved exist in the same sweaty room.
